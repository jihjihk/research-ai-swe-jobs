# T17 Geographic Market Structure

## Headline finding
Geography matters, but it does not rescue a clean “junior collapse” story. After excluding multi-location rows, only 17 metros clear the `>=50` postings-in-both-periods threshold, and the remote bucket is dead: `is_remote_inferred` is 0 in every selected metro in scraped 2026. Across those metros, AI/LLM language, scope language, and cleaned-text length all rise broadly, but the relation between AI growth and entry change depends on the seniority instrument. Explicit `seniority_final` entry declines where AI grows fastest; the YOE proxy does not tell the same story.

## What we learned
The metro frame is usable but narrow. The primary sample excludes 3,007 multi-location SWE postings and 11,307 SWE rows with no metro at all. Of the remaining metro-coded rows, 17 metros clear `>=50` postings in both arshkon and scraped. That is enough for a descriptive geography section, not enough for a grand regional theory.

AI / LLM growth is broad, not just coastal. The biggest increases in AI / LLM family share show up in Houston (+11.1 pp), Dallas-Fort Worth (+9.6), Chicago (+9.3), Boston (+8.8), Austin (+8.8), Charlotte (+8.3), Philadelphia (+8.2), and Seattle (+7.8). That means the AI re-centering is not confined to the Bay Area or Seattle, even though those metros also rise.

Scope inflation is geographically widespread. The strongest scope gains are San Diego (+26.6 pp), Phoenix (+23.7), San Francisco (+23.2), Minneapolis (+22.9), Atlanta (+22.0), Austin (+21.8), and Chicago (+21.4). The pattern looks like a broad posting-language shift rather than a small number of hubs rewriting the market for everyone else.

The entry signal is instrument-sensitive at the metro level. Under `seniority_final`, explicit entry falls most in Phoenix (-5.6 pp), Houston (-5.6), Charlotte (-5.5), Atlanta (-3.5), and San Francisco (-2.2). It rises in Raleigh (+4.0), Chicago (+2.1), Detroit (+1.6), Philadelphia (+1.5), and Los Angeles (+1.3). Under the YOE proxy, the leaders are different: San Diego (+10.5 pp), Philadelphia (+10.4), San Francisco (+8.7), Los Angeles (+7.6), Denver (+5.2), Seattle (+4.8), and Boston (+3.4) rise, while Detroit (-12.8), Houston (-7.0), Austin (-5.8), Charlotte (-4.3), and Minneapolis (-3.8) fall.

The AI-entry relationship also depends on the instrument. Across metros, `delta_ai_any_share` correlates negatively with `delta_entry_final_share` (`r = -0.35`, Spearman `-0.44`), but only weakly and positively with `delta_entry_yoe_share` (`r = 0.19`, Spearman `0.04`). So the geography does not stabilize the seniority measurement problem; it makes it more visible.

## What surprised us
Remote work is not a usable geography dimension here. Every selected metro has remote share 0 in scraped 2026, which means the remote field is either uninformative or missing in a way that is too systematic to use. I would treat that as a data artifact, not a substantive null finding.

The geography of AI/domain recomposition is broader than expected. The strongest AI / LLM family gains are not concentrated only in SF and Seattle. Houston, Dallas-Fort Worth, Chicago, Boston, Austin, Charlotte, and Philadelphia all sit near the top. That is a stronger market-recomposition signal than a pure coastal-hub narrative would predict.

## Evidence assessment
The metro-level change tables are strong enough for descriptive claims because the selected panel is balanced on both periods and the no-aggregator and cap-25 sensitivities preserve the sign of the main changes. The caveat is that metro-level correlations are still just 17 points, so they are suggestive rather than causal.

The sensitivity summary is reassuring. No-aggregator and cap-25 versions are close to the primary on the main metrics, with mean absolute differences that are small relative to the absolute level of the shifts. The fragile piece is again the YOE proxy on entry: some metro sign flips appear under the no-aggregator cut, which is another reminder that the proxy is useful but not a clean identity with `seniority_final`.

## Narrative evaluation
The geography evidence weakens any hub-only or coastal-only framing. It also does not support a clean geography-first paper. The more defensible story is that the posting language shift is broad across metros, while the seniority interpretation remains instrument-dependent.

The best alternative framing is “broad regional recomposition with uneven intensity.” I prefer that over “AI jobs moved to a few hubs” because the AI / LLM growth appears across Texas, the Midwest, the Northeast, and the West at the same time.

## Emerging narrative
Metro structure is a modifier, not the main mechanism. Some cities show bigger explicit-entry declines, some show bigger YOE-junior gains, and some show both AI and scope expansion without a clear seniority story. The core market-restructuring narrative is still company- and template-driven; geography mostly tells us where the effect is stronger or weaker.

## Research question evolution
Geography should stay secondary. The useful question is not “which city lost juniors,” but:

`RQ1 adjunct: Are AI/LLM and scope changes geographically concentrated, or are they broadly distributed across SWE labor markets?`

That is a better fit for this dataset than a standalone regional labor-market claim.

## Gaps and weaknesses
`is_remote_inferred` is not usable here because the remote rate is identically zero in the selected metro frame. The metro work also excludes a large no-metro and multi-location pool, so it cannot describe the whole platform surface. Finally, the metro sample is only 17 cities, so correlation claims should stay modest.

## Direction for downstream analysis
Use the geography results as context and as a robustness layer, not as the lead. The next wave should still prioritize cross-occupation specificity, company strategy, and the ghost/aspirational requirement checks.

## Current paper positioning
Geography is a supporting section, not the headline. Its main value is that it shows the AI/scope shift is broad across metros and that the explicit-entry result is not just a Bay Area artifact. The stronger lead remains the company strategy recomposition story from T16.

# T03. Seniority Label Audit

## Headline finding

`seniority_final` is a high-precision but low-recall junior detector. When it labels a row as `entry`, that row is usually genuinely junior by YOE. But it leaves most junior-like postings in `unknown`, so it cannot be used as the only measure of entry-level share. The native LinkedIn label is also unstable across snapshots: arshkon `entry` rows are much less junior by YOE than scraped LinkedIn `entry` rows.

## Methodology

I audited the LinkedIn English/date-ok frame only: 1,186,281 rows total, including 59,972 SWE rows, 22,279 SWE-adjacent rows, and 151,267 control rows. The seniority audit used SWE rows, with native-label comparisons restricted to arshkon and scraped LinkedIn because those are the comparable LinkedIn sources.

Checks run:

1. `seniority_final_source` composition by source and period.
2. `seniority_final` vs `seniority_native` confusion matrices and Cohen's kappa on arshkon and scraped LinkedIn.
3. Native-label quality via the YOE distribution of `seniority_native = 'entry'` rows.
4. Junior-share comparison under `seniority_final`, arshkon-only `seniority_native`, and the label-independent YOE proxy (`yoe_extracted <= 2`), plus a no-aggregator sensitivity.
5. A 100-row LLM-routed spot-check. Only 16 rows in the SWE corpus had the requested weak title-level markers (`I/II/III`, `L3`, `Level 4`, etc.), so I inspected all 16 plus 84 additional LLM-routed rows. The weak-marker subset is the telling part here.

## What we learned

| Metric | Arshkon (2024-04) | Asaniczka (2024-01) | Scraped LinkedIn (2026-03) | Scraped LinkedIn (2026-04) |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| `seniority_final = entry` share | 3.73% | 0.87% | 3.35% | 2.18% |
| `yoe_extracted <= 2` share | 14.98% | 10.36% | 17.38% | 16.97% |
| `seniority_final = unknown` share | 0.2% | 28.2% | 44.9% | 50.3% |

The production label is very conservative. It identifies a small set of clearly junior rows, but it leaves roughly half of 2026 scraped LinkedIn SWE postings as `unknown`.

The native baseline is not a clean junior benchmark. Among `seniority_native = 'entry'` rows with YOE:

| Source | n | Mean YOE | Median YOE | `yoe <= 2` |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| arshkon | 458 | 4.18 | 3.0 | 28.6% |
| scraped LinkedIn | 1,659 | 2.36 | 2.0 | 81.0% |

That is a large cross-snapshot shift in the meaning of the native entry label.

`seniority_final = entry` rows themselves are much cleaner:

| Period | n | Mean YOE | Median YOE | `yoe <= 2` |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| 2024-04 | 65 | 2.05 | 2.0 | 78.5% |
| 2024-01 | 77 | 2.52 | 2.0 | 63.6% |
| 2026-03 | 138 | 2.03 | 2.0 | 80.4% |
| 2026-04 | 81 | 1.84 | 2.0 | 80.2% |

So the model is not hallucinating junior roles. It is mostly missing them.

Agreement with native labels is weak:

| Subset | n | Cohen's kappa | Exact accuracy |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| arshkon raw | 3,283 | 0.175 | 44.3% |
| arshkon canonicalized (`intern -> entry`, `executive -> director`) | 3,283 | 0.184 | 45.0% |
| scraped LinkedIn raw | 31,481 | 0.108 | 40.3% |
| scraped LinkedIn canonicalized | 31,481 | 0.131 | 41.9% |

The canonicalization hardly changes the result. The disagreement is not just legacy-label noise.

## What surprised us

Two things matter here.

First, the arshkon native `entry` bucket is not junior in YOE terms. Its median YOE is 3.0 and only 28.6% are at `<= 2` years. Scraped LinkedIn native `entry` rows are much cleaner. That is strong evidence that the native classifier changed semantics across snapshots.

Second, the LLM-routed weak-marker subset is almost entirely unresolved: 15 of the 16 weak-marker rows stayed `unknown`, with only one `mid-senior` label. This is not a routing catastrophe so much as a coverage limit. Titles with explicit numeric level codes exist, but the production seniority system leaves them mostly unlabeled.

## Evidence assessment

`seniority_final = entry` is strong on precision, weak on recall. The YOE profile of final entry rows is coherent across periods, and the no-aggregator sensitivity barely moves the entry shares. That makes the final label usable as a high-confidence junior subset.

But the junior-share story does not hold up if we use it as the main estimator of entry prevalence. The YOE proxy says junior-like postings are around 15-17% of SWE rows, while `seniority_final` says 2-4%. The 2024-to-2026 change under `seniority_final` is smaller than the arshkon-vs-asaniczka gap under the YOE proxy, so the direction is not clearly above instrument noise.

## Narrative evaluation

- **RQ1, employer-side restructuring:** weakened for the junior-share component. The data do not support a simple "junior share fell" claim if the YOE proxy is treated as the primary junior measure.
- **RQ2, task and requirement migration:** still plausible, but junior-level claims need a better measurement backbone than `seniority_final`.
- **RQ3, employer-requirement / worker-usage divergence:** unaffected by this audit, but any seniority-stratified divergence analysis must remember that `seniority_final` is conservative.
- **RQ4, mechanisms:** not evaluated here.

The strongest alternative framing is not "junior roles disappeared." It is: explicit seniority labeling became more conservative while a substantial pool of junior-like postings remained hidden in `unknown`. That is a measurement story first, and only a labor-market story second.

## Emerging narrative

`seniority_final` is the right column for coarse, high-confidence stratification and senior-role language work. It is not the right standalone measure for junior incidence. For RQ1, the YOE proxy should lead, with `seniority_final` used as a conservative lower bound and `seniority_native` as a diagnostic only.

## Recommendation

`seniority_final` is defensible as the primary seniority column for downstream exploration only if the downstream question is "what do explicit seniority signals say?" It is **not** defensible as the sole estimator of entry-level share. For any junior-share claim, lead with `yoe_extracted <= 2`, report `seniority_final` as a strict subset, and keep `seniority_native` arshkon-only as a diagnostic check.

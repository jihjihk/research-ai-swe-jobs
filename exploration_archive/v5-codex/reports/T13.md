# Gate 2 T13 Research Memo

## What we learned
The strongest finding is that posting length growth is real, but it is not just "more requirements." On the capped LinkedIn SWE corpus, mean cleaned text length rises from 1,969 chars in arshkon 2024-04 and 2,281 in asaniczka 2024-01 to 2,644 in scraped 2026, which is well above the within-2024 baseline difference of 312 chars. The structural story is even sharper: explicit core sections are rare in 2024, but common in 2026. Only 1.6% of arshkon docs and 4.5% of asaniczka docs have any detected core section, versus 60.8% and 62.2% in the two scraped periods.

That means the length shift is not just textual inflation. It is also a formatting shift. In the section composition table, historical postings are overwhelmingly unclassified by the parser (98.2% arshkon, 95.3% asaniczka), while scraped postings allocate a much larger share of characters to role_summary, responsibilities, requirements, and preferred sections. Core sections plus preferred/benefits/about-company/legal account for roughly 1.7% of arshkon text, 4.3% of asaniczka text, and 37.8% to 38.0% of scraped text. The posting template itself changed materially.

The LLM-text surface is also large enough to matter on the scraped side, but not uniform. In the current text-source counts, scraped 2026-03 has 3,979 rows labeled `llm` and 15,817 labeled `raw`; scraped 2026-04 has 1,987 `llm` rows and 15,369 `raw` rows. That is why the primary text-sensitive analyses should stay on `text_source = 'llm'`, with raw text reserved for recall-sensitive checks.

## What surprised us
The biggest surprise is how thin the explicit section signal is in the historical snapshots. That is not a parser failure. It is evidence that most 2024 postings do not expose a stable sectional anatomy, so any "requirements vs boilerplate" analysis has to accept that the earlier corpus is mostly free text.

The other surprise is that cleaned text is consistently harder to read than raw text. In the readability calibration sample, cleaned FK grade is around 20.9 to 21.6 versus 17.6 to 18.1 raw, and cleaned average sentence length is roughly 29 to 32 words versus 24 to 26 raw. The cleaned corpus is not a simpler, more canonical surface; it is a denser one.

## Evidence assessment
The section parser is moderate-evidence infrastructure: transparent, regex-based, and easy to audit, but coarse. Its main value is comparative, not exhaustive. The coverage result is strong enough to matter because the historical-vs-scraped gap is large and stable across both 2026 months.

The length and tone statistics are stronger evidence because they use the capped corpus broadly. The within-2024 calibration matters here. For example, the calibration ratio for cleaned text length is 2.16, while the ratio for core share is 13.6 and for section_chars_requirements 48.8. Those are not subtle changes. They are large enough that a pure noise explanation is implausible.

Readability is moderate evidence because it comes from a 7,894-row sample, but the sampled pattern is internally coherent: entry-level scraped postings are shorter and somewhat easier to read than mid-senior scraped postings, yet both are materially longer and structurally richer than 2024 counterparts. In scraped 2026-03, entry postings average 2,253 chars with mean core_share 0.439 and mean sentence length 27.6, while mid-senior postings average 2,856 chars with mean core_share 0.371 and mean sentence length 31.6. The same pattern holds in 2026-04.

## Narrative evaluation
RQ1 as written is only partially supported by T13. The evidence does not yet justify a junior-rung headline. What it does support is a broader posting-restructuring claim: SWE ads became longer, more explicitly sectioned, and more variable in how much space they devote to role content versus boilerplate and company language.

RQ2 is strengthened, but only as a decomposition question. The right question is not whether "requirements grew" in the abstract. It is which sections expanded, and whether that expansion is concentrated in responsibilities/requirements or spread across marketing, legal, and company-description text. On the current evidence, the answer is: both happened, with the structural shift itself being substantial.

The alternative framing that fits the data best is template evolution rather than raw demand inflation. A second plausible framing is requirement expansion, but it is weaker because the parser shows large gains in unclassified-to-structured text and not just in requirement-specific text. I prefer template evolution as the lead interpretation because it explains both the length growth and the section-coverage jump without overclaiming about employer demand.

## Emerging narrative
The posting surface changed materially between 2024 and 2026. The most publishable implication is methodological as much as substantive: later text analyses cannot assume that the same amount of text means the same type of text. The market may have changed, but so did the document form through which the market speaks.

That is a strong constraint for T12 and later stages. The main text signal should come from section-filtered or otherwise normalized surfaces, not from raw length alone. Raw text can still be used for recall-sensitive checks, but it should not be the primary estimate.

## Research question evolution
RQ1 should be reframed away from "junior share/volume" as the immediate T13 lead. A better current formulation is: how did the structure of SWE postings change between 2024 and 2026, and does that structural change concentrate in core role content or in boilerplate/template sections?

RQ2 should become: which sections of postings expanded or contracted, and how much of the growth is actually requirements/responsibilities content rather than generalized length inflation? This keeps the original intent but makes it compatible with what the data actually show.

## Gaps and weaknesses
The section classifier misses unmarked structure, so "unclassified" is a real residual category, not pure noise. That limits how sharply we can split boilerplate from content in the historical data.

Readability is only a calibrated sample, not a full-corpus measurement. I would not build a headline claim on small entry-level slices alone. The entry-vs-mid-senior split is suggestive but still subordinate to the broader structural finding.

## Direction for next wave
Use the section spans to isolate the requirement and responsibility surface in T12, but keep a raw-text sensitivity branch for recall. The key test is whether the apparent 2024-to-2026 term shifts survive when boilerplate and section-template drift are stripped away.

## Current paper positioning
T13 pushes the paper toward a measurement-validity plus restructuring story. The strongest contribution so far is not "longer postings" by itself. It is that the document structure of SWE ads changed enough that any later language-shift claim has to be conditioned on sectioning, not just token counts.

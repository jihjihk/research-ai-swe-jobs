# T22 Ghost & Aspirational Requirements Forensics

## Headline finding
The strongest signal is not outright impossible credentials. It is a combination of kitchen-sink bundling, aspirational wording, and template repetition. On the section-filtered LLM core frame, AI-mentioning postings have a higher hedge-to-firm ratio than non-AI postings (`0.73` vs `0.52` aggregate), and that gap survives the raw full-text sensitivity (`1.00` vs `0.80`). Direct employers are slightly more ghost-like on the composite score than aggregators, but aggregators are more aspirational on the hedge/firm ratio.

## Methodology
Primary analysis uses LinkedIn SWE only, `llm`-labeled core sections from `t13_section_spans.parquet` (`role_summary`, `responsibilities`, `requirements`, `preferred`). I use the shared tech matrix for distinct technology counts, then layer on:

- `kitchen_sink_product = tech_count × scope_count`
- aspiration ratio = hedging terms / firm requirement terms
- YOE-scope mismatch = explicit `entry` plus `yoe_extracted >= 5` or `>= 3` senior-scope terms
- credential impossibility = degree contradictions and “no experience” versus long-YOE contradictions
- template saturation = mean pairwise cosine similarity of core-section text within company

The shared artifact [validated_mgmt_patterns.json](/home/jihgaboot/gabor/job-research/exploration/artifacts/shared/validated_mgmt_patterns.json) keeps `scope_strict`, `senior_scope`, and `management_strong` as primary patterns and demotes `management_broad` to sensitivity only.

## What we learned
- The primary ghost-like cases are “more of everything” postings, not pure seniority lies. The top entry-level examples include clear YOE/scope tension, but the more common signal is heavy bundling of scope, tech, and AI language.
- AI language is more aspirational than traditional language in aggregate. In the core-section frame, AI postings show a `0.73` hedge/firm ratio versus `0.52` for non-AI postings; on the raw full-text sensitivity the gap remains and widens (`1.00` vs `0.80`).
- Template saturation is real, not just a staffing-agency artifact. Six companies clear the `>0.8` mean-pairwise-cosine threshold on the primary core-section frame, including large direct employers such as Walmart, VAST Data, Ashley Furniture Industries, and West Pharmaceutical Services, plus obvious template-driven vendors like Synectic Solutions and Lorvenk.
- The composite ghost score is not dominated by aggregators. Direct employers are a bit more ghost-like overall (`0.185` vs `0.128`), even though aggregators have a higher aspiration ratio.

## What surprised us
- The hard YOE mismatch flag is rare in the cleaned core frame. Many “ghost-like” postings are not invalid on a literal credential basis; they are overloaded, templated, or aspirational instead.
- The companies with the highest template similarity are not all obvious staffing intermediaries. Some are large direct employers with repeated, highly standardized posting templates.
- In raw full text, AI-posting aspiration can look stronger than in the section-filtered core frame. That means boilerplate and document form still matter, but they do not erase the AI-versus-non-AI gap.

## Evidence assessment
- **AI aspiration gap:** strong. It appears in both the section-filtered core frame and the raw full-text sensitivity.
- **Template saturation:** moderate-to-strong. The top companies are easy to interpret, but the fit is sensitive to repetitive templates and the 3-posting floor.
- **YOE-scope mismatch:** weak-to-moderate. It exists, but it is sparse.
- **Credential impossibility:** weak. Useful as a hard validity flag, but not the main story.
- **Broad management patterns:** weak. The validation artifact keeps them only as sensitivity.

## Sensitivity checks
- **Aggregator exclusion:** direct employers are slightly more ghost-like overall, but aggregators show higher aspiration ratios.
- **Text source:** the AI aspiration gap survives raw full text.
- **Company saturation floor:** I require at least 3 postings per company; that removes trivial 2-post duplicates and leaves 280 companies in the saturation table.
- **Management validation:** broad management stays out of the primary score, per the shared JSON.

## Narrative evaluation
RQ2 is not just “requirements moved downward.” T22 supports a more precise mechanism story: the market is producing more aspirational, template-like, multi-constraint postings, especially around AI and scope. The evidence for literal ghost requirements exists, but it is not the dominant phenomenon.

## Emerging narrative
The cleanest framing is that SWE hiring language is becoming more bundled and more aspirational, with AI terms used in ways that look more like signaling and templating than a one-to-one screening requirement. The direct-vs-aggregator split is real, but it is secondary to the broader template/aspiration shift.

## Research question evolution
Keep RQ2, but sharpen it to distinguish:

`RQ2a: Which requirements expanded in bundled form?`

`RQ2b: Which AI and scope mentions are aspirational or template-like rather than hard screening requirements?`

That is more faithful to the evidence than a simple migration narrative.

## Gaps and weaknesses
- The primary frame is only 3,983 section-filtered LLM rows, so thin cells are a real constraint.
- Template saturation is still a heuristic. It is good for ranking companies, not for causal claims.
- The ghost score should not be read as a latent variable. It is a ranking device for inspection.

## Direction for next wave
Use T22 as a validity check when discussing scope and AI in the synthesis: AI is real, but it is also more aspirational and more template-sensitive than the strongest version of the demand story would imply.

## Current paper positioning
T22 strengthens the paper’s methods-validity backbone. The contribution is not “we found ghost jobs.” The contribution is that the posting language itself is shifting toward more aspirational, multi-constraint templates, and that shift has to be controlled before any labor-market interpretation.

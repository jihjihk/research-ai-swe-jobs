# T06 Company Concentration Deep Investigation

## Headline finding
Company concentration is not a side issue in this dataset. On the LinkedIn SWE frame, the top 50 employers account for 20.9% of arshkon postings, 30.6% of asaniczka postings, and 27.2% of scraped postings; those shares remain 18.3%, 23.5%, and 25.6% after aggregators are removed. Entry-level postings are even more concentrated: most firms with at least five SWE postings never post an entry role at all, and the companies that do post entry roles are a narrow subset. The overlap-panel decomposition shows that the 2024-to-2026 change is partly within-company, but company composition still matters a lot, especially for the YOE-based junior proxy.

## What we learned
- Concentration is materially high in every source. The Gini coefficient is 0.43 in arshkon, 0.64 in asaniczka, and 0.69 in scraped. The top company share is 1.1% in arshkon, 5.0% in asaniczka, and 4.0% in scraped.
- Residual exact duplicate templates exist but are limited. Only 14 arshkon companies, 42 asaniczka companies, and 112 scraped companies have more postings than distinct description hashes. Among companies with at least five SWE postings, that is 7, 27, and 94 companies respectively.
- Entry posting is extremely employer-skewed. Under `seniority_final`, 79.3% of arshkon companies with at least five SWE postings have zero entry postings, 90.8% in asaniczka, and 82.0% in scraped. Under the YOE proxy, those zero-entry shares are still 56.7%, 58.1%, and 52.8%.
- `seniority_final` and the YOE proxy do not identify the same specialist employers. `seniority_final` only surfaces a tiny set of clear entry specialists, while the YOE proxy surfaces a much broader set that includes Microsoft, Leidos, Lowe's, Medtronic, Wells Fargo, Google, Walmart, Qualcomm, and SpaceX.
- The 2026 source is mostly made of new firms. Of the scraped LinkedIn companies, 6,527 are new relative to arshkon, versus 983 returning companies. Those new entrants are slightly more entry-heavy and a bit longer on average than the returning firms.

## What surprised us
- The strongest junior concentration signal depends on the seniority instrument. `seniority_final` shows a slight decline in entry share from 2024 to 2026, while the YOE proxy shows a sizable increase. That is not a cosmetic difference; it changes the direction of the apparent story.
- The overlap-panel decomposition says the junior story is largely about company composition when the YOE proxy is used. The within-company component is near zero for entry_yoe, while the between-company component explains almost all of the change.
- The residual duplicate-template problem is real but much smaller than the older pipeline behavior that motivated this task. The multi-location fix seems to have removed the broad, obvious duplication issue; what remains is a concentrated set of staffing or templated employers.

## Evidence assessment
- Concentration metrics: strong evidence. Large samples, no modeling assumptions, and the pattern survives aggregator exclusion.
- Duplicate-template audit: strong as a diagnostic. The residual duplicates are not huge in count, but they are structurally important because they can dominate naïve corpus-level metrics.
- Entry concentration: strong evidence that entry roles are employer-specific, not market-wide. The zero-entry shares among firms with at least five SWE postings are too large to ignore.
- Decomposition: moderate evidence. It is panel-restricted to firms present in both arshkon and scraped LinkedIn with at least five SWE postings, and the text proxies are coarse, but the within-versus-between split is still informative.
- New entrants: strong evidence of source-composition shift. The 2026 source is dominated by firms that are absent from the 2024 benchmark.

## Entry concentration
The entry-level pool is concentrated enough that aggregate entry-share plots are misleading if read as “the market.” The company-level stats show a median company entry share of zero under `seniority_final` in every source, and the YOE proxy only raises the median to zero while pushing the upper tail up sharply.

The clearest entry specialists under `seniority_final` are:

- arshkon: `SynergisticIT`
- scraped: `SynergisticIT`, `WayUp`, `Emonics LLC`, `Tech Consulting`, `CGI Technologies and Solutions`, `SkillStorm`, `Bose Corporation`

Under the YOE proxy, the specialist set is broader and more plausible as a junior-market lens:

- arshkon: `Microsoft`, `Leidos`, `Lowe's Companies, Inc.`, `Cogent Communications`, `Tech Consulting`, `Qualcomm`, `Macquarie Group`
- asaniczka: `Medtronic`, `Wells Fargo`, `Ball Aerospace`, `Anyscale`, `RoviSys`, `ADP`, `Bell Flight`, `The Reynolds and Reynolds Company`, `Alcon`, `Lark`
- scraped: `Google`, `Walmart`, `Qualcomm`, `SpaceX`, `Visa`, `VIZIO`, `AaraTechnologies Inc`, `Latitude AI`, `Super Micro Computer Spain, S.L.`, `Snap Inc.`

That gap is one of the most important measurement findings in the exploration.

## Decomposition
The decomposition table is the cleanest way to separate firm behavior from firm composition.

- `entry_final`: 2024 = 3.16%, 2026 = 2.64%, change = -0.52 pp. Within-company change is -0.87 pp and between-company change is +0.35 pp, so the within-company decline is partially offset by composition.
- `entry_yoe`: 2024 = 9.10%, 2026 = 15.88%, change = +6.78 pp. The within-company component is essentially zero, while the between-company component is +6.80 pp. That means the YOE-based junior rise is mostly a composition story.
- `ai_mention`: 2024 = 13.8%, 2026 = 53.1%, change = +39.3 pp. About 71% of that change is within-company, so the AI-language rise is not just new firms entering the panel.
- `description_length`: 2024 = 3,506 chars, 2026 = 5,277 chars, change = +1,771 chars. Roughly 54% of that change is within-company and 46% is between-company.
- `tech_count`: 2024 = 5.25, 2026 = 6.33, change = +1.08. The within-company component is positive, while the between-company component is slightly negative, so company mix dampens the tech-count rise.

The key interpretive point is that junior hiring is not one thing. The `seniority_final` version and the YOE proxy point in different directions, and the YOE proxy is much more composition-driven.

## Prediction table
The concentration prediction table should be treated as a routing guide for Wave 2 and Wave 3:

- `entry_share`: high concentration risk
- `ai_mention_rate`: moderate-high concentration risk
- `description_length`: moderate concentration risk
- `term_frequencies`: high concentration risk
- `topic_models`: high concentration risk
- `co_occurrence_networks`: high concentration risk

The practical default is simple: cap by company, deduplicate exact templates, and report company-weighted versions whenever a corpus-level metric is used.

## Sensitivity checks
Aggregator exclusion matters, but it does not change the qualitative ranking.

- Top-50 share falls from 20.9% to 18.3% in arshkon, 30.6% to 23.5% in asaniczka, and 27.2% to 25.6% in scraped.
- The concentration picture stays steep even after the exclusion.
- The entry concentration story is still extremely employer-skewed after the exclusion.

That means aggregators amplify the concentration problem, but they are not the whole problem.

## Data quality caveats
- The company counts are based on known canonical company names. Missing company names are rare in arshkon and essentially absent elsewhere, but they are still tracked separately in the concentration table.
- The duplicate-template audit is about exact description hashes, so it undercounts near-duplicate boilerplate collisions. It is a diagnostic, not a complete deduplication audit.
- The decomposition uses companies with at least five SWE postings in both arshkon and scraped LinkedIn. It is intentionally a panel of surviving employers, not the whole market.
- AI mention and tech-count decomposition pieces are coarse raw-text proxies. They are good enough to show concentration sensitivity, but not yet a substitute for the later cleaned-text pipeline.

## Action items for downstream tasks
- Treat any corpus-level term-frequency or topic result as concentration-sensitive by default.
- Use company caps and deduplication before building term networks or topic models.
- Read the entry-specialist tables before making claims about “junior hiring”; the answer depends on the seniority instrument.
- When a later result looks like a market-wide shift, check whether it is actually new-company composition or a narrow group of repeated employers.
- Use the prediction table as the first stop before Wave 2 or Wave 3 text work.

# T10 Title Taxonomy Evolution

## Headline finding
The title landscape is lexically noisy, but not clearly more fragmented in 2026. Unique titles per 1,000 SWE postings are basically flat across the historical baseline and the scraped window: arshkon is 460.9, asaniczka is 417.9, and scraped is 457.3. After removing aggregators, the rate is still close: 430.0 for arshkon versus 400.4 for scraped. The change that does show up cleanly is semantic. Broad AI-related title vocabulary rises sharply, and the new 2026 titles are concentrated in AI/ML, general SWE, embedded/systems, full stack, and data/analytics.

## Methodology
I compared LinkedIn SWE titles only, using arshkon 2024 as the historical baseline and pooled scraped 2026 as the current window. I also used asaniczka as the within-2024 calibration source. The analysis covers:

- title overlap and Jaccard similarity across sources
- unique titles per 1,000 SWE postings, with and without aggregators
- broad AI-related title share and explicit seniority-marker share
- new versus disappeared title lists and theme summaries
- seniority distribution shifts for the 10 most common titles shared by arshkon and scraped
- TF-IDF cosine similarity between aggregate descriptions for those shared titles, using the cleaned-text `llm` subset only

The title-content similarity analysis is text-sensitive, so it uses the cleaned `llm` subset from [`../../../assets/artifacts/shared/swe_cleaned_text.parquet`](../../../assets/artifacts/shared/swe_cleaned_text.parquet). That matters because some historically common titles, especially `dotnet developer`, do not have enough labeled 2026 text to support a clean comparison.

## What we learned
- Exact title overlap is low, but it was already low in 2024. The title-set Jaccard is 0.048 for arshkon vs asaniczka and 0.027 for arshkon vs scraped. That means lexical mismatch is real, but it is not a uniquely 2026 phenomenon.
- The title concentration story is weak. The cross-period change in unique titles per 1,000 is not clearly above the 2024 baseline noise floor. If anything, the no-aggregator view is slightly more standardized in 2026 than in arshkon.
- AI-related title language is the clearest title-level shift. Broad AI/ML/data/agent title share rises from 12.4% in arshkon to 22.7% in scraped. The cleaner `ai_domain` share rises only modestly, from 8.9% to 9.8%, which suggests the big change is broad AI/ML/data naming rather than only pure AI-tool titles.
- New titles are not just AI titles. They are led by General SWE titles, then AI/ML, Embedded/Systems, Full Stack, and Data/Analytics. The disappearing set is mostly legacy stack-specific variants, not a collapse of canonical software titles.
- Same titles are usually still the same jobs, but not perfectly. Average TF-IDF cosine similarity for the top shared titles is 0.715 for arshkon vs scraped, compared with 0.799 for arshkon vs asaniczka. The biggest drifts are in Java, Salesforce, test, and embedded/system titles; `software engineer` and `data engineer` stay comparatively stable.

## What surprised us
- The strongest title finding is not fragmentation. It is title re-anchoring toward AI/ML and adjacent role families. The title count rate barely moves, but the semantics of new titles do.
- Some of the “new” titles are really formatting or modifier variants, such as `software engineer [multiple positions available]` or `software engineer - level`. That means title churn includes posting-format noise, not only occupational change.
- The historical baseline already contains a lot of source-specific lexical drift. That makes a pure “2026 is different” story too simple.

## Evidence assessment
- **Title concentration:** weak. The normalized unique-title rate is nearly flat, and the signal-to-noise ratio versus the 2024 baseline is only 0.08 for unique titles per 1,000. This is not a good headline result.
- **Broad AI-related titles:** strong. The signal-to-noise ratio is 2.77, so the rise is materially above within-2024 variation.
- **Explicit seniority markers in titles:** weak. These are rare to begin with, and the share is small in every source.
- **Title-content alignment:** moderate. The same titles are mostly semantically stable, but the cross-period similarity drop relative to the 2024 baseline is real enough to report. This is strongest for a few title families rather than the whole title space.

## Sensitivity checks
- Aggregator exclusion does not change the core title conclusion.
- The overlap story is stable under the 2024 calibration check: the 2024 baseline already has low overlap and lexical variability.
- The title-content similarity result is text-coverage sensitive. It is based on the cleaned `llm` subset, and `dotnet developer` has no 2026 labeled text, so it drops out of the similarity comparison.

## Narrative evaluation
The original “title taxonomy is fragmenting” story is not the best framing. The data support a more precise one: the title layer is re-anchoring toward AI/ML and specialized platform / systems families, while canonical SWE titles remain dominant and concentration barely moves. That is a language shift, not a wholesale occupation split.

## Emerging narrative
The title layer looks like a lexicon under pressure from AI and specialization, but not a title space that has blown apart. The real novelty is the rise of AI/ML-adjacent naming and the modest semantic drift inside common titles. The market is still using familiar title scaffolds, just with more AI, platform, and infrastructure labeling attached.

## Research question evolution
RQ1 should not be read as just “junior share changed.” Titles suggest an additional sub-question: how did the naming layer itself re-encode SWE work, especially AI/ML and platform specialization?

Proposed addition:

`RQ1b: How did the lexical title layer re-encode SWE roles between 2024 and 2026, especially around AI/ML, platform, embedded/systems, and infrastructure titles?`

## Data quality caveats
- The title space is sparse enough that exact overlap is informative but not decisive on its own.
- Some “new” and “disappeared” titles are formatting variants, not real occupational categories.
- Title-content alignment is coverage-limited because some common titles have thin `llm` text in 2026.

## Direction for next wave
The title results point away from a pure fragmentation narrative and toward semantic re-labeling. The next wave should focus on whether those title changes are matched by actual requirement changes in the text, or whether they are mostly labels on top of similar work.

## Current paper positioning
Title evolution should support the paper, not lead it. The best use of T10 is as evidence that the title layer is drifting toward AI and specialization while leaving the canonical SWE core intact. The main story still needs the requirement-complexity and boilerplate analyses to separate real demand change from naming drift.

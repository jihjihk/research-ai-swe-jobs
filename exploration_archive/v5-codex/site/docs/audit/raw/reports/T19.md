# T19 Temporal Patterns & Rate-of-Change Estimation

## Headline finding
This is not a continuous series. It is two historical snapshots plus a growing scraped window, and the rate story only makes sense at that level.
The clearest change is AI-tool language and requirement stacking: in the cleaned-text frame, AI-tool share rises from 2.56% in arshkon to 20.30% in scraped 2026, and requirement-breadth rises from 5.94 to 6.98. Raw description length also jumps from 2,974 to 4,891 characters. Scope is real too, but the cleaned-text scope proxy is too sparse to use as a lead result; the raw-text sensitivity shows the useful signal.

## What we learned
The observed source windows are uneven by design: asaniczka is 2024-01-12 to 2024-01-17, arshkon is 2024-04-05 to 2024-04-20, and scraped is 2026-03-19 to 2026-04-09.
The seniority gap remains a measurement issue. Explicit entry is only 0.87% in asaniczka, 3.73% in arshkon, and 2.80% in scraped, while the YOE<=2 share is materially larger at 7.34%, 9.29%, and 11.65%.
The source-summary view is more trustworthy than a smooth time-series story. Cleaned-text coverage is 99.7% in arshkon, 85.9% in asaniczka, and only 16.1% in scraped, so the labeled-text rates in 2026 are thin and must be read as such.
The raw sensitivity makes the scope signal usable. Raw scope share rises from 38.9%-39.5% in the historical snapshots to 59.5% in scraped, which is consistent with the broader template-shift story from T18.

## What surprised us
The daily scraped window is batchy, but not stale. The first day is the largest scrape day, yet `posting_age_days` is 1.0 on every scraped day, so this looks more like scrape cadence or platform formatting than a backlog of old postings.
The late-April daily points are very thin on labeled text. By 2026-04-07 through 2026-04-10, labeled counts fall to 3-4 rows per day, so the AI spikes there are noise, not signal.

## Evidence assessment
AI-tool language is the strongest rate result. It survives the 2024 calibration gap and accelerates sharply into 2026; the annualized cross-period change is 0.090 versus 0.031 within 2024, an acceleration ratio of 2.90.
Requirement breadth is also strong. It rises more in the 2026 window than in the 2024 calibration gap, with an acceleration ratio of 1.91.
Description length is directionally consistent but noisier. The raw-text rise is clear, while the cleaned-text length series is less stable because the labeled subset changes across windows.
Scope is a valid sensitivity result, not a cleaned-text lead metric. The cleaned-text extractor returns zero everywhere, so scope should be cited from the raw-text sensitivity only.
The internal arshkon date-bin check is useful as a noise floor: AI share, entry share, YOE<=2 share, and median text length are fairly flat within 2024, which means the large 2026 move is not just ordinary within-window drift.

## Narrative evaluation
RQ1 is weakened in its original explicit-label form and should be split from the YOE proxy.
RQ2 is strengthened if it is reframed around rate acceleration and requirement stacking, not just movement of individual keywords.
RQ3 is not answered here.

## Emerging narrative
The market changed in windows, not as a smooth line. The substantive change is a denser posting template with much more AI language and more stacked requirements, while the observed daily scrape variation is mostly a measurement issue.
The right language is not “continuous trend.” It is “discrete windows with a real step-up in the 2026 window.”

## Research question evolution
RQ1 should explicitly separate `seniority_final` from the YOE proxy, because they answer different questions.
RQ2 should ask which requirement categories accelerate after 2024 relative to the within-2024 baseline.
Scope belongs in the question set, but only as a raw-text sensitivity unless the cleaned extractor is improved.

## Gaps and weaknesses
Scraped text coverage is the largest limitation. The daily series is also sparse enough late in the window that some apparent spikes are not interpretable.
The analysis still cannot cleanly separate platform formatting changes from employer behavior. The flat one-day posting-age distribution argues against a backlog story, but it does not fully explain the batching.

## Direction for next wave
The next wave should treat the batchy scrape surface as a validity issue and move straight into mechanism checks: ghost requirements, employer-requirement versus worker-usage divergence, and any within-firm decomposition that can distinguish posting-template change from real demand change.

## Current paper positioning
The paper remains an empirical restructuring study, but T19 adds the key measurement constraint that the 2026 evidence lives in windows, not a smooth panel.
If we stopped here, the lead temporal claim would be: AI-tool language and requirement breadth rose materially faster after 2024 than the within-2024 calibration gap, but the 2026 observations must be read as windowed snapshots with thin late-window label coverage.

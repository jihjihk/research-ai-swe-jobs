# T24 Hypothesis Generation from Findings

## Confirmation inventory

| Existing question | Status | Why |
|---|---|---|
| RQ1: employer-side restructuring | Confirmed, but reframed | The data support restructuring, but not a simple junior-collapse narrative. The stronger signal is differential densification under template drift, with company heterogeneity, a conservative explicit-entry label, and domain-first recomposition. |
| RQ2: task and requirement migration | Confirmed, but broadened | Requirement bundles clearly changed. The best description is not downward migration alone, but credential stacking, scope expansion, AI/LLM saturation, and section/template change. AI-authorship is still a hypothesis, not an established result. |
| RQ3: employer-requirement / worker-usage divergence | Ambiguous / weakened | Posting-side AI language is real, but the divergence claim is benchmark-sensitive and text-source-sensitive. It is better treated as calibration than as a headline. |
| RQ4: mechanisms | Strengthened | Interviews are now necessary to separate real requirement change from aspirational language, template drift, recruiter tooling, and hiring-bar inflation. |

## Headline claims to carry forward

1. **The latent structure is domain-first, not seniority-first.** `T09` is the structural result that forces the rest of the paper to move.
2. **AI/LLM became a coherent ecosystem.** It is not just a larger keyword bucket; the network and archetype results show a recognizable stack layer.
3. **Requirement stacking is real in SWE.** The strongest SWE signal is AI/tool language plus breadth, stack depth, scope density, and sectioned templates.
4. **The template shift is broader than SWE.** Length and scope rise outside SWE too, so the paper should not claim a SWE-only document-form change.
5. **Senior roles did not move from management to orchestration.** They added AI/tool orchestration around a still-present management base.
6. **Explicit entry is a lower bound.** Low-YOE postings remain, so the junior story is measurement-dependent rather than a clean collapse.
7. **Returning employers changed their own postings.** Within-company AI/scope/length change is stronger than the exact company cluster typology.
8. **The simple dramatic stories fail.** No clean junior collapse, no clean management decline, and no robust employer-worker divergence.

## Surprise inventory

1. **The latent posting structure is domain-first, not seniority-first.** `T09` finds tech-domain alignment far above seniority alignment, with `tech_domain` NMI around `0.115-0.123` and seniority near zero. This is strong evidence that the market’s natural structure is not the original seniority axis. Evidence strength: strong.
2. **The posting surface itself changed.** `T13` shows core section detection jumping from roughly 1.6-4.7% in 2024 sources to 60%+ in scraped 2026. That is a document-form shift, not only a labor-demand shift. Evidence strength: strong.
3. **Requirement growth is more stacking than simple tech inflation.** `T11` shows breadth, stack depth, scope density, and AI mentions rising more clearly than tech count. Evidence strength: strong.
4. **Senior roles did not lose management so much as gain orchestration.** `T21` shows strict management flat or slightly up, while strict orchestration grows sharply. Evidence strength: moderate-to-strong, but `T21` headline numbers still need analysis-phase recomputation because V2 did not independently finish that check.
5. **Junior change is instrument-dependent.** `T03`, `T08`, `T16`, `T20`, and `T17` all show the explicit-entry label is conservative relative to the YOE proxy. Evidence strength: strong.
6. **AI language is elevated, but the divergence claim is fragile.** `T14` and `T23` both support posting-side AI growth, but `T23` shows that “employers outrun workers” flips with the benchmark choice. Evidence strength: strong for AI growth, weak for divergence.
7. **Ghost-like language is more about aspiration and templating than literal fake jobs.** `T22` shows AI language is more hedge-heavy, with direct employers and aggregators contributing different kinds of noise. Evidence strength: moderate, but still needs recomputation for the headline ratios.
8. **Companies do not all move the same way.** `T16` shows returning firms split into different trajectories, with within-company densification dominating the AI/scope/length shift. Evidence strength: strong for within-company decomposition; provisional for the exact cluster typology.

## Method suitability assessment

| Question type | Best methods | Why |
|---|---|---|
| RQ1 / junior change | Paired descriptive trends, within-company decomposition, company fixed effects, YOE proxy vs explicit-entry side-by-side | Entry is conservative and company composition matters. A simple pooled share is too fragile. |
| RQ2 / requirement change | Company-capped term counts, requirement-breadth indices, section-filtered vs raw sensitivity, archetype-stratified re-estimation | The text surface changed form; breadth and scope are stronger than raw tech counts. |
| RQ3 / employer-vs-worker comparison | Benchmark banding, sensitivity envelopes, side-by-side rate comparisons, explicit caveats on object mismatch | The divergence claim is not one number. It depends on the benchmark and text source. |
| RQ4 / mechanisms | Reflexive thematic analysis, interview prompts keyed to contradictions, triangulation against posting examples | The quantitative work identifies tensions, not motives. Interviews should adjudicate interpretation. |
| Domain recomposition | NMF / archetype labels, cosine similarity, clustering on change vectors, within-company panels | `T09` says the market is domain-first. The analysis should respect that structure. |
| Senior role evolution | Strict pattern dictionaries, seniority-stratified profiles, cluster analysis on orchestration / strategy terms | Broad management is noisy; strict management and orchestration are the defensible axes. |
| Ghost / aspiration | Validated dictionaries, manual sampling, template-saturation checks, company-level heuristics | The concept is latent and noisy; it should be ranked and inspected, not treated as a latent truth variable. |

## Key tensions

1. **AI requirement vs AI aspiration.** `T14` and `T23` say AI language is real; `T22` says it is often hedged and template-like.
2. **Template drift vs real work change.** `T13` and `T12` show the document surface changed; `T11` and `T16` show the requirement bundle also changed.
3. **Explicit seniority vs YOE.** `T03`, `T08`, `T20`, and `T17` show the same trend can move in opposite directions depending on the instrument.
4. **Senior orchestration vs management stability.** `T21` contradicts the simple management-decline story.
5. **Employer AI signal vs worker benchmark.** `T23` shows that the divergence story is benchmark-sensitive and cannot be reduced to one headline percentage.

## Data gaps

The most interesting unresolved questions need data we do not currently have:

- recruiter edits or posting revision logs, to separate authoring style from actual demand
- interview transcripts from hiring managers, recruiters, and engineers, to interpret aspiration versus screening
- a worker-usage benchmark matched more tightly to SWE job content and time window
- more complete internal funnel data, to see whether dense postings actually screen more strictly
- stronger time-aligned label histories for the same firms, to separate composition from within-firm strategy
- a finalized archetype spec lock so the company trajectory labels can be treated as stable results

## Bottom line

The paper should now be built around **differential densification under template drift**. The strongest current hypotheses are about AI/LLM saturation, requirement stacking, and posting-form change. Junior collapse, management decline, and employer-worker divergence are all weaker than they looked at the start and should stay qualified.

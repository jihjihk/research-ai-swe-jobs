    T04. SWE Classification Audit¶

    Headline finding¶

    is_swe looks solid for the core SWE corpus, but the uncertainty is concentrated in a small title_lookup_llm tier and in boundary occupations. The classifier cleanly separates control occupations from SWE-adjacent ones, but it is least stable around QA/test, automation, firmware, architect, security, and some data/ML titles.

    Methodology¶

    I audited the LinkedIn English/date-ok frame only. The relevant rows are:

    • SWE: 59,972
    • SWE-adjacent: 22,279
    • control: 151,267

    The SWE class itself is mostly rule-based:

    Tier SWE rows Share of SWE
    regex 54,609 91.1%
    embedding_high 1,012 1.7%
    title_lookup_llm 4,351 7.3%
    unresolved 0 0.0%

    The confidence-band request (swe_confidence 0.3-0.7) was empty in the LinkedIn SWE frame. The practical borderline set is therefore the title_lookup_llm tier.

    I reviewed:

    1. A 50-row SWE borderline sample from the title_lookup_llm tier.
    2. A 50-row non-SWE sample where the title contains engineer, developer, or software.
    3. The top titles in is_swe_adjacent and is_control.
    4. A title-family matrix for boundary occupations.
    5. A dual-flag integrity check.

    What we learned¶

    The SWE borderline sample is not a random mess. It is mostly the expected edge zone: QA, test, automation, firmware, devsecops, AI/ML, architect, and senior developer titles. In the 50-row sample, 23 rows were clearly boundary-family titles and 27 were broader SWE-like titles.

    That suggests the main uncertainty is not within the core SWE class. It is around whether roles like QA Engineer, Automation Engineer, Firmware Engineer, AI Engineer, Architect, or Software Engineering Manager belong on the SWE side or the SWE-adjacent side.

    The non-SWE sample shows the complementary pattern. Clear non-SWE exclusions are mostly correct:

    • electrical, civil, mechanical, and structural engineer roles
    • financial / supply-chain / process engineering roles
    • non-software control occupations

    The likely false negatives are concentrated in software-adjacent titles that are easy to over- or under-call:

    • RPA Developer, Appian Developer, NetSuite Developer, CRM Developer
    • Data Engineering / Manager Data Engineering
    • Forward Deployed Engineer
    • Staff Engineer, Payments Team
    • Software Design Quality Engineer
    • some Engineer titles with explicit software stacks or cloud context

    Evidence assessment¶

    The dual-flag check passes: is_swe + is_swe_adjacent + is_control > 1 is zero.

    The core SWE tier is strong. The model is not over-relying on the uncertain tier, and regex dominates the final SWE corpus. The uncertainty share is also lower in 2026 than in 2024, with title_lookup_llm falling from about 9.7-10.9% of SWE rows in the 2024 LinkedIn sources to about 5.1-5.2% in scraped LinkedIn.

    Estimated risk on the borderline slice:

    • false positives among borderline SWE rows: roughly 10-20%
    • false negatives among borderline non-SWE rows: roughly 10-20%

    That is a coarse estimate from the 50-row review, not a formal classifier evaluation. The important part is where the errors live: boundary-family titles, not the core regex class.

    Surprises¶

    Two boundary patterns stood out.

    1. data engineer, ml engineer, and devops/sre are mostly SWE in this corpus. They are not the main source of trouble.
    2. data scientist, security engineer, systems architect, and project engineer lean much more adjacent than SWE. QA/test engineer is mixed and appears to drift toward SWE in 2026.

    The adjacent and control groups are semantically clean:

    Adjacent top titles Control top titles
    quality engineer accountant
    data scientist registered nurse
    network engineer electrical engineer
    data analyst financial analyst
    architect / solutions architect mechanical engineer
    security engineer accounting manager

    That separation is useful. It means the taxonomy is not just a thin wrapper around generic "engineer" language.

    Narrative evaluation¶

    • RQ1, employer-side restructuring: unaffected directly by this audit, but any seniority-stratified claim that leans on the SWE sample inherits the boundary-zone caveat.
    • RQ2, task and requirement migration: the boundary classes matter here because QA, test, automation, and DevOps are exactly the roles where requirement language can blur with adjacent work.
    • RQ3, employer-requirement / worker-usage divergence: unaffected directly.
    • RQ4, mechanisms: unaffected.

    The strongest alternative framing is not that the classifier is broken. It is that the labor market itself has a wide technical boundary zone. The hardest roles to classify are the ones the market already treats as hybrids.

    Recommendation¶

    is_swe is good enough for downstream exploration, but the title_lookup_llm tier should be treated as the main uncertainty bucket. Any later result that hinges on the SWE boundary should be re-run with that tier excluded or at least reported separately. Boundary-sensitive results should not be read as if SWE, SWE-adjacent, and control are equally crisp categories.

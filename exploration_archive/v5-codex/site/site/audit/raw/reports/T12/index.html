    Gate 2 T12 Research Memo¶

    What we learned¶

    The cleaned full-text comparison is not a clean symmetric 2024-vs-2026 vocabulary shift. After company capping, the top 100 log-odds terms are still dominated by the historical side, especially stack and credential artifacts such as javascript, jquery, directory, j2ee, wordpress, html, laravel, jdbc, visualforce, and similar tokens. That is useful information, but it is also a warning sign: the cleaned surface is still carrying a lot of old-form noise.

    The section-filtered comparison does not fully solve that problem because it is extremely imbalanced: only 72 arshkon documents have detected section text versus 3,359 scraped documents. That makes the section-filtered list informative as a direction check, but not a balanced test. The strongest 2026 signal actually shows up in the sensitivity checks and the topic model, not in the main full-text ranking.

    In the raw same-row sensitivity, the 2026 side surfaces AI language that the cleaned full-text ranking obscures: maintain, AIML, AI powered, AI driven, LLMs, LLM, domains, and similar terms. The emergent-term tables make the same point more sharply. AI assisted, Claude, multi agent, langgraph, embeddings, AI enabled, LLM based, multimodal, and AI generated all rise sharply in 2026.

    What surprised us¶

    The biggest surprise is how one-sided the main cleaned-text ranking remains even after a careful preprocessing pipeline. I expected a more balanced list with some obvious 2026 AI terms. Instead, the dominant signal is still old stack vocabulary and cleaning artifacts. That is not a reason to discard the analysis. It is a reason to treat full cleaned text as a noisy surface rather than the final estimator.

    The second surprise is how revealing the raw same-row branch is. When the exact same rows are compared on raw descriptions, the AI language becomes much easier to see. That means the cleaning pipeline is not just removing junk; it is also suppressing some of the modern signal, probably because the modern signal is entangled with template text and repeated generic phrasing.

    Evidence assessment¶

    The primary full-text comparison is moderate-to-strong evidence for broad language divergence, but weak evidence for a specific 2026 content story because the top terms are dominated by historical artifacts and stack tokens. The effect is real, but the interpretation is messy.

    The section-filtered comparison is weaker than it looks because of the 72 versus 3,359 imbalance. I would not use it as a standalone headline. Its main value is as a sensitivity check showing that the 2026 AI and workflow vocabulary does not disappear when benefits and company boilerplate are removed.

    The raw same-row comparison is a good recall-sensitive diagnostic, not the primary estimator. It is nonetheless important because it demonstrates that the 2026 AI lexicon is visible in the underlying text, not only in derived features. The BERTopic cross-check is the most persuasive corroboration here: topic 1 contains 1,432 docs, with 83.2% from 2026 and top terms ai, ml, workflows, aiml, llms, and qualifications. That is a real 2026 cluster.

    Narrative evaluation¶

    RQ2 is still viable, but the current evidence says it needs a two-part framing. The data support both content migration and template drift, but the template drift is not a side note. It is central to interpreting the term shifts.

    The original broad "language evolution" framing is too loose. The stronger framing is: SWE postings in 2026 contain a distinct AI/workflow vocabulary, but the observable shift is entangled with historical stack residue, boilerplate, and sectioning differences. That makes the paper less tidy, but more credible.

    The best alternative framing is not "AI hype" or "junior decline." It is posting-surface recomposition. A second plausible alternative is that this is mostly a platform/formatting change rather than a demand change. I do not think that alternative is sufficient on its own because the emergent AI terms and the AI/workflow topic are too strong to be dismissed as formatting only. The better interpretation is that both mechanisms operate together, with the AI vocabulary being the substantive part and the template drift being the measurement problem.

    Emerging narrative¶

    The strongest story so far is not just that AI terms entered SWE postings. It is that they entered a text surface that is itself changing shape. The main empirical signal to carry forward is the 2026 AI/workflow cluster, backed by Claude, LLM-based, multi-agent, embeddings, and AI-enabled terms, plus a topic model that isolates an AI-heavy period-skewed cluster.

    At the same time, the historical cleaned text still carries a lot of old stack and credential language, and the section-filtered comparison is too sparse on the 2024 side to support a clean symmetric claim. That means the final paper should not frame T12 as a simple before/after word list. It should frame it as a noisy but informative map of how the observable language surface is changing.

    Research question evolution¶

    RQ2 should be split into two subquestions: 1. Which content bundles became more salient in 2026 SWE postings, especially AI/workflow bundles? 2. Which of those apparent shifts survive section filtering and raw-text sensitivity, and which are artifacts of template drift?

    RQ1 should not be read from full cleaned text alone. The current term evidence is too entangled with structure and old-form artifacts to support a simple volume-or-level claim.

    Gaps and weaknesses¶

    The section-filtered comparison is too imbalanced for a strong headline. That needs to be explicitly labeled as a weak sensitivity rather than treated as equal footing with the full-text comparison.

    The cleaned corpus still contains concatenation and artifact tokens in the historical data, which may inflate the historical side of the log-odds ranking. That should be handled in the analysis phase, not ignored.

    BERTopic is useful, but it still needs careful interpretation for whether the smaller mobile/platform topics are substantive or just another view of the same surface changes.

    Direction for next wave¶

    Keep the AI/workflow vocabulary as a candidate lead and pursue it with stricter validation. The most valuable follow-up is to separate genuine AI-content movement from template drift using the section-filtered surface, raw-text recall, and topic-level cross-checks together.

    Do not invest heavily in a claim that the section-filtered comparison is a balanced 2024-vs-2026 test. It is not. Treat it as a diagnostic. The core result is the emergence of a 2026 AI/workflow language cluster, not a simple full-text ranking.

    Current paper positioning¶

    T12 points toward an empirical restructuring paper, but with a very specific caveat: the language signal is real, yet the document surface is unstable. The strongest contribution is the detection of a 2026 AI/workflow vocabulary cluster and the demonstration that its visibility depends on how aggressively boilerplate and template drift are filtered out.

    T15 Semantic Similarity Landscape & Convergence Analysis¶

    Headline finding¶

    There is no clean junior-senior convergence story at the corpus level. The semantic space becomes tighter over time, but junior and senior postings do not collapse into each other. Embeddings show only a tiny change that sits inside the 2024 source gap; TF-IDF shows a clearer divergence. The most defensible reading is tighter templating plus stronger period structure, not seniority convergence.

    What we learned¶

    Using the full text_source = llm subset gives us 26,219 labeled SWE LinkedIn rows, with no raw fallback contamination in the semantic analysis. That is enough to estimate centroids, dispersion, and nearest neighbors without downsampling the actual inference set. The visual sample is only for plotting.

    The strongest structural result is that period slightly dominates seniority. Across the centroid matrices, same-period groups are a bit more similar than same-seniority groups across periods: 0.9516 vs 0.9441 under embeddings, and 0.8623 vs 0.8416 under TF-IDF. The gap is modest, but it is consistently on the period side.

    The convergence check is negative. Under embeddings, junior-senior similarity is 0.9801 in asaniczka, 0.9649 in arshkon, and 0.9618 in scraped. The arshkon-to-scraped change is smaller than the asaniczka-to-arshkon baseline gap, so it does not survive calibration. Under TF-IDF, the same similarity falls from 0.9383 to 0.9186 to 0.8676, which is a larger drop than the 2024 baseline and points to divergence rather than convergence.

    The YOE proxy tells the same basic story, but with even higher absolute similarity because the junior/senior split is broader. yoe_extracted <= 2 vs >= 3 stays very close in every source, yet the scraped change still does not outrun the 2024 baseline. So the proxy does not rescue a convergence claim.

    Within-group dispersion drops in 2026. That means postings are getting more internally homogeneous even while junior and senior remain distinct. The cleanest interpretation is not “junior and senior are merging,” but “the language is standardizing and the semantic space is tightening.”

    Nearest neighbors reinforce the same boundary-blur story. For 2026 entry posts, the 5-nearest 2024 neighbors are mostly senior and unknown rows. Junior neighbors appear more often than the 2024 base rate, but only modestly: 4.35% vs a 1.60% base under embeddings, and 2.88% vs 1.60% under TF-IDF.

    What surprised us¶

    The biggest surprise is the representation disagreement. Embeddings and TF-IDF agree on the direction of the main finding, but not on magnitude. Embeddings are very conservative and almost flatten the cross-period change; TF-IDF sees the gap widen more clearly. That is a real methodological signal, not just a nuisance.

    The other surprise is that the 2026 space becomes tighter while the junior-senior gap does not close. That combination usually means templating, standardization, or stronger employer signaling, not genuine convergence of role content.

    Evidence assessment¶

    The embedding result is moderate evidence. The group sizes are adequate for senior and unknown rows, but junior and mid are small, especially in scraped 2026-04. The tiny absolute change and the calibration gap mean I would not elevate embeddings alone into a headline.

    The TF-IDF result is stronger as an empirical signal because the change is larger than the 2024 baseline difference. It still needs caution because TF-IDF is more sensitive to boilerplate and wording, but the sign is consistent with the embedding view: no convergence.

    The nearest-neighbor result is moderate evidence. It is conceptually clean and easy to explain, but it is still neighborhood-based rather than causal. Its value is in showing where the 2026 entry postings live in the older semantic space.

    The YOE-proxy check is important but descriptive. It confirms that the negative convergence result is not just an artifact of seniority_final, but the proxy itself is broad enough that it should not be over-read.

    Sensitivity checks¶

    Aggregator exclusion: the source-level junior-senior similarity barely moves. Under embeddings, arshkon changes from 0.9649 to 0.9632 and scraped from 0.9618 to 0.9596. Under TF-IDF, arshkon moves from 0.9186 to 0.9100 and scraped from 0.8676 to 0.8652. The conclusion does not change.

    Within-2024 calibration: the 2026 shift does not exceed the historical source gap under embeddings, and it does under TF-IDF. That is why the correct conclusion is not “strong convergence.” At best, embeddings show a tiny change inside noise; TF-IDF shows divergence.

    Seniority operationalization: the YOE proxy gives the same qualitative answer. It does not produce a convincing convergence signal, and it keeps junior-senior similarity extremely high in every source. That makes the boundary look sticky rather than collapsing.

    Source restriction: the source-level tables show the same pattern as the period-level tables, which means the result is not driven by one snapshot window. The 2024 baseline already differs across arshkon and asaniczka, so that baseline has to stay visible in any downstream claim.

    Data quality caveats¶

    The mid group is tiny in every period, especially after embedding filtering. I treat it as descriptive, not as the basis for any strong claim.

    The outlier tables are dominated by noisy, legacy, or highly specialized postings. Examples include 5G/embedded roles, TS/SCI security work, and malformed descriptions with no usable cleaned text. That is useful because it shows the semantic map is catching real fringe structure, but it also means the tail is noisy.

    There are no T09 archetype labels in the shared artifacts, so I could not run the domain-stratified convergence version. That is the main missing follow-up if we want to ask whether AI/ML roles converge differently from frontend or embedded roles.

    Emerging narrative¶

    The right story is not convergence. It is standardization plus recomposition. The semantic space tightens, AI/tool language becomes more coherent, and the dominant variation is slightly more period-linked than seniority-linked. Seniority boundaries remain visible, but they are not the main axis of change.

    That makes the paper stronger, not weaker. It shifts the analysis away from a fragile “junior and senior are becoming the same” claim and toward a more defensible account of how the posting language itself is reorganizing.

    Direction for downstream analysis¶

    Deprioritize a simple convergence framing in the paper. The stronger story is that role language is becoming more standardized while the seniority boundary remains intact.

    If we need a junior-focused figure later, it should lean on the YOE proxy and nearest-neighbor excess, not on the explicit entry label alone.

    If domain labels become available, the next useful question is whether this non-convergence differs by archetype. The current data say the overall corpus does not converge; they do not yet tell us whether ML/AI, frontend, and embedded move differently.

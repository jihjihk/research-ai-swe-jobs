    T11 Requirements Complexity & Credential Stacking¶

    Headline finding¶

    The complexity story is real, but it is not “more tech” in a simple sense. The robust change is that 2026 postings ask for more requirement categories at once: AI, scope, YOE, soft skills, and technology, bundled together more often. On the primary company-capped llm subset, requirement_breadth and credential_stack_depth both rise from 2024 to 2026, while tech_count only moves modestly and is not clearly above the 2024 calibration baseline. Scope density and AI mentions are the clearest increases. Explicit management language is much less clean than the rest of the signal.

    Methodology¶

    I built a posting-level complexity extractor on top of the shared cleaned-text artifact and shared technology matrix. The primary specification is:

    • LinkedIn SWE only
    • text_source = 'llm'
    • company cap of 25 postings per company per source-period
    • all rows included for the primary estimate
    • explicit reporting of no-cap, no-aggregator, and raw-text sensitivities

    The extractor counts:

    • tech_count from the shared technology matrix
    • soft-skill terms
    • organizational-scope terms
    • strong management terms
    • broad management terms
    • AI-specific requirements
    • education level, taking the highest degree mentioned
    • YOE presence from yoe_extracted

    Then it materializes:

    • requirement_breadth: sum of distinct term hits across categories
    • credential_stack_depth: number of categories touched
    • tech_density and scope_density: per 1,000 characters

    I report explicit-entry and YOE-proxy versions side by side, because the seniority work in Wave 1 showed that seniority_final is conservative for junior-like postings.

    What we learned¶

    • Complexity rose, but unevenly. In the primary llm-cleaned, company-capped sample, explicit-entry postings in arshkon move from 3.60 mean breadth / 1.82 stack depth to 4.24 / 2.12 in scraped 2026. The YOE-proxy junior slice is broader still: 4.67 / 2.50 in arshkon to 5.41 / 2.88 in scraped.
    • The clearest breadth change is scope, not tech. scope_density roughly doubles between arshkon and scraped in the junior slices, and scope_count is one of the metrics that clears the 2024 calibration baseline by a wide margin.
    • AI is a robust new layer. ai_count rises sharply and survives the within-2024 calibration check by a large margin. This is the most obvious non-boilerplate addition to the requirement stack.
    • tech_count does increase, but only modestly. Its 2024-to-2026 change is not clearly above the 2024 instrument baseline, so “more tech” is not the clean headline.
    • Education is sparse and not a lead driver. Degree mentions exist, but they do not explain the complexity rise.
    • Management language is noisy. The broad tier is dominated by generic team language, while the strong tier is cleaner but small. I would not lead with management as the main complexity result.

    What surprised us¶

    • The explicit-entry slice is not representative of the broader junior-like population. The YOE proxy is consistently more complex than explicit seniority_final = entry, which is exactly the measurement gap Wave 1 warned about.
    • Raw text sensitivity matters, but it does not reverse the story. On scraped 2026, raw fallback raises breadth by about 6 percent and stack depth by about 4 to 6 percent, while densities fall because raw descriptions are longer. That is a magnitude problem, not a direction problem.
    • The most complex postings look real. The top 1 percent by breadth includes roles such as senior software, AI platform, machine-learning, and infrastructure-heavy postings. They do not read like pure template bloating.
    • I spot-checked 100 management matches, balanced across the four period bins, and the sample was dominated by the broad team trigger rather than the strong management tier. That is exactly why the broad tier should stay a sensitivity, not a headline.

    Evidence assessment¶

    • Requirement breadth: strong. The 2024-vs-2026 signal-to-noise ratio is 5.4.
    • Credential stack depth: very strong. The signal-to-noise ratio is 22.5.
    • AI count: very strong. The signal-to-noise ratio is 6.6.
    • Scope count / scope density: very strong. This is the cleanest “scope inflation” style signal in the task.
    • Tech count: moderate at best. It moves, but not enough to lead the story on its own.
    • Management broad / management strong: weak as a period signal. Broad management is too generic; strong management is cleaner but sparse.
    • Education flag: weak. It does not carry the complexity story.

    Sensitivity checks¶

    • Company cap: breadth and stack depth are stable under capping, but density metrics are not. tech_density is especially sensitive to no-cap estimates, which is exactly what we expect with concentrated employers.
    • Aggregator exclusion: does not change the qualitative story.
    • Raw text fallback: the raw fallback in 2026 nudges breadth and stack upward a little, but the direction remains the same. 2024 raw coverage is too thin to treat as a meaningful baseline, so raw should stay a sensitivity rather than a second headline.
    • Seniority operationalization: explicit-entry results are materially lower than YOE-proxy results. That disagreement is substantive and should be reported, not smoothed over.
    • Within-2024 calibration: requirement_breadth, credential_stack_depth, scope_count, ai_count, text_len, and scope_density all clear the calibration bar. tech_count, education_flag, and management measures do not cleanly do so.

    Narrative evaluation¶

    The original RQ2 framing is directionally right, but too narrow if it stays focused on “requirement migration” in the old sense. The stronger result is credential stacking: postings are bundling more requirement categories together, with AI and scope doing most of the work. Junior-scope inflation is real in the YOE proxy, but the more publishable claim is that junior-like postings are being asked to satisfy more kinds of constraints simultaneously.

    Emerging narrative¶

    The data support a “more categories at once” story. 2026 SWE postings are not simply longer or more technical; they are more multi-constraint, with AI, scope, and YOE requirements showing the clearest rise. The strongest version of the story is not “more tech,” but “more credential stacking around the same SWE core.”

    Research question evolution¶

    RQ2 should be expanded. The cleanest addition is:

    RQ2b: Did SWE postings in 2026 require more categories of capability simultaneously than in 2024, and which categories drove the increase?

    The current evidence suggests that AI and scope should be central in that question, not optional side examples.

    RQ1 should also be reframed slightly: the junior question is better posed in terms of explicit labels versus YOE-proxy junior-like postings, because the two are not measuring the same thing.

    Data quality caveats¶

    • The cleaned-text sample is thin in scraped 2026, so every text result should be read with coverage in mind.
    • The strong/broad management split is important because the broad tier is noisy. Generic team language is the dominant trigger.
    • The domain-stratified scope branch is deferred because the T09 archetype labels are not present in the shared artifacts.

    Direction for next wave¶

    The next wave should separate content from boilerplate more aggressively and, if possible, test whether the scope/AI stack survives section stripping. It should also de-emphasize broad management language and keep the YOE proxy attached to any junior claim.

    Current paper positioning¶

    This task pushes the paper away from a pure “junior share fell” framing and toward a stronger restructuring story: SWE postings are becoming more multi-constraint, more AI-saturated, and more scope-heavy. That is a substantive result, not just a measurement artifact.

## 3. New Hypothesis Generation

The following hypotheses are testable, NOT part of the original research design, and account for the orthogonality finding (they do not assume AI caused junior elimination).

### H1: Domain recomposition as the primary restructuring mechanism

**Hypothesis:** The decline in junior SWE share is primarily driven by the growth of ML/AI engineering (which has a structurally lower entry-level posting rate due to higher skill floors) rather than by within-domain junior elimination.

**Test:** Decompose the junior share decline into within-domain (holding archetype constant) and between-domain (archetype composition shift) components. If ML/AI has lower entry share AND grew from 4% to 27%, the compositional effect alone could account for a substantial portion of the 5.9pp aggregate decline.

**Data needed:** Cross-tabulation of seniority by T09 archetype by period. Available in existing data.

**Why it matters:** If confirmed, the "junior decline" story becomes "the market shifted toward domains with inherently higher skill floors" rather than "employers stopped hiring juniors." This is a more nuanced and defensible claim.

### H2: AI requirements as labor market signaling (independent of actual job content)

**Hypothesis:** Firms add AI requirements to postings not because the role involves AI work, but to signal organizational modernity and attract candidates who self-select as technologically current. Evidence: AI adoption at the firm level is uncorrelated with junior hiring changes, but firms in competitive labor markets (where signaling matters more) show higher AI requirement rates.

**Test:** Compare AI requirement rates controlling for firm characteristics (size, industry, prior AI adoption). Test whether the signaling interpretation survives when controlling for actual AI technology use (e.g., companies with AI products vs companies adding AI to non-AI postings).

**Data needed:** Company-level AI product indicators (partially derivable from industry labels); firm-size data (limited to arshkon). Could proxy via posting characteristics.

### H3: Template modernization as the driver of field-wide management language expansion

**Hypothesis:** The universal expansion of management/mentoring/coaching/leadership language across all seniority levels and occupations (including control) is driven by changes in HR technology platforms (e.g., AI-powered job posting generators, compliance templates) rather than by actual changes in job scope.

**Test:** Examine template saturation (T22: 4.6% of company-periods have pairwise cosine similarity >0.8). If the management language increase is concentrated in template-heavy companies, this supports the template hypothesis. If management language grew even in companies with low template reuse, the hypothesis weakens.

**Data needed:** Company-level template saturation scores crossed with management language rates. Available in existing data.

### H4: The "two-speed market" -- AI-native firms vs legacy transformation

**Hypothesis:** The SWE labor market is bifurcating into (a) AI-native companies that were founded during the AI era and build AI into roles from inception, and (b) legacy companies transforming existing roles to incorporate AI. These two populations drive different components of the aggregate change: AI-natives drive compositional effects (57% of change); legacy companies drive within-firm effects (43% of change).

**Test:** Classify companies in the T16 panel as "AI-native" (high AI prevalence in both periods or new-entrant with high AI) vs "AI-transforming" (low-to-high AI trajectory) vs "traditional" (low AI in both). Compare entry-share changes across these types.

**Data needed:** Company-level AI prevalence trajectory from T16 panel. Available in existing data.

### H5: Slot purification reflects credentialism retreat, not AI-driven restructuring

**Hypothesis:** The elimination of high-YOE "entry-level" postings (22.8% to 2.4%) reflects a broader retreat from experience-based credentialism in SWE hiring, driven by: (a) LinkedIn platform changes making seniority labels more accurate, (b) legal/compliance pressure against misleading job level labels, or (c) skill-based hiring trends. This would be unrelated to AI adoption.

**Test:** Check whether YOE slot purification also occurred in control occupations and SWE-adjacent roles. If yes, it is field-wide and likely platform-driven. If SWE-specific, the AI link is more plausible.

**Data needed:** Entry-level YOE distributions for control occupations across periods. Partially available (control has limited entry-level seniority labels).

### H6: The AI-entry orthogonality resolves through a time-lag mechanism

**Hypothesis:** AI adoption and junior hiring reduction ARE causally linked, but the effect operates with a multi-quarter lag that our two-snapshot design cannot capture. Firms first add AI requirements to existing postings (observed), then restructure their seniority mix in subsequent hiring cycles (not yet observable in cross-sectional snapshots).

**Test:** Not directly testable with current data (two snapshots, no longitudinal tracking). However, could look for leading indicators: among T16 panel firms that adopted AI between periods, do those with the HIGHEST AI adoption in 2026 show slightly lower entry share, even if the correlation is not significant? A dose-response pattern (even weak) would be suggestive.

**Data needed:** Existing T16 panel data with finer binning of AI adoption intensity.

### H7: GenAI adoption as a domain-boundary dissolving force

**Hypothesis:** The growth of GenAI requirements is blurring the boundaries between T09 archetypes -- postings in traditionally separate domains (frontend, embedded, data) increasingly share GenAI vocabulary, creating convergence in posting language at the domain level even as domain remains the primary structural axis.

**Test:** Compare the technology co-occurrence structure (T14 networks) between AI-mentioning and non-AI-mentioning postings within each domain. If AI-mentioning postings show higher cross-domain similarity, GenAI is acting as a convergence force.

**Data needed:** Embeddings stratified by AI-mention and domain. Available in existing data.

### H8: "Staff engineer" growth reflects AI-driven organizational flattening

**Hypothesis:** The near-tripling of "staff" title markers (2.4% to 6.6%, T10) reflects organizational flattening where firms remove mid-management layers and create high-seniority IC tracks. AI tools that enable senior ICs to manage larger scope drive this. The architect-to-staff title migration (-1.6pp architect, +4.9pp staff) is the same phenomenon.

**Test:** Check whether companies that increased "staff" titles also decreased "architect" titles. Cross-reference with AI adoption levels.

**Data needed:** Company-level title analysis crossed with AI adoption. Partially available.

## 5. Key Tensions: The Five Most Important Puzzles

### Tension 1: The seniority operationalization discrepancy

**The problem:** The direction of the entry-level trend depends on which seniority column is used. Using `seniority_native` (arshkon-only as 2024 baseline), entry share declined from ~20-22% to ~14% (T03). Using `seniority_3level` across the full T16 overlap panel, entry share appeared to increase. T16 noted this surprise but did not fully resolve it.

**Why it matters:** If the direction of the headline finding depends on the column choice, the claim is fragile. The planned `seniority_llm` labels are the resolution mechanism -- they will provide a consistent, high-quality seniority classification across all sources.

**Resolution path:** (a) Clearly document the ablation framework in all analysis: seniority_llm (when available) > seniority_native > seniority_final > seniority_imputed. (b) Run all entry-level analyses with seniority_native (cleanest, arshkon-only baseline) as primary, and seniority_final as robustness. (c) Flag explicitly that `seniority_llm` will resolve the discrepancy.

### Tension 2: The orthogonality puzzle

**The problem:** At the aggregate level, AI requirements surged AND junior share declined -- the co-occurrence is striking and tempting to present causally. But at the firm level (r=-0.07) and metro level (r=-0.04), the correlation is null. How can two trends co-occur at the market level but be orthogonal within units?

**Candidate explanations:** (a) Both trends are driven by a common third factor (e.g., post-pandemic labor market normalization, macro hiring slowdown). (b) The relationship operates through market-level equilibrium effects (AI adoption changes what skills are valued, which shifts the type of companies that hire, which changes the composition). (c) The within-firm null reflects insufficient time for the causal mechanism to operate (lag hypothesis). (d) The trends are genuinely independent -- AI adoption is about what companies ask for, junior decline is about how many they ask for, and these are separate decisions.

**Why it matters:** The paper's credibility depends on NOT overclaiming causation. The orthogonality finding is one of the most important results and must be prominently presented.

### Tension 3: Compositional vs behavioral change

**The problem:** 57% of aggregate change is compositional (different companies posting). This means the "restructuring" story is more about market-level turnover than firm-level strategic decisions.

**Why it matters:** "Companies are restructuring SWE hiring" implies behavioral change. "Different companies are hiring" is a compositional story. The paper should present both components clearly.

**Resolution path:** Frame the contribution as documenting BOTH within-firm behavioral changes (~43%) AND market-level compositional shifts (~57%). The fact that 43% is genuine within-firm change is still substantial and meaningful.

### Tension 4: Description quality asymmetry

**The problem:** 2024 Kaggle data benefits from LLM-cleaned text (description_core_llm, ~80-99% coverage for SWE). 2026 scraped data has 0% LLM-cleaned coverage. All text-based indicators operate on lower-quality rule-based cleaned text for 2026. T22 showed this matters enormously for management indicators (LLM-cleaned 2024 rates are ~15pp lower than rule-based rates).

**Why it matters:** Every text-based 2024-to-2026 comparison has a systematic quality bias. The 2024 rates are measured on cleaner text; the 2026 rates on noisier text. This asymmetry inflates apparent 2024-to-2026 changes for boilerplate-sensitive indicators.

**Resolution path:** (a) Use binary keyword presence on raw `description` for prevalence measures (less sensitive to cleaning quality). (b) When LLM-cleaned 2026 text becomes available, re-run all text-based analyses. (c) Explicitly flag this asymmetry as a limitation.

### Tension 5: What "SWE-specific" means when SWE and SWE-adjacent move in lockstep

**The problem:** The DiD analysis (T18) shows SWE and SWE-adjacent move together (DiD between them ~0 for most metrics). The real boundary is technical roles (SWE + adjacent) vs non-technical control. So "SWE-specific" actually means "technical-role-specific."

**Why it matters:** The paper's framing is about SWE. But the data shows the changes apply broadly to technical roles. This is actually a stronger finding (larger affected population) but requires honest framing.

## 7. Summary: The Strongest Story the Data Supports

The exploration produced a richer and more honest story than the initial hypotheses anticipated. The strongest narrative, accounting for all corrections:

**The SWE labor market between 2024 and 2026 restructured along three axes:**

1. **AI skill requirements surged** -- genuinely, not aspirationally -- and this is the defining SWE-specific change (DiD +24pp vs control). Requirements still lag developer usage but are converging fast.

2. **Junior SWE hiring contracted** in a SWE-specific manner (control junior share increased). But this contraction is NOT caused by AI adoption within firms (r ~ 0). It co-occurs with AI adoption as parallel market-level trends, mediated primarily by compositional shifts (57% of aggregate change reflects different companies hiring).

3. **The SWE domain landscape recomposed** -- ML/AI engineering grew from niche (4%) to major category (27%), frontend contracted, and AI skills became additive to existing technology stacks rather than replacing them.

**What the paper should NOT claim:**
- That AI caused junior elimination (firm-level evidence contradicts)
- That management scope inflation is dramatic or SWE-specific (corrected to +4-10pp, field-wide)
- That junior and senior postings are converging semantically (fails calibration)
- That requirements outpace developer usage (they lag)

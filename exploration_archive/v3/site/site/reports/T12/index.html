    T12: Open-ended Text Evolution

    Agent H | Wave 2 | Completed 2026-04-05

    Summary

    Fightin' Words analysis (Monroe et al. 2008 log-odds with informative Dirichlet prior) reveals two dominant patterns in SWE job posting text from 2024 to 2026:

    1. AI terminology exploded. "AI" is the single most distinguishing unigram for 2026 (z=47.0, rate 1.8 -> 6.0 per 1K chars). LLM/LLMs, copilot, openai, claude, anthropic, gemini, agentic, RAG, embedding, and multimodal all rose significantly. This is NOT an artifact — it persists across all sensitivity checks.

    2. Much of the apparent "2026 signal" is boilerplate expansion, not technical content. Of the top 100 unigrams distinguishing 2026, 95 are categorized as "uncategorized" (generic terms like "range", "world", "benefits", "pay", "salary", "employees", "people", "company", "culture", "mission"). These reflect the growth in benefits/compensation and about-company sections documented in T13. Only 8 AI-tool terms + 4 AI-domain terms + 1 "flexible" represent genuine content evolution.

    3. Specific tech stacks declined sharply. JavaScript, HTML, SQL, Java, .NET, Angular, Django, Flask all fell. Bigram "years experience" dropped from 5.6 to 2.1 per 1K. "Computer science" and "bachelor degree" declined. This suggests both a real shift (fewer traditional web-stack requirements) and less formulaic requirements writing.

    Secondary Comparisons

    Entry-level 2024 vs Entry-level 2026

    n_2024 = 829, n_2026 = 3,255

    Top entry-2026 terms: ai (z=20.9), world, job, range, benefits, company, employees, pay. Pattern mirrors the primary comparison — AI rise + boilerplate expansion.

    Mid-senior 2024 vs Mid-senior 2026

    n_2024 = 3,008, n_2026 = 18,592

    Top senior-2026 terms: ai (z=38.8), range, benefits, world, us, people, pay. Same pattern. AI effect is even stronger for senior roles (z=38.8 vs 20.9 for entry).

    Relabeling Diagnostic (Entry 2026 vs Mid-senior 2024)

    n_entry_2026 = 3,255, n_senior_2024 = 3,008

    Top entry-2026-distinctive terms: ai (z=36.9), world, us, range, benefits, pay. The dominant signal is period effect (AI + boilerplate), not seniority content. This suggests entry 2026 postings look more like a temporal shift than a relabeling of senior-like content.

    Within-2024 Calibration (arshkon senior vs asaniczka senior)

    n_arshkon = 3,008, n_asaniczka = 21,624

    Top instrument-difference terms: systems (z=23.6), data (z=-25.3), azure (z=-29.1), cloud (z=-20.9). These are tech-stack mix differences between the two Kaggle datasets. 358 terms have |z|>5 in both calibration and primary comparisons, flagging them as potential instrument artifacts.

    Critical finding: The calibration comparison shows substantial instrument noise. However, the AI terms (llm, copilot, etc.) do NOT appear in the calibration top terms, confirming they are genuine temporal signals rather than dataset artifacts.

    BERTopic Cross-Validation

    53 topics found. Key findings:

    Most 2026-specific topics: - Career site/jobs aggregation boilerplate - Payment/fintech verticals - Compensation/salary range disclosure - Autonomous vehicles / robotics (emerging verticals) - Healthcare AI

    Most 2024-specific topics: - Open source / multi-language stacks (3.7x 2024 lift) - AI chatbot training (Outlier-style gig work, 3.5x lift) - .NET/ASP.NET (3.1x lift) - Django/Flask Python web (2.9x lift) - React/Angular frontend (2.8-2.6x lift) - Salesforce (2.5x lift)

    Cross-validation with Fightin' Words: BERTopic confirms FW findings. The 2024-specific topics are dominated by specific traditional tech stacks. The 2026-specific topics mix genuine AI/vertical signals with boilerplate expansion.

    Key Takeaways for the Paper

    1. "AI" is the real signal. AI/ML/LLM terminology is 3-4x more prevalent in 2026 SWE postings. This is the strongest genuine content change.

    2. Traditional tech stacks declined. JavaScript, HTML, SQL, Java, .NET, Angular/React, and Django/Flask all shrank. The "full-stack web developer" archetype is fading.

    3. Formal requirements language declined. "Years experience", "bachelor degree", "computer science", "proficient" all fell, suggesting a shift toward less formulaic job writing.

    4. Agile/Scrum methodology language declined. Agile, scrum, jira, kanban, sprint all declined. This may reflect either methodology maturity (no longer worth mentioning) or a shift away from these frameworks.

    5. Much of the raw text signal is boilerplate. Pay transparency laws, benefits sections, and about-company sections expanded massively. See T13 for the section anatomy decomposition.

    6. Observation vs inference distinction: The text changes document what employers are writing differently. Whether this reflects actual job content changes vs. marketing/compliance trends requires triangulation with T13 section anatomy.

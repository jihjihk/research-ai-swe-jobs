    T22: Ghost & Aspirational Requirements Forensics

    Status: Complete
    Purpose: Stress-test the paper's headline finding that entry-level management indicators jumped from ~9.4% to ~40.8% (previous wave estimate). Determine whether scope inflation is real hiring-bar expansion or measurement artifact.

    Executive Summary

    The headline management indicator finding is substantially inflated by measurement error. The broad pattern set used in earlier waves captures generic language ("a leading company", "cross-functional collaboration") rather than actual management responsibilities. When cleaned:

    Pattern set 2024 junior 2026 junior Change Interpretation
    Broad (earlier waves) 39.2-39.4% 61.7% +22pp INFLATED
    Strict (validated) 24.6-30.1% 34.3% +4-10pp Moderate, real
    Moderate (careful) 26.2-31.2% 35.3% +4-9pp Moderate, real

    The corrected finding: Management/scope indicators in entry-level SWE postings increased by approximately 4-10 percentage points (not 31pp), representing moderate scope inflation that is real but far less dramatic than initially reported.

    AI requirements are NOT aspirational -- they are actually LESS hedged than non-AI requirements in 2026 (20% vs 30% hedge fraction), reversing a 2024 pattern where AI was more aspirational (40% vs 34%).

    1. Management Indicator Pattern Decomposition

    The Problem: "Leading" Is Not Management

    The single largest contributor to the management indicator rate is the pattern \bleading\b, which matches 33.0% of 2026 entry-level postings. Manual inspection of 1,411 matches reveals:

    Usage type Count Share
    Adjective ("a leading company", "leading-edge technologies") 913 64.7%
    Management verb ("leading a team") 9 0.6%
    Ambiguous 489 34.7%

    Only 0.6% of "leading" matches reflect management activity. The vast majority describe the company itself ("Dice is the leading career destination") or technology ("leading-edge technologies"). This pattern alone inflates the entry-level rate by approximately 20+ percentage points.

    Other Problematic Patterns

    Pattern 2026 entry rate Primary usage
    \bleading\b 33.0% 99%+ adjective/company description
    \bcross[-]?functional\b 19.1% 84% collaboration ("work with cross-functional teams"), 0.5% leadership
    \bleadership\b 11.5% 77% company culture/values, 11% skill desired, 10% technical leadership
    \bstrategic\b 7.2% Mostly company description ("strategic asset")

    Individual Pattern Contributions (2026 Entry-Level)

    Pattern Rate Validity
    \bleading\b 33.0% INVALID -- almost entirely adjective
    \bcross[-]?functional\b 19.1% MIXED -- overwhelmingly collaboration, not management
    \bleadership\b 11.5% MIXED -- mostly company boilerplate or soft skill
    \bstrategic\b 7.2% MOSTLY INVALID in entry context
    \bmentor(ing)?\b 6.1% VALID -- genuine scope expectation
    \bcoach(ing)?\b 3.9% VALID -- genuine scope expectation
    \bproject\smanagement\b 3.5% VALID
    \bbudget\b 2.2% VALID but may be about budget objects, not managing budgets
    \broadmap\b 2.2% VALID
    \bstakeholder\b 1.9% VALID
    \bteam\sof\s\d+\b 1.6% VALID but sometimes describes team size, not reports
    \bprogram\smanagement\b 0.7% VALID
    \blead\steam\b 0.4% VALID
    \bdirect\sreports\b 0.1% VALID
    \bmanage\steam\b / \bmanaging\steam\b 0.1% VALID
    \bpeople\smanagement\b 0.0% VALID (but near-zero for entry)

    Corrected Management Rates

    Using the strict pattern set (excluding \bleading\b, \bleadership\b, and \bstrategic\b in their unqualified forms; keeping only validated management indicators):

    Period Junior (broad) Junior (strict) Senior (broad) Senior (strict)
    2024-01 39.2% 24.6% 56.8% 45.0%
    2024-04 39.4% 30.1% 51.3% 39.7%
    2026-03 61.7% 34.3% 75.6% 55.1%

    The junior-to-senior gap also narrows: Under strict patterns, the 2026 gap is 55.1% - 34.3% = 20.8pp (senior higher), compared to the broad set's 75.6% - 61.7% = 13.9pp. This confirms the broad set was disproportionately contaminated at the junior level.

    2. Section-Level Analysis

    Where do management terms appear in the posting structure?

    Section 2026 entry-level 2024 entry-level
    Requirements/qualifications 25.9% 47.1%
    Responsibilities 40.8% 39.7%
    About company 16.3% 1.3%
    Benefits 14.5% 3.2%
    Unknown/no headers 45.0% 31.0%

    Key insight: In 2026, management terms are much more likely to appear in "about company" (16.3% vs 1.3%) and "benefits" (14.5% vs 3.2%) sections. This suggests a portion of the increase comes from expanded boilerplate/company description sections rather than actual job requirements.

    The shift from requirements (47% to 26%) to about-company and benefits sections indicates that 2026 postings are more templated, with management-adjacent language bleeding from company descriptions into the text analysis.

    3. Management Term Hedging Context

    For management terms that DO appear in 2026 entry-level postings:

    Context 2026 share 2024 share
    Hedged ("nice to have", etc.) 0.7% 1.9%
    Firm/required ("must have", etc.) 5.2% 9.1%
    Neutral (no hedge/firm marker) 94.1% 89.0%

    The overwhelming majority (94%) of management terms appear without any hedging or requirement framing. They are neither required nor optional -- they simply exist in the description, often as descriptions of the company or environment rather than as job requirements.

    4. Text Source Sensitivity

    Management rates vary significantly depending on which text column is used:

    Text source 2024-01 junior 2024-04 junior 2026-03 junior
    description (full, with boilerplate) 54.6% 57.8% 63.6%
    description_core (rule-based cleaned) 43.8% 54.8% 61.7%
    text (best available, LLM-cleaned for Kaggle) 39.2% 39.4% 61.7%

    For 2024, the LLM-cleaned text (description_core_llm, available for Kaggle rows) substantially reduces the management rate compared to description_core, dropping from 54.8% to 39.4% for 2024-04. This confirms that boilerplate removal matters enormously for this metric.

    For 2026, most rows fall back to description_core (LLM cleaning deferred for scraped data), so the rates align. When LLM-cleaned text becomes available for 2026 data, the 2026 management rate will likely drop further.

    5. AI Ghostiness Test

    Are AI requirements more aspirational than traditional requirements?

    Period AI hedge fraction Non-AI hedge fraction Interpretation
    2024-01 40.2% 34.2% AI slightly more aspirational
    2024-04 30.9% 21.9% AI more aspirational
    2026-03 20.0% 30.0% AI LESS aspirational

    Finding: AI requirements REVERSED from aspirational to firm between 2024 and 2026. In 2024, AI terms were more likely to appear in hedged context (40% vs 34% for non-AI). By 2026, AI terms are less hedged than non-AI terms (20% vs 30%). This suggests AI requirements have transitioned from "nice-to-have" signaling to genuine hiring requirements.

    This is evidence AGAINST the hypothesis that AI requirements are purely aspirational.

    6. Ghost Indicator Prevalence

    Kitchen-Sink Score (tech count x org scope)

    Period Junior mean Senior mean
    2024-01 1.2 3.5
    2024-04 2.0 3.2
    2026-03 3.4 6.6

    Kitchen-sink scores increased across both seniority levels, but the senior increase (3.2 to 6.6, +3.4) is larger than junior (+1.4 to +2.2). This reflects growing listing complexity generally, not junior-specific inflation.

    Aspiration Ratio

    Period Junior mean Senior mean
    2024-01 0.72 0.54
    2024-04 0.63 0.59
    2026-03 0.74 0.71

    Aspiration ratios rose for both levels in 2026, with junior consistently higher. The gap narrowed from 0.18 (2024-01) to 0.03 (2026-03), suggesting senior postings became more aspirational while junior stayed similar.

    YOE-Scope Mismatch (Junior Only)

    Period N Mismatch rate
    2024-01 130 6.2%
    2024-04 830 3.0%
    2026-03 3255 7.2%

    Small but real: 7.2% of 2026 entry-level postings have either YOE >= 5 or >= 3 senior scope terms. This is up from 3.0% in 2024-04 but the 2024-01 baseline (6.2%) makes the trend less clear.

    7. Aggregator vs Direct Comparison

    Direct junior Aggregator junior Direct senior Aggregator senior
    Kitchen-sink (2026) 3.3 3.7 6.5 7.2
    Mgmt rate (2026) 61.2% 62.7% 75.7% 74.7%
    AI rate (2026) 54.5% 39.7% 42.6% 29.5%

    Aggregator postings are comparable on management indicators and kitchen-sink scores. The biggest difference is AI rates: direct postings have substantially higher AI mention rates (54.5% vs 39.7% for junior, 42.6% vs 29.5% for senior). Aggregator postings are less likely to include AI requirements, possibly because they template-strip or simplify.

    9. Industry Variation

    Industries with highest kitchen-sink scores (SWE postings, n >= 20):

    Industry N Kitchen-sink Mgmt rate AI rate
    Accounting + IT Services 72 21.5 100% 100%
    Accounting + Business Consulting 236 20.4 89.8% 86.4%
    Insurance 339 19.8 80.8% 39.8%
    Banking + Financial Services 32 16.0 87.5% 37.5%

    Financial services and consulting industries have the highest ghost-like characteristics. Insurance is notably high on kitchen-sink score but lower on AI rates.

    10. Conclusions and Recommendations

    What the Paper Can Claim

    1. Moderate scope inflation at entry level is REAL (strict patterns: +4-10pp increase in genuine management/scope indicators like mentoring, coaching, project management, stakeholder work).

    2. The magnitude previously reported (~+31pp) was an artifact of pattern matching that captured adjective uses of "leading", company descriptions containing "leadership", and collaboration language ("cross-functional") rather than management expectations.

    3. AI requirements transitioned from aspirational to required between 2024 and 2026, evidenced by the reversal in hedge fraction (40% to 20%).

    Required Corrections

    1. The management indicator pattern set MUST be revised before formal analysis. Remove \bleading\b entirely (99.4% false positive for management). Add word-boundary disambiguation for \bleadership\b (only count "leadership skills/experience/qualities" in requirements sections). Remove \bstrategic\b unqualified.

    2. Report the corrected rate (strict: 24.6-30.1% to 34.3%, or moderate: 26.2-31.2% to 35.3%) instead of the broad rate.

    3. Cross-functional collaboration should be discussed as a soft-skill/collaboration indicator, not a management indicator. It's real and growing (19.1% at entry level) but represents collaborative expectations, not supervisory scope.

    4. LLM text cleaning of 2026 data should be prioritized. The 2024 data benefits from LLM boilerplate removal that reduces false positives; 2026 data does not. This creates a systematic bias that inflates the 2024-to-2026 delta.

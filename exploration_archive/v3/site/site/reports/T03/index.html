    T03: Seniority Label Comparison

    Task: Compare all seniority variants -- where do they agree, and does the choice change RQ1 results?

    Date: 2026-04-05 Data: data/unified.parquet (Stage 11 final output, 1.40M rows, 99 columns) Filters: source_platform='linkedin' AND is_english=true AND date_flag='ok' AND is_swe=true

    1. Seniority Coverage Summary (SWE Rows)

    Source Total SWE Native Non-null Imputed Non-unknown Final Non-unknown
    kaggle_arshkon 5,019 68.6% 44.2% 81.0%
    kaggle_asaniczka 23,213 100.0% 69.2% 100.0%
    scraped 24,095 82.6% 54.2% 93.3%

    Key observations: - Asaniczka has 100% native coverage but only mid-senior and associate labels (zero entry-level). This is the critical gap for RQ1. - Scraped data has substantially higher native coverage (82.6%) than arshkon (68.6%), reflecting LinkedIn's more complete metadata in 2026. - seniority_final achieves the best coverage by merging imputed + native backfill: 81% arshkon, 100% asaniczka, 93% scraped.

    2. Cross-Tabulations

    2a. seniority_native vs seniority_final

    46,056 pairs where native is non-null and final is non-unknown.

    Native \ Final entry associate mid-senior director
    entry 3,484 (97.9%) 0 74 (2.1%) 0
    associate 70 (2.3%) 2,205 (71.5%) 808 (26.2%) 1

    • Pattern: Entry labels are highly preserved (97.9%). Associate is problematic -- 26.2% of native associate labels become mid-senior in seniority_final, likely due to title-keyword overriding. Director is poorly resolved: 72.6% of native directors become mid-senior.

    2b. seniority_imputed vs seniority_final

    31,342 pairs where imputed is non-unknown and final is non-unknown.

    Imputed \ Final entry associate mid-senior director
    entry 902 (99.0%) 2 (0.2%) 7 (0.8%) 0
    associate 182 (31.7%) 245 (42.6%) 148 (25.7%) 0
    mid-senior 216 (0.7%) 129 (0.4%) 29,231 (98.8%) 3
    director 0 0 3 (1.1%) 274 (98.9%)
    • Agreement rate: 97.8%
    • Cohen's kappa: 0.806 (strong agreement)
    • Pattern: Imputed entry labels are highly reliable (99% preserved). Imputed associate is the weakest class -- only 42.6% agreement with final. When the imputer fires on ambiguous "associate" titles, it frequently conflicts with native labels that resolve differently.

    2c. seniority_native vs seniority_imputed

    27,967 pairs where both are non-null/non-unknown. This is the direct classifier accuracy test.

    Native \ Imputed entry associate mid-senior director
    entry 293 (38.4%) 182 (23.8%) 289 (37.8%) 0
    associate 72 (6.2%) 150 (12.9%) 937 (80.8%) 1
    mid-senior 132 (0.5%) 148 (0.6%) 25,339 (98.2%) 192 (0.7%)
    director 0 0 195 (84.1%) 37 (15.9%)
    • Agreement rate: 92.3%
    • Cohen's kappa: 0.354 (fair agreement)
    • Critical finding: The rule-based imputer has a severe mid-senior default bias. For native entry-level rows where the imputer also fires, only 38.4% are correctly classified as entry -- 37.8% are called mid-senior and 23.8% associate. For native associate, 80.8% are called mid-senior. The low kappa (0.354) is entirely driven by the non-mid-senior classes.

    Temporal Comparison

    Native Class Arshkon Accuracy Scraped Accuracy Difference
    entry 21.2% 41.9% +20.7pp
    associate 9.4% 8.7% -0.7pp
    mid-senior 98.2% 97.7% -0.5pp
    director 21.1% 15.5% -5.6pp

    Finding: Entry-level accuracy nearly doubles from arshkon (21.2%) to scraped (41.9%). This is NOT temporal instability of the classifier itself -- it reflects that scraped entry-level postings more often use explicit title keywords ("Junior Software Engineer", "Entry Level Developer") that the rule-based classifier can detect. Arshkon entry-level postings may have less explicit title signals, consistent with the much lower imputer coverage rate (17.2% vs 22.7%).

    Associate and director accuracy is consistently poor across both periods -- the imputer is simply unable to identify these classes reliably.

    Important coverage caveat: The imputer only fires for a minority of entry-level rows (17-23%). The accuracy figures above are conditional on the imputer producing a non-unknown label. The imputer's most common behavior for entry-level rows is to produce "unknown" (77-83% of the time), which is then backfilled from native in seniority_final.

    4. Entry-Level Label Provenance

    Where do seniority_final = 'entry' labels come from?

    Source Total Entry native_backfill title_keyword description_explicit
    kaggle_arshkon 830 729 (87.8%) 100 (12.0%) 1 (0.1%)
    kaggle_asaniczka 130 0 (0%) 130 (100%) 0
    scraped 3,255 2,592 (79.6%) 661 (20.3%) 2 (0.1%)

    Critical: Asaniczka's 130 entry-level labels come entirely from title_keyword imputation -- the native labels for asaniczka only contain mid-senior and associate. These 130 are likely titles like "Junior Software Engineer" that were keyword-matched. Given asaniczka's 23K SWE rows, this represents 0.6% entry-level share, which is essentially a data artifact, not a real measurement.

    Entry-level leakage

    74 rows with seniority_native = 'entry' are overridden to seniority_final = 'mid-senior' due to title_keyword rules. These are cases where the title contains mid-senior keywords (e.g., "Senior") despite the platform labeling the posting as entry level. This is a small but systematic issue.

    5. RQ1 Junior Share Comparison

    Four operationalizations, comparing arshkon (2024-04) and scraped (2026-03). Asaniczka is included for reference but its junior share is unreliable due to zero native entry labels.

    Variant Arshkon Junior % (n) Scraped Junior % (n) Asaniczka Junior % (n) Direction
    A: seniority_final (non-unknown) 20.4% (4,066) 14.5% (22,469) 0.6% (23,213) DOWN 5.9pp
    B: seniority_native only 22.3% (3,444) 14.0% (19,896) 0.0% (23,213) DOWN 8.3pp
    C: High-confidence (title_keyword + native_backfill) 21.4% (3,881) 14.9% (21,831) 0.6% (23,213) DOWN 6.5pp
    D: All signals (excl. unknown) 20.4% (4,066) 14.5% (22,469) 0.6% (23,213) DOWN 5.9pp

    Verdict: All variants agree on direction AND approximate magnitude

    All four variants show junior share declining by 5.9-8.3 percentage points from arshkon to scraped. The native-only variant (B) gives the highest arshkon junior share (22.3%) and the largest decline (8.3pp), because it uses the cleanest labels but excludes rows without native metadata.

    The direction is robust: junior share decreased from ~20-22% to ~14-15% regardless of which seniority column is used.

    Critical caveats:

    1. Composition effects: Arshkon and scraped have limited company overlap. The decline could partly reflect different employer mix rather than actual restructuring.
    2. Asaniczka cannot contribute to this comparison. Its 0.6% entry-level share is an artifact of having zero native entry labels.
    3. The absolute level of junior share depends on the operationalization (20.4% vs 22.3% for arshkon), but the change direction is robust.
    4. Variants A and D are identical because the current pipeline has no additional weak signals beyond what feeds seniority_final.

    8. Which Operationalization Is Most Defensible?

    For trend estimation (RQ1 junior share over time)

    Recommended: Variant B (seniority_native only) for the primary estimate, with Variant A as a robustness check.

    Rationale: - Native labels are platform-assigned and consistent across periods - Rule-based imputation has unknown temporal stability and different accuracy rates (21.2% entry accuracy in arshkon vs 41.9% in scraped) - Using only native labels avoids the risk that imputation artifacts create a spurious trend - The 8.3pp decline (variant B) represents the cleanest signal

    Limitation: Native coverage is 68.6% for arshkon and 82.6% for scraped. The 14pp coverage gap between sources means the native-only sample may not be fully comparable. Rows with missing native labels could have different seniority distributions.

    For cross-sectional comparison (within-period analysis)

    Recommended: Variant A (seniority_final) because it maximizes sample size and has the best coverage (81-100%). Within a single period, imputation rules are consistently applied.

    For classification training and validation

    Recommended: seniority_native as ground truth, with separate accuracy reporting by source. The per-class accuracy results (Section 3) show the rule-based classifier is unreliable for non-mid-senior classes. Any classifier trained or validated against seniority_final inherits these biases.

    For robustness/sensitivity analyses

    Report all four variants. The agreement between variants strengthens the finding; disagreement flags fragile results. Given that all four agree on the direction and magnitude of junior share change, the RQ1 finding is robust to operationalization choice.

    The 3-level mapping is not just convenient -- it is necessary

    Associate accuracy is 8.7-9.4% across sources. The associate category is essentially unusable as a standalone label. Collapsing to the 3-level mapping (entry=junior, associate=mid, mid-senior+director=senior) is the only defensible approach for analyses that need more than a binary junior/non-junior split.

    9. Surprises and Unexpected Patterns

    1. Native-vs-imputed kappa is only 0.354. This is worse than the 0.440 reported in the prior run on stage8_final.parquet. The rule-based imputer is even less aligned with native labels than previously measured, primarily because entry-level accuracy dropped to 21.2% in arshkon (down from 31.6% in the prior report). This appears to be driven by slightly different row counts in the unified dataset.

    2. Director labels are severely misclassified. 72.6% of native directors become mid-senior in seniority_final, and the imputer has only 15.5-21.1% accuracy for directors. Director-level analysis should rely exclusively on native labels where available.

    3. The arshkon entry-level imputer accuracy (21.2%) is alarmingly low. Only 28 of 132 entry-level arshkon rows that the imputer classifies are correct. The imputer's value for entry-level detection is almost entirely in the 77-83% of rows where it returns "unknown" and triggers native backfill.

    4. All junior share variants agree on decline of 5.9-8.3pp. This is moderate, not dramatic. Arshkon starts at 20-22% junior, not 50%. The decline is real but should not be characterized as "juniors disappearing."

    5. 74 native entry-level rows are overridden to mid-senior by title keywords. These posting-level contradictions (platform says entry, title says senior) warrant investigation -- they could be platform labeling errors or genuine mismatches.

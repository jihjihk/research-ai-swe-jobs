# T11: Requirements Complexity & Credential Stacking

**Task:** Quantify whether "scope inflation" exists beyond junior share decline. This is the direct test of whether the paper leads with scope inflation or slot elimination.

**Sample:** SWE, LinkedIn-only. For seniority-stratified analyses: arshkon (n=5,019, April 2024) vs scraped (n=24,095, March 2026). asaniczka (n=23,213, Jan 2024) excluded from seniority analysis (no entry-level labels).

---

## Headline Finding

**SCOPE INFLATION IS REAL AND MULTI-DIMENSIONAL.** Entry-level SWE postings in 2026 demand significantly more across nearly every requirement dimension compared to 2024 -- even after length normalization. This is not merely description bloat. The paper should lead with scope inflation as the primary mechanism, not just slot elimination.

Critically, the scope inflation is UNIVERSAL across seniority levels, but its CHARACTER differs:
- **Entry-level:** AI requirements exploded (+24pp binary), management terms appeared where they didn't exist before (+31pp), scope/ownership language grew (+11pp). Meanwhile, YOE requirements FELL (-25pp binary). The junior role is being redefined, not just eliminated.
- **Senior-level:** Similar patterns but with larger absolute baseline. AI requirements surged to 34% presence. Scope language doubled.
- **The complexity increase survives all sensitivity checks** -- aggregator exclusion, company capping, seniority operationalization, and within-2024 calibration.

## The Entry-Level Scope Inflation Test

|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Tech count | 5.48 | 6.91 | +1.43 | +26.1% | <0.001 | 0.156 |
| Language count | 1.47 | 1.74 | +0.27 | +18.7% | <0.001 | 0.122 |
| ML/AI tech count | 0.23 | 1.11 | +0.88 | +383% | <0.001 | 0.279 |
| Soft skill count | 1.78 | 2.65 | +0.87 | +49.2% | <0.001 | 0.264 |
| Scope term count | 0.48 | 0.74 | +0.25 | +52.3% | <0.001 | 0.137 |
| Management indicators | 0.10 | 0.58 | +0.48 | +482% | <0.001 | 0.327 |

Every metric except education level and framework count shows statistically significant increases (Mann-Whitney U, all p<0.001). The largest effect sizes are for management indicators (r=0.327), requirement breadth (r=0.285), ML/AI count (r=0.279), and soft skills (r=0.264).

The percentage increases for AI requirements are enormous across all levels but come from a near-zero base. In absolute terms, senior roles have the highest AI requirement rate (34.1% presence vs 27.5% for junior). Management indicator growth is proportionally largest for junior (+31pp absolute) -- this is the scope inflation signal.

## Length Normalization Deep Dive

Description length grew substantially: +19.7% for junior, +52.3% for senior. This raises a critical question: are complexity increases simply artifacts of longer descriptions?

**Answer: Partly for tech count, but NOT for scope/AI/management metrics.**

### Tech Density (per 1K chars)

| Level | 2024-04 | 2026-03 | Change |
|---|---|---|---|
| Junior | 2.00 | 2.04 | +2.3% (*) |
| Mid | 1.87 | 2.31 | +23.1% (***) |
| Senior | 2.78 | 2.06 | -26.0% (***) |

Tech density is essentially flat for junior (the 2.3% increase is barely significant). For senior, tech density actually FELL -- longer descriptions added proportionally less tech content. This means raw tech count growth is largely explained by description length growth for junior roles.

### But Non-Tech Density Metrics Tell a Different Story

For entry-level specifically:
- Scope density (per 1K chars): 0.047 -> 0.095 (+103%, ***)
- AI density (per 1K chars): 0.008 -> 0.081 (+900%, ***)
- Soft skill density: 0.50 -> 0.61 per 1K chars (+22%, ***)

**Scope, AI, and soft skill density all increased significantly even after length normalization.** The management indicators (+31pp binary) cannot be explained by length -- they either appear or don't.

### Interpretation

The scope inflation is real but nuanced:
- **Tech stack breadth:** Modest real increase (mostly from AI/ML technologies). Traditional tech count growth is largely absorbed by longer descriptions.
- **Organizational scope & ownership language:** Genuine doubling in density. Entry-level roles now explicitly demand ownership, cross-functional collaboration, and stakeholder engagement at rates previously reserved for senior roles.
- **AI requirements:** Entirely new dimension that barely existed in 2024 (3.9% of entry-level postings). Now present in 27.5%.
- **Management indicators:** The most dramatic shift. Entry-level postings mentioning mentoring, team leadership, or hiring went from 9.4% to 40.8%. This is not description bloat -- it's a fundamental redefinition of what "entry-level" means.

## Sensitivity Checks

### (a) Aggregator exclusion

All findings hold. Example for junior:
- All: tech 5.5 -> 6.9, breadth 3.26 -> 3.98, AI 3.9% -> 27.5%
- No aggregator: tech 5.6 -> 6.9, breadth 3.46 -> 4.10, AI 4.2% -> 28.1%

Aggregator exclusion slightly increases baseline 2024 complexity (aggregators have simpler postings) but the 2024-to-2026 change direction and magnitude are unchanged.

### (b) Company capping (max 20 per company-period-seniority)

Junior: tech 5.5 -> 7.1, breadth 3.30 -> 4.01, AI 3.9% -> 27.7%. Nearly identical to uncapped. Large companies are not driving the complexity increase through template repetition.

### (c) Seniority operationalization

Using seniority_final 'entry' vs 'associate' (which maps to 'mid' in 3-level):
- Entry 2024: breadth 3.26; Associate 2024: breadth 3.27 (similar)
- Entry 2026: breadth 3.98; Associate 2026: breadth 4.18
Both show similar magnitude increases. The scope inflation finding is not an artifact of how "junior" is defined.

### (f) Within-2024 calibration

| Source | Tech | Breadth | Scope | AI | Soft Skills |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| arshkon (2024-04) | 6.3 | 3.41 | 0.57 | 7.6% | 1.77 |
| asaniczka (2024-01) | 6.2 | 3.67 | 0.64 | 3.6% | 1.92 |
| **scraped (2026-03)** | **8.3** | **4.60** | **1.22** | **33.2%** | **2.89** |

Within-2024 variation: breadth differs by 0.26 (arshkon vs asaniczka). The 2026 increase of +1.19 (vs arshkon) is 4.6x the noise floor. For AI requirement rate: within-2024 variation is 4.0pp (7.6% vs 3.6%); the 2024-to-2026 change is +25.6pp, or 6.4x the noise floor.

**All complexity increases materially exceed within-2024 cross-source variation.**

## Surprises & Unexpected Patterns

1. **Management indicators in entry-level roles (+31pp):** The single most unexpected finding. 40.8% of 2026 entry-level postings mention management, mentoring, or team leadership -- activities traditionally reserved for senior+ roles. If taken at face value, employers are asking entry-level hires to mentor, coach, or lead. This could represent: (a) genuine scope inflation, (b) aspirational posting language, or (c) AI-generated posting content inflating requirements. This needs qualitative investigation.

2. **YOE dropped while everything else increased:** The decoupling of YOE requirements from other complexity metrics is striking. Employers are simultaneously lowering experience floors and raising breadth expectations. This is consistent with a model where AI tools reduce the need for accumulated experience but increase the need for cross-functional competency.

3. **Education requirements barely changed:** Despite all other dimensions inflating, education level (BS/MS/PhD) distribution is essentially stable. Scope inflation is happening through skill and competency language, not credentialing.

4. **Senior tech density FELL (-26%):** Senior postings got much longer but added proportionally LESS tech content. The extra text is going toward organizational scope, management language, and AI requirements -- not deeper tech stacks. Senior roles are being redefined as broader-scope leaders, not deeper technologists.

5. **Asaniczka (2024-01) breadth is HIGHER than arshkon (2024-04):** 3.67 vs 3.41. This cross-source baseline variation is important to flag -- it means the absolute magnitude of 2024-to-2026 change depends on which 2024 baseline is used. Using arshkon gives a change of +1.19; using asaniczka gives +0.93. Both are large and significant.

## Data Quality Caveats

1. **Text source mismatch:** arshkon 2024 uses LLM-cleaned text (99.4% of junior entries); scraped 2026 uses rule-based cleaned text (100%). Rule-based cleaning retains more boilerplate, which could inflate text-based pattern counts in 2026. However: (a) density metrics normalize for length, and still show increases; (b) binary indicators are robust to text quality since any single mention suffices.

2. **Pattern matching limitations:** Regex-based feature extraction is inherently noisy. False positives (e.g., "coach" in non-management contexts) and false negatives are both possible. However, the patterns are applied consistently across periods, so bias should be symmetric.

3. **Description length confound:** Raw `description_length` includes boilerplate. Density calculations using raw length are conservative for 2024 (LLM-cleaned text is ~50% of raw) and liberal for 2026 (rule-cleaned is ~68% of raw). A more precise density would use cleaned text length, but we computed patterns on cleaned text while normalizing by raw length. This means 2024 densities may be slightly overstated and 2026 densities slightly understated, making the real density increase potentially LARGER than reported.

4. **Entry-level sample size asymmetry:** 830 (2024) vs 3,255 (2026). The larger 2026 sample gives higher precision but the 2024 baseline has adequate power for the effect sizes observed.

---

## Implications for Paper Framing

**The paper should frame the story as scope inflation operating through multiple mechanisms:**

1. **Slot elimination** (from Wave 1): Junior share declined 5.9-8.3pp. Fewer entry-level positions exist.

2. **Scope inflation of surviving slots** (this task): The entry-level postings that DO exist demand fundamentally broader competencies. Requirement breadth increased by 0.72 categories (22%), management expectations appeared essentially ex nihilo (+31pp), and AI competency became a near-standard expectation (+24pp).

3. **YOE deflation** (from T06 + this task): Experience requirements FELL even as scope increased. This suggests employers are seeking a different TYPE of candidate -- less experienced but more broadly capable, consistent with AI tools enabling junior engineers to operate at higher scope.

4. **Universal upward drift:** The complexity increase is not unique to junior roles. All levels show significant increases, but the entry-level shift is the most dramatic in relative terms because the baseline was lowest.

**Recommended paper lead:** "Scope inflation is real, multi-dimensional, and operates alongside slot elimination. Entry-level SWE roles in 2026 demand management capabilities (+31pp), AI competencies (+24pp), and organizational scope language (+11pp) that barely existed in 2024 entry-level postings -- even as YOE requirements fell."

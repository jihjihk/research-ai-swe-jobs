## Headline finding

**Posting concentration is larger than the cross-period entry-share signal — company composition drives the aggregate.** Three interlocking facts:

1. **Scraped 2026 is more concentrated than arshkon 2024** (Gini 0.69 vs 0.43; top-20 share 18% vs 12%) — but most of that extra concentration lives in a handful of *tech-giant intern pipelines*, not in the aggregator class.
2. **79-89% of SWE companies with ≥5 postings have ZERO entry-labeled (J1) rows in every source.** Entry-level posting is a highly specialized activity and ~240 companies (~11% of eligible employers pooled) account for ~65% of the J3-YOE-proxy entry pool.
3. **Excluding the 240 entry-specialist employers flips the J3 cross-period direction.** Full-corpus J3 rises 13.6% → 17.2% arsh→scraped; with specialists removed, J3 moves 13.6% → 14.4% — a ~75% shrinkage in effect size. The cross-period entry-share trend Wave 2 is likely to report is a compositional artifact of which specialists were active in which window.

**Per-finding concentration prediction (§9) is the deliverable downstream tasks must read first.** It dictates capping, dedup, and exclusion defaults for every Wave 2-3 aggregation.

**Stage 4 multi-location dedup works.** Only 16 residual (company × title × description_hash) triples remain within scraped (max dup = 2), validating the expected "verification, not discovery" role of the dup-template audit.

## Sensitivity-disagreement drill-in

The concentration story fundamentally splits along the J1-vs-J3 divide:

- **J1 cross-period change (arshkon → scraped):** +3.73% → +3.12% overall; excluding specialists +3.42% → +2.82% — direction consistent, tiny negative.
- **J3 cross-period change (arshkon → scraped):** +14.98% → +17.19% overall; excluding specialists +13.64% → +14.36% — direction preserved but effect shrinks 67%.
- **J3 within-2024 calibration (arshkon → asaniczka):** +14.98% → +10.36% — within-source noise is MORE than the specialist-adjusted cross-period effect.

**The most likely mechanism:** the J3 YOE-based "entry" definition picks up tech-giant intern pipelines and YOE-lax-phrasing roles. Those intern pipelines ARE more visible in 2026 scraped data (more aggressive scraping, direct-from-careers-page enrichment) than in the 2024 Kaggle snapshots. So the J3 cross-period signal is partly real (tech giants genuinely do post many new-grad roles in 2026) and partly artefact (Kaggle snapshots undercounted them).

**Recommendation for Wave 2+:** use J3 with specialists excluded AND aggregators excluded as the primary entry-share definition. Report J1 as a label-based sensitivity that is underpowered.

1. **Only 5 of 240 flagged entry-specialists trigger under label-based J1/J2.** The vast majority are YOE-only flagged. This contradicts the intuition that "entry-specialist" is detectable from seniority labels.

2. **Stage 4 dedup is validated — only 16 within-source duplicate (co × title × hash) groups in scraped.** The 2025 finding that 23% of entry pool came from 6 bulk-posting companies is *no longer the dominant concentration story*. The new story is tech-giant intern pipelines.

3. **TikTok is the one tech giant with 52% J1 entry share.** Contrast with Google (0.9% J1, 52% J3) and Walmart (0% J1, 74% J3). TikTok's labels genuinely say "entry"; everyone else's don't. Why TikTok and not anyone else is worth asking.

4. **Walmart scraped: 74% J3 entry share, mean YOE 2.2, 233 SWE postings.** Walmart is effectively running a new-grad-pipeline funnel in 2026 scraped data. None are label-entry. The same is true of Google and Microsoft at smaller ratios.

5. **Asaniczka concentration is aggregator-driven.** HHI 54 → 19 when aggregators excluded; ClearanceJobs alone is 5% of asaniczka SWE. This is a corpus artefact, not a real labor market statement.

6. **Aggregator share in scraped has ~doubled** from arshkon 9.2% to scraped 16.6%. Any "aggregator-inclusive" cross-period comparison is conflating platform-composition changes with real changes.

7. **74.5% of scraped companies are new entrants** (not in any 2024 source). The returning/new split exists at every aggregate — and "new entrants" skew aggregator-heavy (20% vs 14%). Between-company decomposition shows most J3 / J4 shift is driven by this composition change, NOT by the same companies changing their mix.

8. **AI-mention rise is 91% within-company** (within the 125-company overlap panel) — the one aggregate metric that is NOT concentration-driven. This is good news for the RQ1/RQ3 hypothesis that employers are rewriting job descriptions to add AI requirements.

9. **Cross-company description-hash collisions are restricted to staffing-firm sibling clusters.** 15 asaniczka collisions cluster on 5-6 small-to-medium staffing firms reposting under sibling brand names. Canonicalization doesn't merge them. This is a small-volume anomaly but a real gap for any "distinct descriptions" aggregate.

## Data quality caveats

- **`is_aggregator` is row-level in Stage 2**, but the specialist cross-tab uses `ANY(is_aggregator)` over company rows; we switched to a majority rule (`agg_rate ≥ 0.5`) after noticing Amazon flipped to "staffing" because 12/508 rows were coded aggregator. Downstream agents loading `entry_specialist_employers.csv` should honor `agg_rate` as the continuous signal.
- **`company_industry` is 0% populated in asaniczka** (explicit column gap). The `mode_industry` column in the shared artifact is NULL for ~30% of flagged companies (mostly asaniczka-only or small scraped-only rows).
- **Overlap panel (§5) uses ≥5 postings in both arshkon and scraped**, yielding n=125. This is small but covers 33% of arshkon postings and 18% of scraped postings — wide enough to be representative of the core establishment, narrow enough to be stable.
- **Specialist flagging uses POOLED (cross-source) counts per company.** A company with 3 arshkon + 3 scraped postings passes the n≥5 threshold even though no single source sees it at 5. This is intentional — we want a stable roster across sources — but agents that need source-specific specialists should use the source-split per-company table `tables/T06/06_company_junior_shares_bysource.csv`.

## Surprise inventory (findings that contradicted pre-exploration priors)

1. **Requirements section SHRANK −19% chars pooled-2024 → 2026 (T13), with senior falling −22%.** Pre-exploration framing assumed scope inflation in requirements. Instead, responsibilities (+67%), benefits (+87%), about_company (+71%), preferred (+152%), role_summary (+75%) expanded while requirements shrank. Pre-exploration intuition failed.
2. **Seniors changed MORE than juniors 2024→2026 (T12, T21).** Cosine shift senior 0.942 vs entry 0.953. Mentor rate ratio mid-senior 1.73× vs entry 1.07×. Original RQ1 was framed around the junior rung as the dynamic cell; the data says seniors moved first.
3. **Seniority boundaries SHARPENED, not blurred (T20).** All three adjacent boundaries (+0.054, +0.084, +0.003) gained AUC; J3-vs-S4 yoe panel gained +0.134 AUC. "Junior/senior convergence under AI" hypothesis cleanly rejected.
4. **Employers UNDER-specify AI vs developer usage (T23).** Every published worker benchmark (SO 2024 62%, Octoverse 73%, Anthropic 75%) exceeds employer ai_broad (46.8%). Direction opposite to pre-exploration.
5. **Domain dominates seniority by 8.6× in NMI (T09 + V1).** Pre-exploration organizing axis was seniority; actual organizing axis is tech domain archetype.
6. **AI/ML archetype growth is 81% new-entrant-driven (T28).** Existing firms pivoting into AI/ML contribute only ~19%; 935 companies appear in the AI/ML archetype only in 2026. "Firms pivoting to AI" narrative is the minor story; "new firms post AI roles" is the major one.
7. **AI surge is geographically uniform: 26/26 metros positive, r(ΔAI, ΔJ2)=−0.11 null (T17).** Top 3 AI-surge metros are Tampa Bay / Atlanta / Charlotte — not tech hubs. Spatial "AI exposure → junior displacement" story is rejected.
8. **`manage` has 14% semantic precision in SWE JDs (T11).** Previous "management language rose" claims were regex-dominated by non-managerial uses ("manage data," "manage state"). Refined pattern landed on `mentor` as the single driving term.
9. **Staff title doubled 2.6% → 6.3% while senior share flat (T10 + T21).** The senior tier is redistributing internally, not growing.
10. **AI-mentioning senior postings have MORE management and orchestration language in 2026, not less (T21).** In 2024 AI was in IC/research roles; by 2026 AI is alongside managerial/orchestration content. The emergent mgmt+orch+strat+AI sub-archetype (97%-2026, n=860) is a new senior role type.

## Key tensions to resolve in analysis phase

**Tension 1 — T11 breadth rises +39% vs T13 requirements shrinks −19%.** Both are real.
- `requirement_breadth` = count of distinct requirement *types* mentioned anywhere in cleaned description (tech + scope + soft + cred + mgmt + AI + YOE + edu).
- `requirements section chars` = character length of text classified into the requirements section.
- The 2026 posting mentions more *kinds* of things across responsibilities, role_summary, and about_company while the formal requirements section contracted. The mechanism is section reallocation + breadth diversification.
- The paper must explain this explicitly — it is not a contradiction but is guaranteed to be raised by a reviewer.

**Tension 2 — T06 says J3 rise is 95% between-company vs T09/T28 say J2 rise is 84% within-archetype.** Compatible: J3 uses a YOE-based proxy that is dominated by new-entrant tech-giant intern pipelines (compositional); J2 uses label-based entry+associate which within-archetype rose via LLM-labeling shift plus content labeling drift. Different operational variables.

**Tension 3 — Seniors changed more than juniors (T12) but boundary sharpened most in ML/AI (T20).** Seniors changed more *in absolute cosine distance*; ML/AI gained the most *boundary clarity* because its junior-vs-senior gap widened. Both true; the paper should separate "who changed how much" from "who became easiest to tell apart."

**Tension 4 — T16 breadth residualized +1.43 within 240 companies vs T13 requirements-section-chars shrank.** Breadth measures types across the whole posting; requirements-section measures chars in one section. Both can rise or fall independently.

**Tension 5 — T29 AI-strict retention 77% (low40 within-period) vs T29 mentor retention 72% (within-period) vs V2 3-feature score gives mentor retention 105%.** The AI-strict estimate is robust across score variants; the mentor estimate is method-sensitive. Analysis phase should cite AI as "15-30% mediated" and mentor as "mediation uncertain, possibly 0-30%."

## Data gaps for the analysis phase

1. **`is_remote_inferred` is 100% False (T17).** Preprocessing pipeline bug. Blocks any remote-work/hybrid analysis. Needs a preprocessing fix before the paper can contain any hybrid claim.
2. **No T13 classifier re-run on full 63,701 corpus.** T29 could not adjudicate the section-anatomy finding under the low-LLM subset; a full-corpus classifier run would close this.
3. **No ground-truth "LLM-authored" labels.** T29's authorship score is corpus-internal; a labeled calibration set (e.g., 100 postings with manually-verified "recruiter-LLM-drafted" vs "human-written") would sharpen mediation estimates.
4. **No pre-2023 baseline.** 2024-01 asaniczka is already post-ChatGPT (Nov-2022). Every attenuation estimate in T29 is a lower bound. An archived pre-2022 SWE JD set (e.g., OSS job-postings corpus, internet archive snapshots) would let us compute a clean baseline.
5. **Indeed cross-validation only at end-of-pipeline levels (T05).** Cannot diagnose LinkedIn native-label semantic drift over time without a period-matched Indeed labeled panel.
6. **Worker-usage benchmarks are self-reported.** Direct observational measures (e.g., GitHub copilot telemetry, IDE plugin counts) would be more authoritative than SO Developer Survey but are not public at the population-weighted scale.

### **H_A — Cross-occupation employer under-specification of AI**

**Statement.** The employer/worker AI-requirement gap (−28pp broad at the central benchmark)
observed in SWE (T23) is a general feature of AI-exposed occupations, not a SWE-specific
phenomenon. In occupations with high AI-task exposure (per Anthropic 2025 Economic Index),
the employer posting-level AI-mention rate trails worker-survey usage rates by a similar
or larger margin.

**Motivating evidence.**
- T23 SWE AI-broad 46.8% vs SO 2024 workers 62% (−15pp at the most permissive benchmark; −28pp at central).
- T18 DiD shows SWE-adjacent (ML eng, data sci, data eng, security eng) ALSO show substantial AI-strict rise (ml_eng +54pp, data_sci +15pp) and rise 95% of SWE-only change on tech_count.
- Anthropic 2025 Labor Market Impacts study: 75% of Computer & Math TASKS are covered by Claude — task-weighted coverage is large across occupations.

**Testable in analysis phase.**
- Use the existing unified.parquet AI-mention features stratified by `is_swe` / `is_swe_adjacent` / `is_control` × ONET-coded occupation (derivable from title regex).
- For each occupation bucket with n≥500 in both 2024 and 2026, compute ai_strict / ai_broad rates and compare against published AI-usage benchmarks from the same occupation (e.g., accountants ~22% AI use per 2024 surveys; nurses ~8%; marketers ~30–40%).
- Expected result: employer/worker gap persists across all high-exposure occupations with a similar direction as SWE.
- Alternative result: SWE is uniquely under-specified because of its tech-savvy hiring audience, in which case the gap should close in occupations with less-expert hiring audiences.

**Novelty / publishability.** **High.** RQ3's inversion is already the paper's most novel single finding. Generalizing to cross-occupation extends the contribution from "SWE labor dynamics" to "employer-side AI signaling in general." Appropriate for labor-economics venues (ILR Review, BE Journal of Economic Analysis & Policy) or science/management venues where cross-occupation framing is welcomed.

**Priority: 1** (novelty high, evidence direct, test feasible with existing data).

### **H_B — Requirement-section contraction as hidden hiring-bar signal**

**Statement.** The SWE-specific shrinkage of the formal requirements section (T13 −19% chars,
T18 −10.7pp share vs control +0.9pp) reflects an *implicit hiring-bar lowering* that is
masked by the simultaneous expansion of narrative sections (responsibilities +67%,
role_summary +75%, about_company +71%, preferred +152%). Employers are removing hard
requirements while expanding the "who we are / what you'll do" frame, which signals
hiring-bar softening without an explicit announcement.

**Motivating evidence.**
- T13 SWE requirements section fell −19% absolute chars, while responsibilities +67%, role_summary +75%, preferred +152%.
- T18: SWE requirements-share fell −10.7pp; adjacent −10.9pp; control +0.9pp (opposite direction).
- V2.9b verified on 500 non-SWE postings: classifier works on control (has_req_rate 73-86%); the divergence is real.
- Meanwhile `lieu degree` emerged as a 2026 bigram (T12) — credential loosening is in the text.
- T28 cross-archetype: universally positive desc_len_chars Δ (+85 to +594) but universally negative requirements-section pattern implied in the diffuse rise of benefits/preferred.

**Testable in analysis phase.**
- Regression on the 34,102 LLM-labeled corpus: dependent = requirements-share (T13 classifier), independent = period × seniority × archetype × is_aggregator × posting_length.
- Sub-analysis: do postings with shrunken requirements-section have higher yoe_extracted distributions or lower? If lower, hiring bar is literally lowering.
- Qualitative: within the 240-co overlap panel, do companies with the largest requirements-section contraction also have the largest J3 (yoe≤2) rise?

**Novelty / publishability.** **Medium-high.** Connects a substantive finding (section restructuring) to a policy-relevant interpretation (hidden bar softening). Candidates for CSCW or CHI: JD-as-signaling literature is active.

**Priority: 2.**

### **H_C — "AI-enabled tech lead" as emergent senior role type**

**Statement.** The mgmt+orch+strat+AI sub-archetype observed in T21 (97% 2026, n=860) is
not an artifact of clustering but a genuinely new senior role: an individual contributor
or team lead whose mandate is to *deploy AI tooling across a team's workflow*. This role
bundles: mentorship (39% mentor-binary in cluster), orchestration (85% density), AI
deployment (100% AI-mention), and moderate management (25%). It is distinct from both
the traditional "manager-track director" and the "IC-track staff engineer."

**Motivating evidence.**
- T21 k-means cluster 2: n=860, 97% are 2026, AI 100%, orch 0.85, mgmt 0.25, mentor 0.39.
- Staff title doubled 2.6% → 6.3% (T10); 2026 staff postings have +16pp mgmt, +11pp strat, +16pp mentor, +5pp AI vs non-staff seniors (T21).
- T20: management_density jumped to |0.42| coefficient at mid-senior-vs-associate boundary in 2026 (from |0.20| in 2024) — managerial language is diffusing downward.
- T16 cluster 4 "consolidating giants" (Google, Microsoft, AWS) shrank breadth but ramped entry-share — consistent with seniors-do-more, juniors-get-hired-in-batches.

**Testable in analysis phase.**
- Within the 860 cluster-2 postings, stratify by title — what fraction are "staff engineer," "tech lead," "principal," "ML lead," "AI engineer"? Is the emergent role labelled?
- Within-company trajectory: do the 62 "consolidating giants" (T16 cluster 4) disproportionately post cluster-2 profile postings in 2026?
- Cross-tab with T28 archetypes: does cluster 2 concentrate in specific domain archetypes (AI/ML, cloud_devops, backend_platform)?

**Novelty / publishability.** **High.** "AI-enabled tech lead" is a qualitative management-literature concept; quantifying its emergence via clustering is novel. Useful for HBR/MIT Sloan style outlets and for RQ4 interviews.

**Priority: 3.**

### **H_D — Senior-specific mentor rise reflects team-multiplier repositioning, not management-ladder promotion**

**Statement.** The 1.46–1.73× rise in mid-senior mentor-binary rate (T21) reflects a shift
from "individual contributor senior" to "team-multiplier senior" driven by AI automating IC
tasks, NOT a conventional management-ladder promotion shift. This predicts: (a) IC titles
with "senior/staff" grew faster than manager titles, (b) mentor language diffused more
strongly in archetypes most affected by AI tooling (per T28 mentor Δ), and (c) within-
company senior headcount didn't shift toward manager titles.

**Motivating evidence.**
- T21 mentor senior-disproportionate: mid-senior 1.73×, entry 1.07× — NOT a corpus-wide shift.
- Staff title doubled but director share essentially flat (T10, T30 S2=0.3% → 0.7%).
- T20: management-density diffused from director-only to mid-senior (|mgmt_z| 2024 director 0.18 → 2026 mid-senior 0.18) — downward diffusion, not upward promotion.
- T28 senior mentor Δ highest in qa_test_automation (+26pp), azure_dotnet_web (+20pp), dotnet_web_sql (+18pp), ai_ml (+17pp) — diverse archetype pattern consistent with "senior is now a reviewer role across the stack."

**Testable in analysis phase.**
- On the 17,713 scraped senior (S1) postings, classify each title as IC / manager via regex (IC = "engineer," "developer," "architect," "staff," "principal" without "manager"; manager = contains "manager," "director," "lead"). Compute mentor-rate separately.
- Expected: IC senior postings carry MORE mentor than 2024; manager postings carry mentor at a SIMILAR rate to 2024. The shift is in IC senior content, not in manager headcount.
- T16 240-co overlap: do companies with falling IC headcount (cluster 0 entry-contracting retreat) also carry more mentor at IC senior level in 2026?

**Novelty / publishability.** **Medium.** The IC-senior-as-team-multiplier narrative is a corporate-management staple but has not been quantitatively demonstrated at scale on JD data. Could anchor a Management Science submission.

**Priority: 4.**

### **H_E — Same-company J1 label drop + same-company J3 YOE rise = hiring juniors by YOE but not labeling them entry**

**Statement.** Companies like Google, Walmart, Microsoft are running large new-grad pipelines
(high J3 yoe≤2 share) but are NOT labeling those postings as `seniority_native='entry'` on
LinkedIn. This is a platform-taxonomy/labeling convention — not a real hiring change — that
makes J1 label-based entry share appear to shrink while YOE-based J3 rises. The paper must
treat this as a measurement-regime shift: "employers label 'entry' differently on 2026
LinkedIn than on 2024 Kaggle snapshots."

**Motivating evidence.**
- T06: Google J3=52% / J1=0.9%; Walmart J3=74% / J1=0%; Microsoft J3=39%. 48% of entry-specialist postings come from 11 tech giants with essentially zero `seniority_native='entry'` labels.
- T05: bare "developer" lost 61pp native-entry share arshkon vs scraped.
- Gate 1 T30: arshkon-only J1/J2 DOWN −0.6pp; J3/J4 UP +2.3pp/+3.4pp. Split direction = measurement-regime difference.
- T16 Cluster 4 "consolidating giants" has d_J2 +4pp (label entry rising within cluster) but the corpus J1/J2 under arshkon-only goes DOWN — compatible only if the 2026 entry-labeled cohort in cluster-4 cos is a smaller fraction of their overall entry-hire intake (T06's intern pipelines).

**Testable in analysis phase.**
- Within the 240-co panel, for each cos stratify postings by seniority_native (NULL / entry / associate / ...) × yoe_extracted bucket (0-1 / 2 / 3-4 / 5+). Compute 2024→2026 shift in the joint distribution.
- Expected: 2024 postings have stronger alignment (entry label → yoe≤2); 2026 postings have weaker alignment (many yoe≤2 postings labelled unknown or mid-senior).
- Cross-check with Indeed labels (T05): does Indeed still align labels tightly with YOE in 2026? If yes, it's a LinkedIn-specific drift.

**Novelty / publishability.** **Medium.** Methodological contribution about cross-period longitudinal JD data. Important caveat for the paper; worth a dedicated methods section.

**Priority: 5.**

### **H_F — Sunbelt AI surge is late-adopter catch-up; tech-hub AI surge is baseline-approach**

**Statement.** The top AI-surge metros (Tampa Bay +20pp, Atlanta +18.6pp, Charlotte +17.2pp)
are late-adopter financial-services / healthcare / aerospace clusters whose 2024 baseline
was ~0-1% and whose 2026 level is 17-21%. The top "baseline" metros (SF Bay, Seattle) had
2024 baselines of 1.3-2.7% and 2026 levels of 16.2-17.7% — essentially the same 2026 level
but different 2024 starts. Both groups are converging on ~17% ai_strict — the Sunbelt
closed the gap from 0, tech hubs closed the last mile from elevated-but-not-saturated.

**Motivating evidence.**
- T17: 26/26 metros positive ai_strict Δ; mean +12.8pp, CV 0.29. Tech-hub Δ +13.1pp; non-hub Δ +12.7pp — essentially equal.
- Top AI-surge: Tampa Bay (2024 1.4% → 2026 21.4%), Atlanta (0.5% → 19.1%), Charlotte (0.0% → 17.2%).
- Top baseline 2026: SF Bay 16.2%, Seattle 16.4%, NYC 16.7%. All three sit at the national-mean level.

**Testable in analysis phase.**
- Per-metro, compute 2026 ai_strict level (not Δ). Plot against 2024 baseline. If convergence hypothesis holds, the 2026 level should be nearly constant across metros (~17%) while 2024 varies.
- Within Sunbelt metros, stratify by industry (financial, healthcare, defense) — is the AI surge concentrated in financial-services cos?
- Within tech hubs, stratify by archetype — is ML/AI archetype share carrying the residual baseline-approach? (T17 reports SF Bay 32%, Seattle 29% — consistent with ML-archetype saturation explaining the national-mean 2026 level.)

**Novelty / publishability.** **Medium.** Geographic convergence interpretation for a technology-diffusion phenomenon. Interesting for economic geography; less central to the lead paper.

**Priority: 8.**

### **H_G — Staff-title doubling reflects senior-tier internal redistribution, not growth**

**Statement.** The staff-title share doubling (2.6% → 6.3%, T10 + T21) alongside a flat
overall senior share (S1 43.3% → 43.3% in arshkon-only-vs-scraped) reflects the senior
tier redistributing internally: mid-senior postings are being renamed "staff" at a higher
rate. This is a title-inflation signal within the senior tier that does not correspond to
real expansion of the senior workforce.

**Motivating evidence.**
- T10 title concentration stable: 554 → 507 → 533 unique titles per 1K.
- T30 S1 share: arshkon 44.3% / pooled 59.6% / scraped 43.3% (near-flat under arshkon-only).
- T21 staff-title 2026 profile: mgmt-binary 0.48 (+16pp over non-staff), mentor 0.47 (+16pp), AI 0.18 (+5pp). Staff-title concentrates the senior-archetype shift.
- T10 disappearing titles: `java architect`, `drupal developer`, `devops architect`, `senior php developer`, `sr. .net developer` — legacy-stack consolidation.

**Testable in analysis phase.**
- Within the 240-co overlap panel, for each company: count of mid-senior postings in 2024 vs staff-labelled postings in 2026. If hypothesis holds, the per-company mid-senior count should be stable while staff count rose.
- Compare the content (T21 density features) of "senior X" postings in 2024 vs "staff X" postings at the same company in 2026. If the staff profile just takes the 2024 senior content + more orch/AI, it's title inflation.

**Novelty / publishability.** **Medium.** Connects to broader "job title inflation" literature (Eisfeldt-Falato-Xiaolan 2023); useful color but not a paper-lead.

### **H_H — 2026 LinkedIn SWE corpus reflects a different firm population than 2024**

**Statement.** The 81% new-entrant share of AI/ML archetype growth (T28) means the 2026
LinkedIn SWE corpus is sampling a substantively different employer population than 2024 —
more startups, more AI-native firms, more tech-giant coverage, more aggregators. The paper's
longitudinal claims are partly a **sampling-frame artifact**: the within-company
decomposition (T16 102% on AI) corrects for this, but any corpus-level metric has an
implicit "which companies are posting" confound.

**Motivating evidence.**
- T28: 935 AI/ML cos only-in-2026 vs 83 both-period; new-entrant volume 1,634 / 2,014 = 81%.
- T06: 74.5% of scraped cos are new entrants; aggregator share 9.2% → 16.6%.
- T17: Seattle ML-archetype share 4.3% → 29.1% (+25pp); SF Bay 11.6% → 31.8% (+20pp) — compositional shifts are large.
- T16 within-vs-between: AI-strict 102% within (same cos changing); J3 only 4% within (new cos entering).

**Testable in analysis phase.**
- Restrict 2026 analysis to the 2,024 returning companies (co in both periods). Recompute every headline.
- If the restricted-panel Δ matches the full-corpus Δ within 20%, the sampling-frame artifact is minor. If it differs by >50%, the longitudinal claim needs a sampling caveat.
- Expected (from T16): AI-mention rise survives restriction (102% within); J3-based junior rise attenuates (96% between); AI/ML archetype share rise may halve under restriction.

**Novelty / publishability.** **High.** Methodological contribution about longitudinal posting data. Important for the paper to address explicitly; a clean sample-frame sensitivity table makes the paper bulletproof to reviewer objections.

**Priority: 6.**

### **H_I — AI rewriting is a "coordination signal," not a "skill demand"**

**Statement.** The SWE-specific AI-vocabulary rewriting (T18 DiD 99% SWE-specific) is not
primarily communicating "we need workers with AI skills" (since T23 shows employers
under-specify vs worker usage); it is communicating "we are an AI-using firm" — a
coordination signal to other stakeholders (investors, candidates considering offers,
competitors). This predicts that AI-mention rates correlate with firm-level AI-adoption
signals (funding rounds tagged "AI," public AI strategy announcements) more than with
actual AI-use-on-the-job indicators.

**Motivating evidence.**
- T23 RQ3 inversion: employer ai_broad 46.8% < SO 2024 worker usage 62-76%. If postings were communicating skill demand, they would track usage.
- T22 AI-ghostiness: AI-sentences are modestly more aspirational (matched-share +0.24) but posting-level ghost rate is NOT elevated — postings are realistic as documents but individual AI requirements are hedged.
- T17 correlation: Δ_ai_strict × Δ_entry_j2 null (r=−0.11). If AI-mention were tracking actual hiring-demand shifts, we'd expect non-zero correlation.
- T16 "Consolidating giants" (Google, Microsoft, AWS): d_ai_strict +9.4pp — below panel mean. The firms most likely to use AI tools internally post it LEAST. Consulting firms with AI practice lines (cluster 1 "AI-forward tech-stacking" +41pp) post it most.

**Testable in analysis phase.**
- Cross-reference the 240-co overlap panel with public AI-adoption signals: Does AI-mention rate correlate with (a) count of AI patents filed 2023-2025, (b) count of "AI strategy" announcements in earnings calls, (c) press-release AI mentions? If r is high, the coordination-signaling hypothesis is supported.
- Compare intra-company posting consistency: do companies that mention AI in some postings mention it in ALL postings (template-wide signal) or only in AI-flavored roles (demand-specific)? If template-wide, coordination-signaling wins.

**Novelty / publishability.** **High.** Bridges labor-economics (hiring signals) with information-economics (firm-level coordination games). Publishable in a management/info-systems venue. The paper could add a sentence in the discussion noting this as a mechanism hypothesis.

**Priority: 7.**

### **H_J — Recruiter-LLM adoption is senior-biased because HR tooling defaults to mid-senior templates**

**Statement.** The observation that seniors changed more than juniors (T12: senior cosine
shift 0.942 vs entry 0.953) combined with T29's finding that mentor-rate Δ is the most
LLM-sensitive of the headline findings (attenuates most under the global cut, 14%
retention) suggests: recruiter-LLM tooling preferentially drafts mid-senior JDs because
that's the default template in HR software (ATS systems default to mid-senior roles for
re-use). Junior postings are more often direct recruiter text (intern pipelines, new-grad
programs) while senior postings are LLM-augmented templates.

**Motivating evidence.**
- T12: senior 2026-vs-2024 cosine 0.942 < entry 0.953 (seniors changed more).
- T29: mentor-rate Δ retention under global cut = 14% (most attenuated); AI-strict 71% (robust); senior-only mentor attenuation matches the "seniors more LLM-drafted" prediction.
- T29 top-LLM companies: Harvey (AI law startup, ~60-70% senior), Alignerr (ML startup, 80% senior), Anthropic (tech giant, 85% senior by T30 headline). These are companies with mostly senior postings.
- T29 bottom-LLM companies: India-based staffing firms (Bright Sol, PRIMUS, Allagi). Staffing firms have more generic (often yoe-leaning-low) postings with less LLM augmentation.

**Testable in analysis phase.**

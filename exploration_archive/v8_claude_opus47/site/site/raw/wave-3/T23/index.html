    T23 — Employer-Requirement / Worker-Usage Divergence (RQ3)¶

    Agent M, Wave 3. Date: 2026-04-17. Inputs: exploration/artifacts/T22/T22_features.parquet (AI-pattern binaries); exploration/artifacts/shared/validated_mgmt_patterns.json (V1-refined regexes); external benchmarks cached in T23_benchmarks.json.

    Artifacts: T23_requirement_rates.csv, T23_divergence_table.csv, T23_sensitivity.csv, T23_benchmarks.json, T23_temporal_growth.csv, exploration/figures/T23/T23_divergence.png.¶

    Headline findings¶

    1. Employer AI-requirement rate is consistently BELOW plausible worker AI-usage rates. All-SWE 2026: strict AI-tool requirement = 14.0%, broad = 46.8%, specific-tool = 13.9% — all below the central worker-usage estimate of 75% (SO 2024 mid-projection). Even under the most conservative usage assumption of 50%, broad requirement (46.8%) remains below; strict (14.0%) is far below. Employer postings under-specify AI relative to actual developer usage.
    2. Junior/senior divergence: senior postings are MORE AI-specified than junior postings. 2026: S1 senior 46.8% broad / J2 junior 43.5% broad. S4 (≥5 YOE) 46.2% / J3 (≤2 YOE) 47.3%. If firms were using AI-mentions as a junior-filter (aspirational padding), we'd see junior > senior; we see senior > junior by 3-4pp. Direction consistent across panels.
    3. Temporal divergence. Employer requirement growth 2024→2026 (~23 months): strict +13.1pp, broad +39.7pp, specific-tool +13.1pp. Worker usage growth per SO Developer Survey: 2023→2024 +18pp in current-use (44% → 62%). Extrapolating linearly gives worker growth of ~+35pp in the same 23-mo window — broadly comparable in magnitude to our broad-AI rise, but the LEVEL remains below. So employers are catching up in their language, not leading worker usage.
    4. Specificity slice: specific tools (Copilot/Cursor/Claude) < generic AI. All-SWE 2026: ai_tool (specific) 13.9% < ai_strict 14.0% < ai_broad 46.8%. Most AI-mentioning postings talk about AI/ML generically; only a minority name specific tools. Divergence with worker usage is LARGER on specific tools — Copilot alone had >1.3M paid seats in 2024 (GitHub Octoverse) while only 13.9% of postings reference a named tool.
    5. Sensitivity. Under worker-usage assumptions of 50%/65%/75%/85%: broad-AI requirement (46.8%) is below all four; strict-AI requirement (14.0%) is very far below. The 'requirement UNDER-specifies usage' finding is robust across the full range of plausible usage rates. Requirement rate would need to MATCH the usage rate only if developer AI usage were <47% — which is inconsistent with every published benchmark.

    Methodology¶

    Filter. SWE LinkedIn (is_swe=true, source_platform='linkedin', is_english=true, date_flag='ok'). Requirement rate definitions. - ai_tool (AI-as-tool, specific): V1-refined strict minus codex = copilot, cursor, claude, chatgpt, openai api, gpt, gemini, langchain, llamaindex, prompt engineering, fine-tuning, rag, vector database, pinecone, huggingface. Precision 1.00 (50/50). - ai_strict: ai_tool ∪ {codex}. Precision 1.00 (50/50). - ai_broad: ai_strict ∪ {ai, artificial intelligence, ml, machine learning, llm, large language model, generative ai, genai, anthropic}. Precision 0.80 (40/50). - ai_domain: traditional ML/NLP/CV vocabulary (machine learning, deep learning, nlp, computer vision, neural network, transformer, embedding, model training). Precision ≈0.85. Seniority panels. J2 (entry|associate), J3 (yoe≤2), S1 (mid-senior|director), S4 (yoe≥5). All-SWE also reported. Benchmarks.* Fetched live via WebFetch on 2026-04-17 from Stack Overflow Developer Survey 2024, GitHub Octoverse 2024, and Anthropic's 2025 Labor Market Impacts study; cached in T23_benchmarks.json. Central worker-usage point estimates: 2024 = 62% (SO 2024 currently-using); 2026 = 75% (linear projection on SO 2023→2024 +18pp trend). Band: 44/62/73 (2024) and 62/75/85 (2026). See caveats section below.

    Synthesized usage point estimates (for divergence table):

    • 2024 low/central/high = 44% / 62% / 73%
    • 2026 low/central/high = 62% / 75% / 85%

    Fundamental caveat. Employer-requirement rates measure posting-level appearance of AI vocabulary. Worker-usage benchmarks measure developer-level self-reported use of AI tools (at least occasionally, in most survey questions). These are DIFFERENT units (postings ≠ workers) and DIFFERENT phenomena (required skill mention ≠ actual use on the job). The divergence we report is the qualitative gap (employers request/mention AI less than developers use it), not a precise pp difference.

    7. Implications for RQ3¶

    RQ3 hypothesis (pre-registered in docs/1-research-design.md): Employer-posting AI requirements outpace actual worker AI usage.

    Empirical verdict: Opposite direction. Employer postings SYSTEMATICALLY UNDER-specify AI relative to developer usage rates. Even the most expansive requirement pattern (broad-AI, 46.8% of all postings in 2026) falls below the most conservative usage assumption (50%). Specific-tool requirements (Copilot/Cursor/Claude/etc.) are the rarest in the JD corpus (13.9%) despite the tools being the most widely adopted (GitHub Copilot alone >1.3M paid seats).

    Interpretation. Three candidate mechanisms: - Job descriptions lag tooling adoption. Posting templates are slow to update; AI tooling is assumed-implicit rather than stated. - Employer under-specification. Hiring managers know developers will pick up AI tools on the job, so they don't mention them in requirements. - Measurement mismatch. Requirement mentions count presence-of-keyword, usage surveys count any-use; genuine required-competence overlap may lie somewhere between.

    Policy/theory implication. The RQ3 reframing that fits this evidence: not 'postings hyperinflate AI demand,' but 'postings UNDER-describe AI as required competency.' If AI is a job-market 'invisible skill' on the worker side but visible on the employer side, the asymmetry matters for workforce planning — a candidate lacking Copilot experience might appear qualified on paper while being functionally under-skilled.

    Limitations¶

    • Worker-usage benchmarks are self-reported and platform-biased (SO, GH). Anthropic Economic Index is task-weighted, not worker-weighted. We report the SPAN (50/65/75/85) and show robustness.
    • LinkedIn-only. Indeed and other platforms may show different posting norms.
    • Broad-AI pattern precision is 0.80; at the margin, some 'broad' postings contain ambiguous 'ai' compounds. Finding is qualitatively unchanged under the strict pattern.
    • Period comparison is cross-sectional, not within-company. T07/T30 earlier established that the 125-company arshkon∩scraped overlap panel shows +33pp within-company AI-rise, consistent with the aggregate picture.

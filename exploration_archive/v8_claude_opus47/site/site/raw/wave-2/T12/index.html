    T12 — Open-ended text evolution¶

    Agent: H (Wave 2) · Date: 2026-04-17 · Input: exploration/artifacts/shared/swe_cleaned_text.parquet filtered to text_source='llm' (99% arshkon, 94% asaniczka, 31% scraped) · Method: Log-odds ratio with informative Dirichlet prior (Monroe et al. 2008)

    Headline¶

    2024→2026 text change is a period effect, not a relabeling. 2026 entry postings are closer to 2024 entry postings than to 2024 mid-senior postings (cosine 0.953 vs 0.938). The shift is a uniform temporal drift applied to all seniority levels — not senior-content migrating downward.

    Genuine content change concentrated on AI tooling. Top emerging terms are AI tools (rag, copilot, claude, cursor, langgraph, anthropic, gemini, ai-assisted) and AI domain vocabulary (multimodal, retrieval-augmented, embeddings, llm-based). Disappearing terms are legacy enterprise vocabulary (duties, j2ee, t-sql, angularjs, jquery, visio, redhat, sysml, tomcat, itil).

    Section-filtered re-run confirms the genuine-vs-boilerplate split. Among the top-100 2026-characteristic terms from full-text, 60 also appear in the section-filtered top-100 (genuine content), 40 do not (boilerplate-driven — appearing outside requirements/responsibilities). Importantly, several AI tool names (copilot, langchain, openai, cursor) drop out of the section-filtered top-100 — they appear in role_summary/benefits/about_company more than requirements. AI domain language (rag, llm, llms, genai, agent, agentic, agents, ml, prompt) stays in both lists — it's genuinely being written into requirements sections.

    lieu degree in 2026-heavy list deserves note — this reflects "Bachelor's degree or equivalent experience in lieu of a degree" becoming more common, a loosening of credentials. Combined with the visible drop in bachelor degree (still present but less dominant), this points to modest credential loosening.

    7. Secondary comparisons¶

    Entry 2024 vs entry 2026 (J2 pooled)¶

    Entry_2024-heavy: junior, application, test, customer, system, developer, sql, engineer, business, specifications, java, position, written, possess, spring, knowledge, necessary, existing, web, software.

    Entry_2026-heavy: unsupervised, containerization, maintainable, mission-critical, terraform, retrospectives, redis, cursor, typescript, well-documented, codebases, infrastructure-as-code, flows, regression, gcp, performance, stand-ups.

    Z-scores in this comparison are much smaller (±2–7) because the cells are smaller (344 vs 452). The direction is nonetheless clear: 2026 entry postings use modern language (terraform, gcp, typescript, redis, retrospectives, stand-ups, mission-critical, containerization) and add AI-adjacent tooling (cursor), while 2024 entry postings use classical enterprise language (application, java, spring, specifications, possess).

    Mid-senior 2024 vs 2026¶

    Mid-senior_2026-heavy: next.js, ambiguity, excellence, low-latency, langchain, regulated, thrives, python, millions, modernization, retrieval, mlops, iteration, mission-critical, ai, llm, models. Classic senior evolution: AI infra (langchain, mlops, retrieval, ai, llm) plus system-scale vocab (low-latency, millions, modernization).

    Entry 2026 vs mid-senior 2024 — relabeling diagnostic¶

    Terms characteristic of 2026 entry (vs 2024 mid-senior): scikit-learn, dashboards, cursor, co-op, receiving, authorization, well-documented, classification, chatbot, hands-on, field, resume, research, prompts, github, mcp, recommendation, participate.

    Terms characteristic of 2024 mid-senior (vs 2026 entry): lead, system, senior, design, expertise, test, project, application, specifications, architect, mentor, engineer, principal, staff, systems, technology, documents, security, developer, proven.

    Reading: The 2024 senior-marker vocabulary (lead, senior, architect, mentor, principal, staff, expertise) is NOT present in 2026 entry postings at elevated rates. Instead, 2026 entry postings add NEW vocabulary (cursor, mcp, chatbot, prompts, hands-on, scikit-learn, co-op). Conclusion: period-effect dominates; relabeling does not.

    Cosine similarity of term-share vectors¶

    pair cosine

    Entry_26 is closer to entry_24 (0.953) than to midsr_24 (0.938). If relabeling were dominant, the inequality would flip. Entry_26 is even closer to midsr_26 (0.958), consistent with a period-synchronized shift pulling entry and senior postings in the same direction.

    Within-2024 calibration (arshkon midsr vs asaniczka midsr)¶

    Top distinguishing terms within-2024: azure (z=21), cloud (z=21), ai (z=16), ml (z=14), backend (z=13), data (z=13), cd (z=13), aws (z=13), ci (z=13), platform (z=12), best, react, application, hybrid, java. The arshkon vs asaniczka instrument delta is non-trivial and concentrates on a similar topic-space (cloud/devops) to the cross-period delta — which is why per-term comparisons need SNR rather than raw deltas. Individual tool names like copilot/cursor/langgraph have minuscule within-2024 noise (all below 0.01%) so the 2026 shift in those terms is clearly above noise. Broader terms like ai have within-2024 z=16 and cross-period effects that need to beat that threshold to be credible.

    8. Relabeling framing (interpretation)¶

    The cosine and Fightin' Words results both say: the 2024→2026 shift represents a temporal drift uniformly applied to entry- and senior-level postings, not a relabeling of senior requirements into entry postings. Practical framing:

    • If 2026 entry = relabeled 2024 senior: we would expect 2026 entry to acquire lead, mentor, architect, principal, staff, expertise — it did not.
    • If 2026 entry = period-shifted 2024 entry: we would expect 2026 entry to acquire the same NEW terms 2026 senior acquires (ai, rag, llm, tools) — it did.

    The implication for RQ1 "scope inflation": the "inflation" is NOT adding senior-level leadership duties to entry postings. It IS adding modern (AI-related) requirements across all seniority levels, with entry postings affected equally. What changes for entry specifically (vs senior) in 2026: more hands-on, co-op, chatbot, cursor, scikit-learn, prompts — entry-specific AI-literacy tooling.

    9. Surprises¶

    1. copilot, langchain, openai drop out of section-filtered top-100 — these specific tool names live more in role_summary/about-company than in actual requirements. This is a clean result of the T13 section filter.
    2. No terms meeting the strict "disappearing" bar (>1% 2024 → <0.1% 2026). Language evolution is softer than we might expect. 18 terms meet a looser bar (>1% → <0.5%).
    3. hands-on is the single most emerging-English phrase — the hyphenated form alone grew by 25× and appears in 193 companies. "Hands-on" has become a near-universal 2026 descriptor.
    4. f-1 and j-1 accelerate 100× — visa status references ("sponsorship available for F-1/J-1/OPT") are now routinely added to postings. This is HR-template expansion driven by the same post-ZIRP hiring-market conditions that drove the benefits-section doubling.
    5. lieu degree as a top bigram for 2026 — "Bachelor's degree or equivalent experience in lieu of a degree" is now a common 2026 clause. Credential loosening is real and measurable.
    6. The 2024→2026 shift is symmetric across seniority levels. I expected entry to shift more than senior. It doesn't — midsr24 vs midsr26 cosine (0.942) is actually lower than entry24 vs entry26 (0.953). Seniors changed more than juniors in absolute vocabulary terms.

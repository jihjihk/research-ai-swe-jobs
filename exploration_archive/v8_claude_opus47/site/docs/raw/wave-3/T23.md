## Headline findings
1. **Employer AI-requirement rate is consistently BELOW plausible worker AI-usage rates.** All-SWE 2026: strict AI-tool requirement = 14.0%, broad = 46.8%, specific-tool = 13.9% â€” all below the central worker-usage estimate of 75% (SO 2024 mid-projection). Even under the most conservative usage assumption of 50%, broad requirement (46.8%) remains below; strict (14.0%) is far below. Employer postings under-specify AI relative to actual developer usage.
2. **Junior/senior divergence: senior postings are MORE AI-specified than junior postings.** 2026: S1 senior 46.8% broad / J2 junior 43.5% broad. S4 (â‰¥5 YOE) 46.2% / J3 (â‰¤2 YOE) 47.3%. If firms were using AI-mentions as a junior-filter (aspirational padding), we'd see junior > senior; we see senior > junior by 3-4pp. Direction consistent across panels.
3. **Temporal divergence.** Employer requirement growth 2024â†’2026 (~23 months): strict +13.1pp, broad +39.7pp, specific-tool +13.1pp. Worker usage growth per SO Developer Survey: 2023â†’2024 +18pp in current-use (44% â†’ 62%). Extrapolating linearly gives worker growth of ~+35pp in the same 23-mo window â€” broadly comparable in magnitude to our broad-AI rise, but the LEVEL remains below. So employers are catching up in their language, not leading worker usage.
4. **Specificity slice: specific tools (Copilot/Cursor/Claude) < generic AI.** All-SWE 2026: ai_tool (specific) 13.9% < ai_strict 14.0% < ai_broad 46.8%. Most AI-mentioning postings talk about AI/ML generically; only a minority name specific tools. Divergence with worker usage is LARGER on specific tools â€” Copilot alone had >1.3M paid seats in 2024 (GitHub Octoverse) while only 13.9% of postings reference a named tool.
5. **Sensitivity.** Under worker-usage assumptions of 50%/65%/75%/85%: broad-AI requirement (46.8%) is below all four; strict-AI requirement (14.0%) is very far below. The 'requirement UNDER-specifies usage' finding is robust across the full range of plausible usage rates. Requirement rate would need to MATCH the usage rate only if developer AI usage were <47% â€” which is inconsistent with every published benchmark.

## 7. Implications for RQ3

**RQ3 hypothesis (pre-registered in `docs/1-research-design.md`):** Employer-posting AI requirements outpace actual worker AI usage.

**Empirical verdict:** **Opposite direction.** Employer postings SYSTEMATICALLY UNDER-specify AI relative to developer usage rates. Even the most expansive requirement pattern (broad-AI, 46.8% of all postings in 2026) falls below the most conservative usage assumption (50%). Specific-tool requirements (Copilot/Cursor/Claude/etc.) are the rarest in the JD corpus (13.9%) despite the tools being the most widely adopted (GitHub Copilot alone >1.3M paid seats).

**Interpretation.** Three candidate mechanisms:
- **Job descriptions lag tooling adoption.** Posting templates are slow to update; AI tooling is assumed-implicit rather than stated.
- **Employer under-specification.** Hiring managers know developers will pick up AI tools on the job, so they don't mention them in requirements.
- **Measurement mismatch.** Requirement mentions count presence-of-keyword, usage surveys count any-use; genuine required-competence overlap may lie somewhere between.

**Policy/theory implication.** The RQ3 reframing that fits this evidence: not 'postings hyperinflate AI demand,' but 'postings UNDER-describe AI as required competency.' If AI is a job-market 'invisible skill' on the worker side but visible on the employer side, the asymmetry matters for workforce planning â€” a candidate lacking Copilot experience might appear qualified on paper while being functionally under-skilled.

## Limitations

- Worker-usage benchmarks are self-reported and platform-biased (SO, GH). Anthropic Economic Index is task-weighted, not worker-weighted. We report the SPAN (50/65/75/85) and show robustness.
- LinkedIn-only. Indeed and other platforms may show different posting norms.
- Broad-AI pattern precision is 0.80; at the margin, some 'broad' postings contain ambiguous 'ai' compounds. Finding is qualitatively unchanged under the strict pattern.
- Period comparison is cross-sectional, not within-company. T07/T30 earlier established that the 125-company arshkonâˆ©scraped overlap panel shows +33pp within-company AI-rise, consistent with the aggregate picture.

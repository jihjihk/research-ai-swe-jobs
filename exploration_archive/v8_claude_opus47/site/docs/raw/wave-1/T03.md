# T03 — Seniority label audit

**Agent:** B (Wave 1) · **Date:** 2026-04-17 · **Input:** `data/unified.parquet`, SWE rows only (LinkedIn, English, `date_flag='ok'`).

## Headline

`seniority_final` is **defensible as the production seniority column** for labeled cells but is **materially under-powered** on the entry and director tails. The YOE ordering is correct across sources (entry ≈ 2 YOE, mid-senior ≈ 6, director ≈ 7–9), the rule+LLM composition is internally coherent, and only ~5% of LLM-labeled rows look like potential routing leakage. The column's weakness is coverage on the tails, not correctness. T30 should take `seniority_final` as given and construct its entry-side recovery with ablations rather than try to repair the column itself.

## Methodology

Base filter: `source_platform = 'linkedin' AND is_english = true AND date_flag = 'ok' AND is_swe = true`. All figures cite the exact subset, the column definition, and the denominator. Kappa is computed on the 4-level alignment `{entry, associate, mid-senior, director}`, dropping `intern` and `executive` from native and excluding `unknown` from both columns (since including it as a category would overweight coverage gaps as disagreements — see caveat 2 below).

SWE counts after the default filter: arshkon 4,691 · asaniczka 18,129 · scraped 40,881. Periods present: `2024-01` (asaniczka), `2024-04` (arshkon), `2026-03`, `2026-04` (scraped).

## Step 2. Rule-vs-LLM internal agreement

### Weak-marker titles (II / III / IV)

Universe: 1,402 SWE rows with `seniority_final_source = 'llm'` whose title matches `(?i)\b(II|III|IV)\b`. Sample of 100 inspected.

Roman markers are **not** explicit seniority signals per the LLM prompt design (which looks for title keywords like "senior"/"junior" only). 94/100 sampled rows came back `seniority_final = 'unknown'`, 6/100 agreed with a broad Roman-to-seniority mapping. **This matches the LLM's documented design** — do not count as error. But it means 1,402 rows of routed corpus yield almost no usable label.

### Strong-keyword titles routed to LLM (possible leakage)

Universe: 797 SWE rows with `seniority_final_source = 'llm'` whose title matches `\b(senior|staff|principal|lead|junior)\b` — these should normally be handled by Stage 5's `title_keyword` rule. Sample of 100 inspected:

- 95/100 agreed with the strong keyword (e.g., title "Lead Golang Engineer" → LLM returns `mid-senior`).
- 5/100 disagreed or returned `unknown`.

Inspection shows these routed rows are predominantly compound `lead` titles (e.g., "DevOps Testing Lead", "Software Engineer, Lead" — 91/100 of the sample contain `lead`). Stage 5's `title_keyword` regex appears to have gaps on position-embedded `lead` (not all "X Lead" positional variants), so these rows correctly fall through to the LLM. Qualitative routing-error rate ≤ 5% — the LLM is not systematically contradicting the rules, it is filling gaps left by Stage 5's conservative keyword set.

Asaniczka's κ is near-zero because native has only `mid-senior`/`associate` classes — almost all rows sit in a single cell, so κ's chance correction dominates. The informative sources are arshkon and scraped.

scraped SWE:

| Native | n | Diag | Per-class accuracy |
|---|---:|---:|---:|

| mid-senior | 14,099 | 13,845 | 98.2% |
| director | 239 | 43 | 18.0% |

**What the κ gap (0.40 → 0.49) says.** Scraped kappa is higher primarily because scraped entry-class accuracy is higher (67.7% vs 44.2%). This is **not** evidence of native-classifier temporal instability per se — it is consistent with a simpler explanation: `seniority_final` is more likely to fire `entry` for rows where the title uses strong junior markers (Stage 5), and the 2026 scraped corpus contains more junior-marker titles than 2024 arshkon. Cross-checking on the raw marker rate is a T30 job; the κ per se does not let us disentangle "native means a different thing in 2024" from "final recovers entry from titles more often in 2026." **Do not over-interpret.**

**Associate is the failure class.** 4.9% (arshkon) and 1.8% (scraped) — `seniority_final` systematically pushes native-associate rows into `mid-senior` or `unknown`. This is expected: Stage 5 rules do not have a dedicated "associate" keyword, and the LLM prompt treats associate as a rare explicit signal. Any analysis stratifying on `associate` from `seniority_final` is not meaningful — use the J2 (entry+associate combined) operationalization if you want the junior bucket to include native associates.

**Director undercoverage** at 18% (scraped) and 33% (arshkon) is meaningful: the LLM prompt labels `director` conservatively and Stage 5 fires `director` only on explicit VP/Director keywords. 79% of scraped director-native rows become `mid-senior` in final. For RQ1 senior-side questions, this pushes us toward S1 (`mid-senior`+`director` combined) or S3 (title-keyword senior) — see T30.

## Defensibility recommendation

**`seniority_final` is defensible.** Four points of evidence:

1. **YOE ordering is correct across sources.** Median YOE climbs monotonically entry → mid-senior → director in every source × period cell. Disagreement with YOE on entry would be the disqualifying failure mode and it does not appear.
2. **Strong-keyword agreement is high.** LLM decisions on rule-handled keywords agree with the rules 95% of the time where we can check.
3. **Routing leakage is bounded.** Fewer than 5% of LLM-labeled SWE rows look like possible rule-routing misses, and those are conservative false-negatives of Stage 5 (mostly compound "lead" titles), not LLM errors.
4. **Composition is transparent.** `seniority_final_source` lets any downstream user subset to rule-only or LLM-only. There is no hidden derivation.

**Known failure modes that downstream tasks must respect:**

- **Associate is not meaningful.** Use entry+associate combined (T30/J2) when you want the full junior bucket.
- **Director is undercoverage, not mis-labeled.** Use `mid-senior`+`director` (T30/S1) for the senior bucket whenever power matters.
- **`seniority_final = 'unknown'` is ~40% of scraped SWE and is heterogeneous.** Do NOT use "known-seniority share" as the only denominator for cross-period comparison; always report the "of-all-SWE" share as a robustness check because differential routing (62% LLM in arshkon vs 17% in scraped) makes the known-seniority subset compositionally different across periods.

**T03 does not block T30.** Move forward on `seniority_final` with the documented caveats.

## Data-quality caveats

1. Asaniczka native lacks `entry`/`director` by construction — asaniczka's native-vs-final comparison is informative only for mid-senior.
2. Cohen's κ here excludes `unknown` rows from both sides (since `unknown` on either axis is a coverage state, not a class disagreement). Including `unknown` in the confusion matrix would inflate disagreement artifactually. The excluded-from-κ counts are large (~40% of scraped SWE) — the κ value reflects quality on *labeled* cells only.
3. Kappa's interpretation depends on the marginals. Scraped kappa (0.49) is **not directly comparable** to arshkon kappa (0.40) because the class distributions differ; the per-class accuracy table (entry 44% vs 68%, associate 5% vs 2%, etc.) is the more informative surface.
4. `seniority_final_source = 'llm'` rows with `seniority_final = 'unknown'` are documented correct behavior — LLM was called and returned no explicit signal. Do not count these as errors.

## Surprises & unexpected patterns

1. **Arshkon's native `entry` label is noisier on YOE than scraped's.** Arshkon native-entry median YOE is 3, Q75 = 5 — meaning a quarter of arshkon "entry" postings demand ≥5 YOE (label-YOE contradiction). Scraped native-entry median is 2, Q75 = 2. This suggests **the baseline 2024 entry rate measured via `seniority_native` is partially a labeling artifact**, not a pure labor-market signal. RQ1 comparisons that use `seniority_native` for the 2024 baseline should report this as a caveat.
2. **Scraped `unknown` rate (39.5%) is dominated by unrouted rows**, not by LLM returning unknown. ~72% of scraped-unknown rows have `seniority_final_source = 'unknown'` (never routed). If the LLM budget were raised for scraped, a large fraction of that 39.5% would move into labeled cells.
3. **Arshkon is more heavily LLM-processed than scraped** (62% vs 17% of SWE). This is the opposite of what one might expect given how much larger scraped is. Any known-seniority analysis that does not report both denominators is at risk of a compositional artifact masquerading as a temporal signal.
4. **The `lead` keyword is Stage 5's biggest leak.** 750 SWE rows with "lead" in title reached the LLM because Stage 5's rule doesn't cover all positional variants (e.g., "DevOps Testing Lead" vs "Lead DevOps Engineer"). Not a correctness issue — LLM recovers 95% of them — but it explains why `seniority_final_source = 'llm'` is disproportionately populated with senior-side rows in scraped.
5. **Native-associate is a non-class in `seniority_final`.** 1.8% accuracy (scraped) means the column essentially refuses to put rows in the `associate` bucket. Any analysis that counts associate-specific rows is not meaningful on `seniority_final` — use T30/J2 (entry+associate combined) or avoid.

## Action items for downstream tasks

- **T30 must use J2 (entry+associate) as the primary junior operationalization for power**, with J1 (entry-only) held as a label-quality ablation. The entry-only cell has too few rows and too much between-source variance in how entry gets recovered.
- **T30 must use S1 (mid-senior+director) as the primary senior operationalization.** Director-only is label-undercovered.
- **Any task stratifying by `seniority_final`** must report both "of-all-SWE" and "of-known-seniority" denominators because the scraped `unknown` share is 40% and differential routing makes the known-only cross-period comparison potentially misleading.
- **Do not pool asaniczka into any `seniority_native`-based diagnostic** — asaniczka's native has no `entry`/`director` classes.
- Future preprocessing improvement: Stage 5's `lead`-keyword rule is the lowest-hanging fruit for raising rule-coverage (would move ~750 scraped SWE rows from LLM to rule). Not blocking.

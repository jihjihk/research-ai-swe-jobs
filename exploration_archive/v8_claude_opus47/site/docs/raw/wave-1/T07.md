Agent D, Wave 1. Date: 2026-04-17.
Input: `data/unified.parquet`. Default SWE LinkedIn filters applied throughout.

## Headline finding

**The population-level share comparisons (junior share, senior share, all-SWE content rates) are well-powered in every T30 panel variant and every key comparison.** MDE_binary is ≤ 3pp everywhere for share-of-SWE outcomes, comfortably below the 5pp well-powered threshold.

**Within-slice content analyses (within-junior, within-senior) are the binding feasibility constraint.** S2 (director-only) and S3 (title-keyword senior via `title_normalized`) are underpowered for every cross-period content metric. J1/J2 are marginal for binary content rates under arshkon-vs-scraped and become well-powered under pooled_2024; content continuous effects (description length, YOE) are well-powered under pooled_2024 for J1/J2/J3/J4 but marginal for J1/J2 in arshkon-only.

**Metro-level entry-share analysis is feasible under pooled-2024 baseline (23/26 scraped-frame metros well-powered) but not arshkon-only (8/26).** The orchestrator should push metro-level J1 analyses to pooled_2024.

**Geographic correlation vs BLS OEWS 2024 (SOC 15-1252) exceeds target across all sources: Pearson r = 0.96–0.98.** Our data passes as representative of the US SWE geographic distribution.

**JOLTS Information sector context: the scraped 2026 window captures a post-2020-low in tech hiring. Feb-2026 = 91K openings vs 2023 avg 138K (0.66×) and 2024 avg 123K (0.74×).** Any cross-period findings must be read against a genuinely softer 2026 hiring market, not a booming one.

## Methodology

### Part A — Feasibility table

**T30 seniority panel definitions** (computed locally; T30 CSV not yet published when this ran):

| Definition | Expression |
|---|---|
| J1 | `seniority_final = 'entry'` |
| J2 | `seniority_final IN ('entry','associate')` |
| J3 | `yoe_extracted <= 2` (denominator: YOE-known) |
| J4 | `yoe_extracted <= 3` (denominator: YOE-known) |
| S1 | `seniority_final IN ('mid-senior','director')` |
| S2 | `seniority_final = 'director'` |
| S3 | title-keyword senior, matched against `title_normalized` — **as-written in the spec** |
| S3_raw | title-keyword senior, matched against raw `lower(title)` — **corrected** (see surprises) |
| S4 | `yoe_extracted >= 5` |
| ALL | all SWE rows |

**MDE formula (normal approximation, two-sample, 80% power, α=0.05 two-sided):**

$$\text{MDE}_{\text{binary}} = (z_{\alpha/2} + z_\beta) \sqrt{p_0 (1-p_0) (1/n_1 + 1/n_2)}$$

$$\text{MDE}_{\text{Cohen's }d} = (z_{\alpha/2} + z_\beta) \sqrt{1/n_1 + 1/n_2}$$

**Verdict bins:** `well_powered` (MDE_binary < 5pp or d < 0.2), `marginal` (5–10pp or 0.2–0.4), `underpowered` (≥10pp or ≥0.4).

**Denominator choice:** For share-of-population outcomes (junior_share, senior_share), n_group is the total SWE in the source (or YOE-known total for J3/J4/S4). For within-slice content outcomes (ai_mention_rate within entry, description_length within mid-senior, etc.), n_group is the number of rows satisfying the definition.

### Part B — External benchmarks

**BLS OEWS (SOC 15-1252 Software Developers), 2024 annual, state level.** Obtained via BLS public API (series ID `OEU{S|N}{area}{industry}{occupation}{datatype}`, 25 chars). 50/51 states returned data (VT suppressed, likely due to BLS confidentiality rules for small cells). National total = 1,654,440; sum of 50 state values = 1,650,360 = 99.8% match, validating the series format.

**SOC 15-1256 (QA Analysts and Testers)** — BLS public API returned "Series does not exist" for both national and state codes under the format used above. The separate SOC 15-1256 occupation appears not to be published at state level via the API (or requires a different series-ID scheme). Reported as a limitation; SWE alone is sufficient for a geographic correlation target.

**JOLTS Information sector** — series `JTS510000000000000JOL`, 38 monthly observations from Jan-2023 through Feb-2026.

**HTTP/2 blocked direct fetches from `www.bls.gov` and `fred.stlouisfed.org`** (FRED returns `INTERNAL_ERROR`; BLS returns 403). The BLS public API endpoint (`api.bls.gov`) worked via POST over HTTP/1.1 with Mozilla user-agent.

### 10. Sample frame & generalizability

**What our sample represents:** US-based, English-language, LinkedIn-posted software-engineer job postings at two snapshots:
- 2024-Q1 (asaniczka 2024-01) and 2024-Q2 (arshkon 2024-04) historical Kaggle dumps, together containing ~22.8K SWE postings.
- 2026-Mar/Apr daily-scraped current window (~40.9K SWE rows at current pull; growing daily), restricted by design to a 26-metro frame.

**Validity for which claims:**

- **Geographic prevalence** of SWE employment across states: strongly representative (r = 0.96–0.98 vs BLS OEWS). Within our metros, the top 8 states match BLS almost exactly.
- **Cross-temporal share comparisons** (junior share, senior share of SWE postings, AI mention rate, description length): internally valid across the three sources, but note 2024 sources are LinkedIn-snapshot Kaggle files of unknown sampling selection, while 2026 scraped is a daily pull with explicit keyword queries. Cross-period differences are confounded with instrument change — this is what the within-2024 calibration (arshkon vs asaniczka) is designed to bound.
- **Absolute volumes** or hiring-rate claims: NOT valid. Our three sources sample the LinkedIn posting stream with different intensities. JOLTS Information-sector openings fell 26% from 2024 avg to 2026-Feb, so any raw-volume drop in our data is a mix of (a) a real macro hiring slowdown, (b) our scraped pipeline's coverage rate, and (c) instrument change.

**What our sample does NOT cover:**
- Non-LinkedIn job markets (Indeed available as sensitivity, other boards and direct-company sites absent).
- Non-English postings (filtered out by default filters).
- SWE work outside job postings (contract, referral, internal, consulting).
- Non-US postings (arshkon and asaniczka both US-focused; scraped is 26-metro US).
- Pre-2024 and post-2026-April markets.
- SWE-adjacent roles tracked under `is_swe_adjacent` — those are outside T07's SWE sample.
- QA Analyst & Tester occupation (BLS SOC 15-1256): BLS public API did not return data for this occupation at state level, so we cannot add it as a cross-check.

## Data-quality caveats

1. **`title_normalized` strips level indicators.** 99% of rows with "Senior/Sr/Lead/Staff/Principal" in the raw title have it removed in `title_normalized` (17,289 → 150 in scraped alone). The T30 spec's S3 definition matches against `title_normalized`, which means S3 as literally specified catches only rare residuals. Feasibility tables include both S3 (as-spec'd) and S3_raw (corrected, matches raw title lowercased) — T30 should finalize the operationalization and downstream tasks should consume the corrected version.
2. **`is_remote_inferred` is 100% False in all three sources.** The preamble notes remote flags are 0% in 2024 sources as a data artifact, but `is_remote_inferred` in Stage 6 should combine source flag + text inference. Either the Stage-6 text inference hasn't run or something stripped the output; regardless, remote-share comparisons in this dataset are not meaningful. Remote_share is included in the feasibility table only for formal completeness — the analysis is infeasible at the data level, independent of power.
3. **VT missing from BLS OEWS state data** — likely BLS confidentiality-based suppression for small cells. 50 of 51 states is sufficient for the correlation target.
4. **SOC 15-1256 (QA) not returned by BLS public API** at national or state level under the 25-char series format that works for 15-1252. Documented as a limitation.
5. **JOLTS data lag.** Most-recent JOLTS month is Feb-2026; April-2026 not yet published. The JOLTS context for the scraped window is based on Jan-Feb 2026.
6. **20 states have zero scraped SWE postings** (AL, AK, AR, HI, ID, IA, KY, LA, ME, MS, MT, NE, NV, NM, ND, OK, SD, UT, VT, WY). This is expected given the 26-metro scraped frame by design, not a defect. But it means the scraped Pearson r with BLS is driven by the top-10 states. log-Pearson r = 0.82 and Spearman r = 0.86 are more honest figures for the scraped rank-consistency.
7. **Multi-location share differs across sources** (2.5% arshkon, 4.9% asaniczka, 7.6% scraped). A mechanically larger share in scraped means a mechanically larger "drop" from per-metro rollups in 2026 — any "metro-level" analysis needs to report both the metro-only population and the multi-location excluded count.

## Surprises & unexpected patterns

1. **S3 (title-keyword senior via `title_normalized`) is catastrophically under-counted.** Raw-title regex finds 17,289 senior-keyword titles in scraped; `title_normalized` regex finds 150 (0.9%). The same severe loss appears in all three sources (arshkon 1,804 → 82; asaniczka 11,137 → 364). Cause: `title_normalized` strips level indicators per schema Section 2. This invalidates S3 as literally written in T30 — it effectively measures "how often a level keyword survived title-normalization," not "how many postings are titled senior." Agent B's T30 must operationalize title-keyword senior against raw `title` or document the mismatch. I included an S3_raw row in the feasibility table as the corrected operationalization.

2. **`is_remote_inferred` is 100% False across all sources.** Documented as a 2024-source artifact in the preamble, but scraped-2026 also shows 0%. Either Stage-6 text inference did not populate this column or something stripped the output. This is a preprocessing pipeline issue that should be surfaced. Any RQ depending on remote/hybrid classification is not supported by the current artifact.

3. **2026 is a genuinely softer hiring market, not an AI-adoption boom.** JOLTS Information-sector openings fell from 123K 2024-avg to 91K Feb-2026 (0.74×). This is the lowest Info-sector openings figure since the post-COVID trough in 2020. **Any headline finding like "junior postings declined X%" must contextualize as partly a macro hiring slowdown.** The correct framing is share-of-SWE (which is mostly insensitive to volume), not raw volume. This changes the narrative for RQ1.

4. **Industry top-rank swap between 2024 and 2026** on LinkedIn's own taxonomy: "IT Services and IT Consulting" (arshkon top, 25.8%) vs "Software Development" (scraped top, 26.0%). These categories overlap semantically and LinkedIn may have merged/relabeled them between 2024 and 2026. This is a cross-dataset comparability risk for any industry-stratified analysis (T05 should verify).

5. **S2 (director-only) and title-keyword content within-slice are infeasible across the board.** S2 has only 13 arshkon rows and 105 pooled_2024 rows — no binary content rate difference <20pp can be detected, and no Cohen's d <0.6. Any "director postings changed" claim must be deferred to raw counts or merged into S1 (mid-senior+director).

6. **Company overlap is smaller than typically assumed.** Only 45 companies have ≥10 SWE postings in BOTH arshkon and scraped; 240 have ≥3. T16's panel of ≥3/≥3 companies captures only 1,792 of 4,691 arshkon rows (38%) and 11,499 of 40,881 scraped rows (28%). The within-company decomposition is a significant sample restriction; the between-company component (entry of new employers) is probably the larger mechanism.

7. **Scraped's geographic fit to BLS is best-in-class on raw Pearson (0.975), worst on log/Spearman (0.82/0.86).** The 26-metro scraped frame produces a distribution that matches BLS in the top-10 but drops to zero in small states. This is by design (the scraped study frame is 26 metros), but it's worth flagging: claims like "our data is nationally representative" should be qualified to "our data is representative of the top US metros where SWE hiring concentrates" — 84% of BLS national SWE employment is in the top-10 states, so the practical coverage is high.

8. **Top-company overlap panel is dominated by well-known giants + aggregators.** Top overlapping companies by combined posting count: Google (15+757), JPMorgan Chase (12+657), Microsoft (19+386), Amazon (31+335), AWS (12+339), Booz Allen (9+303), Wells Fargo (16+288), Capital One (52+223), Motion Recruitment (40+229, aggregator), Actalent (23+238, aggregator). T16's clustering of "how companies changed" will be heavily weighted toward tech giants and a handful of staffing aggregators unless capped.

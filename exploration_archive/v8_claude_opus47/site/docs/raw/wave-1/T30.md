## Headline

**Primary junior operationalization: J2** (`seniority_final ∈ {entry, associate}`). **Primary senior operationalization: S1** (`seniority_final ∈ {mid-senior, director}`). J2 and S1 give the cleanest label quality with adequate power. The junior direction is **consistently up** across all five label-independent and label-based operationalizations (J1–J5 all show a 2024→2026 increase of 1.4–5.1 percentage points). The senior direction is **consistently down** across S1/S3/S4/S5 (−4.3 to −16.2 percentage points), with S2 (director-only) being too sparse to read. These two direction results are the most important findings in this panel — they are stable across operationalization and robust to aggregator exclusion.

**A significant schema issue was discovered and handled**: the task spec says to match J5/S3 on `title_normalized`, but that column has level indicators pre-stripped. This panel uses `lower(title)` instead for J5/S3 so level markers survive. Any downstream task that re-derives J5/S3 must use raw `title`, not `title_normalized`.

## Definitions

Junior side:
- **J1** `seniority_final = 'entry'` (label-based primary, tight but low-power)
- **J2** `seniority_final ∈ {entry, associate}` (combined bucket — recommended primary)
- **J3** `yoe_extracted ≤ 2` (label-independent proxy)
- **J4** `yoe_extracted ≤ 3` (loosened label-independent proxy)
- **J5** `lower(title)` matches `\b(junior|jr|entry[- ]level|graduate|new[- ]grad|intern)\b` (title-keyword)
- **J6** `J1 ∪ J5` (any junior signal)

Senior side:
- **S1** `seniority_final ∈ {mid-senior, director}` (recommended primary)
- **S2** `seniority_final = 'director'` (top-of-ladder only — power-bound)
- **S3** `lower(title)` matches `\b(senior|sr\.?|staff|principal|lead|architect|distinguished)\b`
- **S4** `yoe_extracted ≥ 5`
- **S5** `yoe_extracted ≥ 8`

All regex patterns validated with inline TDD assertions in `exploration/scripts/T30_seniority_panel.py`, covering: trailing periods in `sr.`, hyphen/space variants in `entry-level`/`entry level`, word-boundary edge cases (`jrpg`, `leadership`, `architecture`, `international`, `srcinfo`), and case insensitivity.

## Panel CSV schema (consumed by Wave 2+)

| Column | Meaning |
|---|---|
| `definition` | One of `J1..J6`, `S1..S5` |
| `side` | `junior` or `senior` |
| `period_source` | `arshkon` · `asaniczka` · `pooled_2024` · `scraped_2026` |
| `n_of_all` | SWE row count in this period_source under the base filter |
| `n_of_known` | Subset denominator (`known_filter`) — for label defs: `seniority_final != 'unknown'`; for YOE defs: `yoe_extracted NOT NULL`; for title-keyword defs: equal to `n_of_all` |
| `share_of_all` | n_match / n_of_all |
| `share_of_known` | n_match / n_of_known |
| `mde_arshkon_vs_scraped` | MDE at 80% power, α=0.05, using `share_of_all` baseline and `n_of_all` per side (arshkon vs scraped_2026) |
| `mde_pooled_vs_scraped` | Same, pooled_2024 vs scraped_2026 |
| `within_2024_effect` | `arshkon_share_of_all − asaniczka_share_of_all` |
| `cross_period_effect` | `scraped_2026_share_of_all − pooled_2024_share_of_all` |
| `direction` | Sign of `cross_period_effect` (`up`, `down`, `flat`, `n/a`) |

Extra columns: `within_2024_diff_known`, `cross_pooled_diff_known`, `mde_arshkon_vs_scraped_known`, `mde_pooled_vs_scraped_known` — same metrics under the `known` denominator. Always report both; see "of-all vs of-known" caveat.

## Step 6: Title-keyword content spot-check

### J5-only sample (50 rows matched by J5 but not by J1)

Almost all 50 rows are **unambiguously junior**. The pattern: titles like "Software Engineer Graduate", "New College Graduate - Data Engineer", "Engineer Graduate: Machine Learning — 2026 Start (PhD)" — 35+ rows of these university-graduate-program postings all have `seniority_final = 'unknown'` because Stage 5 doesn't include `graduate` in its junior-title regex and the LLM's description-based inference saw no explicit signal in the body.

Inspection: ~46/50 (92%) are correct junior matches. The ~4/50 flagged as potential FPs are compound titles like "Full Stack Developer - Senior, Mid, or Jr (TS/SCI)" or "(Junior – Senior) Software Engineer - Backend/Platform" where multiple seniorities are listed.

**Precision estimate for J5: ≈92%.**

### S3-only sample (50 rows matched by S3 but not by S1)

48/50 are "Lead <role>" titles — Stage 5's rule did not match due to positional variants ("QA Automation Lead", "Lead, Software Engineer"). Only 2 rows are clearly mis-labeled — both interns whose descriptions include the word "Lead" ("Software Engineer Project Intern (Lead Ads)", "Senior Technical Intern — Data Engineer/Scientist" — the latter title is actually legitimately odd).

**Precision estimate for S3: ≈96%.**

## Step 7: Primary recommendations

### Junior side → **J2** (`seniority_final ∈ {entry, associate}`)

Justification: J2 carries 418 pooled-2024 matches and 1,327 scraped matches — materially larger than J1 (379 / 1,275) without diluting the junior signal (the `associate` class is tiny and the LLM rarely fires it; adding it is almost free). Share_of_all direction (+1.41pp) is essentially identical to J1's (+1.46pp) with slightly better power. **Use J2 as the single junior operationalization for Wave 2/3 structural analyses.**

- **Hold J3 (`yoe_extracted ≤ 2`) as the label-independent primary robustness check.** J3's share of SWE is 3× larger than J2, making it well-powered for sub-group analysis (e.g., per-metro or per-industry entry shares). Any RQ1 junior claim should report J2 as primary and J3 as robustness — the directional agreement between them is the decisive evidence.
- **Use J5 for title-keyword content analysis** (what does a junior posting look like?). J5 is the only definition that captures the dense population of "X Graduate" and "New College Graduate" titles in scraped 2026.
- Do **not** use J1 alone for content analyses. J1's n in pooled-2024 (379) is too small to stratify further by metro/industry.

### Senior side → **S1** (`seniority_final ∈ {mid-senior, director}`)

Justification: S1 has the best label quality (Stage 5 keyword + LLM) and ample power (13,595 / 17,713 matches). Within-2024 arshkon-vs-asaniczka difference is −19.3pp (arshkon 44.3%, asaniczka 63.5%) — a large calibration gap that is a genuine between-source composition difference, not a labeling artifact (see Essential sensitivities below). S3 agrees with S1 within 2pp on direction but has lower label quality. S4/S5 are useful cross-checks but have an unresolved 35% overlap gap with S1.

- **Hold S4 (`yoe_extracted ≥ 5`) as the label-independent primary robustness check.** Same agreement-pattern logic as J3 for the junior side.
- **Use S3 for title-keyword content analysis.** Lead-titles are missed by Stage 5 and S3 recovers them.
- Do **not** use S2 (director-only) as a primary operationalization.

### If the answer differs by metric type

- **Content analyses** (what words, what tools, what skills appear in junior/senior postings): prefer **J5/S3** for the content-stratified corpus because they capture the title-keyword population missed by the LLM-driven `seniority_final`.
- **Structural analyses** (share, count, composition): prefer **J2/S1** for label-quality reasons.
- **Cross-period direction claims** (RQ1 headline): report **both** label-based and YOE-based (J2+J3, S1+S4) and require directional agreement.

## Data quality caveats

1. **`title_normalized` strips level indicators**. Using `title_normalized` for J5/S3 as the task spec suggested yields near-zero matches (e.g., S3 drops from 17,289 to ~600). We used `lower(title)` instead. **Downstream tasks that re-derive J5/S3 must use raw `title`, not `title_normalized`.** Spec file should be updated.
2. **YOE coverage is not 100%** (62% arshkon, 71% asaniczka, 68% scraped). J3/J4/S4/S5 implicitly condition on YOE presence. The `known` denominator for these definitions is the YOE-parsed subset; this is documented in the CSV.
3. **`seniority_final = 'unknown'` is 40% of scraped SWE** vs. 13% of asaniczka SWE. The `share_of_known` denominator for label-based definitions means very different compositions across sources. Always report `share_of_all` as primary and `share_of_known` as a sensitivity.
4. **MDE baselines use `p_baseline` = arshkon/pooled share.** For effect sizes that are large relative to `p_baseline` (e.g., S1 going 59% → 43%), this approximation is slightly conservative (larger MDE than true). For small effects (S2), the MDE is still usable.
5. **S2 is power-bound.** With n_match = 105 (pooled 2024) and 295 (scraped), director-only analysis is not meaningful. Director-specific claims must use broader senior operationalizations.

## Surprises & unexpected patterns

1. **"Graduate" is a huge missed entry signal.** ~35/50 J5-only sample rows are of the form "Software Engineer Graduate" / "New College Graduate - SWE" — clearly junior, but `seniority_final = 'unknown'` because Stage 5 doesn't include "graduate" as a strong junior keyword. This is **~1000 rows of undetected 2026 junior signal**. The preprocessing rule should be updated. This finding is not just a side note — it means the official entry share (J1 = 3.12% in scraped) **understates the true junior share** by likely 1.5-2pp, and J2's +1.41pp cross-period effect may itself be conservative.
2. **Direction is lopsided.** All six junior operationalizations go UP (+1.4 to +5.1pp). All four senior operationalizations (excluding S2) go DOWN (−4.4 to −16.3pp). In a scenario where employers are consolidating roles into more senior buckets (a common pre-exploration hypothesis), we would expect the opposite — juniors down, seniors up. The observed pattern **rejects that specific hypothesis**. However, it is consistent with a different story: the scraped 2026 corpus has more `seniority_final = 'unknown'` (40% vs 13%), and rows that would have been labeled `mid-senior` in 2024 may be getting `unknown` in 2026 due to differential LLM routing rates — this would depress the senior share and mildly inflate the junior share via the label-independent definitions. Wave 2 must test this compositional-routing hypothesis before publishing a "seniors are shrinking" finding.
3. **S2 (director-only) moves up slightly** (+0.26pp), against the rest of the senior bucket. Most likely mechanism: differential LLM routing. Scraped 2026 has a larger LLM-processed fraction of rows with "director" or "VP" or "Head of" titles (the LLM is better at recognizing these than Stage 5's keyword rule). The finding is small and not robust.
4. **J3 and J4 barely overlap with J1/J2.** Only 22% of J1 rows have YOE ≤ 2 — the rest have missing YOE or higher YOE. This is itself a research-worthy observation: titles labeled "entry" routinely ask for 3-5 YOE. Supports the "scope inflation" narrative independently of the direction work. RQ1 can be motivated by this distinct finding without having to claim a direction shift.
5. **Within-2024 senior gap is −19pp (arshkon vs asaniczka).** This is larger than some between-period effects. Any senior-side comparison that collapses arshkon and asaniczka into a single "2024 baseline" is doing so at a real methodological cost — the two 2024 sources are NOT interchangeable on seniority. Future analysis plans should always present arshkon-only and pooled-2024 as two separate baselines.
6. **95% of J5 rows are covered by J6 but only 72% of J6 rows are covered by J5** — J6's distinctive rows come from J1 (LLM-labeled entry) rather than from title keywords. This means the LLM-entry signal and title-keyword signal are genuinely complementary — combining them yields 1,348 scraped matches vs 1,275 (J1) or 1,030 (J5) alone.

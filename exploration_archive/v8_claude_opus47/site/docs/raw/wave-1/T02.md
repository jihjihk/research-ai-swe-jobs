## Headline finding and verdict

**Asaniczka `associate` is NOT usable as a junior proxy.** It fails on **all four** decision-rule signals — directional behavior is consistently "between arshkon `associate` and arshkon `mid-senior`", not "like arshkon `entry`". On the most diagnostic signals (senior title-cue rate and YOE distribution) asaniczka `associate` is **much closer to arshkon `mid-senior` than to arshkon `entry`**.

**Downstream implication:** Analyses that need a 2024 baseline for juniors must use T30's **J2** (combined `entry`+`associate` under `seniority_final`) or one of the **YOE variants (J3 `yoe≤2`, J4 `yoe≤3`)** instead of pooling asaniczka `associate` rows under a proxy-entry label. J1 (`seniority_final='entry'`) is still the purest label variant but is power-bound in 2024 (379 pooled SWE rows).

## Methodology

The narrow question: given asaniczka has zero native entry-level labels, do its `associate`-labeled SWE rows behave enough like arshkon `entry` that downstream tasks can treat the union `asaniczka.associate ∪ arshkon.entry` as a 2024 junior baseline?

Comparison cells (all filtered to `is_swe AND source_platform='linkedin' AND is_english AND date_flag='ok'`):

| Cell | source | `seniority_native` | cell n (SWE) |
|---|---|---|---:|
| arshkon_entry | kaggle_arshkon | `entry` | 738 |
| arshkon_associate | kaggle_arshkon | `associate` | 263 |
| arshkon_mid_senior | kaggle_arshkon | `mid-senior` | 2,231 |
| asaniczka_associate | kaggle_asaniczka | `associate` | 1,820 |

Decision rule from the task spec: asaniczka_associate passes if it behaves directionally like arshkon_entry on ≥3 of 4 signals: (1) top-title Jaccard, (2) explicit junior/senior title-cue rates, (3) `yoe_extracted` distribution, (4) `seniority_final` distribution conditional on native label.

Title-cue patterns (TDD-validated with inline asserts in the script):

- **Junior cues:** `\bjunior\b`, `\bjr\.?\b`, `\bentry[- ]?level\b`, `\bassociate\b`, `\bgraduate\b`, `\bnew[- ]?grad\b`, `\bintern\b`, `\btrainee\b`, `\bapprentice\b`, `\bearly[- ]?career\b`, `\bi\b(?!\s*/)`, `\bii\b`
- **Senior cues:** `\bsenior\b`, `\bsr\.?\b`, `\bstaff\b`, `\bprincipal\b`, `\blead\b`, `\barchitect\b`, `\bdistinguished\b`, `\bhead\s+of\b`, `\bdirector\b`, `\bvp\b`, `\bvice\s+president\b`, `\bchief\b`, `\biv\b`, `\bv\b(?!\s)`
- Applied to raw `title` (lowercased, word-boundary-safe). Asserts cover level codes, Jr./Sr. abbreviations, and non-firing on "senior" when looking for "junior".
- Known limitation: `\blead\b` matches "team lead" (intended) and also "lead developer" (intended) but would match "lead generation" (unintended). Expected noise is small since we're scoring SWE titles, not marketing titles. Precision of this pattern is acceptable for a directional diagnostic.

All prevalence, share, and distribution numbers below use these exact patterns on raw `title`, subset to `is_swe AND default filter AND source=<src> AND seniority_native=<native>`; denominator is "of all rows in cell" unless noted.

|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|

Read: asaniczka_associate's top-50 title list shares **set** similarity of 0.32 with arshkon_entry (marginally higher than with arshkon_associate 0.23 or arshkon_mid_senior 0.27). On weighted Jaccard the ranking is the same (asaniczka_associate vs arshkon_entry = 0.30).

**Signal 1 verdict: DIRECTIONAL MATCH TO ENTRY — but only on generic SWE titles.** The "closest" similarity of 0.32 is largely driven by generic titles (`software engineer`, `data engineer`, `developer`) that appear at the top of every SWE seniority cell. Absolute similarity is low across the board. The signal is weak but tilts toward entry.

### Signal 2 — Explicit junior / senior title-cue rates

| Cell | junior cue rate | senior cue rate |
|---|---:|---:|
| arshkon_entry | **12.6%** | 2.3% |
| arshkon_associate | 8.0% | 19.8% |
| arshkon_mid_senior | 2.2% | **57.6%** |
| **asaniczka_associate** | **6.9%** | **30.6%** |

Pattern: `\bjunior\b|\bjr\.?\b|\bentry[- ]?level\b|\bassociate\b|\bgraduate\b|\bnew[- ]?grad\b|\bintern\b|\btrainee\b|\bapprentice\b|\bearly[- ]?career\b|\bi\b(?!\s*/)|\bii\b` (junior) and the senior analogue above. Subset: SWE, default filter, cell-specific `seniority_native`. Denominator: of all rows in the cell.

Read:
- Junior cue rate: asaniczka_associate (6.9%) is closer to arshkon_associate (8.0%) than to arshkon_entry (12.6%). It is **~55% of entry's junior-cue rate**.
- Senior cue rate: asaniczka_associate (30.6%) is **13.3x arshkon_entry's 2.3%** and is on the path from arshkon_associate (19.8%) toward arshkon_mid_senior (57.6%).

**Signal 2 verdict: FAILS — asaniczka_associate is much more senior-titled than arshkon_entry.** The senior-cue gap is not ambiguous: arshkon_entry has essentially zero senior titles (2.3%); asaniczka_associate has a substantial senior title share (30.6%).

Concrete confirmation: asaniczka_associate's top-20 titles include "senior software engineer" at rank 7 (23 rows). Arshkon_entry's top-20 has zero titles with the word "senior".

|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|

| arshkon_mid_senior | 2,231 | 66.2% | **5.0** | 5.61 | 4.0 | 7.0 | 10.0 | 8.2% | 68.7% |

Read: asaniczka_associate's YOE distribution lies **between** arshkon_associate and arshkon_mid_senior, closer to the former. The median (4) is one year above arshkon_entry's median (3). The mean (4.5) is 0.3 years higher than arshkon_entry's 4.2 but 1.1 years below arshkon_mid_senior's 5.6.

The strongest specific gap: the **≤2 YOE share** — the classic junior-requirement signature. Arshkon_entry has 28.6% of YOE-known rows demanding ≤2 years; asaniczka_associate has only 21.9%. The 6.7-point gap is meaningful and directionally against the "entry-like" hypothesis.

The **≥5 YOE share** — the classic mid-senior signature. Arshkon_entry 42.6%, asaniczka_associate 47.9%, arshkon_mid_senior 68.7%. Asaniczka_associate is 5.3 points above arshkon_entry and 20.8 points below arshkon_mid_senior — it is the "nearer to entry, closer to middle" pattern.

**Signal 3 verdict: FAILS.** The YOE distribution is not "directionally like arshkon_entry" — it is directionally between `associate` and `mid-senior`, with median +1 year over entry and ≥5-YOE share elevated by 5 points.

### Signal 4 — `seniority_final` conditional on native label

Share of each native-labeled cell that `seniority_final` ends up assigning to each level (Stage 5 strong-rule + Stage 10 LLM):

| Cell | final entry | final associate | final mid-senior | final director | final unknown |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| arshkon_entry | **7.2%** | 0.3% | 8.8% | 0.0% | 83.7% |
| arshkon_associate | 8.0% | 1.5% | 21.7% | 0.0% | 68.8% |
| arshkon_mid_senior | 0.9% | 0.1% | **62.4%** | 0.2% | 36.3% |
| **asaniczka_associate** | **3.8%** | 0.8% | **33.5%** | 0.2% | 61.6% |

Read: when Stage 5+10 are allowed to relabel these rows based on title keywords and the LLM:
- Arshkon_entry: only 7.2% survive as entry in `seniority_final` (the rest become unknown 83.7% or mid-senior 8.8%). The native entry label is mostly lost by the time `seniority_final` is computed — a structural feature of the schema, not a defect, because Stage 5 deliberately ignores native labels and fires only on explicit title keywords.
- Asaniczka_associate: the pipeline re-classifies these rows as `mid-senior` 33.5% of the time but only 3.8% as `entry`. The LLM's own view of these rows is that 1/3 are mid-senior — **not** entry.
- For direct comparison: the ratio of (final mid-senior share) / (final entry share) in asaniczka_associate is **8.7x**. In arshkon_entry it's 1.2x.

**Signal 4 verdict: FAILS.** The pipeline's own labelers — with no knowledge of the native seniority column — classify asaniczka_associate rows as mid-senior 8.7x as often as they classify them as entry.

### Decision-rule summary

| Signal | Result |
|---|---|
| 1. Top-title Jaccard | marginal-match to entry (0.32 vs 0.23 / 0.27); low absolute values; weak evidence driven by generic titles |
| 2. Explicit title cues | **FAIL** — senior-cue rate is 13x arshkon_entry's; junior-cue rate is only 55% of entry's |
| 3. `yoe_extracted` distribution | **FAIL** — median +1 year, ≥5-YOE share +5pp, ≤2-YOE share −7pp vs arshkon_entry |
| 4. `seniority_final` re-classification | **FAIL** — asaniczka_associate → mid-senior 8.7x as often as → entry |

**Verdict: NOT USABLE as a junior proxy.** Passes at most 1 of 4 signals (Jaccard, and only marginally). Fails the three most diagnostic signals.

## Surprises & unexpected patterns

1. **Asaniczka `associate` is compositionally mid-level, not junior-skewed.** The reason the schema-preamble warning about asaniczka's lack of entry labels matters is sharper than it reads: asaniczka_associate isn't "mostly junior with some mid" — on the best diagnostic signals it is **mostly mid-senior-like with a long junior tail**. 33.5% of asaniczka_associate rows end up labeled `mid-senior` in `seniority_final`; only 3.8% end up `entry`. Any proxy that mapped asaniczka_associate → entry would systematically contaminate entry-level findings with mid-senior rows.

2. **LinkedIn's own `associate` label has drifted between the two 2024 snapshots.** Arshkon `associate` (263 SWE rows) has a very different profile from asaniczka `associate` (1,820): arshkon is meaningfully more junior-looking (8.0% junior cue, 19.8% senior cue, median YOE 3) than asaniczka (6.9% junior cue, 30.6% senior cue, median YOE 4). Same platform label, same year (2024-Q1/Q2), different sampling — yet different compositional mix. This is direct evidence of the "differential native-label quality across snapshots" risk flagged in the schema.

3. **Even arshkon `entry` mostly becomes `unknown` in `seniority_final`.** 83.7% of arshkon_entry SWE rows end up with `seniority_final='unknown'`. Only 7.2% stay as `entry`. The production column (`seniority_final`) discards most of the native entry labels. This is by design — Stage 5 deliberately doesn't consult `seniority_native`, and the LLM only fires when a row is routed. The consequence is that for the T30 panel J1 variant, the 2024 entry pool is effectively "strong-rule entry" + "LLM entry", not "native entry". Downstream entry-level claims are further from the native LinkedIn label than they look.

4. **asaniczka_associate YOE median is 4, perfectly between 3 (entry) and 5 (mid-senior).** The 3-year-experience mode ("requires 3 YOE") is the single largest peak in asaniczka_associate's distribution — consistent with LinkedIn treating `associate` as "1–4 YOE" historically. This is directly at odds with an `entry` equivalence but strongly consistent with a "combined entry+associate" (T30 J2) grouping.

5. **Senior title prevalence in asaniczka_associate (30.6%) is a composition artifact to investigate.** "Senior Software Engineer" at rank 7 in the asaniczka_associate top-20 is strange — an `associate`-labeled row that titles itself senior. Three possible mechanisms: (a) the Kaggle curator pulled multi-level postings and LinkedIn's `associate` seniority flag applies loosely to "individual contributor" rather than YOE level in this export; (b) the label was machine-inferred on the asaniczka ingest side, introducing noise; (c) some LinkedIn postings legitimately reach "senior" titles at what LinkedIn still tags as associate seniority. Either way, the finding is that LinkedIn's `associate` tag in asaniczka is a **weak** indicator of junior-level content. Worth noting for T03 (seniority audit) since it feeds into how much to trust `seniority_native` as an anchor at all.

6. **Weighted Jaccard(asaniczka_associate, arshkon_associate) = 0.12 is lower than (asaniczka_associate, arshkon_entry) = 0.30.** Counterintuitive — you'd expect the two `associate` cells to look most alike. Two effects: (a) arshkon_associate's cell is tiny (263 rows) and its top-50 title mass is dominated by small numbers, so weighted Jaccard is low everywhere against it; (b) asaniczka_associate's top-title mass is concentrated on the same generic "software engineer"-family titles as arshkon_entry, because both cells are SWE-heavy. The "entry-like" weighted Jaccard reflects shared **generic** SWE vocabulary, not shared **junior** vocabulary.

7. **The scraped entry pool is rule-keyword-dominated (79%).** In 2024, the strong-rule share of entry was 54% (arshkon) and 55% (asaniczka); in 2026 scraped it is 79%. Two candidate mechanisms: (a) 2026 postings use more explicit junior-keyword titles ("Junior SWE", "Associate Software Engineer") — a title-language shift; (b) the scraped LLM frame doesn't fire on as many scraped rows (the 30.5% extraction coverage from T01), so the LLM doesn't get to re-classify as many scraped entry candidates. Either way, any 2024-vs-2026 entry-share finding should be cross-checked for composition sensitivity to `seniority_final_source`.

## Data-quality caveats

- The title-cue patterns are directional diagnostics only. "Lead" can match "lead generation" (not a seniority signal) but this is acceptable noise for a SWE-filtered sample; a more careful validation is T03's job.
- Arshkon_associate (263 SWE rows) is a small cell. Its diagnostic value for T02 is limited — but the three other comparison cells are adequately powered, so the decision rule still fires.
- `yoe_extracted` is missing for ~30–40% of each cell. All YOE summaries are on the YOE-known subset; share reported in the table.
- `seniority_final` will change as Stage 9/10 budgets are re-run. Today's 175 arshkon-entry LLM labels and 83 asaniczka-entry LLM labels depend on the balanced LLM frame; a larger budget would expand them.

## Action items for downstream tasks

1. **Do NOT pool asaniczka.associate with arshkon.entry under any "combined junior" variant.** That move produces a contaminated junior baseline where ~33% of the asaniczka-half rows are structurally mid-senior.

2. **For 2024 junior baselines, use T30's J2 (combined entry+associate under `seniority_final`), J3 (`yoe≤2`), or J4 (`yoe≤3`).** These already operate on the resolved column and sidestep the native-label issue.

3. **Cross-source entry-level sanity checks must be arshkon-only** for anything that anchors on `seniority_native` — confirmed in this task. This is the documented caveat and T02 makes it concrete.

4. **Flag the `seniority_final_source` composition for 2024 vs 2026 junior pools.** 2026 junior is 79% rule-driven, 2024 junior is ~55% rule-driven. Any entry-share or entry-stratified finding should report under a `title_keyword`-only sensitivity variant in addition to the full pool.

5. **Treat asaniczka_associate in analyses as "mid-heavy mixed" rather than "junior-adjacent".** For tasks that stratify by seniority level (T08, T09, T14, T20, T21, T22), asaniczka_associate rows are best treated as a separate mixed bucket, not merged with either `entry` or `mid-senior`.

6. **T03 (seniority audit, Agent B):** The native `associate` label behaves very differently across arshkon (junior-leaning) and asaniczka (mid-leaning) even though both sources are 2024 LinkedIn. This is evidence that `seniority_native` has cross-snapshot drift — worth isolating in the T03 kappa / per-class accuracy tables.

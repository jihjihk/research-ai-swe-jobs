# T16: Cross-Occupation Comparison (SWE vs SWE-Adjacent vs Control)

## Finding

The junior-share decline is SWE-specific, not a macro-labor-market trend. Between April 2024 (arshkon) and March 2026 (scraped), SWE junior share fell from 21.0% to 13.9% (-7.1pp), while control (non-technical) junior share rose from 22.5% to 43.5% (+21.0pp) and SWE-adjacent held roughly steady (18.5% to 20.7%). AI keyword growth, while strongest for SWE (3x), is also present in SWE-adjacent (2.7x) and even control (6.5x from a very low base), indicating AI-as-requirement is a broad phenomenon but AI-tool-specific requirements are concentrated in technical roles. The divergent seniority pattern establishes that the SWE junior decline is not confounded by general hiring trends.

## Key results

### 1. Junior share trends (arshkon 2024-04 vs scraped 2026-03)

This comparison uses only arshkon and scraped, the two sources where entry-level native labels exist. Asaniczka (2024-01) cannot be used for seniority comparisons because it has zero entry-level labels -- all its postings are native-labeled mid-senior or associate, artificially showing <1% junior share.

| Group | Apr 2024 junior % (n known) | Mar 2026 junior % (n known) | Change (pp) | Direction |
|-------|---------------------------|---------------------------|-------------|-----------|
| SWE | 21.0% (n=3,553) | 13.9% (n=3,948) | -7.1 | DECLINED |
| SWE-adjacent | 18.5% (n=1,223) | 20.7% (n=565) | +2.2 | Stable |
| Control | 22.5% (n=7,117) | 43.5% (n=3,917) | +21.0 | INCREASED |

The SWE junior decline is directionally opposite to both the control group and SWE-adjacent group. This is strong evidence that the decline is occupation-specific rather than driven by macro-economic conditions or platform-level changes in how seniority labels are assigned.

The control group's large increase in junior share may reflect a different dynamic: non-technical occupations are seeing more entry-level postings, possibly due to post-pandemic hiring normalization or LinkedIn usage patterns. This is the opposite of what we see in SWE.

**Key observations:**
- **AI-tool-specific growth multipliers (2024-01 to 2026-03):** SWE 9.3x, SWE-adjacent 12.5x, Control 6.5x. All groups show large relative growth, but absolute levels differ enormously: 26% for SWE vs 1.3% for control.
- **SWE-adjacent tracks SWE** but at ~70% of the SWE rate, consistent with SWE-adjacent roles (DevOps, data engineering, QA automation) requiring similar but not identical AI tool proficiency.
- **Control AI growth is real but tiny in absolute terms:** 0.2% to 1.3% for AI tools. This likely reflects general "AI literacy" language entering non-technical job descriptions, not specific tool requirements.
- **AI/ML engineering mentions** grew 2x for SWE (14.8% to 29.2%) but remained near-zero for control (0.1% to 0.4%), confirming this is a technical-occupation phenomenon.

### 4. Assessment: Are observed changes SWE-specific?

**Junior share decline: YES, SWE-specific.** The control group shows the opposite trend (increasing junior share), and SWE-adjacent is stable. This rules out the hypothesis that the junior decline is driven by macro-economic conditions, changes in LinkedIn's platform, or shifts in how seniority labels are assigned across the board.

**AI requirement growth: Partially SWE-specific.** The absolute level of AI requirements is overwhelmingly concentrated in SWE and SWE-adjacent roles (55% and 40% respectively vs 7% for control). However, the relative growth rate is high everywhere, suggesting AI-as-buzzword is permeating all job descriptions, while AI-as-functional-requirement is concentrated in technical roles. The AI-tool-specific category (LLM/GPT/Copilot) is the cleanest discriminator: 26% for SWE vs 1.3% for control.

**Combined pattern:** SWE is experiencing a unique combination of (a) declining junior share and (b) surging AI requirements. Control occupations show AI buzzword growth but increasing junior hiring. This strongly suggests the SWE patterns are driven by AI-specific restructuring of the occupation, not by general labor market dynamics.

## Implication for analysis

- RQ1 junior-share finding passes the cross-occupation test: the decline is SWE-specific, not confounded by macro trends.
- RQ3 AI divergence finding is primarily a technical-occupation phenomenon (SWE + adjacent), not a general labor market trend.
- The control group's increasing junior share may itself be an interesting contrast to cite: while AI is reducing entry-level SWE demand, non-technical occupations continue to grow their entry-level pipeline.
- SWE-adjacent tracking SWE at ~70% intensity suggests a gradient of AI impact from core SWE to adjacent technical roles to non-technical roles.

## Data quality note

- **Asaniczka seniority bias:** The 2024-01 period (asaniczka) shows <1% junior share for ALL groups because asaniczka has zero entry-level native labels. The seniority_patched CTE falls through to native_backfill for most asaniczka rows, which only returns mid-senior or associate. Junior share comparisons should use arshkon (2024-04) vs scraped (2026-03) only.
- **SWE-adjacent sample in 2026-03 is small:** n=598 total, n=565 with known seniority. Results are directionally informative but not precise.
- **Control group composition may differ by period.** Control postings in arshkon may not be the same occupation mix as control postings in scraped data. This limits the precision of the control comparison but does not invalidate the direction (opposite trend to SWE).
- **"Other" occupations (86% of data) are not analyzed.** The three-way comparison covers ~14% of rows. The unclassified majority could show different patterns.

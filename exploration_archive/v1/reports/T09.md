# T09: Seniority Source Sensitivity

## Finding

The junior-share finding is **not robust** across seniority operationalizations. The arshkon-to-scraped direction flips depending on whether native platform labels are included:

- **Variants with native labels (A, C, D):** Entry share **decreased** by 7.1-7.4pp (from 21-23% to 14-15%). All three agree on magnitude (-32% to -34% relative change).
- **Variants without native labels (B, F):** Entry share **increased** by 1.2-1.6pp (from 4-5% to 6-7%). Both agree on magnitude (+28% to +32% relative change).

The native-label cluster and the title-only cluster are internally consistent but disagree on direction. This is the same blocker identified in Gate 1 (T02).

### Why the direction flips

The disagreement is mechanical, not statistical:

1. **Native labels capture entry-level that titles miss.** Arshkon has 693 native entry-level labels vs 87 title-detected. The title-based classifier has ~30-61% recall for entry-level (T02 finding). Variants A/C/D include the native-detected entry-level postings; B/F do not.

2. **Coverage differs by period.** Arshkon has 70% native coverage; scraped has 83% native coverage. Both have similar title-strong coverage (~39% vs ~46%). The denominator shift interacts with the numerator.

3. **The broken pipeline (B) is doubly biased.** It misses native labels for SWE (the bug) AND has higher unknown rates (55% arshkon, 45% scraped), meaning its "known" pool is selected on title-keyword detectability.

### Agreement assessment

- **Same direction?** NO. The 5 variants split 3-2.
- **Magnitude within 2x?** Within each cluster, yes. The native-label cluster spans -7.06 to -7.42pp (range < 0.5pp). The title-only cluster spans +1.23 to +1.61pp (range < 0.4pp). But across clusters, the magnitudes are not comparable.
- **Which cluster is more trustworthy?** The native-label cluster (A, C, D) is based on 3-7x more entry-level observations (545-745 vs 87-132) and has much higher coverage (80-100% vs 45-55% known). The title-only cluster has severe selection bias because it detects only a fraction of entry-level postings.

### Asaniczka note

Asaniczka (2024-01) is excluded from the trend comparison because it has zero native entry-level labels. Its entry shares (0.0%-0.88% depending on variant) are artifacts of title-keyword detection only and do not represent the true entry-level share. Cross-period trends must use arshkon vs scraped.

## Implication for analysis

1. **RQ1 direction remains uncertain without seniority_llm.** The native-label variants provide a stronger signal (more observations, higher coverage) and suggest entry share is declining. But until the LLM seniority classifier runs, we cannot rule out that native label coverage differences between arshkon and scraped are biasing the result.

2. **The patched workaround (Variant A) is the best available estimate.** It has the broadest coverage, uses the strongest signals first, and agrees closely with the native-only and high-confidence variants. It should be the primary reporting variable.

3. **Variant B (broken pipeline) should not be used for primary analysis.** It misses most entry-level postings and its direction (increasing) is driven by small-sample noise (89 vs 132 entry-level observations).

4. **The LLM seniority classifier (seniority_llm) is critical for resolving this.** It would provide an independent classification for the ~20% of rows that remain unknown even after the patched workaround, and would validate or challenge the native label patterns.

## Data quality note

1. The direction disagreement is a **known, expected consequence** of the native_backfill bug and the low recall of title-based entry-level detection. It is not a data corruption issue.

2. Sample sizes for the title-only variants (B, F) are thin: 87-132 entry-level postings. These are not powered to detect changes smaller than ~3pp.

3. The native-label variants have much stronger statistical power (545-745 entry observations) but their validity depends on the assumption that LinkedIn's seniority labeling process did not change systematically between 2024 and 2026.

4. Asaniczka contributes nothing to the entry-level trend analysis regardless of variant. It is useful only for mid-senior level questions.

## Action items

1. **Use Variant A (patched) as primary, report B (broken) as sensitivity.** The patched variant is better-powered and better-grounded.
2. **Flag the direction disagreement in all RQ1 reporting.** Until seniority_llm resolves this, state the finding conditionally: "Conditional on native platform labels reflecting true seniority, entry share declined 7pp (-34%). Title-only classification suggests a possible increase of 1-2pp but is underpowered."
3. **Prioritize the Stage 5 bugfix and seniority_llm run.** This is the highest-value data improvement for RQ1.
4. **Investigate whether LinkedIn's seniority labeling methodology changed between 2024 and 2026.** If the platform's own labeling shifted, native labels would introduce temporal confounding.
5. **For downstream analyses using seniority:** always report the number of entry-level observations to make clear when results rest on thin samples.

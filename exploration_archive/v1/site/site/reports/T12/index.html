    T12: Requirements Parsing

    Finding

    Junior/entry SWE roles in 2026 show clear scope inflation in organizational competencies but not in formal credentials. Compared to 2024, entry-level postings in 2026 demand significantly more cross-functional collaboration (+10pp), ownership language (+8pp), end-to-end responsibility (+6pp), and collaboration keywords (+24pp). AI keyword prevalence nearly tripled (15% to 44%). However, median stated YOE actually decreased from 3.0 to 1.0 years, and MS/PhD requirements held flat (~13%). The scope inflation pattern is not junior-specific -- mid-senior roles show similar or larger increases in soft/organizational language, suggesting a field-wide shift in how SWE roles are described, not a junior-targeting squeeze.

    Implication for analysis

    RQ1 (scope inflation): Strong evidence of scope inflation in behavioral/organizational requirements but not in formal credential requirements (YOE, education). The inflation appears across seniority levels, which complicates the "junior squeeze" narrative. The more precise claim is: employers are demanding more organizational maturity (ownership, cross-functional, end-to-end) at all levels, and entry-level roles are not exempt. The YOE decrease at entry level may reflect a relabeling effect -- formerly "mid-senior" roles now being posted as "entry" with higher soft expectations.

    RQ2 (task migration): The ownership/end-to-end/cross-functional increases at junior level (+6-10pp) are consistent with tasks migrating downward: junior engineers are now expected to own more scope. Meanwhile leadership language actually decreased slightly at junior (-4.5pp), suggesting the "lead a team" responsibility is not migrating down.

    RQ3 (AI divergence): AI keyword prevalence jumped from 15% to 44% at entry level, with similar jumps at mid-senior (17% to 56%). This establishes the employer-side demand trajectory that RQ3 will compare against actual developer usage.

    Data quality note

    1. Extraction coverage: Requirements sections found in 61% of postings; YOE extractable from 51%; education from 54%. Coverage is adequate for aggregate statistics but individual-row extraction has gaps.
    2. YOE paradox at entry level: The median YOE drop from 3.0 (arshkon 2024) to 1.0 (scraped 2026) for entry-level roles is surprising. It may reflect (a) the seniority_patched workaround pulling in genuinely lower-YOE postings that were previously "unknown", or (b) real changes in how entry-level is defined. The arshkon entry-level median of 3.0 YOE is itself suspicious for "entry" roles and may reflect misclassification or ghost posting behavior.
    3. Description length confound: Descriptions grew ~50% from 2024 to 2026 (T05). Raw keyword counts are inflated; length-normalized tech density shows near-zero change (1.2 to 1.3 per 1K chars), while raw tech count rose (4 to 5). Soft skill percentages (binary: mentioned or not) are less affected by length but not immune.
    4. Sensitivity check: Repeating the junior comparison with original seniority_final (smaller n: 213 vs 132) shows consistent direction for most metrics (collaboration +25pp, AI +22pp, ownership +5pp) but weaker magnitudes, supporting the patched results.
    5. Collaboration inflation caveat: The +24pp jump in "collaboration" keywords may partly reflect longer descriptions having more space for boilerplate language about teamwork. Length-normalization for binary flags is not straightforward.

    Action items

    1. For T17/synthesis: The scope inflation finding is robust for organizational/behavioral language but must be presented alongside the field-wide baseline shift. Entry-level inflation exists but is not disproportionate relative to mid-senior.
    2. For analysis phase: Use length-normalized tech density (not raw tech count) as the comparable metric. For soft skills, consider a composite "organizational complexity" score (cross-functional + ownership + end-to-end + stakeholder) to reduce multiple-comparison issues.
    3. YOE investigation needed: The entry-level YOE drop warrants deeper investigation -- check whether it is driven by specific companies or a broad shift.
    4. AI keyword list refinement: The current AI regex is broad (includes "ml", "nlp", "machine learning"). For the RQ3 analysis, separate "AI-as-tool" keywords (copilot, ai coding, prompt engineering) from "AI-as-domain" keywords (machine learning, deep learning, NLP).

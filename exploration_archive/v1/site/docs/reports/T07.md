# T07: Representativeness

## Finding

All three datasets achieve strong geographic representativeness against BLS OES state-level employment for SOC 15-1252 (Software Developers). Pearson r ranges from 0.96 to 0.98 (linear) across datasets, well above the 0.80 target. The combined dataset achieves r=0.978 across all 50 states+DC. Industry representativeness is moderate (Spearman rho=0.70), with systematic overrepresentation of Information-sector postings (+9.3pp) and underrepresentation of Professional/Technical Services (-18.5pp) -- consistent with LinkedIn's skew toward tech-branded companies versus IT consulting embedded in non-tech firms. JOLTS and Indeed benchmarks confirm that all three data collection periods fall within a persistent post-2022 SWE hiring trough, with the Indeed Software Development posting index flat at roughly 70 (vs. a Feb 2020 baseline of 100) across all three periods.

## Implication for analysis

**RQ1-RQ3 (cross-period comparisons):** The high geographic correlations (all r>0.93) mean state-level variation in our data reliably tracks actual SWE employment concentration. Cross-period comparisons of geographic hiring patterns are well-grounded.

**Industry bias caveat:** The Professional/Technical Services sector -- the largest SWE employer (41.8% of BLS employment) -- is underrepresented at 23.3% of our postings. This sector includes IT consulting, staffing firms, and contract SWE roles. Analyses of industry composition should note this gap. The overrepresentation of Information (29.3% vs 20.0%) means pure-play tech firms are overweighted.

**Temporal context:** The JOLTS Information sector series shows volatile month-to-month swings (98K-177K range in 2024 alone), so monthly snapshots carry noise. However, the Indeed SWE index -- a more granular and less volatile metric -- shows all three periods clustered tightly between 69.9 and 71.9, confirming they sample from essentially the same labor market regime. The 2024-to-2026 comparison is therefore not confounded by a major hiring cycle shift.

**Scraped data geographic coverage:** The scraped dataset covers only 30 states (vs. 47-50 for Kaggle sources) due to the 26-metro search frame, producing a high linear r (0.98) but lower log-space r (0.78) driven by missing small states. This is by design, not a bias concern, but geographic analyses should note the narrower coverage.

## Data quality note

1. **Industry mapping is approximate.** LinkedIn company_industry labels do not map cleanly to NAICS sectors. 17.3% of industry labels in our data (arshkon+scraped) could not be mapped, mostly compound multi-industry labels from scraped LinkedIn data (e.g., "Accounting, Financial Services, and Business Consulting and Services"). Most unmapped labels belong in Professional/Technical Services or Information, so the true gap between our data and OES is likely smaller than reported.

2. **OES is employment, our data is postings.** OES measures total employment stocks (May 2024 survey); our data measures job posting flows on LinkedIn. These are conceptually different: posting volume reflects hiring activity, not installed base. High correlation confirms geographic alignment but does not validate volume levels.

3. **SOC 15-1256 retired.** The May 2024 OES no longer publishes SOC 15-1256 (the combined Software Developers/QA code). The analysis uses 15-1252 (Software Developers) as the sole benchmark, which aligns well with our is_swe classification.

4. **JOLTS is sector-level, not occupation-level.** JOLTS Information sector covers all occupations in NAICS 51, not just SWE. It serves as a directional context indicator, not a direct benchmark.

5. **Vermont (VT) has suppressed OES data** (reported as "**" in the source). This state is excluded from the correlation with zero imputation.

6. **Scraped daily counts are limited.** Only 2 scraping days (Mar 20-21) are in the deduped stage8 dataset, so daily-rate comparisons with JOLTS monthly figures are illustrative only.

## Action items

1. **Geographic weighting not needed.** The r>0.93 correlations across all datasets confirm that geographic representativeness is adequate for unweighted cross-state analysis. No poststratification required.

2. **Industry sensitivity analysis recommended.** For RQ1/RQ2 analyses that touch industry composition, run a sensitivity check excluding Staffing/Recruiting (aggregator proxy) and reweighting to OES sector shares. The Professional Services underrepresentation could affect results about contract-vs-direct hiring patterns.

3. **Note scraped state coverage in methods.** The 30-state coverage of scraped data is by design (metro-targeted search) and should be disclosed. For state-level temporal comparisons (2024 vs 2026), restrict to the 30 overlapping states.

4. **JOLTS/Indeed context for paper framing.** The Indeed SWE index at ~70 (vs. peak of ~229 in Feb 2022) confirms our data captures a sustained post-pandemic hiring contraction. All three collection windows sample from a flat-to-declining SWE demand floor. This context should appear in the paper's methods section.

5. **Unmapped industry labels (17.3%).** A future refinement could parse compound LinkedIn industry labels into component NAICS sectors. This is low priority given the moderate overall industry correlation (rho=0.70).

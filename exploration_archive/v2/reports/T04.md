# T04: SWE classification audit
## Finding
The SWE classifier is strong on the core positive set and noisy on the border. Among filtered LinkedIn rows, SWE postings are mostly tagged by `regex` (26,822 rows, 86.5%), with `title_lookup_llm` covering 3,559 rows (11.5%) and `embedding_high` 619 rows (2.0%). Dual-flag violations are zero.

On the intentionally borderline audit sample, 27/50 nominal SWE rows were clearly primary SWE and 23/50 were better treated as SWE-adjacent rather than primary SWE. In the borderline non-SWE sample, 6/50 were true false negatives, 11/50 were adjacent-but-not-primary SWE, and 33/50 were clearly non-SWE. The borderline slice therefore implies roughly 54% strict precision on difficult positives and about 12% strict false-negative rate on difficult negatives.

## Implication for analysis
The classifier is good enough for core SWE counting, but the paper should keep `is_swe_adjacent` as a separate comparison group and avoid over-reading the boundary cases. The most important leakage is into DevOps, QA, MLOps, architect, and test roles on the positive side, and into application/BI/embedded/software-systems titles on the negative side.

## Data quality note
This is a border-case audit, not a population-wide error estimate. The sample was deliberately drawn from ambiguous rows, so the rates are meant to characterize the classifier's weakest region rather than its average performance. The positive/negative split is still clean enough for the main SWE sample, but adjacent technical roles remain a meaningful source of ambiguity.

## Action items
Use `is_swe` for the core SWE sample, but keep `is_swe_adjacent` in downstream robustness checks and task-migration work. If a later validation pass is needed, prioritize the ambiguous families observed here: QA, DevOps, MLOps, platform engineering, software architecture, and application-development titles.

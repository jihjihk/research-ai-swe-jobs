# T05: Cross-Dataset Comparability

## Headline Finding

The three datasets differ substantially on multiple dimensions, but **within-2024 calibration reveals that much of the divergence reflects collection-method artifacts rather than temporal change.** The two 2024 sources (arshkon vs asaniczka) already show significant differences on description length (KS=0.18), geographic distribution, and company composition -- establishing a high baseline of cross-source variability. Cross-time differences (2024 vs 2026) are larger on most metrics, but only ~1.5-2x the within-2024 baseline, suggesting the real temporal signal is modest relative to source noise.

**Most trustworthy cross-time comparisons:** Title vocabulary (top-50 Jaccard actually higher for arshkon-scraped than arshkon-asaniczka), YOE distributions within matched title x seniority cells (median YOE is remarkably stable), and the platform labeling stability test (native seniority labels for the same titles are broadly consistent). **Least trustworthy:** Description length (collection method confound), company overlap (Jaccard ~0.11-0.14 everywhere, reflecting sampling rather than market structure), and raw seniority distributions (dominated by the asaniczka entry-level labeling gap).

## Methodology

- Population: LinkedIn, English, date_flag='ok', is_swe=true
- Pairwise comparisons across three sources: arshkon (2024-04, n=5,019), asaniczka (2024-01, n=23,213), scraped (2026, n=35,062)
- Statistical tests: KS tests for continuous distributions, chi-squared with Cramer's V for categorical distributions, Jaccard similarity for set overlap
- Seniority ablation: all analyses repeated with seniority_llm, seniority_native, seniority_final, and seniority_imputed

## 1. Description Length

| Comparison | KS (full desc) | KS (core) | Cohen's d (full) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Arshkon vs Asaniczka (within-2024) | 0.184 | 0.050 | -0.35 |
| Arshkon vs Scraped (cross-time) | 0.339 | 0.335 | -0.84 |
| Asaniczka vs Scraped (cross-time) | 0.212 | 0.379 | -0.52 |

Median description lengths: arshkon 2,954 chars, asaniczka 3,825, scraped 5,175. The scraped 2026 descriptions are ~57% longer in median than the arshkon 2024 baseline. This is a **large** effect (Cohen's d = -0.84).

**Artifact assessment:** The within-2024 KS of 0.18 shows that different Kaggle datasets from the same era already differ substantially. The cross-time KS is 1.84x the within-2024 baseline, suggesting roughly half the observed difference may be real (longer postings in 2026) and half is collection-method noise. Core-length KS is much smaller within-2024 (0.05) but larger cross-time (0.34-0.38), suggesting the boilerplate removal step normalizes within-era artifacts but the real content difference in 2026 is genuine.

## 2. Company Overlap

| Comparison | Jaccard (all) | Shared | Jaccard (top 50) | Shared |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Arshkon vs Asaniczka | 0.114 | 741/6,503 | 0.136 | 12/88 |
| Arshkon vs Scraped | 0.117 | 914/7,834 | 0.220 | 18/82 |
| Asaniczka vs Scraped | 0.136 | 1,366/10,030 | 0.163 | 14/86 |

Company overlap is low across all pairs (~11-14% Jaccard), with the within-2024 pair showing essentially the same overlap as cross-time pairs. This indicates that company sampling is the dominant source of variation, not temporal change. The job posting market has a very long tail of companies.

**Notable:** Top-50 Jaccard is actually **highest** for arshkon vs scraped (0.22), suggesting the largest employers are more stable across time than across collection methods. Asaniczka's top companies are dominated by aggregators (Recruiting from Scratch, ClearanceJobs, ClickJobs.io), which inflates its apparent company diversity.

## 3. Geographic Distribution

| Comparison | Chi-squared | Cramer's V |
|---|---|---|
| Arshkon vs Asaniczka (within-2024) | 224.8 | 0.108 |
| Arshkon vs Scraped (cross-time) | 267.3 | 0.090 |
| Asaniczka vs Scraped (cross-time) | 1,611.0 | 0.187 |

All pairwise differences are statistically significant, but the within-2024 Cramer's V (0.11) is comparable to the arshkon-scraped V (0.09). The asaniczka-scraped difference is larger (V=0.19), likely driven by asaniczka's different search query design.

Key state-level shifts: California share increases from 20-21% (2024) to 25% (2026). Washington increases from 5-6% to 8%. Florida and Ohio drop. These could reflect real geographic concentration of SWE hiring in tech hubs, or differential scraper coverage.

## 4. Seniority Distributions (Ablation Framework)

### Entry-level share across operationalizations

| Variable | Arshkon 2024 | Asaniczka 2024 | Scraped 2026 |
|---|---|---|---|
| **seniority_llm** (primary) | 16.7% (84/504) | 6.4% (39/613) | **27.7%** (180/649) |
| seniority_native | 22.3% (769/3,444) | 0.0% (0/23,213) | 13.7% (3,972/29,077) |
| seniority_final | 21.0% (848/4,035) | 0.6% (129/23,213) | 14.2% (4,656/32,808) |
| seniority_imputed | 6.7% (151/2,238) | 1.3% (218/16,151) | 6.7% (1,288/19,337) |

**CRITICAL FINDING -- directional disagreement across operationalizations:**

- **seniority_llm** shows entry share **increasing** from 16.7% (arshkon 2024) to 27.7% (scraped 2026) -- a +11 pp increase
- **seniority_native** shows entry share **decreasing** from 22.3% to 13.7% -- a -8.6 pp decrease
- **seniority_final** similarly shows a decrease from 21.0% to 14.2% -- a -6.8 pp decrease
- **seniority_imputed** shows no change (6.7% in both periods)

This is the most important finding in the task. The direction of the entry-level trend **depends on the seniority operationalization.** The LLM classifier (which uses uniform explicit-signal-only logic) sees an increase; the native platform labels see a decrease. This could indicate:
1. LinkedIn's platform labeling has shifted (more roles that the LLM considers entry are being labeled mid-senior natively)
2. The LLM classifier is more generous with entry-level labels than the native platform
3. The LLM labeled sample is small (504 / 613 / 649 rows with known seniority) and may not be representative

**Small-N warning for seniority_llm:** The LLM-labeled entry counts are 84, 39, and 180. These are too small for confident trend estimates. The seniority_native and seniority_final results are based on 10-60x more observations.

### Chi-squared tests

| Variable | Comparison | Cramer's V |
|---|---|---|
| seniority_final | Arshkon vs Asaniczka | 0.391 |
| seniority_final | Arshkon vs Scraped | 0.083 |
| seniority_final | Asaniczka vs Scraped | 0.252 |
| seniority_llm | Arshkon vs Asaniczka | 0.223 |
| seniority_llm | Arshkon vs Scraped | 0.207 |
| seniority_llm | Asaniczka vs Scraped | 0.319 |

The within-2024 seniority_final Cramer's V (0.39) is **much larger** than the arshkon-scraped V (0.08), confirming the asaniczka entry-level gap dominates seniority comparisons. For seniority_llm, the within-2024 V (0.22) is comparable to arshkon-scraped (0.21), suggesting the LLM variable partially resolves this artifact but introduces its own cross-source variability.

## 5. Title Vocabulary

| Comparison | Jaccard (all) | Jaccard (top 50) |
|---|---|---|
| Arshkon vs Asaniczka | 0.048 | 0.389 |
| Arshkon vs Scraped | 0.030 | **0.429** |
| Asaniczka vs Scraped | 0.040 | 0.266 |

Full-title Jaccard is very low (~3-5%) reflecting the enormous long tail of unique titles. But top-50 Jaccard is substantial (27-43%), and importantly the arshkon-scraped top-50 overlap (0.43) is the highest of all pairs. This indicates that the core SWE title vocabulary is stable across time.

12,971 titles appear uniquely in scraped (not in either 2024 source). Many contain AI-specific language ("ai and distributed systems backend engineer", "genai python systems engineer", "ai data engineer") or consulting-firm standardized formats with level suffixes ("- manager", "- consultant", "- director"). 8,316 titles appear in 2024 but not in scraped.

## 6. Industry Comparison (Arshkon vs Scraped)

The largest industry shift: "IT Services and IT Consulting" dropped from 36.3% to 16.2%, while "Software Development" increased from 23.6% to 37.5%. "Technology, Information and Internet" doubled from 3.7% to 7.8%. Chi-squared: 1,294 (p < 1e-267), Cramer's V = 0.22.

**Artifact assessment:** This shift could reflect LinkedIn reclassifying industries, companies changing their LinkedIn profiles, or a genuine recomposition of the SWE hiring market away from IT consulting toward product companies. The absence of asaniczka industry data prevents within-2024 calibration.

## 7. Artifact Diagnostic Summary

| Metric | Within-2024 baseline | Cross-time signal | Trustworthiness |
|---|---|---|---|
| Description length | KS=0.18 | KS=0.34 (1.8x) | **Medium** -- real lengthening likely, but magnitude uncertain |
| Company overlap | Jaccard=0.11 | Jaccard=0.12 | **Low** -- no signal above baseline noise |
| Geographic shares | V=0.11 | V=0.09-0.19 | **Medium** -- CA/WA increases may be real |
| Seniority (native) | V=0.39 (artifact) | V=0.08 | **Medium** -- arshkon-scraped is well-behaved; asaniczka confounds everything |
| Seniority (LLM) | V=0.22 | V=0.21-0.32 | **Low-Medium** -- small N, directional disagreement with native |
| Title vocabulary (top 50) | J=0.39 | J=0.43 | **High** -- core titles are stable |
| YOE per title/seniority | Stable | Stable | **High** -- most stable metric across all comparisons |

## 8. Within-2024 Calibration

The within-2024 comparison (arshkon vs asaniczka) establishes that datasets from the same era can diverge substantially:
- Description length KS = 0.18 (significant)
- Company Jaccard = 0.11
- Geographic Cramer's V = 0.11
- Seniority dominated by asaniczka's missing entry labels

This means any cross-time difference must exceed these baselines to be interpreted as temporal change.

## 9. Platform Labeling Stability Test

For the top 20 shared titles between arshkon and scraped, the native seniority label distributions are **broadly similar but show systematic differences:**

- "software engineer": entry share drops from 16.2% (arshkon) to 20.6% (scraped) -- actually a slight increase on this anchor title
- "data engineer": entry drops from 22.2% to 14.4%
- "devops engineer": entry drops from 14.3% to 13.3% (essentially stable)
- "machine learning engineer": entry increases from 5.4% to 12.9%
- "embedded software engineer": entry drops from 22.6% to 13.2%
- "test engineer": entry drops from 36.0% to 31.0%
- "software developer": entry drops from 37.5% to 26.0%

For the flagship "software engineer" title, entry share slightly increased. But for several other titles, entry share declined. The pattern is **mixed, not uniformly in one direction**, which argues against a simple platform relabeling artifact.

**YOE stability:** Within matched title x seniority cells, YOE distributions are remarkably stable. For "software engineer" mid-senior, median YOE is 5.0 in both periods. For "software engineer" entry, median YOE is 2.0 in both periods. This consistency suggests that when a role has the same title and native seniority label, its actual experience requirements have not changed -- the shifts are in **composition** (which seniority levels are posted for which titles), not **content** (what each level requires).

**Indeed cross-validation:** Indeed scraped data (using seniority_imputed, the only available method for Indeed) shows entry share of 7.3%, very close to LinkedIn's seniority_imputed of 6.7%. This similarity across platforms suggests the seniority_imputed baseline is platform-independent and thus not a LinkedIn-specific artifact.

## Surprises & Unexpected Patterns

1. **Directional disagreement on entry-level trend is the most consequential finding.** seniority_llm shows +11 pp entry increase; seniority_native shows -8.6 pp decrease. This must be resolved before RQ1 analysis can proceed.

2. **Asaniczka's top "companies" are aggregators and data artifacts.** "Recruiting from Scratch" (1,699 SWE postings), "ClearanceJobs" (920), and "ClickJobs.io" (483) are not real employers but job aggregators that passed through the canonicalization pipeline. "We are deploying software-defined" (196) and "Commitment Full-time" (189) appear to be description text fragments misclassified as company names.

3. **Description length increased 57% in median (2,954 to 5,175 chars).** This is a large, real effect that will bias any text-density metric. All text-based analyses must control for description length.

4. **AI engineer counts exploded.** "ai engineer" went from 6 postings in arshkon to 321 in scraped -- a 50x increase. This title barely existed in 2024 and is now a major category.

5. **Top-50 company overlap is highest for the cross-time pair** (arshkon-scraped at 0.22 > arshkon-asaniczka at 0.14), suggesting the large employers are more stable across time than the full company distribution.

6. **Industry recomposition is dramatic:** "IT Services and IT Consulting" halved its share while "Software Development" grew by 14 pp. If real, this represents a structural shift in which types of companies hire SWEs.

## Data Quality Caveats

1. **seniority_llm sample sizes are binding constraints.** Only 504/613/649 non-unknown labeled rows per source. Trend estimates from these have very wide confidence intervals.
2. **Asaniczka has company name data quality issues.** Several "companies" are text fragments, not real employers. Any analysis involving asaniczka company-level grouping should filter for known-good company names.
3. **Remote work flags are 0% across all sources** (data artifact, not real). Do not use for any analysis.
4. **Description length differences confound all text-based cross-source comparisons.** Use density metrics (per 1K chars) or length-matched samples.
5. **Industry data is available only for arshkon and scraped.** The asaniczka gap prevents within-2024 industry calibration.

## Action Items for Downstream Tasks

1. **Resolve the entry-level directional disagreement** before proceeding with RQ1. The most promising approach: expand the seniority_llm sample size (currently small) and test whether the disagreement persists. If it does, the analysis must present both findings with appropriate caveats.
2. **All text-density analyses must normalize for description length.** The 57% length increase makes raw counts unreliable.
3. **Consider excluding asaniczka from entry-level analyses entirely** and relying solely on arshkon as the 2024 baseline -- the entry-level labeling gap makes asaniczka a liability.
4. **Company-level analyses should exclude known aggregator artifacts** from asaniczka (Recruiting from Scratch, ClearanceJobs, etc.) or use the is_aggregator flag consistently.
5. **The stable YOE pattern within title x seniority cells** is a strong anchor for analysis: use this to argue that composition changes (which seniority levels are posted) rather than content changes (what each level requires) are the primary signal.

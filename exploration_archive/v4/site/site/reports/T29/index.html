    T29 — LLM-authored description detection

    Agent O, Wave 3 (EXPLORATORY). Completed 2026-04-09.

    Headline verdict: HYPOTHESIS REJECTED

    The low-LLM-score subset of the 2024-2026 corpus exhibits the SAME Wave 2 findings as the full corpus and the high-LLM-score subset, often with larger percentage changes. Credential stack depth ≥7 grows +786% in the low-LLM subset versus +384% in the high-LLM subset. Description length grows +77% in the low-LLM subset versus +79% in the high-LLM subset (identical). AI mention grows +339% low vs +269% high. Tech density falls -22.8% low vs -34.3% high. Requirement breadth grows +42% low vs +35% high.

    Not a single Wave 2 headline weakens when the LLM-style subset is removed. If anything, the low-LLM subset shows cleaner, stronger versions of every change.

    This rejects the hypothesis that recruiter LLM-adoption mediates the 2024-2026 labor market shifts. Whatever is driving the changes (credential stacking, AI mentions, length growth, mid-senior restructuring) is happening uniformly across postings regardless of whether they read as LLM-style marketing prose or as human-written technical copy. The text-source composition shift (2024 91% LLM-cleaned vs 2026 21% LLM-cleaned, flagged by V1) does contribute to the +72% aggregate length rise, but even under text-source control the length growth persists at +26% (V1) and T29's headline metrics (credential stacking, AI mentions) are well above the text-source baseline.

    Caveats: 1. The composite "authorship score" captures polished-marketing-prose style more than it captures LLM-specific tells. Some highest-scoring 2024 companies (Microsoft, Raytheon, Booz Allen, Anduril) are likely producing human-written but marketing-polished copy, not LLM-generated copy. 2. Under text-source-controlled comparison, em-dash density rose 9-11×, LLM-vocab density rose 36-63%, and sentence length roughly doubled. These ARE suggestive of 2026 postings having more LLM-style surface features. But even if we accept those increases are genuine LLM-adoption evidence, the Wave 2 labor-market findings are independent of them. 3. Spot-check validation showed the score discriminates qualitatively well (high = flowing marketing "Do you want to be part of a world-class organization..."; low = terse "seeking .NET/C# Developer, Full Time"). But "LLM-style" and "marketing-style" are intrinsically confounded.

    The hypothesis was testable and testable is good — the answer is that recruiter LLM adoption is not a parsimonious unified mechanism for the Wave 2 findings.

    1. Authorship signals and validation

    1.1 Features (defined in T29_01_features.py)

    Computed per posting on the raw description column (not Stage 9 cleaned, to preserve formatting):

    Feature Definition Role
    llm_vocab_density_1k Count of classic LLM tells (leverage, pivotal, cutting-edge, seamless, harness, robust, vibrant, foster, delve, embark, ...) per 1K chars Primary vocabulary signal
    emdash_density_1k Em-dash (— and --) per 1K chars Strong LLM stylistic tell
    bullet_density_1k Bullet markers (•, *, -, 1.) per 1K chars Structural; ingestion-sensitive
    ttr Type-token ratio of lowercased word tokens LLM prose has lower TTR
    mean_sent_len_chars, std_sent_len_chars Sentence length mean and std LLMs write longer, more uniform sentences
    mean_para_len, paragraph_count Paragraph stats via \n\n splits Ingestion-sensitive
    hedge_phrase_count "but not limited to", "such as but", "not limited to" LLM hedge
    closer_count "in conclusion", "we look forward", "join us" Boilerplate closer
    comma_density_1k, exclaim_density_1k Punctuation density Style

    Pre-flight asserts (in script): regex patterns validated against hand-crafted positive/negative cases before running on corpus.

    Composite score (llm_score_robust): z-sum of four ingestion-robust features: llm_vocab_density_1k, hedge_phrase_count, comma_density_1k, and 1 - ttr. Excludes em-dash, bullets, paragraph features because those are too sensitive to ingestion pipeline differences across sources.

    1.2 Spot-check validation

    Drew 25 high-score (llm_vocab_count≥11) and 25 low-score (llm_vocab_count=0) samples. Qualitative read (see exploration/tables/T29/spotcheck_samples.csv):

    High-score examples read like corporate marketing copy: - "Do you want to be part of a growing world-class organization focused on bettering our world? Are you passionate about significantly improving the future of medicine?" (AmplifyBio 2024) - "At Function Health, our mission is to empower individuals to take control of their health. Through our innovative platform..." (Function Health 2024) - "Senior Backend AI Engineer... In this pivotal role, you will be at the forefront..." (Pop-Up Talent 2024)

    Low-score examples read as terse, skills-list style: - "If you are a Sr. Software Engineer with experience, please read on! We are seeking a Senior Signal Processing Engineer..." (CyberCoders 2024) - "Syms Strategic Group (SSG) is seeking a talented .Net/C# Developer. Department: Veterans Affairs. Type: Full Time. Min. Experience: Experienced..." (SSG 2024) - "Secure our Nation, Ignite your Future..." (ManTech 2024)

    Conclusion: The score discriminates "polished marketing prose" from "terse technical" reliably. But it does NOT directly discriminate "LLM-generated" from "human-written". A 2024 AmplifyBio description with 15 LLM-tell hits is more likely human-written marketing copy than LLM-generated; a 2024 LLM probably would have written even more fluently. This is an important limitation of the approach.

    1.3 Score levels

    • Raw corpus (n=63,294): 2024 mean z-score -0.48, 2026 mean z-score +0.39 (Δ +0.88 standard deviations)
    • LLM-cleaned subset (n=33,220 — text-source-controlled): 2024 -0.14, 2026 +0.51 (Δ +0.66 SD)

    The shift is ~1 SD upward from 2024 to 2026. Under text-source control (LLM-cleaned subset), the shift is still 0.66 SD — substantial.

    2. Distribution by period — text-source-controlled

    Raw corpus (mixed text sources), no aggregators:

    Feature 2024 2026 Ratio
    llm_vocab_density_1k 0.337 0.468 ×1.39
    emdash_density_1k 0.040 0.142 ×3.59
    ttr 0.571 0.538 ×0.94
    hedge_phrase_count 0.190 0.273 ×1.43
    comma_density_1k 11.1 12.2 ×1.10
    composite score (z-sum) -0.65 +0.50 Δ +1.16

    LLM-cleaned subset (text-source-controlled), no aggregators:

    Feature 2024 2026 Ratio
    llm_vocab_density_1k 0.275 0.448 ×1.63
    emdash_density_1k 0.012 0.110 ×9.08
    ttr 0.597 0.580 ×0.97
    hedge_phrase_count 0.078 0.072 ×0.92
    comma_density_1k 10.4 11.8 ×1.14
    composite score (z-sum) -0.21 +0.59 Δ +0.80

    Under text-source control: - Vocabulary density rises MORE (+63% vs +39%) — text cleaning normalizes away some 2024 marketing flourishes, so post-normalization 2024 looks less LLM-ish, widening the gap. - Em-dash density rises 9× — still huge but slightly smaller than the 3.6× raw. The 2024 asaniczka baseline of 0.012 is very low; 2024 LLM-cleaned text rarely contains em-dashes. 2026 scraped LLM-cleaned text has 0.110, 9× higher. This is the most striking signal that survives text-source control. - Hedge phrases disappear entirely as a signal under text-source control (ratio 0.92). The raw-corpus hedge growth was mostly the 2026 raw text retaining more boilerplate that the LLM cleaner removes. - Comma density grows only +14% — modest. - TTR drops trivially (0.02pp) — not supportive of the "LLM uniformization" hypothesis.

    Cross-posting variance (std of composite score within each year):

    Subset 2024 std 2026 std
    raw 2.250 2.192
    ctrl (LLM-cleaned) 2.023 2.184

    2026 is NOT more uniform than 2024. Cross-posting variance is essentially unchanged (raw barely dropped; ctrl slightly rose). The "LLMs would uniformize recruiter writing" prediction is NOT supported. If recruiters were offloading to the same LLM, we'd expect variance to contract. It hasn't.

    2.1 Interpretation

    Two features survive text-source control and are worth taking seriously as LLM-tell candidates: 1. Em-dash density 9-11× — very large and specific 2. Vocabulary density +36-63% — clear but smaller

    Four features fail to support LLM-adoption hypothesis: 1. TTR essentially unchanged — LLMs don't appear to be reducing vocabulary diversity 2. Cross-posting variance unchanged — no uniformization 3. Hedging phrases disappear as signal under text-source control — the raw-corpus rise was an artifact 4. Sentence length doubling is partly ingestion (scraped markdown fragments, 2026 Stage 9 output structure)

    See exploration/figures/T29/fig1_score_distribution.png and period_distribution.csv.

    Consulting firms (Deloitte, PwC) are the biggest adopters — they went from below-median to strongly above-median LLM-style prose, and dramatically increased posting volume simultaneously (22→450 and 63→419). This is suspicious — it suggests Deloitte and PwC may have started using LLM drafting tools AND scaled their hiring.

    Companies with highest 2024 scores (early adopters or marketing-heavy writers):

    Company 2024 score 2026 score Δ
    Microsoft 5.81 7.06 +1.26
    Raytheon 3.60 6.00 +2.39
    Booz Allen Hamilton 2.91 1.57 -1.34

    BLUE ORIGIN 2.65 1.13 -1.52
    Figma 2.48 4.53 +2.05

    Microsoft is the single highest-scoring company in both 2024 and 2026 — 5.8 SD above the mean in 2024, 7.1 SD in 2026. Microsoft's postings are extremely marketing-polished. This is likely a company writing-style fingerprint, not LLM adoption.

    Booz Allen, BLUE ORIGIN, General Motors all show score DECREASES — they became less marketing-polished in 2026. This is compatible with either (a) real style regression or (b) text-ingestion pipeline differences (scraped 2026 text is less clean).

    Interpretation: Per-company analysis produces a plausible but not conclusive list of LLM-adoption candidates. The consulting-firm cluster (Deloitte, PwC, American Express) is the strongest single signal. Microsoft's stability as a top scorer shows the signal is partly company-writing-style-invariant.     4. Correlation with Wave 2 findings

    Spearman correlations between llm_score_robust and Wave 2 per-posting metrics, computed separately by year:

    Metric 2024 ρ 2026 ρ
    description_length (text_len) 0.457 0.522
    requirement_breadth 0.338 0.339
    credential_stack_depth 0.327 0.344
    mgmts_mentor 0.210 0.169
    ai_mention 0.096 0.175
    tech_density -0.055 -0.132

    All Wave 2 metrics are POSITIVELY correlated with the LLM-style score, in BOTH periods. Longer postings, broader requirements, deeper credential stacks all correlate with more polished prose. This is expected: longer descriptions have more room for flowing prose, and companies willing to write marketing copy are also willing to stack credentials.

    The correlation structure is stable across periods. The 2024 credential-stack-vs-LLM correlation (0.327) is basically identical to 2026 (0.344). If LLM adoption were causing credential stacking, we'd expect the correlation to strengthen substantially in 2026 as the LLM-adoption population expanded. It didn't.

    Tech density has a small negative correlation — higher LLM-style postings have slightly lower tech density. This is consistent: marketing prose dilutes technical content per character. Note: |ρ| is small (0.06-0.13), so this effect is minor.

    See wave2_correlations.csv.

    Split: Within each year, divided postings into quartiles by composite LLM-score. Q1 = lowest-LLM (most "human"), Q4 = highest-LLM. Compared Wave 2 headline metrics across Q1 and Q4 subsets.

    Metric All (Δ%) Q1 low-LLM (Δ%) Q4 high-LLM (Δ%) Hypothesis prediction
    Description length +72.7% +76.8% +79.1% Low should weaken
    Credential stack ≥7 share +445.9% +785.7% +383.8% Low should weaken
    AI mention rate +296.0% +338.9% +269.4% Low should weaken

    Requirement breadth +37.3% +41.8% +35.5% Low should weaken

    Not a single headline metric weakens in the low-LLM subset. Several STRENGTHEN. The credential-stack growth is nearly 2× larger in the low-LLM subset (+786%) than in the high-LLM subset (+384%). AI-mention growth is +339% in low-LLM vs +269% in high-LLM. Description length growth is essentially identical (+77 vs +79%).

    5.1 Interpretation

    The hypothesis that recruiter LLM adoption mediates the Wave 2 findings is rejected. If LLMs were the mechanism, removing LLM-authored postings would wipe out (or at least substantially shrink) the aggregate rises. Instead, the rises persist in the human-written subset, sometimes at larger relative magnitudes.

    Alternative interpretations for the features that DO show 2024→2026 change: - Em-dash 9× and vocab density +36% may reflect genuine LLM-adoption at the edges but are not the main channel for the labor market findings. - The "polished marketing prose" axis is real and growing — there are more marketing-polished postings in 2026 — but the labor market content changes are happening underneath this style variable. - T13's "57% length growth is real content" is now stronger: the low-LLM subset (presumably least affected by authoring tool changes) shows a near-identical +77% length rise. The content expansion is real; it's not an authoring-tool artifact.

    The original hypothesis motivation — "LLM authorship could unify length growth, credential stacking, AI mention rise, and mid-senior tone shift" — is attractive but not what the data shows. Those findings need separate explanations.

    See headline_retest.csv.

    6. Caveats, limitations, and what I would do differently

    Signal/content confound. The composite score correlates with description length (ρ≈0.5) and credential stacking (ρ≈0.33) even at fixed authorship. A company that writes longer, more polished job descriptions will score high regardless of whether an LLM or a human wrote them. The Q1/Q4 split by this score therefore mechanically stratifies on description length and credential stacking, making the headline re-test a stronger-than-necessary test: it shows the within-quartile trajectory is the same shape at every length/credential level.

    Text ingestion dominates some signals. Em-dash, bullets, and paragraph structure are heavily influenced by how each source ingested raw HTML or text. Scraped 2026 text is formatted with markdown headers and bullet delimiters; asaniczka 2024 text is frequently single-block. I used only the ingestion-robust features (vocab, hedge, comma, TTR-inverse) for the composite score. Even so, the absolute levels of "LLM-style" differ systematically across sources.

    The score measures polished marketing prose, not LLM-generated text. Microsoft and Raytheon score 5-7 SD above the mean in 2024, before widespread recruiter LLM adoption; their postings read as traditional corporate marketing copy. This limits the score's ability to identify LLM authorship specifically. A more specific test would require: - Training a classifier on labeled LLM-vs-human job descriptions (there is no such labeled dataset available) - Or using perplexity under a known-human language model, flagging low-perplexity postings (requires a large language model at generation time)

    Cross-posting variance is the one assumption I can test cleanly, and it failed. If recruiters were outsourcing to a common LLM, 2026 postings would be more uniform than 2024. They are not (raw std 2.25→2.19, LLM-cleaned std 2.02→2.18). This is the most rigorous piece of evidence against the hypothesis.

    The LLM-cleaned subset is small for 2026 (n=7,308). Only 21% of scraped postings got Stage 9 LLM extraction. That subset is not a random sample of 2026 — it's biased toward postings the LLM extraction pipeline successfully cleaned. This weakens the text-source-controlled comparison.

    What I would do differently: 1. Use a pretrained perplexity-based LLM detector (e.g., GLTR, DetectGPT) instead of hand-crafted vocabulary signals. 2. Build a paired within-company comparison: for the same company in both periods, did their writing style change more than company-specific baselines? This would control for company-writing-style confounds. 3. Restrict to description_core_llm in both periods to control text source, and acknowledge sample reduction.

    8. Bottom line

    • Verdict: rejected. Recruiter LLM adoption is not a parsimonious mechanism for the Wave 2 labor-market findings.
    • Stylistic shifts ARE happening in 2026 (em-dashes 9×, vocabulary +36-63%), but they are decoupled from the headline content changes.
    • T13's +72% length growth is real content expansion — the low-LLM subset shows +77%, essentially identical.
    • T11's 5.4× credential stacking is real — it is actually LARGER (+786%) in the low-LLM subset than in the high-LLM subset.
    • T08's AI mention rise is real — +339% in low-LLM subset, +269% in high-LLM subset.
    • Cross-posting variance is unchanged 2024→2026, contradicting the "LLMs uniformize" prediction.
    • Per-company analysis identifies Deloitte, PwC, American Express, and Collins Aerospace as candidate LLM-adoption firms, but this signal is confounded with marketing-prose style.
    • Microsoft has been the highest-scoring company in both 2024 and 2026, suggesting the score is measuring company writing style more than LLM authorship.

    The hypothesis was worth testing and the answer is clean: authorship-tool changes are not the story. The Wave 2 findings require non-authorship explanations, which is the defensible position for the paper.

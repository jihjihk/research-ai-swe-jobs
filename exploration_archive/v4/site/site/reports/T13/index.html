    T13 — Linguistic & Structural Evolution of SWE LinkedIn Postings

    Date: 2026-04-09 Scope: SWE LinkedIn only (is_swe=true, source_platform='linkedin', is_english=true, date_flag='ok'), n=63,294 (2024 = 28,232; 2026 = 35,062). Primary seniority signal: combined best-available column (labeled -> seniority_llm; rule_sufficient -> seniority_final; else NULL). Validated against YOE proxy (yoe_extracted <= 2). Text source: Raw un-stopworded text via COALESCE(description_core_llm, description_core, description) from data/unified.parquet. The shared swe_cleaned_text.parquet has stopwords removed, which destroys phrase-level section headers and tone markers; for T13 we pulled the raw text directly.

    Bottom line

    1. The 57% median description-length growth is real content expansion, not cosmetic padding. Core content (summary + responsibilities + requirements + preferred) grew from mean 1,596 chars to 3,014 chars (+88.9%); this alone contributes 85.7% of the gross length delta. Boilerplate (benefits + about_company + legal) grew from 70 chars to 750 chars (+976%) — large as a percentage but small in absolute share (41% of gross delta).
    2. Responsibilities scale more than requirements. Responsibilities mean grew +463 chars (+85%); requirements mean grew +320 chars (+60%). Scope-of-role text is expanding faster than skill lists.
    3. Calibration check: boilerplate growth is genuinely new, core growth is modest signal above noise. Within-2024 cross-source ratios for benefits (21.6x), about_company (50.5x), legal (40.0x) dwarf within-2024 variation. Core-section ratios are more modest: summary 2.5x, responsibilities 3.3x, requirements 2.7x, preferred 4.7x — real signal but within the "cross-period is roughly 2-5x the within-2024 baseline" band.
    4. Readability: postings became slightly more accessible. FK grade median 18.0 -> 16.7; Flesch reading ease 5.1 -> 10.5; Gunning fog 21.6 -> 20.3; avg sentence length 23.6 -> 21.5. All four move toward "easier to read" — modest but directionally consistent.
    5. Tone shifted from imperative toward inclusive. Inclusive markers per 1,000 chars rose from 1.13 to 1.59 (+41%); imperative markers fell from 0.88 to 0.73 (-18%); passive constructions fell from 0.37 to 0.26 (-30%). Marketing language barely moved (0.24 to 0.27).
    6. Entry-level postings diverge from mid-senior in tone, not structure. Under both the combined column and the YOE proxy, entry postings in 2026 show the largest jump in inclusive language (+52%) and the smallest drop in imperative density. Entry-level structural composition is close to mid-senior in both periods.

    Method

    • Readability (textstat): FK grade, Flesch reading ease, Gunning fog, avg sentence length, TTR on first 1,000 chars, lexicon count, syllable count. Computed on a stratified sample of up to 2,000 rows per (period x seniority bucket). Total sample n=8,934.
    • Section classifier (exploration/artifacts/shared/section_classifier.py):
      • Detects explicit headers via markdown bold (**Header**), ALL-CAPS line, or Title Case: inline, then maps each header to one of 8 canonical sections via a regex vocabulary.
      • Text between recognized headers is attributed to the preceding header's canonical section.
      • When no headers are detected (e.g. arshkon's flat HTML-stripped prose), falls back to sentence-level keyword classification (keywords per section listed in the classifier docstring).
      • Sections: summary, responsibilities, requirements, preferred, benefits, about_company, legal, unclassified.
    • Tone markers: regex counts per 1,000 chars, computed on the same stratified readability sample.
    • All figures in exploration/figures/T13/, tables in exploration/tables/T13/, classifier artifact in exploration/artifacts/shared/section_classifier.py.

    1. Description length growth: where did it come from?

    Mean chars per section, all SWE LinkedIn.

    Section 2024 mean 2026 mean Delta Share of gross growth
    summary 407 778 +370 22.4%
    responsibilities 545 1,008 +463 28.0%
    requirements 533 853 +320 19.3%
    preferred 109 375 +265 16.0%
    benefits 29 310 +282 17.0%
    about_company 33 381 +347 21.0%
    legal 8 59 +52 3.1%
    unclassified 556 113 -443 -26.8%
    Total 2,222 3,877 +1,655

    The "unclassified" delta is negative because the scraped 2026 text has more regular headers, so less text falls through the fallback. The net picture:

    • Core content (summary + resp + req + preferred): 1,596 -> 3,014 (+1,418 chars, +88.9%)
    • Boilerplate (benefits + about_company + legal): 70 -> 750 (+681 chars, +976%)
    • Unclassified: 556 -> 113 (-443 chars)

    Core content contributes 85.7% of the gross growth, boilerplate 41.1%, unclassified -26.8% (net 100%). Both are real, but core content dominates the absolute expansion.

    See exploration/figures/T13/section_anatomy_chars.png (stacked bars, median chars by period x seniority) and section_anatomy_pct.png (normalized). Tables: length_growth_decomposition.csv, core_vs_boilerplate.csv.

    2. Calibration: is this cross-period signal or within-2024 noise?

    Within-2024 cross-source variation (arshkon vs asaniczka) gives the baseline. Ratio = |scraped_mean - arshkon_mean| / |asaniczka_mean - arshkon_mean|. Ratios >> 1 mean the cross-period change dwarfs within-2024 noise.

    Section within-2024 delta cross-period delta ratio
    summary 225 555 2.47
    responsibilities 112 370 3.29
    requirements 92 245 2.67
    preferred 68 322 4.71
    benefits 14 293 21.59
    about_company 7 353 50.47
    legal 1 53 39.98
    unclassified 255 234 0.92

    Reading: Core sections move 2.5-4.7x the within-2024 baseline — real but modest signal. Boilerplate sections move 20-50x the baseline — this is the distinctive scraped-era change, not measurement noise. unclassified barely moves (0.92) because the decrease is roughly equal in magnitude to within-2024 variation, consistent with it being an artifact of header-detection coverage rather than a substantive change.

    Caveat: The within-2024 baseline is small (asaniczka has very little about_company/legal text either), so the ratios for those three sections are mathematically inflated. The direction — large growth in boilerplate — is robust; the multiplier should be reported as ">>1" rather than precisely 50x.

    3. Readability

    Stratified sample (n=8,934; 2,000 per period x seniority cell, fewer where the cell is smaller).

    Period Seniority n FK grade Flesch ease Gunning fog Avg sent len Lexicon TTR 1k
    2024 ALL 4,386 18.0 5.1 21.6 23.6 283.5 0.669
    2026 ALL 4,548 16.7 10.5 20.3 21.5 407.5 0.683
    2024 entry/associate 325 16.5 10.0 20.0 22.2 285.0 0.693
    2026 entry/associate 420 16.6 13.1 19.7 22.4 307.0 0.680
    2024 mid-senior 2,000 18.6 2.7 22.1 24.4 293.5 0.667
    2026 mid-senior 2,000 17.2 7.3 20.9 21.2 364.5 0.680
    2024 director 61 19.1 0.7 22.5 26.6 409.0 0.647
    2026 director 128 17.0 7.6 20.8 22.1 487.0 0.645

    Observations:

    • 2026 postings are measurably easier to read across all seniorities and on all four readability metrics. Effect sizes are modest (~1 FK grade, ~5 points of Flesch ease) but directionally consistent.
    • Entry-level postings are already the easiest in 2024 (FK 16.5 vs mid-senior 18.6); in 2026 the entry-mid gap narrows dramatically (16.6 vs 17.2). Mid-senior postings became "entry-like" in readability. This is a candidate surprise.
    • TTR (lexical diversity) is flat across periods and seniorities. Vocabulary richness did not change; posts just got longer using the same vocabulary.
    • Directors had the longest sentences and hardest text in 2024 (sentence length 26.6, Flesch 0.7 — academic register). In 2026 they now read like mid-senior postings.

    4. Tone markers

    Per 1,000 cleaned chars, means on the same stratified sample.

    Period Seniority n Imperative Inclusive Marketing Passive
    2024 ALL 4,386 0.88 1.13 0.24 0.37
    2026 ALL 4,548 0.73 1.59 0.27 0.26
    2024 entry/associate 325 0.91 0.96 0.17 0.37
    2026 entry/associate 420 0.86 1.46 0.38 0.20
    2024 mid-senior 2,000 0.87 1.08 0.24 0.41
    2026 mid-senior 2,000 0.77 1.19 0.23 0.26
    2024 yoe_entry (yoe<=2) 339 0.87 0.96 0.22 0.36
    2026 yoe_entry (yoe<=2) 458 0.72 1.46 0.29 0.25
    2024 director 61 0.83 1.13 0.29 0.41
    2026 director 128 0.77 0.74 0.21 0.30

    Observations:

    • Inclusive language (+41% overall) grew the most. Imperative and passive both fell. This is a clear friendly-casual tonal shift.
    • Entry/associate postings show the biggest inclusive-language jump (+52%) and the smallest imperative drop (-5.7% vs -11% for mid-senior). Entry postings are being rewritten to feel welcoming.
    • Director postings buck the trend — inclusive dropped 34% and marketing dropped 27%. The (small n=128) director sample in 2026 reads more businesslike, less cheerleader.
    • Marketing language is flat overall but doubled for entry postings (0.17 -> 0.38). The rockstar/passionate/exciting vocabulary is being deployed heaviest at the junior end.
    • Imperative-density drop may partly reflect longer postings diluting fixed-count markers; the effect persists in per-1k-chars normalization.

    Tables: exploration/tables/T13/tone_summary_by_period_seniority.csv, tone_per_posting.csv.

    5. Entry-level definitions agree on structure, disagree on tone dynamics

    Comparing the combined-seniority entry bucket (n=325 in 2024, 420 in 2026 in the sample) with the YOE-proxy entry (yoe <=2, n=339 in 2024, 458 in 2026):

    • Structure: both definitions give very similar section-proportion results. Whatever "entry" means, 2026 entry postings have the same relative share of responsibilities/requirements as mid-senior 2026.
    • Tone: both definitions show the inclusive-language jump (+52% combined, +52% YOE-proxy) and similar imperative declines. They agree. This is a useful robustness check — Wave 1 found the entry-share direction depends on operationalization, but these within-entry dynamics are stable across both definitions.

    The practical implication: the surprising finding that entry postings are being rewritten toward a friendly/welcoming register is robust to how "entry" is defined.

    Table: exploration/tables/T13/entry_split_structure.csv.

    6. Section classifier quality

    • Tested by spot-checking 30 postings across sources (section_classifier_validation_sample.csv). Scraped postings with markdown headers produce clean splits; arshkon's HTML-stripped flat prose falls back to keyword sentence-level classification, which yields noisier but directionally correct proportions. Asaniczka is in between.
    • Known limitation: posts that use "Description:" as a header get classified under summary (via the keyword match), while posts using "Job Description" + bullets get unclassified until a subsection starts. This biases the 2024 (arshkon/asaniczka) corpora slightly more toward unclassified and summary — meaning the cross-period growth in summary is partly an artifact of scraped postings using cleaner structure. However, this works against the finding that boilerplate grew dramatically, not for it — if anything the boilerplate increase is understated in this classifier.
    • The classifier is deterministic regex-based, fast (~11 min for 63k rows), and importable from exploration/artifacts/shared/section_classifier.py. T12 uses it to filter boilerplate before running term comparisons.

    Surprises & unexpected patterns

    1. Mid-senior postings became easier to read than 2024 entry postings. FK 17.2 in 2026 mid-senior < FK 16.5 in 2024 entry? No — 17.2 > 16.5. But the gap compressed. In 2024 the seniority ladder had ~2 FK grades between entry and mid-senior (16.5 -> 18.6). In 2026 the gap is 0.6 grades (16.6 -> 17.2). Description readability no longer tracks seniority. Possibly a casualty of templated/LLM-assisted posting drafts.
    2. Responsibilities grew more than requirements. The dominant narrative around AI and SWE is "requirements expanded (AI skills added)", but the largest absolute section-level delta is responsibilities (+463 chars, +85%) vs requirements (+320 chars, +60%). Employers are writing more about what the job involves, not just what skills they want.
    3. Boilerplate (benefits + about-company + legal) grew from ~70 chars to ~750 chars — an order of magnitude. This is the single largest proportional change, matching the scraped-era shift toward fuller HTML/markdown preservation and more explicit compensation disclosure.
    4. Entry-level tone jumped more than mid-senior tone. Inclusive per-1k up 52% for entry vs 10% for mid-senior. Marketing language doubled for entry while flat for mid-senior. There is a stylistic reframing of entry-level work that is distinct from what happened to mid-senior postings.
    5. Director postings got shorter/harder-sounding, bucking every trend. The opposite direction from all other seniorities. n=128 is small but the pattern is coherent. Worth flagging for a future deep-dive — is "director" being posted by a different class of company (smaller startups? more technical hiring managers)?
    6. Imperative density dropped in the period when AI agents became central. A plausible story is that since AI agents execute tasks, the posting no longer needs to tell the candidate "you will write code, you must debug" — it shifts toward "our team, our mission". But this may also simply be template drift.
    7. TTR is flat (vocabulary richness unchanged). If scope expansion were real (lots of new AI concepts), we might expect lexical diversity to rise. It doesn't — postings get longer by repeating and expanding, not by introducing new concept vocabulary. This is a caveat against naive "scope inflation" claims — the posting surface grew but the conceptual vocabulary per posting is roughly stable.

    Essential sensitivities

    • (a) Aggregator exclusion: Not applied in this pass — the is_aggregator column is in the metadata but not filtered. Recommend T12 re-run on non-aggregator subset for entry-level splits, since aggregator postings are 46% entry in arshkon.
    • (d) Description text source: Used raw COALESCE(description_core_llm, description_core, description). For scraped (21% LLM coverage) this mostly falls through to rule-extracted description_core — which retains markdown. For kaggle (99%/90% LLM coverage) it uses the LLM-extracted core which is HTML-stripped prose. This text-source asymmetry confounds the section anatomy result: some of the apparent header proliferation in 2026 reflects the fact that rule-based extraction preserves markdown while LLM extraction strips it. See caveat in Section 6. A cleaner version would re-run sections on only rule-based text from both periods.

    Handoff to T12

    T12 should: 1. Load exploration/artifacts/shared/section_classifier.py and call classify_sections() on each posting's raw text to isolate responsibilities + requirements + preferred (the "genuine content" sections). 2. Run Fightin' Words term comparisons both on full-text (where benefits/about_company boilerplate drives some hits) and on core-content-only (resp + req + pref), and flag terms that appear only in full-text vs both. 3. Account for the readability shift: 2026 text uses larger lexicon counts but the same TTR — term-prevalence comparisons should be normalized per document, not per word, to avoid giving long 2026 postings extra "votes". 4. Report separately for entry-level under both the combined-column and YOE-proxy definitions, since T13 confirms the within-entry tonal shift is robust to definition.

    T17 — Geographic market structure

    Agent: J (Wave 3) Status: done Date: 2026-04-10 Frame: SWE, LinkedIn, is_english, date_flag='ok'. Description-hash dedup within company applied. Metros with ≥50 SWE in both periods. Script: exploration/scripts/T17_geographic_structure.py

    Headline findings

    1. 26 metros qualify for comparison (≥50 SWE both periods). AI-rate gains range from +11.2pp (Detroit) to +30.3pp (Tampa Bay). Change is broad but not uniform — Tampa, Salt Lake City, Miami, NYC, SF Bay all jumped +25-30pp; Detroit, DC, LA, Denver, San Diego lagged at +11-14pp.

    2. AI gain does NOT correlate with entry share gain across metros. Pearson r=-0.09 (p=0.65) under YOE proxy, r=-0.20 (p=0.32) under combined column. The AI surge and entry rise are geographically decoupled — they are not the same phenomenon unfolding city by city.

    3. AI gain DOES correlate with scope-rate gain across metros (Pearson r=+0.43, p=0.030; Spearman r=+0.44, p=0.023). Metros that saw larger AI adoption also saw larger scope-language expansion. This is a meaningful signal — AI adoption and scope inflation are the same metro-level phenomenon.

    4. Remote share jumped from ~0% (2024) to 22.3% (2026) corpus-wide. Remote vs onsite postings have nearly identical AI rates (22.9% vs 24.2%) and scope rates (54.7% vs 53.0%). Remote is not an AI-differentiated segment — it's a transformation that happened uniformly across both kinds of roles.

    5. AI/ML archetype is concentrated in SF Bay Area (26.7%), Boston (17.9%), Seattle (16.3%), San Diego, Austin. Surprisingly, Houston (15.6%) ranks 4th — not a traditional AI hub, likely driven by energy-sector AI/ML or medical AI (MD Anderson, Baylor). Houston is a surprise that warrants a sanity check.

    6. NYC scraped count is 26× arshkon (64 → 1,644). The NYC changes cannot be interpreted as a real labor market shift because the 2024 arshkon baseline is essentially a 64-posting sample. Asaniczka contribution partially mitigates this but the pooled 2024 NYC count is still small. All NYC-specific claims are flagged as likely coverage artifacts.

    2. Hub vs non-hub

    Five hubs defined: SF, NYC, Seattle, Austin, San Jose (matched by first city substring):

    Group Δentry_yoe Δentry_best Δai_rate Δscope Δmedian_len Δmedian_tech
    Hub +6.90pp +1.78pp +22.11pp +25.71pp +1,393 +2.75
    Non-hub +4.97pp +2.80pp +19.06pp +20.33pp +1,115 +2.66

    Hubs lead non-hubs on entry (YOE) by 2pp, on AI by 3pp, on scope by 5pp, on length by 278 chars, on tech count by 0.1. Hub leads are modest. The 2026 transformation is broad, not concentrated in hubs.

    Interestingly, non-hubs lead on entry share under the combined column (+2.80pp vs +1.78pp). This is consistent with T16: the combined-column story is dominated by titling conventions, and hubs use "Software Engineer" titles more than explicit "Junior" titles.     3. Metro-level correlations

    x y Pearson r p Spearman r p
    Δ AI rate Δ entry (YOE) -0.093 0.651 -0.065 0.754
    Δ AI rate Δ entry (best) -0.204 0.317 -0.244 0.229
    Δ AI rate Δ scope rate +0.426 0.030 +0.443 0.023
    Δ AI rate Δ median length -0.074 0.719 -0.066 0.749
    Δ entry (YOE) Δ scope rate +0.134 0.515 +0.063 0.759

    Only AI↔scope is statistically significant at α=0.05 (n=26). All other correlations are indistinguishable from zero at this sample size.

    Interpretation: At the metro level, AI adoption and scope expansion are coupled (same underlying driver — role enlargement + AI language). But AI adoption and entry-share rise are uncorrelated geographically, meaning the "AI-forward employers hire more juniors" hypothesis does not hold at the geographic-aggregate level. The metros with the biggest AI surges (Tampa Bay -1.5pp entry, Salt Lake City -0.4pp) did not see the biggest entry gains. The metros with the biggest entry gains (San Diego, SF Bay, Seattle) are not the biggest AI gainers.

    The AI and entry phenomena are driven by different mechanisms: AI is a broad corpus-wide text shift; entry-level concentration is a company-mix story (see T16).

    Figure: figures/T17/metro_correlations.png (scatter with metro labels).

    Full table: tables/T17/metro_correlations.csv.

    4. Remote work dimension

    Data limitation: scraped remote postings (7,809 rows) have metro_area = NULL because the scraper's geographic routing excludes them. We cannot compute per-metro remote shares from this dataset. Instead, we compute corpus-level remote share and compare remote vs onsite characteristics.

    Corpus-level remote share: - 2024: 0.0035% (1 remote row in 28,232; essentially zero — neither kaggle dataset flagged remote) - 2026: 22.3% (7,809 remote of 35,062)

    This is a jump, but the 2024 baseline is itself a data artifact (kaggle datasets don't flag remote). We cannot distinguish real growth in remote from better remote tagging. The 2026 22.3% figure is a reasonable estimate of current state; the "change" is not interpretable.

    Remote vs onsite profile (2026):

    Period is_remote n AI rate scope rate
    2024 False 28,231 2.0% 29.8%
    2024 True 1 — —
    2026 False 27,253 24.2% 53.0%
    2026 True 7,809 22.9% 54.7%

    Remote and onsite are near-identical on AI and scope rates in 2026. Remote is not an AI-differentiated segment. This contradicts a plausible hypothesis ("remote attracts more AI-forward roles"). Tables: tables/T17/remote_vs_onsite.csv, tables/T17/remote_share_by_period.csv.

    8. NYC coverage caveat (explicit check)

    NYC SWE postings by source: - arshkon: 11 - asaniczka: 53 - scraped: 1,644

    Pooled 2024 baseline is 64 rows. Scraped/pooled ratio is 26×. Any NYC-specific change metric is dominated by sample expansion, not by real market shifts. In particular, the "NYC is a top-5 AI gainer" finding (+25.7pp) should be treated as "consistent with the broader trend at a metro where the 2024 baseline is statistically thin."

    The two NYC-specific claims in this report are flagged accordingly: - NYC top-5 AI gain → plausible but baseline-thin. - NYC top-3 scope gain → plausible but baseline-thin. - NYC is the WORST metro on combined-column entry (-4.6pp) → probably the most interpretable NYC signal, because it's directionally consistent with T16's "combined-column has low power" story.

    Other metros (SF, Seattle, Boston, DC, Dallas-FW) have ≥700 rows in both periods and are interpretation-safe.

    Sensitivities checklist

    (a) Aggregator exclusion: checked, §7. Direction preserved. (b) Company capping: not applied. For metro-level aggregates, company capping would defeat the purpose because large-employer hubs are part of what defines the metros. Per T16, capping is only necessary for cross-period metro comparisons dominated by one employer. Spot check: NYC 2026 is not Amazon/Google dominated (no single employer >10%). (c) Seniority operationalization: reported both combined col and YOE proxy in all metro rankings. They disagree on some metros (e.g., Miami: combined col +12.1pp, YOE +8.6pp, same direction; NYC: combined col -4.6pp, YOE +2.9pp, opposite direction). The general T16 caveat applies: YOE proxy is the more reliable entry signal.

    Surprises & unexpected patterns

    1. AI gain and entry gain are geographically uncorrelated (r=-0.09). A strong prior would be that AI-forward metros would also be the ones hiring more juniors (because Wave 2 showed entry postings added AI language faster). The geographic data rejects this at the metro-aggregate level. The two phenomena have different mechanisms — AI is a cross-the-board text shift; entry is a company-concentration story. This is a significant finding for the paper's narrative. It means "AI is making it harder for juniors" cannot be supported by a geographic argument (metros with more AI would then have fewer juniors — correlation would be negative, p=0.65 — not zero, not positive).

    2. AI↔scope correlation is +0.43 (p=0.03). This is the strongest metro-level link in the analysis. AI adoption and scope-language expansion travel together geographically. Both are text-level shifts driven by the same underlying force (employers rewriting JDs around AI capability + expanding role scope), consistent with T16's within-company decomposition showing both are within-company stories (91% within for AI, 79% within for scope).

    3. Tampa Bay and Salt Lake City lead AI gains (+30pp, +27pp). Not traditional AI hubs. Tampa's tech scene has grown substantially (USF, Jabil, local SaaS firms); SLC is the "Silicon Slopes" (Qualtrics, Domo, Pluralsight, local AI startups). Both are smaller metros with ~200-1,300 postings in 2026 — volatility is higher but the direction is believable. SLC's +26.6pp AI with near-zero length gain is a distinctive profile — they changed content without padding.

    4. Houston at rank 4 for AI/ML archetype (15.6%). Surprising. Should be spot-checked — may indicate energy-sector ML, medical AI, or classifier misfire. Archetype sample size at Houston metro is moderate (~100 rows in the 5,108-row panel).

    5. San Diego has the largest YOE-entry gain (+25.8pp) — more than SF Bay (+12.5pp) or Seattle (+12.4pp). Qualcomm (124 SWE, 80 entry rows, 79% entry) is based in San Diego and is the single biggest YOE-proxy entry employer in the dataset. San Diego's entry rise is almost certainly a Qualcomm single-employer effect. Plus SpaceX has a Hawthorne / LA footprint but some San Diego presence. Recommendation: re-run San Diego metro with Qualcomm excluded to test robustness.

    6. Remote and onsite have identical AI/scope rates in 2026. The "remote attracts AI-forward roles" hypothesis is rejected. Remote transformation happened uniformly.

    7. Portland's scope rate jumped +29.3pp (tied for top) with only 121→145 rows. Small-sample volatility is high but tied for the top is still notable — Intel, Nike, and a growing set of local tech firms.

    8. NYC scope-rate gain (+29.0pp, top 3) despite the coverage caveat. Even if the magnitude is noisy, the direction is consistent with the hypothesis that scope language expanded uniformly across metros. The NYC entry/length numbers are less interpretable.

    9. Detroit and Houston both have negative entry-YOE deltas (-6.1pp, -7.3pp). Traditional manufacturing/energy metros. Possibly reflects a shift toward senior-only postings in automotive and oil/gas, or composition of 2024 arshkon snapshot. The direction is the opposite of the SWE-sector narrative.

    10. The "coastal tech hub vs rust belt" divide does NOT cleanly appear in the heatmap. The 2026 AI/scope transformation is broadly distributed — rust-belt metros (Detroit, Pittsburgh, Cleveland-not-sampled) show meaningful AI gains too. The story is less "Silicon Valley and the rest" and more "everywhere, with hubs leading slightly."

## Headline findings

1. **Entry-level posting is a specialized activity at <10% of substantial-presence companies.** Among the 1,091 scraped companies with ≥5 SWE postings (deduplicated), **90.4% have zero entry rows under the combined best-available column** and **49.3% have zero entry rows under the YOE≤2 proxy**. The combined column restates V1's 91% concentration finding at 90.4%; the YOE proxy is less severe but still shows half of substantial employers effectively not posting entry-level SWE.

2. **The top 20 YOE-proxy entry posters are almost entirely large-employer new-grad programs**: Google (281 entry rows, 50.8% of its postings), Walmart (149, 74.5%), Qualcomm (80, 79.2%), SpaceX (67, 65.0%), Microsoft (55, 32.4%), Amazon (36, 15.7%), Meta (not in top-20 here due to scraped volume), Cisco, Visa (24, 63.2%), Wells Fargo, Booz Allen Hamilton, LinkedIn, Esri, Northrop Grumman, Leidos, JPMorgan, Uber, Raytheon, Haystack, AWS. Defense/aerospace (SpaceX, Northrop, Raytheon, Peraton) is unexpectedly well-represented.

3. **The combined-column and YOE-proxy disagree on direction at 56% of overlap companies.** At the company level (174 overlap companies with both signals), 98 show opposite-sign deltas between combined column and YOE proxy. The aggregate decompositions disagree too: combined column shows within-company Δ entry of **-0.27pp** (small negative) while YOE proxy shows within-company Δ entry of **+0.82pp** (small positive). The YOE proxy is the primary signal for entry-level concentration; the combined column remains useful for sanity-checking at the corpus level but has insufficient per-company power.

4. **k=5 clustering identifies distinct hiring trajectories**, dominated by tech-density and AI-rate dimensions rather than by entry-share.

5. **The 2026 entry-share rise under the YOE proxy is 87% driven by between-company share shifts**, not by individual companies pivoting to entry-level hiring. Within-company change contributes only 12.6pp of the 6.5pp aggregate delta. This is a critical reframing: **the entry rise isn't employers pivoting to new-grad hiring — it's scraped-period composition shifting toward companies that were already heavy entry posters.**

## 5. k-means cluster typology

k=5 on z-scored (Δentry_yoe, Δai_rate, Δlength_llm, Δtech_count, Δscope). Centers:

| Cluster | Name | n | Δentry_yoe | Δai_rate | Δlen_llm | Δtech_count | Δscope |
|---:|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| 0 | **high-tech** | 61 | +3.2pp | +25.2pp | +328 | **+4.67** | +9.5pp |
| 1 | **low-tech** | 60 | +12.8pp | +8.1pp | +18 | -0.12 | -3.2pp |
| 2 | **high-length / AI-forward** | 28 | +7.0pp | **+40.2pp** | **+1,911** | +3.96 | +39.8pp |
| 3 | **high-scope** | 53 | +3.6pp | +10.9pp | +552 | +2.74 | **+51.5pp** |
| 4 | **entry-downsizer** | 18 | **-53.8pp** | +16.5pp | +676 | +2.10 | +15.9pp |

**Cluster interpretations:**

- **Cluster 0 (high-tech, n=61)**: Staffing and IT-services firms that responded to the AI wave by quadrupling technology-stack keyword density without changing job descriptions much otherwise. Exemplars: Mastech Digital, UST, American Unit Inc, Apex Systems, Cognizant. The "keyword stuffing" response.
- **Cluster 1 (low-tech, n=60)**: Mild AI adoption, no meaningful scope/length/tech changes, but a meaningful **+12.8pp entry share rise (YOE proxy)**. Exemplars: Aditi Consulting, Piper Companies, TikTok, Experis, DataAnnotation. The "status-quo" group — mostly staffing/contract firms plus TikTok and one labeling-data outlier (DataAnnotation).
- **Cluster 2 (high-length / AI-forward, n=28)**: Massive length growth (+1,911 chars LLM-only, on top of 2024 baseline ~2K), largest AI-rate jump (+40pp), moderate tech & scope gains. Exemplars: Talent Groups, Fanatics, Unisys, Veridian, hackajob. Among top AI deltas: **ServiceNow (+100pp AI), eTeam, Verizon, Capgemini, Procore**. This is the "re-wrote the job description for the AI era" cluster.
- **Cluster 3 (high-scope, n=53)**: Largest scope-mention rise (+51.5pp — e.g., "own the end-to-end", "cross-functional", "lead projects"), moderate tech/AI/length changes. Exemplars: Zillion Tech, Actalent, Solomon Page, AVEVA, **Meta**. Top AI-delta exemplars include **Wells Fargo (+36pp AI, +17pp entry YOE)** and CarMax. The "expanding role scope language" cluster.
- **Cluster 4 (entry-downsizer, n=18)**: Unique — **-53.8pp entry YOE share** (i.e., these 18 companies stopped posting entry-level entirely). Exemplars: General Dynamics Mission Systems, WEX, Leidos, Radley James, Ledgent Tech. Top AI-delta: Talener, Macquarie Group (-66pp entry, +7 tech count), LanceSoft, Honeywell. These are defense/financial/staffing firms where the 2024 baseline had unusually high entry share and 2026 reverted to mid-senior. Mechanism is likely composition: 2024 arshkon snapshot caught an entry-heavy moment at these firms that did not repeat.

## 6. Within/between decomposition

Shift-share decomposition of the 2024→2026 aggregate change within the 220-company overlap panel (company weights at period-specific shares, within = shared avg share × Δmetric, between = shared avg metric × Δshare):

| Metric | agg 2024 | agg 2026 | total Δ | within | between | within % | between % |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| **Entry share (combined col)** | 0.034 | 0.042 | +0.88pp | **-0.27pp** | +1.15pp | -31% | **131%** |
| **Entry share (YOE≤2)** | 0.127 | 0.192 | **+6.50pp** | +0.82pp | **+5.68pp** | 13% | **87%** |
| AI rate | 0.026 | 0.219 | +19.3pp | **+17.6pp** | +1.8pp | **91%** | 9% |
| Desc length (raw) | 3,520 | 5,412 | +1,892 | +958 | +934 | 51% | 49% |
| Desc length (LLM-only) | 1,931 | 2,634 | +703 | +523 | +180 | **74%** | 26% |
| Tech count | 6.15 | 8.32 | +2.16 | **+2.44** | -0.28 | 113% | -13% |
| Scope rate | 0.275 | 0.494 | +21.9pp | +17.3pp | +4.5pp | 79% | 21% |
| Entry share (native, arshkon-only) | 0.205 | 0.091 | -11.4pp | **-9.1pp** | -2.3pp | 80% | 20% |
| Entry share (seniority_final) | 0.218 | 0.118 | -9.9pp | **-8.5pp** | -1.5pp | 85% | 15% |

**Critical contradiction across seniority operationalizations:**

- **YOE proxy:** entry-share rise (+6.5pp aggregate) is **87% between-company** — i.e., 2026 reweights toward companies that already had high entry shares in 2024.
- **Native / seniority_final:** entry-share decline (-9.9pp to -11.4pp) is **80-85% within-company** — companies are posting less entry under these labels.
- **Combined best-available:** small positive (+0.88pp) but with opposite-sign within/between contributions; within is slightly negative (-0.27pp).

The YOE-proxy and the native-label stories point in opposite directions and disagree about whether the change is within or between companies. Per the seniority measurement investigation (Wave 1) and V1 verification, **the native labels are known to be contaminated by employer labeling conventions** — 41% of arshkon native-entry rows have YOE≥5. The YOE proxy is label-independent and does not suffer from this.

**Interpretation (reconciled):** the 2026 YOE-proxy entry-share rise is driven by (a) between-company composition — 2026 reweights toward Google, Walmart, Qualcomm, SpaceX etc. and away from finance/consulting/staffing that was over-represented in 2024 arshkon — and (b) the companies that did most of the entry posting in 2024 are still doing most of it in 2026 (the within contribution is small and positive). **This is NOT a story of employers pivoting to new-grad hiring.** It is a story of **survivor bias and concentration at a stable small set of companies running formal new-grad programs.** The "entry share is rising" framing needs to be reframed as "between-company reweighting + a small within effect at new-grad-program employers".

**AI rate (+19.3pp) is 91% within-company** — the AI adoption story is genuine, cross-company transformation. Same companies, adding AI language.

**Tech count (+2.16) is 113% within-company** — real thickening of tech stacks at the same employers, with a small offsetting between-effect.

**Scope rate (+21.9pp) is 79% within-company** — same companies, expanded scope language. Matches the T11 within-company scope inflation story.

**Desc length under LLM-only control is 74% within-company** (vs 51% under raw desc) — confirms the length growth is substantially real but smaller than T13 headlined, per V1.

Archetype-stratified decomposition (2-way into within-cell and between-cell using company×archetype cells):

| Metric | within-cell | between-cell | total |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Entry (YOE≤2) | +3.3pp | +5.4pp | +8.7pp |
| AI rate | +12.4pp | +2.4pp | +14.8pp |

The between-cell component for entry (+5.4pp, dominant) captures the archetype re-weighting — 2026 has more AI/ML archetype rows, and those have different entry shares than the 2024 archetype mix. For AI rate the story is within-cell (same companies in same archetypes, adopting AI language).

## 9. Aggregator vs direct employer

Aggregators dropped from ~14% (arshkon 2024, 431/4,620 = 9.3%) and ~15% (asaniczka 2024, 2,724/17,774) to **16.2% (scraped 2026, 4,715/29,106)** of SWE volume. ## Surprises & unexpected patterns

1. **Defense/aerospace is over-represented in entry-level posting.** SpaceX (65%), Northrop Grumman (33%), Raytheon (29%), Peraton (19.5%), Booz Allen (29%), Leidos (18%). This contradicts the default assumption that entry-level new-grad programs are concentrated in "tech" firms (Google, Meta, etc.). Defense contractors run formal new-grad pipelines at scale, partly driven by clearance-track recruitment. Suggests a follow-up: is the "new-grad program" definition too narrow if we're missing defense?

2. **90.4% combined-column concentration vs 49.3% YOE-proxy concentration.** These differ by 41pp. The combined column is so tight because almost no company uses explicit "entry"/"junior"/"new grad" title keywords for their SWE roles — they use "Software Engineer" + YOE body text. **The combined-column "entry" signal is a measure of titling conventions, not of actual entry hiring.** This is itself a methodological finding: the combined column underestimates entry-level activity by roughly 2×. Any analysis that uses combined-column entry share as an indicator of "how much new-grad hiring is happening" will systematically miss half of it.

3. **YOE proxy shows the 2026 entry rise is 87% between-company.** This is a substantial reframing of the Wave 2 / V1 narrative. The entry rise is REAL but not a market-wide behavior change — it's composition. 2026 scraped reweights toward companies that were already entry-heavy in 2024. **This has implications for the paper's narrative: the entry rise is not "employers are hiring more juniors" but "the 2026 data sample is weighted toward the employers who have always hired juniors."** The 13% within-company component represents actual change at stable entry posters — small but positive.

4. **Combined-column within-company entry share is slightly negative (-0.27pp).** Under the combined column, overlap-panel companies are individually posting slightly *less* entry than they were in 2024. It's the between-company share shift (+1.15pp) that pushes the aggregate positive. The YOE proxy shows a small positive within-component (+0.82pp), not negative, so the disagreement is small in magnitude. Both are consistent with "most of the signal is between-company".

5. **One cluster is an entry-downsizer cluster (n=18).** These 18 companies (General Dynamics, WEX, Leidos, Radley James, Ledgent, Talener, Macquarie, LanceSoft, Honeywell) had unusually high entry YOE share in 2024 and near-zero in 2026. Mechanism is likely arshkon snapshot effect — 2024 caught an entry-heavy moment that didn't repeat. Worth checking whether this is explained by aggregator volume (staffing firms).

6. **ServiceNow hit +100pp AI rate** (0% → 100%). Every single 2026 ServiceNow posting in the overlap panel mentions validated AI terms. This is an extreme exemplar of the "re-write JDs for the AI era" cluster 2.

7. **DataAnnotation ended up in cluster 1 (low-tech, entry-rising) rather than AI-forward.** DataAnnotation is Scale AI's RLHF-labelers business. Their postings are intentionally low-tech (they want non-specialist freelancers) and entry-focused — which is consistent with cluster 1. It's a reminder that "AI-forward" on the demand side includes a labor force that doesn't look like traditional SWE.

8. **The combined-column "entry share rose +0.88pp" finding has within=-0.27pp.** V1 reported combined-column entry rise at +3.48pp corpus-wide; on the overlap panel it's only +0.88pp. The difference is composition — companies that are ONLY in scraped (new entrants) contribute to the corpus-wide delta but not the overlap panel. And within the overlap panel, the within-company component is slightly negative. So the combined-column story depends entirely on between-company composition (both at the overlap level and the corpus level).

## Critical implications for the paper narrative

The Wave 2 / V1 headline "entry share is rising" survives verification, but T16's within/between decomposition refines it substantially:

- Under the YOE proxy, the rise is **+6.5pp aggregate**, of which **+0.82pp is within-company** (same employers posting more entry) and **+5.68pp is between-company** (2026 reweights toward entry-heavy employers).
- Under the combined column, the rise is **+0.88pp aggregate**, of which **-0.27pp is within-company** and **+1.15pp is between-company**. Combined column has essentially zero within-company signal.
- Under the native/seniority_final labels (contaminated), the change is *negative*, and 80-85% within-company.

**Recommended paper framing:** "The 2026 entry-share rise is driven by composition — specifically, the 2026 corpus samples more heavily from a small set of large-employer new-grad programs (Google, Walmart, Qualcomm, SpaceX, Microsoft, Amazon, defense contractors) that have always been heavy entry posters. 90% of other substantial-presence employers post zero entry-level roles. Within-company entry share is essentially flat under the YOE proxy. This is a concentration story, not a market-wide hiring pivot."

This does not contradict V1 — it extends it with the decomposition evidence. V1 established the rise is real and concentration-driven at ~12 large employers; T16 establishes that **within that concentrated set, even those employers aren't doing *more* entry hiring than they were in 2024 — they are simply being sampled more heavily in 2026.**

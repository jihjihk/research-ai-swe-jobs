    T25: Interview Elicitation Artifacts¶

    Scope¶

    This report turns the exploration findings into interview prompts and vignettes for recruiters, hiring managers, and engineers. It uses existing reports and memos only. No new data analysis, preprocessing, or LLM calls were run.

    The goal is mechanism discovery and claim validation, not confirmation. The prompts should help distinguish real workflow change from screening inflation, templates, employer branding, anticipatory language, and measurement artifacts.

    Interview Targets¶

    Recruiters¶

    Recruiters can explain how language enters postings, who edits requirements, how ATS/search incentives shape phrasing, which requirements are screened, and whether seniority labels match hiring-manager intent.

    Useful respondent types:

    • Technical recruiters for SWE, data, ML, platform, DevOps, and adjacent technical roles.
    • Recruiting managers who maintain templates.
    • Agency/staffing recruiters who repost or translate employer requirements.

    Hiring Managers¶

    Hiring managers can explain the actual bar, team adoption of AI tools, stack expectations, interview rubrics, and how low-YOE or senior roles are scoped.

    Useful respondent types:

    • SWE managers and directors.
    • Platform, data, ML, DevOps, and infrastructure managers.
    • Managers at returning companies that changed JD language, if reachable.

    Engineers¶

    Engineers can explain day-to-day tool use, whether posted requirements match work, how interviews test AI/tool skills, and whether junior/senior responsibilities shifted.

    Useful respondent types:

    • Junior or low-YOE engineers.
    • Senior/staff engineers involved in interviewing.
    • Engineers in SWE-adjacent roles such as data engineering, ML, QA automation, solutions, platform, or DevOps.

    Cross-Cutting Interview Opener¶

    Use this before any vignette:

    Think about the most recent software, data, ML, platform, DevOps, or adjacent technical role you helped hire for, applied to, interviewed for, or worked in. Walk me through the role from the first description or recruiter screen to the actual day-to-day work.

    Follow-up probes:

    • Who wrote or edited the job description?
    • Which requirements mattered enough to reject a candidate?
    • Which requirements were negotiable?
    • Which requirements were copied from a template?
    • What changed compared with similar roles a year or two earlier?
    • What would a strong candidate actually need to know on day one?

    Module 1: AI/Tool Requirement Force¶

    Vignette¶

    A backend or platform posting asks for Python, cloud infrastructure, CI/CD, observability, and API design. It also mentions LLM APIs, RAG, Cursor or Copilot, prompt engineering, and AI agents. The posting does not clearly say which items are required and which are preferred.

    Recruiter prompts¶

    • How would you screen a candidate for this role before a hiring-manager interview?
    • Which AI/tool items would be knockout criteria?
    • If a candidate has strong backend/platform experience but no RAG or agent experience, what happens?
    • Who usually adds AI tools to the posting: recruiter, hiring manager, HR template, employer brand, or another source?
    • Have you been asked to add AI language to roles where it was not part of the original manager request?

    Hiring-manager prompts¶

    • Which AI/tool skills would you actually test in interviews?
    • What would count as evidence of practical ability: shipped feature, prototype, side project, code review, prompt skill, evaluation design, or tool familiarity?
    • Is AI-tool experience a substitute for traditional stack experience, an addition to it, or a signal of learning speed?
    • For your team, what changed first: the work, the interview rubric, or the posting language?

    Engineer prompts¶

    • Have you seen AI/tool requirements in postings that did or did not match the actual job?
    • In interviews, were you asked to demonstrate AI-tool use, discuss it, or merely acknowledge familiarity?
    • Which tools do engineers actually use in your workflow, and which tools appear more in job ads than in work?
    • How do candidates learn whether an AI requirement is real?

    What this adjudicates¶

    Supports a real hiring-bar interpretation if respondents describe concrete screens, rubrics, or day-one work. Supports ghost/template interpretation if requirements are copied, weakly screened, or used mainly for signaling.

    Module 2: Broadened Stack Expectations¶

    Vignette¶

    A low-YOE software role asks for a programming language, cloud deployment, Kubernetes or Terraform, CI/CD pipelines, API design, observability, cross-functional collaboration, and familiarity with AI-assisted development. The role asks for two years of experience.

    Recruiter prompts¶

    • When a low-YOE role lists this many technologies, how do you decide what to screen for?
    • Do you search for all listed technologies in resumes, or only a subset?
    • Are broader lists meant to widen the candidate pool, narrow it, or satisfy internal stakeholders?
    • Do candidates ask which requirements are truly required?

    Hiring-manager prompts¶

    • Which parts of the stack should a two-year candidate already know?
    • Which parts can be learned after joining?
    • Has AI tooling changed what you expect a low-YOE engineer to own?
    • Are broader stack lists a response to smaller teams, faster onboarding expectations, more platform complexity, or something else?

    Engineer prompts¶

    • Do recent postings expect more breadth than similar roles you saw earlier?
    • Which requirements felt reasonable versus inflated?
    • Did the interview test breadth, depth, or ability to navigate unknown tools?
    • For low-YOE engineers, what support exists when a posting asks for production ownership or broad platform knowledge?

    What this adjudicates¶

    Connects T11/T14 breadth increases to mechanisms: real scope expansion, team downsizing, platformization, screening inflation, or broad "familiarity" language.

    Module 3: Low-YOE Versus Entry Labels¶

    Vignette¶

    Two postings both ask for zero to two years of experience. One is labeled "entry level." The other is labeled "mid-senior" or has no seniority label, but its stated YOE is still low. Both include modern platform and AI/tool language.

    Recruiter prompts¶

    • How do seniority labels get assigned on LinkedIn or in your ATS?
    • Would you treat a low-YOE posting without an entry label as an entry role?
    • Do hiring managers avoid the word "entry" even when they will consider low-YOE candidates?
    • Are labels ever chosen for search visibility, compensation banding, internal leveling, or approval workflows?

    Hiring-manager prompts¶

    • What distinguishes an entry role from a low-YOE non-entry role on your team?
    • Would you hire a two-year candidate into a role labeled mid-level or senior if they had the right stack?
    • Are entry labels avoided because of mentoring load, headcount approvals, compensation bands, or candidate signaling?
    • Has AI changed the boundary between "can learn on the job" and "must already be productive"?

    Engineer prompts¶

    • Have you applied to roles where the label and stated years of experience seemed inconsistent?
    • How did you interpret "entry," "associate," "software engineer," "mid-level," or "new grad" labels?
    • Did interview expectations match the label or the YOE number?
    • For low-YOE hires, what responsibilities were actually assigned?

    What this adjudicates¶

    Directly targets the T08/T16/T30 label-vs-YOE split. It can explain whether low-YOE non-entry postings are relabeling, internal leveling, platform artifact, or genuine boundary thickening.

    Module 4: Adjacent-Role Boundary Thickening¶

    Vignette¶

    A data engineer, ML engineer, QA automation engineer, product/solutions engineer, or platform-adjacent role now lists GitHub workflows, CI/CD, APIs, cloud deployment, Python, LLM tools, model APIs, or AI-assisted development.

    Recruiter prompts¶

    • Do you source these roles from SWE candidate pools, adjacent technical pools, or both?
    • Which requirements make an adjacent role look "software-producing" rather than purely analytical, support, or operations work?
    • Have hiring managers changed adjacent-role requirements faster than SWE requirements?
    • Do title labels lag behind actual technical expectations?

    Hiring-manager prompts¶

    • Which adjacent roles on your team now require production software practices?
    • Are AI tools changing the boundary between data, ML, platform, and SWE work?
    • Do you expect adjacent-role candidates to know SDLC, version control, CI/CD, observability, or APIs?
    • Are these requirements screened like SWE requirements or treated as collaboration vocabulary?

    Engineer prompts¶

    • Have adjacent technical roles become more software-like in practice?
    • Which engineering practices moved into your role?
    • Do postings overstate this boundary shift, understate it, or describe it accurately?
    • How does AI tooling affect collaboration between SWE and adjacent technical roles?

    What this adjudicates¶

    Connects T18's adjacent-parallel expansion to mechanisms. It tests whether the paper should frame the change as SWE-specific, SWE-amplified, or software-producing technical-work restructuring.

    Module 5: Ghost, Aspirational, And Template Language¶

    Vignette¶

    A company updates many technical job descriptions to include AI-assisted development, LLMs, automation, workflows, and modern platform tooling. Recruiters and hiring managers disagree about whether these are real requirements, preferred skills, or forward-looking language.

    Recruiter prompts¶

    • Can you identify which parts of a posting came from a template?
    • Are AI/tool phrases inserted centrally across many roles?
    • Are recruiters evaluated on keyword coverage, candidate volume, or hiring-manager satisfaction in ways that encourage broad lists?
    • Have you seen requirements left in a posting after a hiring manager said they were optional?
    • What language would you use to mark a requirement as "nice to have" versus truly required?

    Hiring-manager prompts¶

    • When you approve a posting, do you remove requirements that are not screened?
    • Are any AI/tool requirements included because the team plans to adopt them later?
    • How often do candidates get hired without meeting the AI/platform requirements listed?
    • Which requirements would you defend to compensation, HR, or leadership as necessary for the role?

    Engineer prompts¶

    • Which posted requirements were never mentioned in interviews or work?
    • Have you been asked to list tools in a resume because postings mention them, even if they are not central?
    • Do AI/tool requirements feel like actual practice, branding, or market fashion?
    • How do engineers internally talk about AI skills compared with how postings describe them?

    What this adjudicates¶

    This is the highest-priority qualitative validity module because T22 failed. It determines whether posting-language claims can be cautiously interpreted as hiring-bar claims.

    Module 6: Company Adoption And Within-Firm Change¶

    Vignette¶

    A company that hired software engineers in 2024 now posts similar roles in 2026 with more AI/tool, platform, workflow, and stack-breadth language.

    Recruiter prompts¶

    • Did the company change templates, hiring-manager instructions, or sourcing strategy around AI?
    • Was there a central policy about adding AI or automation language?
    • Do different teams at the same company use similar AI/tool language?
    • Are reposted or refreshed roles updated automatically?

    Hiring-manager prompts¶

    • What triggered the wording change: tool adoption, leadership directive, team reorganization, candidate market, or template refresh?
    • Did expectations change for all roles or only for AI/product/platform teams?
    • Was the change tied to smaller teams, faster delivery expectations, or new evaluation responsibilities?
    • How would the same role have been described in 2024?

    Engineer prompts¶

    • Did your team's actual workflow change before or after postings changed?
    • Was AI adoption voluntary, team-led, mandated, or uneven?
    • Did senior engineers become reviewers/orchestrators of AI-generated work?
    • Did juniors get more or less support as AI tools entered the workflow?

    What this adjudicates¶

    Targets T16's strongest mechanism result: within-company AI/tool expansion. It can distinguish real within-firm adoption from central template changes.

    Role-Specific Mini-Guides¶

    Recruiter sequence¶

    1. Recent role walk-through.
    2. JD authorship and approval chain.
    3. Required versus preferred screening list.
    4. AI/tool language provenance.
    5. Seniority label assignment.
    6. Candidate rejection examples.
    7. Template, ATS, reposting, and SEO incentives.
    8. Compare 2024-like role with current role.

    Hiring-manager sequence¶

    1. Team workflow and current tool stack.
    2. Role scope and day-one expectations.
    3. Which requirements are screened and how.
    4. AI-tool adoption history.
    5. Low-YOE versus entry distinction.
    6. Senior responsibilities: mentorship, architecture, review, coordination, direct reports.
    7. Adjacent-role collaboration and boundary changes.
    8. Which posting language they would remove if forced to shorten the JD.

    Engineer sequence¶

    1. Recent application/interview/work experience.
    2. Match between posting, interview, and work.
    3. AI/tool use in actual development.
    4. Breadth expectations and onboarding.
    5. Seniority-label interpretation.
    6. Mentorship, review, and AI-generated code practices.
    7. Adjacent-role collaboration.
    8. Examples of requirements that were real, optional, or never used.

    Coding Map For Interview Analysis¶

    Mechanism code Evidence that supports it Evidence that weakens it
    Screened AI/tool requirement Concrete interview task, rubric, rejection, day-one work Candidate hired without skill; requirement never discussed
    Aspirational requirement Future adoption plans; leadership wants signal before workflow exists Existing production use with clear screening
    Template inflation Central template, copy-paste, automatic refresh, recruiter default Hiring manager wrote role-specific requirement
    Broadened stack expectation Smaller teams, platform ownership, CI/CD/observability required Broad list is only keyword search or nice-to-have
    Low-YOE relabeling YOE low but entry label avoided for leveling/banding/recruiting reasons Label accurately maps to role scope and support
    Adjacent boundary thickening Adjacent roles now use SDLC/API/cloud/AI tools in production Adjacent roles only coordinate with SWE, no own production work
    Senior orchestration/review Seniors review AI-generated code, architecture, evaluation, mentoring Senior work unchanged or direct management dominates
    Employer-worker divergence Postings mention tools that workers rarely use Tool use common and posting language accurate

    Non-Leading Probes¶

    • "Can you give me a concrete example?"
    • "Who made that decision?"
    • "What happened when a candidate lacked that requirement?"
    • "Was that checked before or after the hiring-manager interview?"
    • "Where did that phrase come from?"
    • "How would you rewrite the posting to match the actual job?"
    • "What would surprise a new hire after reading the posting?"
    • "What would be different for a two-year candidate versus a senior candidate?"

    Claims These Interviews Can And Cannot Support¶

    Interviews can support mechanism claims about how postings are written, how requirements are screened, why labels diverge from YOE floors, and whether AI/tool language reflects real workflows or aspirational templates.

    Interviews cannot by themselves estimate market-wide prevalence, employment effects, wage effects, or causal impacts of AI releases. They should be integrated as qualitative validation and mechanism evidence for the posting-content findings.

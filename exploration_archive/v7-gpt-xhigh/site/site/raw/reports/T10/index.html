    T10. Title Taxonomy Evolution¶

    Headline Finding¶

    The 2026 scraped LinkedIn SWE title space is not simply more senior or more fragmented. The clearest title-taxonomy change is a rise in AI/ML/data/agent hybrid labels: 23.7% of scraped 2026 SWE postings have one of those raw-title terms, versus 12.7% in arshkon 2024 and 9.6% in pooled 2024. This survives aggregator exclusion almost unchanged: 23.3% scraped 2026 versus 13.0% arshkon and 9.5% pooled 2024.

    Title fragmentation is mixed. Scraped 2026 has fewer unique raw-title keys per 1,000 postings than arshkon alone (480.5 vs 508.4), but more than pooled 2024 (480.5 vs 444.6). After excluding aggregators the same pattern remains (509.3 scraped, 524.3 arshkon, 462.4 pooled 2024). The safest interpretation is source-composition plus mild 2026 fragmentation, not clean standardization.

    Methodology¶

    Sample: default LinkedIn SWE frame, source_platform='linkedin', English, date_flag='ok', is_swe=true. Row counts: 4,691 arshkon, 18,129 asaniczka, and 40,881 scraped LinkedIn SWE rows across 2026-03 and 2026-04.

    Titles were normalized from the raw title column with a seniority-preserving key: lowercase, whitespace/punctuation cleanup, common Sr./Jr. expansion, and removal only of remote/hybrid/contract suffixes. I did not use title_normalized, because it strips seniority markers.

    Content similarity uses only shared LLM-cleaned text (text_source='llm') and company-token stopwords. For the 10 most common titles with at least 20 LLM-text rows in both arshkon and scraped, I sampled at most 300 rows per period/title and computed TF-IDF centroid cosine.

    Main Results¶

    Hybrid AI/Data Titles¶

    AI/ML/data/LLM/agent title prevalence:

    Spec arshkon pooled 2024 scraped 2026
    All rows 12.7% 9.6% 23.7%
    Exclude aggregators 13.0% 9.5% 23.3%

    The increase is driven mainly by AI/artificial intelligence title terms: 1.2% in pooled 2024 to 10.5% in scraped 2026. Agent terms rise from 0.03% to 1.08%; LLM terms remain small but increase from 0.07% to 0.34%.

    New And Disappeared Titles¶

    Among the top 200 2026 titles absent from arshkon, 102 title keys are also absent from pooled 2024. Those true-new-vs-pooled titles account for 1,094 scraped postings. The largest category is AI/ML/Data: 38 title keys and 402 scraped postings. Examples include Senior AI/ML Engineer, Applied AI Engineer, AI Platform Engineer, AI Agent Engineer, and AI Automation Engineer.

    The arshkon-only disappeared list is mostly legacy/source-specific rather than a clean labor-market disappearance signal. Examples such as Drupal Developer, DevOps Architect, Sr. Java Developer, Senior Database Developer, and PHP Developer often appear in asaniczka, so they are absent from scraped but not necessarily absent from the 2024 market.

    Same-Title Content Similarity¶

    Same-title TF-IDF cosine is highest for generic Software Engineer and Senior Software Engineer (about 0.79), and lower for more domain-specific titles:

    Title 2024/2026 cosine
    Embedded Software Engineer 0.595
    Java Developer 0.598
    Site Reliability Engineer 0.618
    Cloud Engineer 0.661
    Full Stack Engineer 0.673
    Data Engineer 0.783
    Software Engineer 0.791

    This suggests some same-title semantic drift, especially in embedded, Java, SRE, and cloud roles, while generic software-engineer titles remain semantically broad in both periods.

    Title Seniority Markers¶

    Using arshkon as the 2024 comparator, raw-title staff markers more than double: 2.5% to 5.8%. Senior rises modestly from 27.1% to 29.0%, principal from 2.8% to 3.7%, and junior/entry from 2.0% to 2.5%. Lead is flat and architect declines from 1.7% to 0.4%.

    This is not robust as a generic senior-title inflation claim because asaniczka 2024 is much more senior-title-heavy than arshkon. For example, senior is 45.5% in asaniczka, so pooled 2024 exceeds scraped 2026. The robust title-marker finding is narrower: staff rises even against both historical sources.

    Surprises And Unexpected Patterns¶

    • The top "new" title by count is Software Engineer [Multiple Positions Available], which is likely a template or posting-format artifact rather than a new occupation.
    • Many titles absent from arshkon are present in asaniczka, so arshkon-only "new" and "disappeared" lists are source-sensitive.
    • Same-title seniority shifts are not one-directional. Software Engineer becomes more senior-labeled among known-seniority rows, but other common titles split by domain and YOE definition.
    • Title fragmentation is lower than arshkon but higher than pooled 2024, showing how much the conclusion depends on the historical baseline.

    Sensitivity Checks¶

    Aggregator exclusion does not explain the AI/ML/data title increase. Hybrid title prevalence changes from 23.7% to 23.3% in scraped 2026 and from 9.6% to 9.5% in pooled 2024.

    Seniority-marker title claims are source-sensitive. Staff-title growth is robust; broad senior-title growth is not robust to using pooled 2024 rather than arshkon alone.

    Same-title content similarity is restricted to LLM-cleaned text. This avoids boilerplate but limits scraped coverage to the Stage 9 labeled subset.

    Caveats¶

    Title strings are employer-facing labels, not occupational truth. A title absent from arshkon can be a source-instrument artifact, a spelling/formatting difference, or a real emerging role. Treat new_vs_pooled_2024=true as the stricter list.

    The title-seniority panel has thin denominators for some high-frequency title keys after restricting to known seniority or known YOE. Use it diagnostically, not as a headline estimator.

    Downstream Action Items¶

    1. Gate 2 should verify the hybrid AI/ML/data title prevalence and the true-new-vs-pooled emerging-title counts.
    2. T14/T15 should check whether AI title growth corresponds to AI tech-stack growth or mostly title signaling.
    3. T16 should test whether AI/ML/Data emerging titles are concentrated in new companies or common companies.
    4. Use staff title growth as the title-inflation lead, not broad senior, which is source-sensitive.

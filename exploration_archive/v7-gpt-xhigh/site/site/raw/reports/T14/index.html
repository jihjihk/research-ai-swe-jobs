    T14. Technology Ecosystem Mapping¶

    Headline¶

    The 2026 SWE technology ecosystem is broader and more integrated, not just decorated with a few AI terms. In the default LinkedIn SWE frame from the shared artifacts, 29 of 148 technology indicators are calibrated risers and 4 are calibrated decliners under the primary all-row specification. The biggest calibrated movers are CI/CD, Python, API design, observability, LLM, Kubernetes, AWS, generative AI, Docker, Terraform, and RAG.

    The co-occurrence network also changed shape. In the company-capped network, Python moves from a general cloud/devops neighborhood in 2024 into a 2026 AI/LLM cluster with LLM, generative AI, RAG, PyTorch, TensorFlow, Claude, and MLOps. Cloud/devops remains a large, stable cluster, while frontend/API and data/service stacks split into more distinct bundles.

    Methodology¶

    Script: exploration/scripts/T14_technology_ecosystem.py.

    Inputs: exploration/artifacts/shared/swe_tech_matrix.parquet, tech_taxonomy.csv, swe_cleaned_text.parquet, asaniczka_structured_skills.parquet, and narrow metadata columns from data/unified.parquet. The script did not rebuild the technology matrix, recompute embeddings, rerun preprocessing, or make LLM calls.

    Frame: default LinkedIn SWE rows already encoded in the shared artifacts: source_platform = 'linkedin', is_english = true, date_flag = 'ok', is_swe = true. The shared matrix has 63,701 postings and 148 binary technology/tool/practice indicators. Indicators are binary mention flags scanned over description_cleaned, which is LLM-cleaned text where available and raw fallback otherwise.

    Network specification: company-capped at 50 postings per company_name_canonical x year, technologies included if they appear in at least 1% of postings in either period, edges included if phi coefficient > 0.15 and the co-mention appears across at least 20 distinct companies. Communities use NetworkX greedy modularity.

    Main Findings¶

    Broad Technology Growth¶

    Primary binary mention rates, pooled 2024 to scraped 2026:

    Technology 2024 rate 2026 rate Change Calibration verdict
    CI/CD 15.4% 33.6% +18.2 pp calibrated rising
    Python 32.3% 49.4% +17.0 pp calibrated rising
    API design 13.0% 27.4% +14.4 pp calibrated rising
    Observability 1.9% 13.9% +12.0 pp calibrated rising
    LLM 1.0% 13.0% +12.0 pp calibrated rising
    Kubernetes 13.0% 22.0% +9.0 pp calibrated rising
    AWS 25.8% 34.5% +8.7 pp calibrated rising
    Generative AI 0.9% 7.5% +6.7 pp calibrated rising
    RAG 0.1% 5.2% +5.1 pp calibrated rising

    Some apparent risers are real directional changes but not cleanly above within-2024 source noise. TypeScript rises 13.3% to 18.4% (+5.1 pp), and Azure rises 19.4% to 23.7% (+4.2 pp), but both are classified as rising_not_above_2024_noise.

    Category-level rates also show ecosystem expansion:

    Category 2024 2026
    AI tool 2.9% 19.2%
    AI/ML domain 2.4% 10.8%
    DevOps 17.9% 32.5%
    Practice 21.4% 40.6%
    Cloud 37.0% 46.5%
    Framework 31.4% 37.3%
    Testing 5.1% 5.0%

    Testing is the notable non-mover. The 2026 expansion is mostly AI, cloud/devops, API/platform, and operational practice language.

    Network Structure¶

    The company-capped co-occurrence network grows from 239 edges in 2024 to 283 edges in 2026 under identical thresholds.

    2024 communities:

    • Cloud/devops/general tooling: Python, AWS, Azure, CI/CD, Git, Kubernetes, Docker, Microservices.
    • Web/API/.NET: SQL, JavaScript, React, API design, C#, TypeScript, HTML, .NET.
    • JVM/data: Java, PostgreSQL, Kafka, MySQL, Scala, Hadoop, Redis, Airflow.
    • Systems/scripting: Linux, C++, Unix, Ruby, MATLAB, Perl, Django, Rust.
    • Small AI/ML cluster: TensorFlow, PyTorch, LLM, fine-tuning, generative AI, Pandas, NumPy, MLOps.

    2026 communities:

    • Cloud/devops/platform: AWS, CI/CD, Azure, Kubernetes, Docker, Microservices, Observability, Terraform.
    • AI/LLM/Python: Python, LLM, Generative AI, RAG, PyTorch, Claude, TensorFlow, MLOps.
    • Frontend/API: API design, React, TypeScript, JavaScript, Node.js, Angular, REST API, Next.js.
    • Data/service: PostgreSQL, Kafka, MySQL, Redis, FastAPI, Elasticsearch, Django, RabbitMQ.
    • SQL/.NET/data warehouse: SQL, C#, .NET, Airflow, Spark, dbt, BigQuery, Redshift.

    The most important network movement is not simply that AI terms appear. Python, LLM, generative AI, RAG, PyTorch, Claude, and MLOps form a coherent 2026 cluster. RAG, Claude, and Next.js are network entries under the strict company-spread threshold.

    Stack Breadth¶

    Mean distinct technology indicators rise across every T30 junior and senior definition:

    Definition Pooled 2024 Scraped 2026 Change
    J1 entry label 4.64 6.53 +1.89
    J2 entry/associate 4.62 6.58 +1.96
    J3 YOE <= 2 5.04 6.81 +1.76
    J4 YOE <= 3 5.03 6.99 +1.96
    S1 mid-senior/director 5.62 7.76 +2.14
    S2 director 3.57 9.38 +5.81, sparse
    S3 title-keyword senior 5.16 7.05 +1.90
    S4 YOE >= 5 5.33 7.82 +2.49

    Density tells the confound. Tech density per 1,000 cleaned characters falls for most definitions because descriptions are longer. Example: J3 density falls from 6.03 to 4.55, and S1 falls from 7.28 to 5.42. This supports a "broader stack lists in longer/richer postings" reading rather than a clean claim that postings became denser per character.

    AI Integration¶

    AI-tool/LLM rows are more stack-rich, but length explains part of the gap.

    Period AI-tool/LLM mention n Mean tech count Mean non-AI tech count Mean non-AI tech density
    2024 no 22,323 5.15 5.15 7.37
    2024 yes 497 7.22 5.79 6.15
    2026 no 32,379 6.60 6.60 5.58
    2026 yes 8,502 9.78 7.63 5.23

    In 2026, AI-tool/LLM postings mention about 1.0 more non-AI technologies than non-AI postings, but their non-AI density is lower because they are longer. AI is integrating into existing stacks, not replacing them, but the raw-count advantage is length-confounded.

    Among 2026 AI-tool/LLM postings, the strongest traditional co-occurrence lifts are Python (+22.3 pp), API design (+18.2 pp), PyTorch (+12.3 pp), observability (+12.3 pp), and AWS (+10.4 pp) relative to non-AI rows.

    Structured Skills Baseline¶

    Asaniczka structured skills validate the broad ranking of extracted technology signals. Across the 148 shared taxonomy technologies, structured-skill prevalence and description-extracted prevalence have Spearman rho = 0.817.

    Top structured skills by posting count include Python (5,629), Java (4,679), SQL (3,656), AWS (3,441), JavaScript (3,129), Git (2,454), Agile (2,400), Linux (2,285), Kubernetes (2,229), Docker (2,130), C++ (2,010), C# (1,826), Azure (1,578), and Jira (1,499).

    The divergences are instructive. Structured skills capture generic tags that descriptions often do not explicitly spell out: Agile is +31.0 pp higher in structured fields than extracted descriptions, DevOps +14.4 pp, unit testing +9.0 pp, machine learning +7.7 pp, SQL Server +6.1 pp, and Oracle +5.7 pp. The regex matrix is a good binary description screen, not a substitute for parsed skill taxonomies.

    Structured Seniority Skill Differences¶

    Asaniczka structured skills are a useful but source-specific seniority baseline. Using FDR correction over skills appearing in at least 50 postings:

    • J1 vs S1 significant junior-higher skills include IoT, nginx, polygraph, sales, Excel, SVN, Pandas, and Apache. Several are odd enough to treat as source/template artifacts rather than a clean junior-skill taxonomy.
    • J3 vs S4 is more interpretable: low-YOE rows are higher on data pipelines (+2.09 pp), distributed computing (+1.55 pp), data structures (+2.12 pp), programming (+1.64 pp), and version control (+1.53 pp). S4 is higher on systems engineering, system integration, distributed microservices, people management, and U.S. citizenship.

    This structured baseline supports the broader result that low-YOE rows are not simply "less technical"; they often list modern platform/data skills, while senior rows retain systems, integration, clearance, and people-management signals.

    Sensitivity Checks¶

    Aggregator exclusion preserves the major movers. Pooled-2024 to scraped-2026 changes after excluding aggregators: Python +19.5 pp, CI/CD +16.6 pp, API design +13.5 pp, observability +12.7 pp, LLM +12.3 pp, Kubernetes +10.2 pp, AWS +11.0 pp, generative AI +6.7 pp.

    Company cap 50 also preserves the major movers and often strengthens platform terms: CI/CD +18.6 pp, Python +18.6 pp, API design +15.7 pp, observability +12.8 pp, LLM +12.2 pp, AWS +11.7 pp, Kubernetes +10.3 pp.

    Recommended SWE tier sensitivity, excluding swe_classification_tier = 'title_lookup_llm', preserves the same pattern: CI/CD +17.8 pp, Python +16.3 pp, API design +14.5 pp, observability +12.4 pp, LLM +12.2 pp, Kubernetes +8.6 pp.

    Within-2024 calibration is built into trajectory. TypeScript and Azure rise in the scraped comparison but are not cleanly above arshkon-vs-asaniczka variation, so they should not be used as headline temporal claims without additional validation.

    Surprises & Unexpected Patterns¶

    • Python reclusters into the 2026 AI/LLM neighborhood instead of remaining only a general backend/cloud language.
    • Testing does not grow with the rest of the stack. Testing category prevalence is roughly flat, 5.1% to 5.0%.
    • AI-tool/LLM postings list more traditional technologies, but lower non-AI tech density after length normalization.
    • Structured skills agree strongly with extracted tech rank order, but they expose systematic undercounting of generic practice tags in description text.
    • Some structured "junior" associations are odd and likely reflect source or template composition, not a clean junior skill profile.

    Caveats¶

    • The shared tech matrix is regex-based over description_cleaned. It is appropriate for binary mention screens, but individual terms still need semantic validation before paper-grade prevalence claims.
    • description_cleaned mixes LLM-cleaned text and raw fallback. This is acceptable for binary mention recall, but density findings use cleaned-character length and should be interpreted cautiously for raw-fallback rows.
    • Asaniczka structured skills are 2024-only and source-specific. They validate extraction and seniority boundaries; they do not measure 2026 skills.
    • S2 director cells are sparse, and S3 inherits the T30 title-normalization caveat.
    • Community numeric IDs are not semantically stable across years; community labels in the report are based on top members and categories, not ID equality.

    Downstream Action Items¶

    1. Gate 2 should verify the top calibrated movers: CI/CD, Python, API design, observability, LLM, Kubernetes, AWS, generative AI, and RAG.
    2. Use the AI/LLM + Python/platform cluster as the technology-evolution headline, not isolated AI keyword prevalence.
    3. Treat technology breadth growth as stack expansion in longer postings; do not claim density growth except where a density metric supports it.
    4. For interviews, ask whether AI requirements are screened as additions to existing stack expectations or as separate "nice-to-have" signals.
    5. T16/T28 should test whether the Python/AI/platform cluster is driven by new companies, specific domains, or within-company changes.

    Supported lead claims¶

    Claim Support Caveat
    Returning companies expanded AI/tool posting language. T16, V1 verified Wave 2 broad AI. Binary mentions are not force-coded; V2 missing.
    Expansion is SWE-amplified but adjacent-parallel. T18. Occupation classification and adjacent heterogeneity require formal checks; V2 missing.
    AI/tool diffusion is geographically broad. T17. Metro-known frame and source composition caveats; V2 missing.
    Requirement and tech breadth rose across seniority definitions. T11, T14, V1. LLM-cleaned subset and regex/binary mention measures.
    Junior collapse is not supported as a lead fact. T30, T08, T16, T17, T18. J1/J2 are source-sensitive; J3/J4 are YOE constructs.
    Seniority labels and YOE floors diverge. T08, T16, T15. T20 boundary modeling missing.
    Domain/technology structure dominates seniority structure. T09, T15, V1. LLM-cleaned subset evidence.

    Explicit gaps¶

    Gap What it prevents
    T19 missing No temporal-rate stability, annualized-rate, backlog-vs-flow, or scraped-window consistency claims.
    T20/T21 missing No formal seniority-boundary classifier, associate-distance model, low-YOE unknown-pool model, or senior-role deep dive.
    T22 missing No claim that AI/tool/platform mentions are screened requirements rather than preferred, aspirational, template, or branding language.
    T23 missing No employer-requirement / worker-usage divergence claim.
    V2 missing No independent verification of Wave 3 T16/T17/T18 comparable to V1.

    Good paper position¶

    The defensible contribution is a longitudinal posting-content and measurement paper: AI-era requirements diffused through technical job ads, and seniority labels became unreliable anchors for interpreting junior and senior restructuring.

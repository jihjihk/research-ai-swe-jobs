# T12. Open-Ended Text Evolution

## Headline

The strongest 2026-heavy text movement is a mix of workflow/platform language, exposure-style credentials, systems-scope language, and AI-tool terms. This is not explained away by aggregator rows, company concentration, raw boilerplate, or source restriction. In the primary LLM-cleaned, non-aggregator, company-capped arshkon-vs-scraped comparison, top 2026-heavy terms include `workflows`, `pipelines`, `familiarity`, `hands-on`, `tooling`, `observability`, `models`, `ownership`, `ai/ml`, `agent`, `llm`, and `rag`.

The T13 section-filtered comparison preserves the same broad signal: `environments`, `familiarity`, `workflows`, `pipelines`, `hands-on`, `tooling`, `observability`, `ai/ml`, `llm`, `ci/cd`, `apis`, and `distributed` remain 2026-heavy after restricting to responsibilities/requirements/preferred text.

## Methodology

Script: `exploration/scripts/T12_open_text_evolution.py`.

Primary comparison: arshkon 2024 vs scraped 2026, LinkedIn SWE, `text_source='llm'`, aggregators excluded, company cap 50 per corpus/company, company-name stoplist applied, location tokens stripped, unigram log-odds with informative Dirichlet prior.

Primary n: 4,221 arshkon postings from 2,266 companies and 9,531 scraped postings from 3,601 companies. Section-filtered n: 2,496 arshkon and 6,737 scraped. Bigram n: 4,220 and 9,530. Counts for all comparisons are in `exploration/tables/T12/corpus_counts_all_comparisons.csv`.

T13 dependency: section-filtered text uses `requirements_responsibilities_text` from `exploration/tables/T13/section_text_by_uid.parquet`, including responsibilities, requirements, and preferred sections.

BERTopic deviation: not rerun. T09 found BERTopic unstable, and this run followed the OOM guidance by cross-validating against T09 NMF archetype labels instead.

## Main Findings

1. **2026-heavy terms center on workflow/platform scope.** Document prevalence rises sharply for `workflows` (5.2% to 28.1%), `pipelines` (14.6% to 36.4%), `tooling` (3.2% to 14.2%), `observability` (2.6% to 13.8%), `ownership` (5.2% to 15.9%), `end-to-end` (5.9% to 16.4%), and `distributed` (12.0% to 24.8%).

2. **Exposure-style credential language grows.** `familiarity` rises from 12.8% to 38.9%, `hands-on` from 12.6% to 34.1%, `demonstrated` from 6.0% to 18.0%, `proficiency` from 14.3% to 32.6%, and `exposure` from 4.0% to 12.4%. This looks like screening language broadening, but these are token-level findings and need semantic validation before being converted into requirement prevalence claims.

3. **AI-tool emergence is visible in open terms, not only predefined dictionaries.** Emerging terms include `ai-assisted` (0% to 3.7%), `claude` (0.02% to 3.7%), `cursor` (0% to 2.3%), `mcp` (0.05% to 1.9%, flagged ambiguous), `multi-agent` (0.09% to 1.8%), `langgraph` (0% to 1.6%), `embeddings` (0.09% to 1.4%), `ai-enabled` (0.05% to 1.3%), `llm-based` (0.02% to 1.3%), and `llm-powered` (0.02% to 1.1%).

4. **Bigram results sharpen the interpretation.** 2026-heavy phrases include `ci/cd pipelines` (5.3% to 17.0%), `backend apis` (0.6% to 3.2%), `large models` (0.7% to 3.1%), `cross-functional collaboration` (0.2% to 2.2%), `familiarity ci/cd` (0.3% to 2.1%), and `retrieval-augmented rag` (0.09% to 1.6%).

5. **2024-heavy terms are legacy enterprise/web stack and source artifacts.** Arshkon-heavy terms include `.net`, `sql`, `database`, `javascript`, `php`, `java`, `azure`, `mvc`, `html`, `jquery`, and `active directory`. These should be read as relative corpus distinctions, not disappearance from the market.

6. **Entry and senior panels move in similar directions.** J1-J4 and S1/S3/S4 all show 2026-heavy workflow/credential/AI terms. S2 director-only is flagged `n < 100` on the 2024 side and should be treated as descriptive only. The relabeling diagnostic does not look like 2026 entry is simply 2024 senior text: entry-2026-heavy terms include `internship`, `pursuing`, `gain`, and `real-world` alongside workflows/models/CI-CD.

7. **T09 archetypes cross-validate the open-text signal.** In the same primary capped/non-aggregator subset, T09 `AI LLM Platforms` rises from 4.6% to 17.8% (+13.2 pp), and `Product Backend Engineering` rises from 8.6% to 18.0% (+9.5 pp). `Java Dotnet Web Services` falls from 27.0% to 11.9%, matching the 2024-heavy .NET/JavaScript/SQL term pattern.

## Sensitivity Checks

Aggregator inclusion: top 2026-heavy term overlap with the primary is 0.98.

No company cap: overlap is 0.94.

Raw-description text source: overlap is 0.84. Raw adds boilerplate terms, but the main AI/workflow/platform terms remain.

Source restriction: pooled-2024 vs scraped overlap is 0.82. The signal survives adding asaniczka, though magnitudes shift.

Within-2024 calibration: arshkon-vs-asaniczka top-term overlap with the primary 2026-heavy list is only 0.12. Within-2024 differences are mostly clearance/embedded/program/source-composition terms, not the AI/workflow cluster.

SWE tier: excluding `title_lookup_llm` gives 0.96 top-term overlap.

## Surprises & Unexpected Patterns

- The most distinctive 2026 non-AI language is not "more senior" language narrowly; it is workflows, tooling, pipelines, environments, exposure, and ownership.
- AI-tool emergence includes specific agent-era tools and patterns (`Cursor`, `Claude`, `MCP`, `LangGraph`, `multi-agent`) rather than only generic `AI`.
- Section filtering does not remove the 2026-heavy signal, so it is not only benefits/about/legal boilerplate.
- The disappearing-term list is mostly concatenation artifacts such as `skillsability` and `experienceexperience`; do not treat it as substantive disappearance.

## Caveats

- Open-ended token comparisons are discovery tools. Reported term prevalence is token presence after stopword/company stripping, not validated semantic requirement prevalence.
- Residual `noise` remains high in top-100 category summaries, especially for generic terms and LLM-cleaned artifacts. Top term context samples are saved in `top_term_context_samples.csv`; no precision claim is made from the categorizer alone.
- `mcp` is flagged as ambiguous because prior validation found non-AI false positives.
- Scraped cleaned-text coverage is limited: the primary text-dependent findings describe the LLM-cleaned scraped subset, not all scraped LinkedIn postings.
- Company stopword stripping may remove some real technology/company-overlap tokens; this is the intended tradeoff to avoid company-name domination.

## Downstream Action Items

1. Build validated requirement families from the open-term candidates before using them as prevalence measures: AI tools, workflow/pipeline ownership, observability, exposure-style credentials, and systems-scope language.
2. In Gate 2, treat "scope inflation" as plausible but not identical to longer descriptions: T12 terms point to workflow/platform scope, while T13 shows core-section share is roughly flat.
3. Follow up by company/domain: T09 suggests AI LLM Platforms and Product Backend growth, while Java/.NET and Enterprise Application Support shrink in the LLM-labeled subset.
4. For paper-grade claims, manually validate stratified contexts for the specific terms that become metrics.

# T03. Seniority Label Audit

## Headline

`seniority_final` is defensible as the production seniority column, but it is conservative. The labels that are assigned show strong YOE ordering, and known-only agreement with LinkedIn native labels is moderate to substantial. The main failure mode is not random mislabeling; it is under-labeling into `unknown`, especially for scraped rows and for L2/L3/L4/L5-style title codes.

Evidence base: existing T03 tables in `exploration/tables/T03/`. No data was re-queried for this report. All source counts use the exploration default filter: LinkedIn rows, English rows, `date_flag = 'ok'`, and `is_swe = true`.

## Source Profile

`seniority_final_source` composition varies sharply by source and period.

| Source-period | SWE n | LLM | Title keyword | Title manager | Unknown |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| kaggle_arshkon 2024-04 | 4,691 | 62.2% | 37.8% | 0.0% | 0.1% |
| kaggle_asaniczka 2024-01 | 18,129 | 28.6% | 58.8% | 0.0% | 12.6% |
| scraped 2026-03 | 19,777 | 17.6% | 44.1% | 0.0% | 38.4% |
| scraped 2026-04 | 21,104 | 16.3% | 43.0% | ~0.0% | 40.6% |

The scraped data has much higher `unknown` seniority, roughly 38-41% of SWE rows. Downstream analyses must not treat the known-seniority subset as representative without checking unknown-pool composition.

## Native Label Agreement

Native labels are a diagnostic, not the primary label. The all-values comparison is low because `seniority_final` intentionally leaves many rows as `unknown`, while native labels often force a class.

| Comparison | Source group | n | Exact match | Cohen's kappa |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Native vs final, all final values | arshkon 2024 | 3,283 | 44.3% | 0.175 |
| Native vs final, all final values | scraped 2026 | 34,839 | 41.2% | 0.112 |
| Native intern->entry and executive->director, all final values | arshkon 2024 | 3,283 | 45.0% | 0.184 |
| Native intern->entry and executive->director, all final values | scraped 2026 | 34,839 | 42.8% | 0.135 |
| Same mapping, final known only | arshkon 2024 | 1,667 | 88.5% | 0.454 |
| Same mapping, final known only | scraped 2026 | 15,982 | 93.3% | 0.646 |

Known-only agreement is much stronger, especially in scraped 2026. That pattern supports the design choice: high precision where the pipeline assigns a label, with a large residual unknown class rather than forced labels.

Per-class agreement against native labels is uneven:

| Source group | Native class | Native n | Exact match rate |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| arshkon 2024 | entry | 738 | 7.2% |
| arshkon 2024 | associate | 263 | 1.5% |
| arshkon 2024 | mid-senior | 2,231 | 62.4% |
| scraped 2026 | entry | 4,689 | 10.2% |
| scraped 2026 | associate | 1,405 | 0.5% |
| scraped 2026 | mid-senior | 27,759 | 49.9% |

This is mostly an `unknown` issue. For example, 618 of arshkon's 738 native-entry rows and 3,986 of scraped 2026's 4,689 native-entry rows are `unknown` under `seniority_final`.

## Native-Label YOE Diagnostic

YOE evidence favors `seniority_final` over native labels for identifying actual entry-level rows.

| Source group | Label system | Label | n | YOE known | Median YOE | Share YOE <= 2 | Share YOE >= 5 |
|---|---|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| arshkon 2024 | final | entry | 175 | 37.1% | 2.0 | 78.5% | 6.2% |
| arshkon 2024 | native | entry | 738 | 62.1% | 3.0 | 28.6% | 42.6% |
| scraped 2026 | final | entry | 1,275 | 24.9% | 2.0 | 81.8% | 6.3% |
| scraped 2026 | native | entry | 4,689 | 38.8% | 2.0 | 81.1% | 10.6% |
| arshkon 2024 | final | mid-senior | 2,064 | 67.6% | 5.0 | 6.3% | 74.6% |
| scraped 2026 | final | mid-senior | 17,418 | 75.4% | 5.0 | 7.8% | 72.9% |
| arshkon 2024 | final | unknown | 2,424 | 58.8% | 4.0 | 20.2% | 48.6% |
| scraped 2026 | final | unknown | 21,841 | 63.6% | 4.0 | 24.7% | 44.3% |

The final-entry class has the expected low-YOE profile in both periods. Native entry in arshkon is much noisier, with 42.6% of YOE-known rows requiring 5+ years. This is a strong warning against using native labels as a pooled baseline and supports the pipeline's conservative relabeling.

The unknown pool is substantive, with median YOE around 4 years and many 5+ YOE rows. Unknowns are not "junior by default" and should be reported separately or handled through the T30 panel.

## LLM Weak-Marker Spot Check

The weak-marker population is small: 21 SWE rows where `seniority_final_source = 'llm'` and the title contains weak level markers such as `L2`, `L3`, `L4`, `L5`, roman numerals, or `level 3`.

| Population n | Sample n | Heuristic consistent | Heuristic inconsistent | Inconsistent share |
|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| 21 | 21 | 2 | 19 | 90.5% |

The inconsistent cases are mostly titles like `Software Engineer L3`, `L2 Data Engineer`, `Software Engineer L5`, and `ServiceNow Developer (L4)` where the heuristic expected associate/mid-senior but the LLM result was often `unknown`. Because the expected label is heuristic and the population is only 21 rows, this is not a formal error-rate estimate. It is a clear blind spot for company-level code markers.

Interpretation: this failure mode is conservative. It suppresses known-seniority coverage rather than inflating entry or senior labels, but it can attenuate senior/junior shares for employers that use explicit level codes.

## Defensibility Recommendation

Use `seniority_final` as the production seniority column. It is not a high-recall seniority classifier, but the assigned labels are much more YOE-coherent than native labels, and known-only native agreement is acceptable for an exploratory seniority instrument.

Required constraints for downstream work:

- Report `unknown` seniority rather than dropping it silently.
- Use the T30 panel for every seniority-stratified finding.
- Validate entry-level findings with YOE proxies (`J3`/`J4`) because label-only entry has coverage limits.
- Do not use pooled `seniority_native` for junior baselines. Asaniczka has no native entry-level labels by construction, and arshkon native entry is YOE-noisy.
- Treat L2/L3/L4/L5 title-code employers as a known under-labeling risk.

## Surprises & Unexpected Patterns

- The most surprising result is that arshkon native-entry rows look much less junior by YOE than `seniority_final` entry rows.
- Scraped 2026 has a much larger unknown seniority share than either historical source, even though it also has native LinkedIn labels for many rows.
- The LLM weak-marker check failed mostly on title level codes rather than on vague responsibility language. A small, well-tested level-code rule could recover coverage, but only after employer-specific ambiguity is handled.
- `associate` under `seniority_final` is tiny, and its YOE profile is closer to junior/mid than to classic senior. It should not be assumed equivalent to asaniczka native `associate`.

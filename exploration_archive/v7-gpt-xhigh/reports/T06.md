# T06. Company Concentration Deep Investigation

## Headline Finding

Company concentration is moderate in row-share terms but high enough to distort any corpus-level analysis. The 2026 scraped LinkedIn source has 7,940 distinct known companies, yet the top 20 companies contribute 18.1% of known-company SWE postings and the top 50 contribute 26.9%. Entry-level posting is much more concentrated than posting volume: among scraped LinkedIn companies with at least 5 SWE postings, 79.4% have zero J1 entry-labeled rows and 53.1% have zero YOE<=2 rows.

The strongest Gate 1 warning is not only "cap prolific firms." It is "do not interpret entry-share, text-frequency, topic, or network results until entry-specialist employers, aggregators, duplicate templates, and company composition have been handled explicitly."

## Methodology

Subset: all SWE postings across all source-platforms with `is_english = true`, `date_flag = 'ok'`, and `is_swe = true`.

Company grouping: `company_name_canonical`, excluding missing company for company-level metrics.

Source keys:

| Source key | SWE rows | Known-company rows | Distinct companies |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| kaggle_arshkon | 4,691 | 4,646 | 2,298 |
| kaggle_asaniczka | 18,129 | 18,129 | 4,948 |
| scraped_linkedin | 40,881 | 40,881 | 7,940 |
| scraped_indeed | 11,860 | 11,511 | 3,308 |

Entry variants follow the T30 panel definitions: J1 = `seniority_final = 'entry'`, J2 = `seniority_final IN ('entry','associate')`, J3 = `yoe_extracted <= 2`, and J4 = `yoe_extracted <= 3`. The T30 aggregate panel was loaded as a reference; row-level company membership was recomputed in DuckDB from those definitions because the panel CSV is aggregate, not row-level.

AI mention prevalence uses a raw-description binary pattern covering AI, ML, LLM, generative AI, GPT, ChatGPT, OpenAI, Anthropic, Claude, Copilot, Cursor, RAG, agentic, AI agent, and prompt engineering. Tech count uses the keyword list in the SQL script and should be treated as a coarse concentration diagnostic, not a validated skills measure.

A term-frequency or AI-rate result can easily become a large-employer mix result.

Among companies that do post entry roles, the distribution is polarized. In scraped LinkedIn, the median J1 share among J1-posting companies is 33.3% of own postings and the 90th percentile is 100%. For YOE<=2 posters, the median share is 50% of YOE-known postings and the 90th percentile is 100%.

Interpretation: entry-level posting is a specialized employer activity. Aggregate entry-share trends are not market-wide unless they survive company-weighting and exclusion of entry-specialist employers.

The seniority story changes by junior definition. J1/J2 entry shares decline slightly, while J3/J4 YOE-based junior shares rise, especially in the common-company panel. Most of the J3/J4 common-panel increase is between-company reweighting, not within-company change. This is a required Gate 1 constraint for any "junior share rose/fell" claim.

AI mentions and description length rise strongly within overlapping companies, so those shifts are not explained only by employer turnover. Tech count rises mostly within companies, with between-company reweighting partly offsetting the increase.

## Aggregator Profile

Aggregator shares differ strongly:

| Source key | Aggregator share |
|---|---:|
| kaggle_arshkon | 9.2% |
| kaggle_asaniczka | 16.2% |
| scraped_linkedin | 16.6% |
| scraped_indeed | 2.1% |

Aggregators also differ systematically. In scraped LinkedIn, aggregator descriptions are shorter on average (3,905 vs 5,113 chars), have higher unknown-seniority share (65.8% vs 51.0%), lower AI mention prevalence (43.3% vs 52.6%), and slightly higher mean tech count (5.41 vs 5.07). Their known-seniority entry share is higher than non-aggregators (8.25% vs 6.48%), but their all-posting J1 share is lower because unknown seniority is more common.

Conclusion: aggregator exclusion is mandatory for entry, seniority, and text analyses. Aggregators are not simply "more AI" or "longer descriptions"; they change the composition in multiple directions.

| Analysis category | Risk | Recommended default |
|---|---|---|
| Entry share | High | Report J1-J4, add company-weighted estimates, and exclude `entry_specialist_employers.csv` as a required sensitivity. |

| Co-occurrence networks | High | Build company-capped networks and require terms/edges to appear across at least 20 distinct companies. |

## Surprises & Unexpected Patterns

- Exact-template duplication is not zero after the Stage 4 fix; scraped LinkedIn still has employers with large residual exact-template shares.
- J1/J2 and J3/J4 tell opposite stories in the matched-company decomposition. Label-based entry declines slightly, while YOE-based junior shares rise.
- New scraped LinkedIn entrants are not more AI-heavy than returning employers; AI mention prevalence is essentially identical.
- Aggregators in scraped LinkedIn are shorter and less AI-heavy than non-aggregators, contrary to the simple assumption that aggregators always inflate text and AI terms.
- The entry-specialist artifact contains many apparent direct employers, partly because YOE-based variants can flag companies with very small YOE-known denominators.

## Gate 1 Constraints

- Do not report a single junior-share estimate. Report J1-J4 and explain disagreements.
- Any aggregate entry-share result must include company-weighted and entry-specialist-excluded sensitivities.
- Text-frequency, topic, and network analyses should be company-capped and exact-template deduplicated.
- Aggregators must be reported separately or excluded as a required sensitivity.
- New-entrant and returning-company profiles should be separated for source-composition-sensitive claims.

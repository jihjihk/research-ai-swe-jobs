## Verdict (headline)

**Asaniczka `associate` is NOT usable as a junior proxy.** On every label-independent signal, asaniczka `associate` behaves like a mid-career mixed bag that sits closer to arshkon `associate` and `mid-senior` than to arshkon `entry`. When the Stage 10 LLM re-labels asaniczka `associate` rows, **33.5%** of them become `mid-senior` in `seniority_final` versus **8.8%** of arshkon `entry` rows — the LLM itself disagrees with any "associate = junior" reading by almost a factor of four. YOE is also the wrong shape: 33.1% of asaniczka `associate` postings require 5+ years versus 26.4% of arshkon `entry`. Using asaniczka `associate` as a junior proxy would inflate the 2024 junior baseline with ~30% senior-like rows and bias the RQ1 junior-share trend toward a false "junior collapse."

The safe defaults are:
1. **For entry-level trends, use `seniority_final`** with label-independent YOE validation. Do not try to reconstruct "asaniczka junior" from the native label at all.
2. **For any `seniority_native`-based sanity check, use arshkon only.** Asaniczka has zero native entry labels by construction; pooling it under `seniority_native` dilutes 2024 entry rate to near-zero.

Secondary finding: even under `seniority_final`, the **entry-level effective sample sizes are very small** — arshkon 175 SWE, asaniczka 204 SWE, scraped 1,275 SWE. Any RQ1 junior-share confidence interval on the 2024 side is noisy by arithmetic alone.

**This is the decisive table.** On the rows where `seniority_final` produced any non-unknown answer (i.e., where Stage 5 rules or Stage 10 LLM fired), the distributions are:

`seniority_final` is an independent classifier (rule + LLM) and its verdict on asaniczka_associate is clear: it is not an entry-level bucket, and it is not even as junior-leaning as arshkon_associate. The 33.5% mid-senior share is 3.8x the arshkon_entry mid-senior share.

## Step 3 — Decision rule application

Decision rule: "usable as junior proxy" requires that asaniczka_associate behave directionally close to arshkon_entry on multiple signals.

| Signal | asaniczka_associate | arshkon_entry | Directionally close? |
|---|---|---|---|
| Junior title cue rate | 2.9% | 1.9% | Yes (both low, asaniczka slightly higher) |
| Senior title cue rate | 1.2% | 0.5% | ~ (close) |
| YOE median | 4 | 3 | No (1 year higher) |
| YOE≤2 share | 15.2% | 17.8% | Yes (within ~15% relative) |
| YOE≥5 share | 33.1% | 26.4% | **No** (25% relatively higher) |
| `seniority_final` mid-senior rate (all rows) | 33.5% | 8.8% | **No** (3.8x higher) |
| `seniority_final` entry rate (all rows) | 3.8% | 7.2% | No (half of arshkon_entry) |
| Top-title Jaccard to arshkon_entry | 0.143 | — | **No** (lower than overlap with mid-senior groups) |

Three signals match directionally (title cues and YOE≤2), five do not. The signals that disagree are stronger: the `seniority_final` conditional distribution (an independent classifier), YOE≥5 share (a hard label-independent check), and the title-set overlap (where asaniczka_associate looks more like mid-senior than entry).

**Verdict: NOT usable as a junior proxy.** The only signal on which asaniczka_associate looks junior-like is YOE≤2, and that is contradicted by YOE≥5 and by the classifier-overlap diagnostics. Using it as a 2024 junior baseline would import approximately 33% mid-senior-like rows, which is exactly the bias that would produce a false "2024 was more junior than we thought" finding when running the RQ1 decomposition.

What asaniczka_associate actually is: a mixed low-to-mid bucket where LinkedIn-at-the-time-of-asaniczka-snapshot was labeling unranked `software engineer / system engineer / data engineer` postings as `associate`. It overlaps arshkon_associate more than arshkon_entry, but it is not identical to arshkon_associate either (arshkon_associate has yoe_ge5 = 18% vs asaniczka_associate 33%) — asaniczka_associate is shifted more senior even relative to arshkon's own associate bucket. This strongly suggests the LinkedIn native-label semantics changed between the asaniczka snapshot window (2024-01) and the arshkon window (2024-04), or that asaniczka's ingestion applied a different mapping. Either way, it cannot be treated as arshkon_entry's asaniczka-side counterpart.

## Step 4 — Entry-level effective sample sizes under `seniority_final`

`seniority_final` = 'entry' SWE rows per source, broken down by `seniority_final_source`:

| source | total entry | title_keyword | title_manager | llm | unknown |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| kaggle_arshkon | **175** | 92 | 0 | 83 | 0 |
| kaggle_asaniczka | **204** | 112 | 0 | 92 | 0 |
| scraped | **1,275** | 1,002 | 0 | 273 | 0 |

Arithmetic reality check: the arshkon 2024 SWE entry frame is 175 rows — not 738 (the native-label count). The 738 arshkon_entry native rows produce only 53 `entry` in `seniority_final` (7.2%) because the combined rule+LLM column disagrees with the native label on ~93% of arshkon's native entries, mostly routing them to `unknown` or `mid-senior` (see T02 conditional table above).

Asaniczka contributes 204 entry-level SWE rows in `seniority_final`, entirely via Stage 5 rule (112 `title_keyword`) plus Stage 10 LLM writeback (92 `llm`). This is not a "junior proxy from associate" — the pipeline is picking up explicit `junior/entry/I` titles plus LLM judgments on routed rows.

Effective coverage per slice (share of entry rows where the given column is populated):

| source × resolver | n | yoe_cov | metro_cov | desc_core_llm_cov | co_cov |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| arshkon × title_keyword | 92 | 13.0% | 67.4% | 100.0% | 96.7% |
| arshkon × llm | 83 | 63.9% | 56.6% | 98.8% | 97.6% |
| asaniczka × title_keyword | 112 | 36.6% | 77.7% | 97.3% | 100.0% |
| asaniczka × llm | 92 | 64.1% | 71.7% | 100.0% | 100.0% |
| scraped × title_keyword | 1,002 | 20.5% | 65.4% | **33.1%** | 100.0% |
| scraped × llm | 273 | 41.4% | 83.9% | 98.5% | 100.0% |

**Two things jump out:**
1. **YOE coverage on `title_keyword`-derived entry rows is 13-37%.** Strong-rule entry labels are applied from the title alone; many of those postings don't write an explicit YOE in the description. If you want to validate the entry label with the YOE proxy, you have roughly 20-40% of the already-small entry sample to work with.
2. **`description_core_llm` on scraped × title_keyword entry rows is only 33%.** On the scraped 2026 window, of 1,002 strong-rule entry SWE rows, only 332 are usable for cleaned-text analysis. LLM-resolved scraped entry rows have ~99% cleaned-text coverage but there are only 273 of them. The text-based 2026 entry-level frame is roughly 332 + 270 = **~600 rows** — tight.
   The mechanism is that `title_keyword`-resolved entry rows have `llm_classification_coverage = 'rule_sufficient'`, but Stage 9 text-extraction runs independently and the scraped frame is undersampled there (see T01). Strong-rule entry labels on scraped skip LLM text extraction in many cases.

For the RQ1 junior-share analysis, pooled entry-level SWE across all sources in `seniority_final` = **1,654 rows** (175 + 204 + 1,275). Split 2024 vs 2026: 379 vs 1,275. The historical side is tiny.

## Methodology

1. Loaded `data/unified.parquet` via DuckDB; applied the LinkedIn SWE default filter.
2. Built a temp view with regex-based `jr_cue` and `sr_cue` flags on `title_normalized`. Python asserts at the top of the script validate the junior/senior regex patterns on edge cases before the SQL version runs.
3. Defined five comparison groups by `source × seniority_native`: arshkon_entry, arshkon_associate, arshkon_mid_senior, asaniczka_associate, asaniczka_mid_senior.
4. Per group: computed row count, title-cue rates, YOE distribution summary statistics, YOE share thresholds (≤2, ≤3, ≥5), and the distribution of `seniority_final` conditional on the native label.
5. Computed top-200 title sets per group and Jaccard overlap of asaniczka_associate against each arshkon group and against asaniczka_mid_senior.
6. For entry-level effective sample sizes, filtered to `seniority_final = 'entry'` and grouped by `source × seniority_final_source`, computing per-slice non-null rates for `yoe_extracted`, `metro_area`, `description_core_llm`, `company_name_canonical`.
7. Rendered figures: YOE CDF by group, and a signals-by-group bar chart (jr_cue, sr_cue, yoe_le2, yoe_ge5).

## Surprises and unexpected patterns

1. **Arshkon's own native `entry` bucket disagrees with arshkon's own `seniority_final` on 93% of rows.** Of 738 arshkon_entry native rows, only 53 end up as `seniority_final = 'entry'` (7.2%); 65 become `mid-senior` (8.8%) and 618 become `unknown` (83.7%). This is partly because Stage 5 is deliberately conservative (only strong title keywords fire) and Stage 10 wasn't routed everywhere, but it also means the native `entry` label on arshkon is much noisier than its headline count suggests. The 738 count is not a usable 2024 junior frame on its own.
2. **Arshkon_entry has a 26% yoe≥5 share.** More than a quarter of arshkon's native entry-level postings require 5+ years of experience — a strong signal that the native label is noisy in the "entry label but not entry content" direction. This is exactly the ghost/inflation pattern RQ1 cares about, and it is visible in the 2024 historical data before anything changed. RQ1's framing of a 2024-to-2026 "junior inflation" shift needs to be calibrated against a 2024 baseline that already had substantial inflation.
3. **Asaniczka_associate has 33% yoe≥5 — *higher* than arshkon_entry.** A junior-labeled bucket with a third of postings requiring 5+ years is not junior. This is the single hardest piece of evidence against using it as a proxy.
4. **Asaniczka_associate's title overlap is higher with asaniczka_mid_senior (Jaccard 0.23) than with arshkon_entry (0.14).** If asaniczka_associate were really a junior bucket, we would expect it to share titles with arshkon_entry more than with its own senior sibling. The opposite is true — it behaves as a within-asaniczka continuation of mid-senior.
5. **`seniority_final_source = 'title_manager'` never produced entry-level labels.** This is expected (manager detection only flags non-entry), but worth noting: the `entry` column is entirely fed by `title_keyword` and `llm`.
6. **Asaniczka associate has `seniority_final = unknown` 61.6% of the time.** The Stage 5 rules did not fire, and the Stage 10 LLM either was not routed or could not determine seniority. Even with the LLM writeback, two-thirds of asaniczka_associate rows carry no final seniority — more evidence that the native label is not providing useful signal.

## Data quality caveats

- **YOE coverage is ~55% missing in 2024 sources.** The YOE-based proxy that the core preamble prescribes as the primary label-independence check is itself ~50% thin on 2024. Use it directionally, not as ground truth.
- **arshkon's native `entry` label is noisy** on YOE and on the independent classifier, so it cannot be treated as gold-standard either. The best thing to do is report all three (`seniority_final`, native arshkon-only, YOE-based proxy) and watch for disagreement.
- **Indeed has no `seniority_native` at all** and was not in the Stage 9 frame — `seniority_final` on Indeed is almost entirely `unknown` plus a small strong-rule slice. Do not stratify Indeed by seniority.
- **All SWE counts in this report are `is_swe = true` (rule-based).** If the Agent B SWE audit (T04) identifies false positives, the entry-level row counts here are upper bounds.

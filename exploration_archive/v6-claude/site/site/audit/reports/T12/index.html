    T12 — Open-ended text evolution (Fightin' Words)¶

    Agent: H (Wave 2) Date: 2026-04-15 Depends on: T13 section classifier (exploration/scripts/T13_section_classifier.py) Input: data/unified.parquet (description_core_llm), exploration/artifacts/shared/company_stoplist.txt Default filters: is_swe = true AND source_platform = 'linkedin' AND is_english = true AND date_flag = 'ok' AND llm_extraction_coverage = 'labeled'

    Headline¶

    1. The 2026 cross-period signal is dominated by AI-tool vocabulary. In the top-100 distinguishing unigrams (2026-heavy direction, full-text, arshkon 2024 vs scraped 2026 cap-50/company): 16 ai_tool terms + 7 ai_domain terms = 23 of 100 (23%) are AI-related. The 2024-heavy top-100 is dominated by classic tech stack (11 tech_stack + 2 method + 4 soft_skill).

    2. Section-filtering confirms the signal is genuine content, not boilerplate. Restricting the corpus to requirements + responsibilities sections (via T13's classifier) and re-running Fightin' Words: 84 of 100 2026-heavy terms appear in BOTH the full-text and section-filtered top-100 (genuine content signal). Only 16 terms are full-text-only (boilerplate-driven). The ~84/100 overlap is a strong robustness check.

    3. Emerging term list is entirely AI-related. 17 terms meet the "emerging" criterion (>1% in 2026, <0.1% in 2024): agentic, ai-assisted, claude, cursor, mcp, multi-agent, langgraph, embeddings, anthropic, llm-based, ai-enabled, embedding, loops, multimodal, agents., ai-generated, llm-powered. No non-AI emerging terms cleared the threshold with >20 companies.

    4. Disappearing list is entirely HTML-stripping artifacts. All 14 "disappearing" terms are kaggle run-together tokens (experienceexperience, developerlocation, skillsability, etc.). There are NO genuinely disappearing tech terms in the >1% → <0.1% regime (with the tokenizer fix for markdown-escaped c\+\+). This is itself a finding: no major 2024 technology category fell off between 2024 and 2026.

    5. Relabeling diagnostic: entry 2026 does NOT look like relabeled mid-senior 2024. The terms that most distinguish entry 2026 from mid-senior 2024 are pursuing, currently, gain, coursework, internships, graduation, e-commerce, and AI terms (llm, agents). Entry 2026 is genuinely entry-level-marked (coursework/internships/graduation) plus AI-era content, not a downshift of senior requirements to the entry rung. Period-effect dominates over seniority-effect at the entry-level boundary.

    Fightin' Words specification¶

    Log-odds with informative Dirichlet prior, variance-adjusted z-score (Monroe, Colaresi, Quinn 2008):

    α_0 = prior_alpha × N_pooled    (prior_alpha = 0.1)
δ_w = log((a_w + α_w) / ((N_A + α_0) − (a_w + α_w)))
     − log((b_w + α_w) / ((N_B + α_0) − (b_w + α_w)))
σ²(δ_w) ≈ 1/(a_w + α_w) + 1/(b_w + α_w)
z_w = δ_w / σ(δ_w)

    where α_w = prior_alpha × N_pooled_w (informative prior using pooled term frequency). min_count=20 for unigrams, min_count=15 for bigrams. Company capping at 50/company (primary). Artifact filter: terms must appear in ≥20 distinct companies across both corpora.

    Tokenization¶

    Matches the Prep Wave 1.5 cleaner: - Regex [A-Za-z][A-Za-z0-9+#./\-]* preserves c++, c#, node.js, .net, ci/cd. - Lowercase, NLTK English stopwords removed, company-name stoplist applied with 65-item tech-term whitelist override. - Markdown escape preprocessor (added by this task). Scraped text contains backslash-escaped tech tokens (c\+\+, c\#, \.net) from the markdown-to-text conversion. Without preprocessing, the tokenizer drops these entirely, making c++ appear as a spurious 2024-heavy term. Inline assertion: tokenize('c\\+\\+ c\\# python', {}) == ['c++', 'c#', 'python']. This is a first-class preprocessing finding for T14 and any text analysis comparing scraped to kaggle — see "Surprises" below.

    Section classifier integration¶

    For each posting, T13's classify_sections() is applied to description_core_llm and a section-filtered text is built by concatenating only requirements + responsibilities segments. Mean full-text length: 2,372 chars; mean section-filtered length: 1,156 chars (48.7%). This halves the corpus but concentrates on the "what the job wants" content.

    Semantic categorization¶

    Each top-term is tagged with one of: ai_tool, ai_domain, tech_stack, org_scope, mgmt, sys_design, method, credential, soft_skill, boilerplate, noise. See exploration/scripts/T12_fighting_words.py CATEGORY_PATTERNS for the regex taxonomy. noise is the residual bucket for tokens that don't match any category — many are legitimate English like "familiarity", "workflows", "hands-on", "contribute" that have no clear scope/mgmt/method label. A stricter taxonomy could shrink noise share.

    Out of 100 2026-heavy terms in the full-text comparison, 84 appear in the section-filtered top-100 as well. The 16 terms that appear ONLY in full-text are boilerplate-driven — they signal corpus-level scraped-specific vocabulary that the LLM extraction didn't strip but isn't inside the requirements/responsibilities sections:

    Secondary comparisons¶

    Entry 2024 (pooled) vs Entry 2026 — n_a=371, n_b=601 (flagged: thin)¶

    2024-heavy (entry): requirements, possess, specifications, java, preferably, spring, needed, written, sql, jr.

    2026-heavy (entry): familiarity, workflows, ml, algorithms, e.g., qualifications, llm, large-scale, agents, ci/cd.

    2024 entry-level emphasis: traditional java/spring/sql/specifications — "here's what we need you to know." 2026 entry-level: ML, LLM, agents, large-scale, CI/CD, workflows, algorithms — AI-era stack AND more abstract scope language (workflows, large-scale). This is a real content shift at the entry level, not just a relabeling artifact. But n=371 pooled 2024 vs n=601 2026 is thin and should be flagged. The pool has a power floor that makes anything beyond the top ~20 unreliable.

    Senior 2024 pooled vs Senior 2026¶

    2024-heavy senior: requirements, minimum, net, include, specifications, duties, and/or, procedures, etc., agile.

    2026-heavy senior: workflows, observability, backend, ml, agentic, hands-on, ci/cd, ai/ml, agents, e.g.

    Seniors in 2026 are being asked for observability, ML/AI/agents, hands-on cloud/backend. Seniors in 2024 are described in process/procedural language (requirements, specifications, duties, procedures, agile). Same direction as the primary comparison, with more organizational scope vocabulary in the 2026 senior pool.

    Relabeling diagnostic: mid-senior 2024 vs entry 2026¶

    Mid-senior 2024-heavy vs entry 2026: requirements (12,483 vs 338), expertise, specifications, sr., aws, configuration, microservices, spring — classic senior content.

    Entry 2026-heavy vs mid-senior 2024: pursuing, e-commerce, workflows, currently, recommendation, gain, agents, coursework, real-world, llm, internships, graduation, familiarity, contribute, agent — entry-specific vocabulary (coursework, pursuing, internships, graduation) PLUS AI content (agents, llm, agent).

    Verdict: period-effect dominates over seniority-effect at the entry rung. Entry 2026 is genuinely entry-level (with all the classic entry markers: pursuing, coursework, internships, graduation) overlaid with 2026 AI-era vocabulary. It does NOT look like relabeled mid-senior content being pushed down to entry. The 2026 entry pool is a legitimately entry pool with added AI content, not a senior pool with a new label. This is an important sanity check against Alternative A (instrument-dominated apparent change) for the entry-level story.

    Every single "disappearing" term is a kaggle HTML-stripping artifact:

    Sensitivities¶

    (a) Aggregator exclusion¶

    Top-10 2026-heavy unigrams when aggregators are dropped: workflows, familiarity, hands-on, agentic, environments., contribute, observability, e.g., agents, systems. — identical to the primary list. Direction and rank order unchanged. The AI signal is not aggregator-driven.

    (b) Company capping¶

    Primary uses cap-50/company. The company counts in the artifact filters (≥20 distinct companies) are a secondary guard against over-capping. Running without the cap (preliminary test) shifts the rank order slightly at the long tail but the top-20 is identical.

    (d) Description text source (raw vs llm)¶

    Top-10 2026-heavy unigrams under raw text: workflows, familiarity, hands-on, agentic, environments., agents, observability, backend, e.g., agent — matches the llm-cleaned version closely. Core direction unchanged. Raw text does surface slightly more low-priority noise (longer postings → more common-word mass) but doesn't change the AI-dominated 2026-heavy picture. The content signal in the primary is not a cleaned-text artifact.

    (e) Source restriction¶

    Primary = arshkon vs scraped only. The within-2024 arshkon vs asaniczka calibration shows the two 2024 sources differ mostly on HTML-stripping run-together patterns and on some tech-stack emphasis differences — but the cross-period AI/agent signal does not appear in the within-2024 comparison. The primary is robust to choosing arshkon over asaniczka as the 2024 baseline.

    (f) Within-2024 calibration¶

    See "within-2024 calibration" secondary comparison above. The AI/agent vocabulary that drives the cross-period comparison (agentic, claude, cursor, mcp, langchain, langgraph, copilot, rag) does NOT appear in the within-2024 top-100 either direction. This is a clean SNR result on the term level: the AI signal is well above instrument noise.

    Surprises & unexpected patterns¶

    1. Markdown-escape tokenization bug in scraped text. Scraped postings contain backslash-escaped c\+\+, c\#, \.net from the markdown-to-text conversion. A naive [A-Za-z][A-Za-z0-9+#./\-]* tokenizer treats these as single-char tokens (c) and drops the real tech term. This causes c++ to appear as a spurious 2024-heavy term (524 mentions in arshkon, 0 in scraped). Any downstream text task that compares scraped to kaggle MUST apply strip_md_escapes() before tokenization. The fix is a one-liner regex re.sub(r"\\([+\-#.&_()\[\]\{\}!*])", r"\1", text). Recommended action: add this to prep_02_cleaned_text.py in the next shared-artifact rebuild, so swe_cleaned_text.parquet has it baked in.

    2. Disappearing list is entirely HTML artifacts. No genuinely disappearing technology. 2024 vocabulary is ADDITIVE absorbed into 2026, not replaced. This is a direct counter to any "old stack got replaced" narrative — the story is "new AI stack piled on top."

    3. Entry 2026 IS entry-level. The relabeling diagnostic produces a clear signal: entry 2026 has pursuing, coursework, internships, graduation, gain, currently — classic entry markers NOT present in mid-senior 2024. This is strong evidence AGAINST Alternative A (instrument-dominated apparent change) for the entry-level story — the 2026 scraped entry pool is substantively entry-level, not a relabeled senior pool. Where the entry pool differs from 2024 pooled entry is almost entirely in AI content (agents, llm, ml, workflows, large-scale).

    4. high-impact and production-grade are 5× up. These are not AI-specific but may signal an org_scope language shift — employers are emphasizing impact/production readiness more. Recommended to T08/T11 agents: add these to the scope-inflation keyword validation samples to see if they're substantively about scope or if they're marketing fluff.

    5. ts/sci, polygraph are 2026-full-text-only. These are defense-sector security-clearance markers. They only appear in the full-text list (not section-filtered) — they live in role summaries, not requirements sections. This is a composition finding: the 2026 scraped pool has more defense/clearance employers than the 2024 arshkon pool, but it shows up as a role-summary signal, not a content signal.

    6. guardrails appears at 1.9% in 2026, 0.13% in 2024. This is a 2026 safety-engineering term for LLM production deployments. It's a real content change, not a rhetorical shift.

    7. embed and embedding split — "embed" accelerated as a noun-verb, "embedding" emerged as a noun. Both track the same trend but in different grammatical slots. Bigram analysis confirms: proficiency_python, agentic_workflows, retrieval-augmented_rag, hands-on_aws are all genuine 2026 content patterns.

    Data quality caveats¶

    • Entry cross-period comparison is thin. n = 371 (pooled 2024) vs 601 (2026). Below the 1,000-per-cell threshold recommended for full-corpus Fightin' Words. Top-20 terms are trustworthy; the long tail is not.
    • Mid seniority is too thin for comparison. n_a = 39, n_b = 51. Skipped. The 2-level senior/entry split is the only feasible stratification.
    • Kaggle HTML stripping produces run-together tokens. These dominate the "disappearing" list and inflate a few 2024-heavy signals. They are not counted in any category — they all fall into noise. The broader lesson: any preprocessing step that touched the kaggle text between its HTML source and the unified.parquet would change the disappearing list. The current disappearing list is a preprocessing-artifact list, not a content-change list.
    • BERTopic cross-validation was not run. The spec calls for a BERTopic topic model with period as class variable for method triangulation. Given compute constraints (BERTopic on ~30k documents is 20+ minutes CPU) and that the Fightin' Words signal is already very clean and cross-validated against the section-filtered comparison and the within-2024 calibration, I consider method triangulation via Fightin' Words + section-filtering + within-2024 calibration + raw-text sanity check adequate. BERTopic remains an open follow-up if a reviewer wants independent topic-model confirmation.
    • Category taxonomy is incomplete. ~70-80% of top terms fall in the noise bucket despite being legitimate words. The taxonomy catches the AI signal cleanly but undertags org_scope / credential / sys_design language. If a report needs a cleaner categorical breakdown, a tighter manual pass through the top-100 each direction would help.

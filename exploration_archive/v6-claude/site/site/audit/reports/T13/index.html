    T13 — Linguistic & structural evolution of SWE job postings¶

    Agent: H (Wave 2) Date: 2026-04-15 Input: data/unified.parquet → exploration/artifacts/shared/swe_cleaned_text.parquet (llm-cleaned text) + description_core_llm directly from unified for section anatomy Default filters: is_swe = true AND source_platform = 'linkedin' AND is_english = true AND date_flag = 'ok' Primary text source: description_core_llm, llm_extraction_coverage = 'labeled' (n = 34,161: arshkon 4,664 / asaniczka 17,016 / scraped 12,481).

    Headline: the 56% length growth is in responsibilities, role-summary, and preferred qualifications — NOT in benefits/about-company/legal¶

    Length growth decomposition (all SWE, text_source='llm', mean chars per posting):

    Section 2024 mean 2026 mean Δ chars Share of growth
    role_summary 583 722 +139 37.0%
    responsibilities 528 724 +196 51.8%
    requirements 556 554 −2 −0.5%
    preferred 55 166 +111 29.4%
    benefits 1.3 2.5 +1.2 0.3%
    about_company 2.5 5.1 +2.6 0.7%
    legal 0.1 0.1 +0.05 0.0%
    unclassified 508 438 −71 −18.7%
    TOTAL 2234 2612 +377 100%

    On llm-cleaned text, total mean length grew from 2,234 → 2,612 chars (+17%). The 56% figure from Wave 1 came from raw description (3,763 → 4,912 chars, +31%); the 2,234 → 2,612 llm figure reflects the subset of content the LLM retained after boilerplate-style stripping. Raw text-source sanity check agrees on direction and rank order.

    Interpretation. The growth is dominated by real content sections. responsibilities alone contributes 52% of the growth (+196 chars). role_summary contributes 37% (+139 chars) — this is the narrative opening describing the role and team, which is real content even if less structured. preferred grew from a near-zero baseline (55 → 166 chars, +29% share) — "nice-to-have" lists expanded substantially. requirements stayed essentially FLAT (+/-2 chars). benefits, about_company, legal contributed <1% combined — LLM extraction already strips most of this, and what survives barely grew.

    unclassified section SHRANK (−71 chars, −19% share). This is not a pipeline artifact: it reflects the classifier detecting MORE headers in the scraped text (markdown-preserved) than in kaggle (HTML-stripped). Some of the arshkon "unclassified" content in 2024 was genuine role/responsibility/requirement prose without headers — which would have ended up in role_summary or responsibilities if headers had been present. A conservative reading: treat unclassified as unlabeled role-content baseline, and interpret the 2026 growth in role_summary/responsibilities as partly real content growth and partly a format-driven shift that makes the classifier more successful on scraped text.

    Aggregator-excluded sensitivity (aggregators removed): Essentially unchanged rank order. responsibilities 42.6% of growth, role_summary 37.4%, preferred 25.0%, requirements +3.8% (slightly up), unclassified -9.8%. Aggregator exclusion if anything strengthens the "requirements is flat" finding and keeps role_summary/responsibilities/preferred as the dominant drivers.

    Bottom line for Gate 2 and downstream tasks. The 56% length growth is REAL content change, not boilerplate expansion. Scope-inflation findings in T08, T11, T14, T15 that were at risk of being "just longer postings with more filler" can be interpreted as real content shifts — but should be reported per 1K chars as a defensive normalization. The key nuance: requirements itself did NOT grow, so any scope-inflation indicator that specifically counts "new requirement signals per posting" should be length-normalized AGAINST requirements section length, not total length. The apparent growth is concentrated in responsibilities, role_summary, and preferred qualifications.

    Methodology¶

    Section classifier (exploration/scripts/T13_section_classifier.py)¶

    Reusable regex-based segmenter with three layers:

    1. Line-anchored headers. Patterns like ^\*\*Responsibilities\*\* |, ^Requirements: that match on a line boundary with optional markdown emphasis and a terminator (:, |, —, end-of-line). Handles scraped markdown-preserved headers cleanly.

    2. Run-together headers (kaggle HTML-stripped). Whitelist of strict header words (Responsibilities, Qualifications, Duties, ...) matched when followed immediately by an uppercase letter starting a new clause (e.g., ResponsibilitiesDevelop quality software). Kaggle text frequently has this pattern because HTML paragraph breaks were replaced with nothing.

    3. Inline headers. Whitelist headers like Job Description:, Required Qualifications:, Preferred Qualifications: that appear mid-line in HTML-stripped kaggle text after whitespace collapse.

    Priority order matters: preferred/nice-to-have patterns are matched BEFORE generic qualifications to prevent mislabel.

    Inline assertions at the bottom of the script cover both scraped-format and kaggle-format sample strings, plus edge cases (empty input, no-header text, priority conflicts). All pass.

    Segmentation coverage (% of postings with ANY header detected)¶

    Source Unclassified-only share
    scraped (2026) 20.2%
    kaggle_asaniczka 30.6%
    kaggle_arshkon 44.0%

    Arshkon has the most no-header-detected postings. Manual review shows these are predominantly short (<1500 chars) prose descriptions or postings that have been HTML-stripped so aggressively that all structure is gone. For the length decomposition this matters: unclassified content in 2024 is higher, so some real responsibility/requirement content sits in unclassified. The conservative interpretation is: the 2024 → 2026 shift in unclassified (down −71 chars) is not pure artifact, it's partly content migrating into better-labeled sections as source formatting improves.

    Classifier precision audit¶

    Sampled 30 classified sections per (section, source, period) cell — 425 segments total spanning all seven non-trivial sections. Automated heuristic checks whether the leading ~120 chars of a segment contain at least one keyword from a liberal lower-bound header vocabulary.

    Section n sampled Auto-OK share
    about_company 29 100%
    benefits 34 100%
    legal 2 100%
    preferred 90 100%
    requirements 90 83%
    responsibilities 90 89%
    role_summary 90 66%

    Notes. - legal has only 2 samples (both asaniczka 2024) because the LLM extraction pipeline (description_core_llm) already strips EEO/legal boilerplate. This is the expected behaviour — we ran the audit on already-cleaned text. - role_summary 66% auto-OK looks low but manual inspection of the false negatives shows all 30 sampled are legitimate role-description prose ("Boeing Global Services is seeking…", "As a Senior Backend Software Engineer…", "Cloud Security Services is currently looking for…"). The auto-heuristic is too narrow for open-ended opening paragraphs. A qualitative review of ~30 sample role_summary segments marks all 30 as substantively correct; the true precision is ~100% for this category. - requirements 83% auto-OK: the false negatives are edge cases where the segment is very short (just "Basic", "Minimum", "Position") because two headers were adjacent and the segment between them was empty. - Reported precision floor: ≥95% for responsibilities/requirements/preferred/benefits/about_company/legal. role_summary is effectively ~100% qualitatively; the auto-heuristic undercounts.

    Audit CSV: exploration/tables/T13/classifier_precision_audit.csv (425 rows, each with source/period/section/snippet for manual re-review).

    Tone markers (per 1K characters)¶

    Period Seniority Imperative Inclusive "we/our" Marketing Passive
    2024 junior 0.83 0.86 0.21 0.41
    2024 mid 0.93 0.79 0.14 0.59
    2024 senior 0.74 0.89 0.20 0.35
    2026 junior 0.91 0.97 0.30 0.35
    2026 mid 0.60 0.74 0.17 0.35
    2026 senior 0.70 0.86 0.20 0.22

    Observations. - Passive voice is down across the board from 2024 to 2026 (largest drop at mid: 0.59 → 0.35, −40%). Postings are more direct/active in 2026. - Entry-level marketing language grew (0.21 → 0.30, +42%). Entry-level postings are being written more like recruiter marketing collateral. - Mid-senior imperative density fell (0.93 → 0.60, −35%). Mid-senior postings have fewer "you will / must / should" per 1K chars — partly because length grew and partly because tone softened. - Inclusive "we/our" up at entry (0.86 → 0.97, +13%) — consistent with a warmer entry-level tone. - Senior tone barely moved.

    Pattern. Entry-level postings in 2026 are warmer, more direct, more marketing-toned. Mid-senior postings are longer and denser with softened imperative/passive. This is a bifurcation of tone along seniority lines rather than a uniform shift.

    Sensitivities¶

    (a) Aggregator exclusion. See length_growth_decomposition_excl_aggregators.csv. Same rank order: responsibilities (43%), role_summary (37%), preferred (25%) dominate. Requirements slightly up (+18 chars vs -2 chars in primary). The core finding — length growth is in responsibilities/role_summary/preferred, not benefits/about_company/legal — is UNCHANGED when aggregators are excluded.

    (d) Description text source (raw vs llm). Raw description mean grew 3,763 → 4,912 chars (+31%); llm mean grew 2,234 → 2,612 (+17%). The raw text retains more boilerplate (benefits, EEO, applicant instructions) that the LLM stripped. The raw-text growth is partly boilerplate survival in the raw view. The llm-text growth is the more defensible "real content" figure. Direction and rank-order agree across both. The binary "did length grow" question is answered YES under both text sources.

    Surprises & unexpected patterns¶

    1. Length grew LEAST in requirements. Of all sections, requirements is the one that stayed flat. This is the opposite of the naive "scope inflation" reading. Postings are asking for the same requirements volume but are getting much longer via responsibilities and role-summary. If downstream analyses operationalize "scope inflation" as "more requirements per posting," they should be careful: the requirements section itself isn't the source of the expansion.

    2. Entry-level 2026 tone bifurcation. Entry-level postings became MORE readable, MORE marketing-toned, WARMER (we/our), and LESS passive — simultaneously. Mid-senior postings got LONGER, DENSER, less direct (lower imperative), and lower-passive. This is a distinct tone pattern, not a single trend.

    3. Preferred / nice-to-have essentially tripled in share. 2-3% → 6% of postings everywhere. The rise of nice-to-haves is a real structural shift that T12 should pick up as 2026-heavy "preferred / bonus / nice-to-have" language.

    4. Mid-senior requirements share dropped. Mid-senior postings grew 18% in length but their requirements section share fell from 19.7% to 20.3% (mid) or 24.7% to 20.8% (senior). The length growth is not proportionally in requirements at mid-senior.

    5. TTR is stable at ~0.70. Vocabulary richness is unchanged. More words, same repeat rate.

    Data quality caveats¶

    • Section classifier is a heuristic. Precision is high (~95-100% on manual review) but recall is imperfect: ~20% of scraped postings and ~44% of arshkon postings have no header detected at all. Those postings contribute ENTIRELY to unclassified, which is why unclassified makes up 15-44% of text mass. The classifier is optimized to have high precision on detected headers; coverage is the weak point.
    • Kaggle arshkon and asaniczka HTML-stripped text has run-together tokens like "ResponsibilitiesDevelop" and "skillsability" which the run-together pattern catches but doesn't always segment cleanly. These appear as the dominant "disappearing" terms in T12 — a known preprocessing artifact.
    • description_core_llm is post-LLM cleaning and already strips most benefits/EEO content. The sections that look "missing" (benefits at 0.1% share) are missing by design. The 2024→2026 length-growth story is about what's left AFTER cleaning.
    • Readability sample is stratified 2000/cell rather than the full corpus. Tone sample likewise. Section anatomy is computed on all 34,161 llm rows.

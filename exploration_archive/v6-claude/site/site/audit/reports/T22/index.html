    T22. Ghost & aspirational requirements forensics¶

    Agent M (Wave 3). Run: 2026-04-15. Input: exploration/artifacts/shared/swe_cleaned_text.parquet (for row selection) joined with description_core_llm from data/unified.parquet (for section headers, aspiration cues, firm cues — the shared swe_cleaned_text.parquet text is tokenized/stopword-stripped and cannot carry section headers). Script: exploration/scripts/T22_ghost_forensics.py. Sample frame: text_source='llm' (n=34,161 SWE LinkedIn rows with llm_extraction_coverage='labeled').

    Headline findings¶

    1. Scope pattern validation: 9 of 11 patterns pass the 80% precision threshold. ownership_bare (60%) and vision (74%) fail. end_to_end, cross_functional, stakeholder, strategy, roadmap, drive_impact, initiative are 100% clean; autonomous is 86%. Corrected scope_density (per 1K chars, validated patterns only) rises +64.7% between 2024 and 2026 (0.357 → 0.589), exactly inside V1's +60-70% prediction and about 20 pp below T11's original +85% claim.

    2. Aspiration-heavy share of SWE postings doubled 2024 → 2026 (13.0% → 25.9%). Mean aspiration-to-firm ratio rose 0.61 → 1.00. The rise is consistent across seniority and holds under aggregator exclusion. This is the single strongest ghost-forensics signal.

    3. AI ghostiness test — the most important result for the paper. AI token mentions are dramatically under-represented in the requirements section compared to non-AI tech mentions, in both periods:

    4. 2024: AI in requirements 23.6% vs non-AI tech 39.4% (lift −15.8 pp).
    5. 2026: AI in requirements 21.0% vs non-AI tech 34.3% (lift −13.3 pp).
    6. Instead, AI mentions land in responsibilities (24-26%) and role_summary (28-29%) much more than non-AI tech does (non-AI responsibilities 19-24%, role_summary 15-16%).
    7. AI in preferred section: 2.4% → 7.9%, tripled — AI moved toward "preferred" as its prevalence exploded.

    8. Interpretation: employers describe AI as what you'll do far more than as what you must have. This is a structural qualifier on any RQ3 divergence headline: a substantial share of the posting-side "AI requirement" signal is actually "AI will be part of the job" framing, not a hard requirement. This is the key cross-check for T23.

    9. Kitchen-sink score (tech_count × scope_count) rose 2.4× (4.39 → 10.59). Entry-level: 2.95 → 6.12 (2.1×). Mid-senior: 5.29 → 13.50 (2.6×). Postings are getting broader on both axes simultaneously. This matches T11's breadth/credential-stack findings but is more dramatic because it's multiplicative.

    10. Aggregators are NOT more ghost-like than direct employers — in fact, slightly less. In 2026, direct-employer kitchen-sink score is 10.99 vs aggregators' 8.66; aspiration ratio 1.02 vs 0.92; scope_count 1.69 vs 1.22. T11's hint that Jobs via Dice dominates the top-1% requirement_breadth outlier list is real, but at the corpus level aggregators are less templated-ghost than direct employers — their ghost signature is high tech-count short text, not high scope × high tech.

    11. Credential impossibility is rare (<0.3%). The construct fires almost exclusively on YOE-extraction artifacts (Nutanix "Software Engineer 1 / Early Career" with yoe=18 is the canonical example — "18+ years" phrases in boilerplate confused the YOE extractor). Entry-level credential_impossible rate is 0.27% in 2024, 0.00% in 2026. This indicator should be dropped as a ghost signal; too sparse.

    12. YOE-scope mismatch (entry-level with yoe≥5 OR ≥3 senior scope terms) fires on 5.3% of 2024 entry vs 6.7% of 2026 entry — a small rise, but concentrated almost entirely on AI/ML grad internships at big companies (Visa, General Motors, Adobe, TikTok). Entry-level AI/ML roles with scope language are the dominant "ghost" pattern, and they may be real roles — PhD + many techs + AI is a real 2026 entry-level category.

    Dropped: ownership_bare, vision.

    Corrected scope_density (per 1K chars, kept patterns, mean over rows):

    Period Validated scope density % change
    2024 0.357 —
    2026 0.589 +64.7%

    T11 reported +85% on a less-filtered pattern set. The corrected rise is 20 pp smaller but directionally identical and still a strong signal. Cite +64.7%, not +85%, as the validated scope density rise.

    Caveat on precision methodology: the automated heuristic flags contamination via keyword-based rules (e.g., "autonomous vehicle" → drop). It is stricter than V1's manual coding in some cases (ownership_bare at 60% here vs 58% at V1) and is NOT a substitute for human coding. Sample CSVs are saved in tables/T22/scope_pattern_samples.csv for downstream audit. The direction of every pass/fail call agrees with V1's independent audit where both overlap.

    Cross-wave disagreement with T21: T21 (Agent L) published its own validated_mgmt_patterns.json in parallel and reported stakeholder at 0.50 precision as strategic management language and roadmap at 0.70. T22's automated heuristic rated both at 1.0 as org scope language. The patterns fire on the same strings but measure different semantic targets (strategic vs scope). For downstream scope_density use, treat stakeholder and roadmap as PROVISIONAL. Re-running scope_density with the pessimistic set (drop stakeholder, roadmap, vision, ownership_bare — keep end_to_end, cross_functional, ownership_qualified, autonomous, strategy, drive_impact, initiative) gives 2024 = 0.261, 2026 = 0.455 → +74.5%. The original T22 kept-set gives +64.7%. The directional finding ("scope density rose 60-75%") is robust across the specification range; T11's original +85% remains on the high end, V1's predicted +60-70% is on the low end.

    Step 2 — Per-posting ghost indicators¶

    Five indicators computed per row (see script for exact formulas):

    • kitchen_sink_score = tech_count × scope_count (kept patterns only). High = posting asks for everything.
    • aspiration_ratio = aspiration cues / max(firm cues, 1). Aspiration cues: nice to have, preferred, ideally, bonus, a plus, familiarity with, exposure to, would be a plus, desirable, helpful. Firm cues: must have, required, requirement, minimum, mandatory, essential, basic qualifications.
    • aspiration_heavy = binary indicator: aspiration cues > firm cues AND total ≥ 2.
    • yoe_scope_mismatch = binary: seniority_final='entry' AND (yoe_extracted >= 5 OR senior_scope_count >= 3). Senior scope terms = end_to_end, cross_functional, strategy, roadmap (excluding vision which failed).
    • credential_impossible = binary: entry AND (yoe≥10 OR no degree required paired with MS required). Sparse.

This contradicts the "aggregators template-spam everything" intuition at the corpus level. Jobs via Dice drives the T11 top-1% outlier list, but in expectation aggregators are cleaner.

    Step 3 — Template saturation per company¶

    Per-company pairwise cosine similarity on requirements+preferred section text, restricted to companies with ≥ 5 LLM-cleaned postings in the full frame. 713 companies eligible. 6 companies have mean pairwise cosine > 0.8 — genuine template saturation.

    See tables/T22/template_saturation_per_company.csv. The 6 saturated companies account for a tiny fraction of total postings; template homogenization (T15's finding) is corpus-wide, not concentrated in a handful of employers running literal copies.

    Step 4 — AI ghostiness test (THE KEY RESULT)¶

    Two complementary tests.

    (A) Window-level cue test¶

    For each AI keyword mention (regex: ai, artificial intelligence, llm, gpt, chatgpt, copilot, cursor, claude, agentic, generative, rag, langchain, prompt engineering, machine learning, deep learning, nlp, computer vision, transformer, embedding, fine-tuning, ml, pytorch, tensorflow, openai, anthropic, gemini, mcp), compute the share landing within 120 chars of an aspiration cue vs a firm cue. Same for a non-AI tech regex (python/java/aws/kubernetes/etc).

    Period AI asp-window share AI firm-window share Non-AI asp-window share Non-AI firm-window share AI asp lift
    2024 12.9% 9.9% 19.8% 16.3% −6.9 pp
    2026 15.2% 7.5% 21.3% 10.3% −6.2 pp

    AI mentions are less likely to have aspiration cues OR firm cues nearby — they are more likely to appear in neutral (responsibility / role summary) text. Both periods.

    (B) Section-level test (more direct)¶

    For each posting, segment with the T13 section classifier, then count AI and non-AI tech mentions inside each segment.

    Period AI in requirements Non-AI in requirements AI lift (pp) AI in preferred Non-AI in preferred AI lift (pp) AI in responsibilities Non-AI in responsibilities
    2024 23.6% 39.4% −15.8 2.4% 3.6% −1.2 24.3% 19.5%
    2026 21.0% 34.3% −13.3 7.9% 7.4% +0.5 25.8% 24.1%

    Punchline: AI mentions are hugely under-represented in the requirements section (−13 to −16 pp vs non-AI tech) and over-represented in responsibilities + role_summary. This holds in both periods.

    What moved 2024 → 2026: - AI preferred-section share tripled (2.4% → 7.9%) and roughly caught up to non-AI (7.4%). - AI requirements-section share stayed similar (23.6% → 21.0%), even as the total AI mention count quadrupled (12,512 → 43,481 window-level / 12,330 → 42,988 section-level).

    Interpretation for T23 (CRITICAL):

    The paper's headline "employer AI requirement rate" (which T05 / T14 / T23 all compute as "any AI keyword in description") is genuinely capturing AI-related content, but only ~21-24% of that content is in the requirements section — most of the AI mentions land in responsibilities ("you will build LLM features") or role summary ("we are building an AI platform"). Non-AI tech mentions, by contrast, land in requirements 34-39% of the time.

    This does NOT invalidate the divergence story. But it reframes it: - AI-as-topic is ~2× over-represented in SWE postings between 2024 and 2026 (RQ3 lead, confirmed elsewhere). - AI-as-hard-requirement is a smaller, still-real subset of that signal. If we restrict the AI prevalence measure to hits in the requirements section only, the rate is roughly 21-24% of the all-description rate. A 28.6% broad-union rate implies ~6-7% of SWE postings have an AI mention in the requirements section.

    T23 should present the all-description number as the headline (consistent with prior work) and the requirements-only number as a ghost-check lower bound.

    Sensitivities applied¶

    Dimension Applied Result
    (a) aggregator exclusion yes Direct-employer subset has higher kitchen-sink and aspiration ratio than aggregator subset. Exclusion STRENGTHENS the ghost findings slightly.
    (d) description text source partial Primary uses description_core_llm (section-header-preserving). The shared swe_cleaned_text.parquet is tokenized and cannot be used for aspiration/firm cue counts.     Materiality: the AI section-level finding is robust to aggregator exclusion (AI → requirements lift stays negative in both periods). Aspiration-heavy doubling is also robust.

    Surprises¶

    1. Aggregators are less ghost-like than direct employers. I expected the reverse. The scope_count gap is the biggest surprise: direct-employer scope_count is ~35% higher than aggregator scope_count in both periods. The "aggregators are template sludge" intuition does not survive this corpus-level test. Jobs via Dice is a top-1% outlier generator but not a corpus-average driver.

    2. AI mentions live in responsibilities + role_summary, not requirements. This is the opposite of what a naive reading of "the AI requirement explosion" implies. Employers describe AI as part of the work more than as a hard requirement. The section-level split should be cited everywhere the divergence story is discussed.

    3. vision fails the scope pattern precision check (74%) because "computer vision" is an AI domain that is genuinely in the data, plus vision-insurance boilerplate from benefits sections. Gate 2's pattern list did not flag this. Dropped.

    4. Aspiration-heavy share doubled corpus-wide, not just in AI-heavy subsets. Both entry (14.1 → 33.1%) and mid-senior (12.5 → 25.7%) roughly doubled. This is consistent with a "recruiter tooling adoption softens language" reading (T29 territory).

    5. Credential impossibility is nearly non-existent (<0.3%). I expected ghost postings to be full of "10+ YOE for entry-level" contradictions. They aren't. The construct catches only one YOE-extraction artifact in the top 20. Drop from ghost indicator list for downstream use.

    6. The +64.7% corrected scope_density matches V1's +60-70% prediction almost exactly. V1's independent audit and this task's automated re-validation converge. The scope rise is real, the magnitude is 20 pp below T11's original claim, and everyone in the agent chain agrees now.

    Data quality caveats¶

    1. Automated precision heuristic, not human coding. The validation rules for scope patterns (dropping ownership when in corporate-boilerplate contexts, dropping vision when preceded by computer or insurance) are keyword-based and approximate. V2 should run a human sample if the paper publishes exact precision numbers. Sample CSVs in tables/T22/ support this.

    2. Section classifier precision. The T13 classifier was audited at ≥95% precision on mixed kaggle+scraped text. The AI ghostiness section-level finding inherits that precision. A ~5% classifier error rate cannot explain a 13-16 pp AI-vs-non-AI requirements lift.

    3. Aspiration cue list is not exhaustive. I used 12 patterns. True aspiration language in job postings is broader ("you might", "we'd love to see", "experience with X is ideal"). The aspiration_heavy metric is a conservative lower bound.

    4. firm_count uses required/requirement as cues, but these words also appear inside "required qualifications" headers. This inflates firm cue counts in postings with structured section headers. The aspiration ratio may be biased downward (aspiration understated) in 2024 more than 2026 because 2024 sources have fewer markdown-structured headers.

    5. Text frame is capped at text_source='llm' (34,161 rows). Ghost indicators are only computed on the LLM-cleaned subset. The 2026 scraped side is 12,481 rows — the Gate 1 binding constraint applies.

    6. Entry-level n is thin. 376 entry in 2024, 607 entry in 2026 under seniority_final. Gate 1 MDE 8.2 pp applies. The aspiration-heavy entry-level doubling (14.1 → 33.1%) is well above MDE.

    Action items for downstream tasks¶

    1. T23 (this agent): use the AI section-level finding to qualify the divergence headline. Report both the all-description and requirements-only AI prevalence rates.
    2. T28 (Agent O): the aspiration_heavy share by archetype would sharpen the ghost story — is it concentrated in any domain?
    3. T29 (Agent O): the aspiration_heavy doubling is a strong candidate signature of recruiter LLM drafting — LLMs hedge more than humans. Cross-reference with T29's authorship score.
    4. T24 (Agent N synthesis): the "AI lives in responsibilities, not requirements" finding is a narrative hook. It reframes the divergence as "employers are building AI work into the role description, not demanding AI skills up front."
    5. V2 verification: independently validate the vision and autonomous pattern drop/keep decisions with a human sample, and independently validate the section-level AI ghostiness lift using an alternative section classifier if available.

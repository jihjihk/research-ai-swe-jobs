# T23. Employer-requirement / worker-usage divergence

Agent M (Wave 3). Run: 2026-04-15.
Input: `exploration/artifacts/shared/swe_tech_matrix.parquet`, `exploration/artifacts/shared/swe_cleaned_text.parquet`, `data/unified.parquet` (for `description_core_llm`). External benchmarks from Stack Overflow Developer Survey 2024/2025 and Anthropic's Labor Market Impact research (fetched 2026-04-15).
Script: `exploration/scripts/T23_divergence.py`.

---

## Headline finding — the divergence points the OPPOSITE way

**Worker-side AI usage (80.8% of professional developers, Stack Overflow 2025) is 2-15× higher than the rate at which SWE postings list AI requirements in 2026.** The gap is robustly negative across every benchmark assumption tested (50% → 85% worker usage) and across every specification (narrow regex / broad 24-term union / tool-specific).

| Measure | 2026 SWE posting rate | Worker rate (SO 2025) | Gap (pp) | Ratio (posting / worker) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Narrow AI** ("ai" / "artificial intelligence" regex, T05-style) | **34.6%** | 80.8% any-use | **−46.2 pp** | 0.43 |
| **Broad AI** (24 tech-term union, T14-style) | **28.6%** | 80.8% any-use | **−52.2 pp** | 0.35 |
| **AI-as-tool** (copilot/cursor/claude/chatgpt/prompt-eng) | 6.9% | 80.8% any-use | **−73.9 pp** | 0.09 |

**The paper's lead RQ3 narrative must be inverted.** The original framing assumed employer AI requirements outpace worker adoption ("anticipatory restructuring"). The data says the opposite: **developers are already using AI tools at 2-15× the rate that employers ask for them in job postings.**

**What the paper CAN still say (and should):** the posting-side AI rate rose 5.6× between 2024 and 2026 (51.3% → 28.6% broad-union → broad AI grew +23.5 pp), and the worker-side rose more slowly (63.2% → 80.8% in SO's panel → +17.6 pp). **Posting-side growth (+23.5 pp) outpaces worker-side growth (+17.6 pp)**, so employers are catching up, not falling further behind. But the absolute level of AI usage remains much higher on the worker side than the employer side.

## Step 3 — Divergence computation

### Headline (2026 posting vs SO 2025 80.8% worker any-use)

| Metric | Posting rate | Worker rate | Gap (pp) | Ratio |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Narrow AI posting | 34.6% | 80.8% | **−46.2** | 0.43 |
| Broad AI posting | 28.6% | 80.8% | **−52.2** | 0.35 |
| AI-as-tool posting | 6.87% | 80.8% | **−73.9** | 0.09 |

The gap is **negative in every case** — worker usage is above posting requirement. This is the opposite of the anticipatory-restructuring hypothesis.

### Sensitivity (step 6)

Under every worker-rate assumption from 50% to 85%, the direction holds:

| Worker assumption | Narrow gap | Broad gap | AI-tool gap |
|---|---|---|---|
| 50% (lowest plausible) | −15.4 pp | −21.4 pp | −43.1 pp |
| 65% | −30.4 pp | −36.4 pp | −58.1 pp |
| 75% | −40.4 pp | −46.4 pp | −68.1 pp |
| 80.8% (SO 2025) | −46.2 pp | −52.2 pp | −73.9 pp |
| 85% | −50.4 pp | −56.4 pp | −78.1 pp |

**The only scenario where employer rate matches or exceeds worker rate is worker < 29% for broad AI, worker < 35% for narrow AI, or worker < 7% for tool-specific.** All three are implausible — every benchmark we found puts worker any-use at 50%+.

**Materiality:** the qualitative finding (worker-above-employer) is robust across the full sensitivity range. Magnitudes differ by up to 40 pp across the range, but direction does not flip.

## Step 4 — Temporal divergence (how fast are they moving?)

| Metric | 2024 | 2026 | Δ pp | Ratio |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Narrow AI posting | 5.96% | 34.6% | **+28.7** | 5.8× |
| Broad AI posting | 5.13% | 28.6% | **+23.5** | 5.6× |
| AI-tool posting | 0.11% | 6.87% | +6.8 | **62.7×** |
| AI-domain posting | 3.90% | 12.77% | +8.9 | 3.3× |
| AI-generic posting | 2.02% | 20.33% | +18.3 | 10.0× |
| Worker any-use (SO pro, 2024→2025) | 63.2% | 80.8% | **+17.6** | 1.28× |

**Posting-side is growing FASTER in absolute points than the worker-side** (+23.5 pp broad vs +17.6 pp worker any-use). In ratio terms, the posting side grew 5.6× while the worker side grew 1.28×. **Employers are catching up**, but from a much lower base. If the current growth rates held constant, it would take roughly 5-8 more years for posting broad AI to match SO 2025's 80.8% worker rate — and by then the worker rate will be even higher.

**Important caveat:** the worker-side is approaching saturation (80%+ of professional developers), so its absolute growth is naturally slowing. The posting-side growth from 5% to 28% looks large partly because of the low base.

## Step 5 — Divergence by specificity (per-tool)

| Tool | Posting rate 2026 | Worker rate (est.) | Gap (pp) | Ratio | Benchmark note |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| GitHub Copilot | 3.77% | **54.9%** | **−51.1** | 0.07 | 67.9% among AI-users × 80.8% any-use |
| ChatGPT | 0.61% | **66.0%** | **−65.4** | 0.01 | 81.7% among AI-users × 80.8% any-use |
| Claude Code | 3.37% | **33.0%** | **−29.6** | 0.10 | 40.8% among AI-users × 80.8% any-use |
| Cursor | 1.91% | — | — | — | No published benchmark |

**Punchline:** employers list specific AI tools in 1-4% of postings, while 33-66% of workers use them. **The tool-specific divergence is 10-100× larger than the broad-AI divergence.** Employers are not asking for Copilot / ChatGPT / Claude by name — they're asking for AI literacy broadly (if at all) and leaving tool choice to the developer.

**This is the single most important per-tool finding for the paper.** Copilot alone has 20M users (GitHub) and is used by ~55% of pro developers, but shows up in only 3.8% of 2026 SWE postings. The ratio of worker usage to posting requirement is **14.5×** for Copilot.

**Claude Code has the smallest (but still large) gap:** 10% posting-to-worker ratio. Its absolute posting rate (3.4%) is the highest relative to its worker base (33%), which suggests Claude Code is somewhat more likely to be named in job postings than Copilot or ChatGPT.

## Step 7 — Ghost cross-check (from T22)

Using T22's section-level AI ghostiness finding: AI mentions land in the `requirements` section only 21% of the time in 2026 (vs 34% for non-AI tech), so the "hard AI requirement" rate is a small subset of the all-description AI rate.

| Measure | 2024 | 2026 | Δ pp |
|---|---|---|---|
| Broad AI (all sections) | 5.13% | 28.61% | +23.5 |
| **Broad AI (requirements section only, adjusted)** | **1.21%** | **6.00%** | **+4.8** |
| AI mention share in `requirements` section | 23.6% | 21.0% | −2.6 |
| AI mention share in `preferred` section | 2.4% | 7.9% | +5.5 |
| AI mention share in `responsibilities` section | 24.3% | 25.8% | +1.5 |

**When we restrict the AI posting rate to mentions in the requirements section only, the 2026 rate drops from 28.6% to about 6.0%.** That is a plausible "hard AI requirement" estimate for the market.

**Worker usage at 80.8% vs requirements-only AI at 6.0% gives a gap of −74.8 pp**, and a ratio of **0.07** — **the divergence is even LARGER when restricted to hard requirements**. This is the defensible version of the divergence claim: not "employers want AI skills they don't have" (wrong direction) but "developers are using AI tools in their jobs whether or not the posting says so — by a factor of 10-15×."

The AI-mention share in `preferred` sections TRIPLED (2.4% → 7.9%) while the `requirements` share held steady (23.6% → 21.0%). **Growth of posted AI content is disproportionately landing in "preferred" / nice-to-have language**, not in hard requirements. This confirms T22's ghostiness finding: as AI mentions explode on the posting side, they're increasingly hedged.

## Surprises

1. **The divergence points the opposite of RQ3's original hypothesis.** The paper's prior framing (employer AI requirements outpace worker usage as "anticipatory restructuring") must be inverted. The new framing: **worker AI usage is so broad and early that employers are effectively describing the job to developers who already use AI every day** — and the posting side is slowly catching up.

2. **Per-tool divergence is 10-100× larger than aggregate divergence.** The Copilot gap (ratio 0.07) is over 5× larger than the broad AI gap (ratio 0.35). This tells us employers are increasingly AI-literate in broad terms but very rarely name specific tools. This could be intentional (leaving tool choice to the developer) or it could be a lag (HR/recruiters don't know which tool to ask for).

3. **ChatGPT has the largest tool gap** (ratio 0.01 — posting rate 0.6% vs worker rate 66%). It's the most-used tool by developers but the least-listed in postings. This is likely because ChatGPT is seen as a general productivity tool, not a named engineering skill — employers don't list it the way they don't list "Google Docs."

4. **The 2026 posting-side AI rate is 28.6% (broad) but only 6% lands in the requirements section.** Most of the posting-side AI content is in responsibilities / role summary, not hard requirements. When we restrict to hard requirements, the 6.0% hard-requirement rate against 80.8% worker rate is the cleanest single number for the paper: **a 13.5× gap between worker AI usage and posting AI hard requirements.**

5. **Aggregators have LOWER AI rates than direct employers** (24.6% vs 29.4% broad). Consistent with T22's finding that aggregators are less ghost-like at the corpus level.

6. **Mid-senior 2026 AI rate slightly exceeds entry-level** (31.2% vs 29.1% broad). The "employers demand more AI at the junior rung" hypothesis is not supported; if anything, senior postings mention AI slightly more. Entry-level is higher only on the ai_domain (ML/DL/CV) subset.

## Data quality caveats

1. **Self-selection bias in SO 2025:** Stack Overflow's 80.8% figure is self-reported by developers who respond to the survey. Respondents likely skew more AI-engaged than the average professional developer. The Anthropic Labor Market Impact study reports Computer Programmer observed exposure at 75%, consistent with SO 2025, and the sensitivity range from 50% to 85% covers the plausible range. **Even at the lowest plausible worker rate of 50%, broad posting rate (28.6%) is 21 pp below worker rate.**

2. **Posting rate is a LINGUISTIC signal, not a hiring intention.** The 28.6% broad AI posting rate means "the posting text mentions an AI term somewhere." It does NOT mean "the employer will reject applicants without AI skills." T22's section analysis (only 21% of AI mentions are in the requirements section) suggests the actual "hard requirement" rate is ~6%. The apples-to-apples comparison with "workers use AI tools in their day job" is closer to the 6% requirements-only figure — but even then, the direction of divergence is worker-above-employer by more than 10×.

3. **Worker usage includes general tools (ChatGPT, Copilot).** A developer using ChatGPT to look up a regex counts as "using AI" in SO's survey. This inflates the worker rate. But developers at 80.8% self-reported any-use is still enormous compared to 28.6% posting mention rate — the direction holds even if we halve the worker rate.

4. **AI-tool vs AI-domain vs AI-generic categories are imperfect.** `langchain` and `rag` could be AI-tool OR AI-generic depending on context. The categorization follows the Gate 2 guidance and is approximate.

5. **Narrow AI regex has false positives on boilerplate** (e.g., "we use AI responsibly", "no AI tools allowed"). T05 reported 925 SNR on this pattern, which validates its discriminative power — but the absolute level is inflated by boilerplate to some unknown degree. The 34.6% narrow rate should be read as an upper bound; the broad 28.6% is a tighter measure.

6. **The tech-matrix regexes were validated in the shared artifact build.** `c++`, `c#`, `.NET` were flagged with markdown-escape bugs (Gate 2 note 9); AI tech patterns were not affected.

The new lead is: **"Worker AI tool usage (80.8% SO 2025) is 10-15× the rate at which employers list AI tools in job postings, and tool-specific naming is almost entirely absent from postings."** This is still a RQ3 win — it's just a different win.

2. **T26 (Agent N SYNTHESIS.md):** integrate the per-tool gap (Copilot 14.5×, ChatGPT ~100×, Claude 10×) as the headline divergence number rather than the narrow/broad aggregate. The tool-specific gap is the cleanest signal.

4. **V2 verification:** independently validate the 28.6% broad posting rate using a different AI term list and the 80.8% worker benchmark against a non-SO source. Also verify the per-tool rates on the 2026 scraped subset only (arshkon has ~0 AI-tool mentions in 2024).

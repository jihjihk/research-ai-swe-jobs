# T07 — External benchmarks & power/feasibility analysis

Agent D, Wave 1. Run date: 2026-04-15. Input: `data/unified.parquet`.

**Primary deliverable:** `exploration/tables/T07/feasibility.csv` — drives downstream wave-planning decisions about which comparisons are trustworthy.

## Headline findings

1. **At the aggregate and mid-senior level, arshkon-vs-scraped is well-powered.** All-SWE (n=4,691 vs 40,881) and mid-senior (n=2,064 vs 17,418) can detect binary effects as small as **2.2 pp** and **3.3 pp** respectively at 80% power / α=0.05 worst-case (p=0.5). Continuous MDEs: d=0.04 and d=0.07.
2. **Entry-level arshkon-vs-scraped is underpowered.** Only 175 arshkon entry SWE postings. Worst-case binary MDE is **11.3 pp**. Even the key RQ1 metric — entry share over time — cannot be compared to arshkon at fine resolution without pooling asaniczka or relying on the YOE proxy.
3. **Metro-level comparison is feasible for 18 metros** at ≥50 per period and **8 metros** at ≥100 per period. 17,483 SWE rows (27% of the LinkedIn filtered pool) have `metro_area = NULL`; of those, 4,100 are `is_multi_location = true` and structurally excluded. The 2024 metro counts (arshkon-only) are the binding constraint, not scraped.
4. **Company overlap panel (T16): 240 companies** with ≥3 SWE in both arshkon and scraped (this is the panel size at that threshold; it drops to 125 at ≥5 and 45 at ≥10).
5. **BLS geographic validation: Pearson r = 0.97** between BLS OES state employment for SOC 15-1252 and our posting counts (both arshkon and scraped). Well above the 0.80 target. Our sample frame is not geographically biased relative to the official labor statistics benchmark.
6. **JOLTS Information sector openings declined 29%** between our 2024 window (avg 136k) and our late-2025/early-2026 window (avg 97k). Our two sampling periods straddle a real cooling in information-sector demand — temporal effects detected in the data are not happening in isolation.

## Part A — Feasibility / power analysis (primary output)

### Filtering applied throughout

Default filter: `source_platform='linkedin' AND is_english=true AND date_flag='ok' AND is_swe=true`. Seniority column: `seniority_final`.

### Group sizes

| source | entry | associate | mid-senior | director | unknown | ALL |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| kaggle_arshkon (2024-04) | 175 | 15 | 2,064 | 13 | 2,424 | **4,691** |
| kaggle_asaniczka (2024-01) | 204 | 24 | 11,426 | 92 | 6,383 | **18,129** |
| scraped (2026-03/04) | 1,275 | 52 | 17,418 | 295 | 21,841 | **40,881** |

Surprises in the raw counts:
- **Arshkon has only 175 entry-labeled SWE postings.** This is the binding constraint on all cross-period entry-level analysis against arshkon. The issue isn't absent data; it's that entry is a small slice of the SWE sample in any snapshot.
- **Scraped has 53% `seniority_final = 'unknown'` for SWE** (21,841 / 40,881). This is because many rows were not routed to the Stage 10 LLM, so Stage 5 strong-rule labels are the only source of seniority. Any analysis that drops `unknown` loses half the scraped SWE sample.
- **Asaniczka has 11,426 mid-senior SWE** — 2.5x arshkon's mid-senior pool. Pooling asaniczka under `seniority_final` boosts mid-senior power significantly but introduces instrument heterogeneity.

### Feasibility table

Power parameters: two-sided α=0.05, power=0.80, two-sample z test for binary (variance under p=0.5 worst case; p=0.2 also reported for realistic entry-share magnitudes), two-sample t test in standardized units (Cohen's d) for continuous.

| analysis | comparison | n1 | n2 | MDE bin (p=0.5) | MDE bin (p=0.2) | MDE d | verdict |
|---|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---|
| seniority-stratified | entry: arshkon vs scraped | 175 | 1,275 | 0.113 | 0.090 | 0.226 | **underpowered** |
| seniority-stratified | senior (mid-senior): arshkon vs scraped | 2,064 | 17,418 | 0.033 | 0.026 | 0.065 | **well-powered** |
| all-SWE | all SWE: arshkon vs scraped | 4,691 | 40,881 | 0.022 | 0.017 | 0.043 | **well-powered** |

Verdict rules (worst-case p=0.5):
- **well-powered** = MDE_binary ≤ 5 pp AND both cells ≥ 500
- **marginal** = MDE_binary ≤ 10 pp AND both cells ≥ 100
- **underpowered** otherwise

### Metro-level feasibility detail

73 distinct metros have ≥1 SWE in at least one of (arshkon, scraped).

- **18 metros qualify at ≥50 per period** (both arshkon AND scraped):
  Atlanta, Austin, Boston, Charlotte, Chicago, Dallas-Fort Worth, Denver, Detroit, Houston, Los Angeles, Minneapolis-Saint Paul, Philadelphia, Phoenix, Raleigh-Durham-Chapel Hill, San Diego, San Francisco Bay Area, Seattle, Washington DC.
- **8 metros qualify at ≥100 per period**:
  Atlanta, Boston, Chicago, Dallas-Fort Worth, Los Angeles, San Francisco Bay Area, Seattle, Washington DC.

Excluded from all per-metro analysis:
- **17,483 rows** have `metro_area IS NULL` (27.4% of SWE LinkedIn sample).
- Of those, **4,100 rows** are `is_multi_location = true` and structurally excluded — the posting covers multiple metros so no single assignment exists.
- The remaining ~13,383 null-metro rows are non-metro geographies or unresolved locations.

**The binding constraint is arshkon, not scraped.** Even the smallest qualifying metro (Raleigh-Durham, 51 arshkon rows vs 558 scraped) has plenty of scraped coverage. The 2024 baseline is what limits metro-level feasibility. Aggregating multiple 2024 sources into a pooled baseline would likely double this to ~36 metros at ≥50; recommend T16 consider this.

Per-metro detail: [`exploration/tables/T07/metro_feasibility.csv`](../tables/T07/metro_feasibility.csv)

**Important caveat on per-metro binary MDE.** The worst-case per-metro binary MDE (28 pp) reflects the binding smallest metro. It does not mean metro-level analysis is useless — it means any _single metro_ comparison is underpowered for subtle effects. A mixed-effects or fixed-effects model that pools across the 18 metros and exploits within-metro variation is the right approach and is well-powered in aggregate (n = 2,116 arshkon + 24,850 scraped across the 18). Treat metro as a covariate, not as a stratification variable.

## Part B — External benchmarks

### BLS OES state-level validation (SOC 15-1252 Software Developers)

**Data source:** BLS Public API v1 for series ID pattern `OEUS{state_fips}00000000000151252 01` (state × cross-industry × occupation × employment, annual May 2024 release). 51 states/DC requested, 50 returned valid employment values (Wyoming came back null-value — small-sample suppression). Total across all states: 1,650,360, which matches the national total (1,654,440) within rounding (0.2%).

BLS national aggregate downloads (oesm24st.zip) were **blocked by Akamai** (403 Forbidden from curl/WebFetch); the Public API v1 endpoint was reachable with HTTP/1.1 and returned data without authentication. Industry-specific BLS OES national series (SOC × NAICS) were not reachable via the v1 API (all returned "no data") — this is a known limitation of the API v1 endpoint, which primarily exposes the all-industries cross-sections.

**SOC 15-1256 (Software QA / testers) was not retrieved.** The second request chunk silently failed and was not retried; this could be fixed by splitting the request further, but it's not critical for the primary validation since 15-1252 alone covers the bulk of SWE employment.

**Correlations (BLS OES 15-1252 state employment vs our SWE posting counts):**

| our source | Pearson r (raw) | Pearson r (log-log) | Spearman ρ | n |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| arshkon | **0.9718** | 0.9447 | 0.9278 | 47 |
| scraped | 0.9740 | 0.7738 | 0.8746 | 31 |
| arshkon + scraped pooled | **0.9765** | 0.8843 | 0.9065 | 47 |

Both sources comfortably exceed the r > 0.80 target. Arshkon's log-log correlation is higher than scraped's (0.94 vs 0.77) — scraped over-represents the largest states (CA, TX, NY, WA) relative to BLS, consistent with LinkedIn search prioritizing high-volume metros. Scraped only has 31 states with nonzero SWE counts (vs 47 for arshkon), reflecting the scraped window's LinkedIn search strategy focusing on the 26-metro study frame rather than covering all states.

## Sample frame and generalizability

**What population does our sample represent?**

- **Language / platform:** English-language LinkedIn SWE postings. Indeed is excluded from the primary frame (sensitivity only).
- **Geography:** US-weighted but with a top-metro tilt, especially in scraped. Correlates at r=0.97 with BLS OES state employment. Arshkon covers 47 states, scraped covers 31 states (bounded by the 26-metro search frame plus spillover).
- **Occupation:** `is_swe = true` — primarily software developers (SOC 15-1252 analog). A Stage 5 rule tier plus Stage 10 LLM classification is the operational definition. Title-lookup LLM has an elevated false-positive rate; sensitivity (g) addresses this.
- **Time:** Two snapshots of a flow, not a stock. arshkon = 2024-04, asaniczka = 2024-01, scraped = 2026-03 / 2026-04. The ~24 month gap is real, but our scraped window is a growing current window that will keep expanding. Each rerun gets a different scraped sample.
- **Company set:** Dominated by employers who posted on LinkedIn during the scraped windows. Company concentration matters — see T06 for that analysis.

**Where we can generalize:**
- Aggregate posting-level statistics (AI prevalence, tech stack, management language) for LinkedIn SWE jobs in top US metros — comfortably.
- Mid-senior comparisons between 2024 and 2026 — comfortably.
- State-level counts — comfortably (r=0.97 with BLS).

**Where we CANNOT generalize:**
- Entry-level market-wide trends: our entry sample in 2024 is too small (175 arshkon) to produce a confident point estimate; the YOE-based proxy is the primary validator for this RQ per the core preamble.
- Non-LinkedIn hiring channels (referrals, internal moves, direct applications).
- Job-seeker behavior or outcomes (postings ≠ hires).
- Rural / small-state labor markets — scraped has almost no coverage outside the 26-metro frame.
- Early-career roles specifically at small companies — we have limited company_size coverage outside arshkon.

## Recommendations for Wave 2/3

**DO NOT ATTEMPT** (underpowered):
1. Per-metro entry-share comparison (entry × metro × period) without multi-level pooling. The smallest metro cell would be O(5-10 entry rows), which supports only enormous effect sizes. Must be aggregated or modeled.
2. Arshkon-only entry share change as the primary RQ1 estimate without pairing it to the YOE-based proxy and to the pooled-2024 estimate.
3. Fine-grained (director / associate) seniority comparisons — sample sizes in the low hundreds across sources.
4. Single-metro deep dives below San Francisco / Seattle / Washington DC / Boston / NY tier.

**DO ATTEMPT** (well-powered or supported):
1. All-SWE and mid-senior cross-period binary and continuous comparisons (AI keyword rate, tech stack, description length, management language) — arshkon vs scraped is fine; pooled 2024 vs scraped is even better.
2. Metro × period _modeled_ analyses (mixed-effects with metro as random effect, or fixed-effects with metro dummies). The 18-metro panel has ~27k observations and is well-powered in aggregate.
3. Company-level panel of 240 companies for T16 (within-company change in metrics). For company-level _binary_ outcomes with N=240, effect sizes below 13 pp are underpowered; treat as exploratory.

**Flag for sensitivity reporting:** Entry-share findings must always report both `seniority_final` and the YOE-based proxy (yoe_extracted ≤ 2), plus the pooled-2024 baseline, per the core preamble and per the power table above.

## Surprises & unexpected patterns

1. **Scraped `seniority_final = 'unknown'` rate is 53%**, much higher than arshkon's 52%. I expected scraped to be much better labeled given Stage 10 LLM availability, but most scraped rows were not routed to the LLM under budget constraints. This means the "known-seniority sample" for scraped is 19k, not 41k — cut in half.
2. **Arshkon entry-level SWE is only 175 rows.** I expected ~500 based on the dataset-size headline. Raw arshkon has entry-level postings across all job types; restricting to SWE + LinkedIn + English + date_flag_ok cuts the pool by ~95%. This is the single biggest feasibility constraint in the whole project.
3. **Pearson r = 0.97 between BLS state employment and our posting counts.** I expected ~0.85 based on prior research on scraped labor data. The very high agreement suggests the LinkedIn SWE posting density is a near-perfect proxy for state-level employment magnitude (though not for rate or flow).
4. **JOLTS Information openings are at 97k in early 2026 — below the 2023 trough.** The sector-level hiring picture is worse than I had assumed going into this. This is context that every cross-period RQ1/RQ2 finding needs to carry.
5. **The company overlap panel is 240 companies at ≥3 in both sources.** Larger than I expected — the arshkon sample of ~4,700 SWE rows and scraped sample of ~41k have a meaningful set of recurring employers.
6. **`is_multi_location = true` affects only 4,100 rows (6.4% of SWE).** Smaller than I'd expected given how prevalent "multiple locations" postings are on LinkedIn. The preprocessing must be classifying most multi-location postings into a specific metro when the primary location is unambiguous.
7. **`metro_area = NULL` but `is_multi_location = false` for ~13,383 rows.** That's 80% of the null-metro rows — these are postings outside the 26-metro study frame or unresolved locations, not multi-location cases.

## Data quality caveats

1. **BLS bulk downloads failed (Akamai 403)** — we relied on the Public API for state employment and could not validate against the industry-level OES files. Geographic correlation is solid; industry comparison against OES is not available.
2. **SOC 15-1256 (QA/testers) BLS data not retrieved** — the second API request chunk failed silently. Adds ~100k to the total employment baseline; does not change the state-level correlation conclusion.
3. **JOLTS Information sector** is a proxy for SWE demand, not a direct measure. The sector has ~3M total employment; SWE are a minority of Information sector jobs, and many SWE jobs are outside Information.
4. **State-level coverage gap for scraped:** 31 states have nonzero scraped SWE. The 20 states with zero scraped coverage are small states (WY, SD, ND, AK, etc.) where LinkedIn scraping is not focused. BLS has employment values for all of them. This suppresses Spearman ρ for scraped (0.87 vs 0.93 for arshkon).
5. **Arshkon `state_normalized` coverage is high (~98%)**, but arshkon entry SWE is only 175 rows — even complete coverage doesn't fix a small denominator.

## Action items for downstream tasks

1. **T08 (distribution profiling):** Must compute entry share using both `seniority_final` and the YOE proxy (`yoe_extracted ≤ 2`) for every period. Report both side by side. Disagreement is itself a finding.
2. **T16 (company panel):** Use the N=240 panel at the ≥3 threshold. Consider stratifying by company size at the ≥5 threshold (N=125) for higher-confidence subsample.
3. **Metro-level tasks:** Always use the 18-metro frame (≥50 per period). Do not attempt single-metro deep dives below the top 8. Use mixed-effects or fixed-effects with metro as a covariate.
4. **Report format:** Any cross-period entry-level finding needs the pooled-2024 comparison too (since arshkon-only entry is underpowered). Both should agree in direction or the disagreement should be investigated.
5. **JOLTS framing:** When reporting any "declining X" cross-period finding, note that the information-sector labor market tightened ~29% over the same interval. Declining entry share in a cooling labor market is different from declining entry share in a hot labor market.
6. **BLS industry-level validation:** If industry-level benchmarking is needed, download `oesm24in.zip` manually from outside this environment and store it under `data/external/bls/` for future runs.

## Headline

**Senior SWE roles are redefining toward technical orchestration, not away from people management, and NOT toward strategic scope.** On validated ≥80%-precision patterns only:

| Profile | Mid-senior 2024 | Mid-senior 2026 | Δ | Director 2024 | Director 2026 | Δ |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| People management (per 1K) | 0.186 | **0.232** (+25%) | ↑ | 0.228 | 0.181 (−21%) | ↓ |
| Technical orchestration | 0.168 | **0.332** (+98%) | ↑↑ | 0.118 | **0.302** (+156%) | ↑↑↑ |
| Strategic scope | 0.045 | 0.053 (+17%) | ~flat | 0.035 | **0.076** (+117%) | ↑ |

**The rebuilt pattern set overturns T11's "manage fell −47%" finding.** On the validated narrow set — which requires object-noun-phrase after `manage` (manage_team, manage_people) and drops `hire`/`hiring` and bare `manage` — people-management density **ROSE +25% at mid-senior**. T11's −47% was a pattern artifact caused by the standalone-noun exclusion and HR boilerplate filtering. The `mentor` +55% claim survives as a clean component. The broader point: **strict people-management language did not fall; it rose, but the rise is dominated by mentoring/team-leading rather than headcount/direct-reports**.

The **dominant** shift, though, is technical orchestration (+98% at mid-senior, +156% at director). This is the cleanest "new senior archetype" signal: 2026 senior postings describe architecture review, system design, code review, agent workflows, prompt engineering, evals/guardrails, technical vision, and "setting engineering standards" at twice the 2024 density.

**Strategic scope is the smallest shift and it is NOT a field-wide finding.** Bare `stakeholder`, `roadmap`, `prioritization`, and `cross-functional` all FAILED the ≥80% precision check (they are used as soft-skill/collaboration filler, not as strategic-ownership markers). The narrow strategic patterns that passed (business_impact, product_strategy, resource_allocation, budget_ownership, drive_revenue, executive_partner, strategic_initiative, influence_direction, business_requirements) show ~flat density at mid-senior but a real rise at director (+117%). Strategic language is concentrated in the director band and that's where it moved.

**Senior title compression verdict.** "Senior" in titles dropped 41.7% → 28.9% (−12.8 pp). "Staff" rose only 2.7% → 5.8% (+3.1 pp). Staff DOES NOT explain the senior drop. Within the 150-company overlap panel (n≥10 in both periods), mean senior_delta is **−9.1 pp** per company; staff_delta is **+1.6 pp**. The senior drop is real and not a between-company composition artifact. The most likely mechanism: **employer template rewriting under LLM-drafting tools that remove the "Senior" prefix from generic SWE postings** — all 100% of "senior"-titled rows are Stage 5 keyword-matched (`seniority_final_source='title_keyword'`) and 99.5% of them still classify as `seniority_final='mid-senior'` in both periods, so this is not a classifier drift. It is a literal vocabulary shift in the title field.

**Cross-seniority management comparison.** Mid-senior people-management density rose (0.186 → 0.232, +25%), but **entry-level people-management density FELL** (0.064 → 0.056, −13%), and director fell (0.228 → 0.181, −21%). This is NOT a field-wide template shift — it is a mid-senior-specific rise. If management language were being boiler-plated into every posting by LLM-drafting tools, entry would have risen with mid-senior. Instead, mid-senior specifically is being redefined as the mentoring/team-leading rung.

## Methodology

### Pattern validation (50-row stratified precision check)

Source text: `description_core_llm` (raw LLM-cleaned prose, NOT the stopword-stripped `description_cleaned` from the shared artifact, because validation requires natural prose for context checks). Filter: `is_swe = true AND source_platform='linkedin' AND is_english=true AND date_flag='ok' AND llm_extraction_coverage='labeled' AND seniority_final IN ('mid-senior','director')`. n=19,018 senior LLM-text rows.

Per pattern: sample up to 25 matches per period (25/25 balanced), compute rule-based acceptance using pattern-specific validators. Precision = accepted / sampled. Threshold: ≥0.80. Validators are conservative — when in doubt, reject — so they produce a lower bound on true precision. The validator code is in `T21_validate_patterns.py` and each validator's logic is documented inline.

44 patterns tested. **36 passed, 8 dropped.**

### Density computation

After validation, each profile (people_mgmt, tech_orch, strategic) is encoded as a disjunction of its kept patterns. Per posting: count matches, divide by `max(desc_len_chars, 200) / 1000`. The 200-char floor prevents single-word short descriptions from inflating density.

### Senior sub-archetype clustering

k-means (k=4) on z-scored (people_density, orch_density, strat_density). Cluster labels assigned by which raw-density center is largest (or "generic (low all)" if total density < 0.3).

### Sensitivity

- (a) aggregator exclusion — applied to the density table.

## Key results

### Density by profile × period × seniority (primary)

| seniority | period | n | people_density | orch_density | strat_density | people_any | orch_any | strat_any |

**Binary (share with at least one match) confirms direction.** Mid-senior any-people-mgmt rose 30.2 → 41.0 pp (+10.8 pp); any-tech-orch rose 28.6 → 48.9 pp (+20.3 pp). Tech-orchestration is the dominant shift at both seniority levels.

### Senior sub-archetype clustering

Four k-means clusters on z-scored densities, labeled by dominant raw-density component:

| cluster | people | orch | strat | 2024 share | 2026 share | Δ share |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| generic (low all) | 0.05 | 0.08 | 0.01 | 70.2% | **59.8%** | **−10.4 pp** |
| tech-lead | 0.20 | **1.05** | 0.02 | 7.8% | **16.9%** | **+9.1 pp** |
| people-manager | **0.86** | 0.25 | 0.01 | 14.5% | 14.7% | flat |
| strategist | 0.24 | 0.23 | **0.52** | 7.5% | 8.6% | +1.1 pp |

**The "tech-lead" sub-archetype more than doubled (7.8% → 16.9%, +9.1 pp), at the expense of the "generic" bucket (70.2% → 59.8%, −10.4 pp).** The people-manager sub-archetype is nearly unchanged. The strategist sub-archetype grew slightly. **The senior redefinition is a tech-lead specialization story**: postings that used to be bucket-generic are being written with more system design / code review / agent workflow / tech vision language, shifting into the tech-lead cluster.

**In 2024, AI-mentioning senior postings were indistinguishable from non-AI senior postings on every profile.** In 2026, AI-mentioning senior postings have tech_orch density **+0.21** over non-AI (0.482 vs 0.274, 76% higher). People-management density is identical whether or not the posting mentions AI. This is the single sharpest domain-specific signal in T21: **the technical orchestration rise is specifically concentrated in AI-mentioning postings**. The AI senior is the "tech lead / orchestrator" senior, not the "people manager" senior.

### Director-specific deep dive

|---|---|---|---|---|
| n | 99 | 12,745 | 112 | 6,062 |

**In 2024, director was {higher people, lower orch, lower strat} than mid-senior** — the classic "people boss" director. **In 2026, director is {lower people, ~equal orch, higher strat} than mid-senior** — the director profile flipped. Directors went from "more mgmt than mid-senior" to "more strategic than mid-senior." Orchestration matched mid-senior (both ≈0.30). The director role is narrowing toward strategic + orchestration and dropping people management. T20's mid-senior→director boundary BLUR is the structural reflection of this — directors became so close to mid-senior on orchestration that the two levels are less discriminable on a structured feature vector.

### Cross-seniority management comparison

| seniority | period | n | people | orch | strat | people_any share |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| entry | 2024 | 376 | 0.064 | 0.105 | 0.050 | 10.9% |
| entry | 2026 | 607 | **0.056** | 0.159 | 0.037 | 9.9% |
| mid-senior | 2024 | 12,745 | 0.186 | 0.168 | 0.045 | 30.2% |
| mid-senior | 2026 | 6,062 | **0.232** | 0.332 | 0.053 | 41.0% |
| director | 2024 | 99 | 0.228 | 0.118 | 0.035 | 37.4% |
| director | 2026 | 112 | **0.181** | 0.302 | 0.076 | 42.9% |

**The people-management rise is ONLY at mid-senior.** Entry fell −13% (0.064 → 0.056), director fell −21% (0.228 → 0.181), mid-senior rose +25% (0.186 → 0.232). This is not a field-wide template shift and not downward migration; it is a specific rewiring of the mid-senior posting to emphasize mentoring/team-leading. If recruiter LLM-drafting tools were inserting "mentor the team" into every posting, entry would have risen with mid-senior; instead entry fell.

Note: `people_any` (share with ≥1 mention) shows entry 10.9% → 9.9% (−1.0 pp), mid-senior 30.2% → 41.0% (+10.8 pp), director 37.4% → 42.9% (+5.5 pp). Binary rose at mid-senior and director but fell at entry, while density fell at director (because 2026 directors have longer descriptions diluting the mentions). The "any mention" signal agrees with the density signal on direction at mid-senior and entry; director is the only cell where density and binary disagree.

### Senior title compression deep dive

Raw title markers across ALL SWE rows (n=22,820 in 2024, 40,881 in 2026):

| marker | 2024 share | 2026 share | Δ pp |
|---|---|---|---|
| senior | 41.7% | 28.9% | **−12.8** |
| staff | 2.7% | 5.8% | +3.1 |
| principal | 5.2% | 3.7% | −1.6 |
| lead | 9.3% | 6.1% | −3.2 |

**Within-company (150 companies with n≥10 in both periods):**
- Mean senior_delta = **−9.1 pp** (senior share fell at the average company too)
- Mean staff_delta = +1.6 pp
- 90/150 companies had senior share drop
- 34/150 companies had staff share rise
- Only ~22% of the senior drop is even potentially absorbed by staff rises

**Mapping titles to seniority_final:**
- 100% of `senior`-titled rows and `staff`-titled rows are from `seniority_final_source='title_keyword'` (Stage 5 rule-match). No LLM drift.
- 99.5% of senior-titled rows classify as `seniority_final='mid-senior'` in BOTH periods.
- 99.6% of staff-titled rows classify as `seniority_final='mid-senior'` in both periods.
- Director rows are a tiny fraction (33/2024, 65/2026) of senior-titled.
- **Classifier is stable; vocabulary is what changed.**

The drop is real (both aggregate and within-company), is not explained by staff rising (only 22% absorbed), is not a classifier drift, and is not a seniority-band redefinition inside `seniority_final`. Best-supported mechanism: **2026 posters are dropping the "Senior" prefix from plain-SWE postings**. Possible reasons:
(a) Staff ladder adoption — but staff only gained ~1.6 pp within-company on average, insufficient.
(b) LinkedIn title-normalization drift — the scraped source uses the raw LinkedIn field; could be that LinkedIn's own title display changed formatting.
(c) Employer LLM-drafting — titles like "Senior Software Engineer" are being rewritten to "Software Engineer" or to AI-variant titles like "AI Engineer", "Software Engineer [multiple positions available]" (the #1 new 2026 title per T10).

**Most-supported explanation:** (c). T10 documented that the #1 new title in 2026 is "software engineer [multiple positions available]" (n=88) — a template-sounding compound title — and 13 of the top 20 new titles are AI-compound variants. This is consistent with a vocabulary-level rewriting that bypasses the "Senior" prefix. The within-company magnitude (−9.1 pp) is larger than can be explained by the staff rise (+1.6 pp), so staff ladder adoption is NOT the primary driver.

## Surprises & unexpected patterns

1. **The management finding is RESURRECTED but narrower.** T11 said `manage −47%` and got overturned by V1. I said that finding was an artifact of pattern exclusion rules and rebuilt with object-noun-phrase requirements. The rebuilt patterns show **people management rose +25% at mid-senior on valid narrow patterns**. This is a notable correction of the Gate 2 narrative: "general management rose" was contradicted; "narrow people management rose at mid-senior" is the corrected surviving claim.

2. **Technical orchestration nearly doubled at mid-senior and 2.5×'d at director.** Tech orchestration (+98% mid-senior, +156% director) is by far the largest profile shift. The composition — code_review, system_design, agent_workflow, prompt_engineering, evals, technical_vision — is unmistakably the "AI-era tech lead" vocabulary.

3. **Strategic scope is NOT rising at mid-senior.** This is the biggest single null I found. Gate 1 / T11 assumed strategic scope was rising ("stakeholder", "roadmap", "prioritization", "cross-functional"); but the high-n candidates all FAILED precision validation, and the surviving narrow strategic pattern is essentially flat at mid-senior. Strategic scope rose only at director (+117%) where it was already the rarest profile. **The "senior archetype shift" is mostly orchestration, not strategic scope.**

4. **The tech-lead sub-archetype more than doubled** (7.8% → 16.9%) while the people-manager sub-archetype was flat (14.5% → 14.7%). If the question is "which senior sub-type grew?", the answer is unambiguously tech-lead / orchestrator, not people-manager.

5. **Cross-seniority: people management FELL at entry and director but rose at mid-senior.** This non-monotonic pattern rules out "field-wide template shift" as the mechanism; it specifically points to mid-senior redefinition. The mechanism candidates narrow to:
   - LLM-drafted senior postings that insert mentoring language specifically for the senior level (not templated across levels), or
   - real market shift where senior positions are being hired to mentor/team-lead in ways they weren't in 2024.

6. **AI × senior interaction is entirely in the orchestration profile.** AI-mentioning senior postings have the same people-management density as non-AI senior postings, but their tech_orch density is 0.482 vs 0.274 — nearly double. The orchestration rise is **concentrated among AI-mentioning postings**. This is the cleanest single finding that ties T14's AI-explosion to T21's senior redefinition.

7. **Director people-management density FELL.** Directors are LESS about people management in 2026 than in 2024. That's a reversal of the typical "higher = more management" gradient and is the single largest deviation from the 2024 baseline among senior levels. It pairs with T20's mid-senior→director boundary blur — directors became less of a distinct "people-manager" archetype.

8. **The senior-title drop is 5.7× larger than the staff-title rise.** Staff is not "the new senior" at company level. The senior compression is about something else — probably title-field rewriting under LLM drafting tools, which also produces the T15 template homogenization finding.

## Data quality caveats

1. **Validators are rule-based and conservative.** The "dropped" patterns (stakeholder, prioritization, roadmap, cross-functional) might be only weakly boilerplate-contaminated and a human coder would save some borderline cases. My rule-based validator rejected them and I elected to treat that as a precision floor. Rerunning with human-coded samples is a Wave 4 V2 task.

2. **Director cells are small** (99 in 2024, 112 in 2026). Every director-specific finding is directionally plausible but should not be cited with precision. The director-orchestration rise is large enough (+156%) that it would survive a much larger sample, but the director people-mgmt fall (−21%) could noise out.

3. **Density uses `description_core_llm` natural text**, NOT the stopword-stripped `description_cleaned` from the shared artifact. This is deliberate for validation (needed for context checks). For comparability with T11 which also used natural text proxies, the numbers should be roughly comparable; for comparability with T20's mgmt_density (which uses `description_cleaned` from the shared artifact, stopwords removed) there is a constant-factor difference. T20's mgmt_density scale is different but T20's verdicts (sign of coefficients, direction of AUC change) are unaffected.

4. **Entry sample (376 / 607) is thin.** The "entry people management fell −13%" claim has small-n error bars. Direction is likely correct but magnitude is uncertain. The flat-to-falling entry result is consistent with T11's earlier entry-level findings, so I do not think this is a fluke.

5. **Within-company overlap panel is 150 companies, no top-cap applied.** Per T06, the top-50 2024 employers account for ~40% of 2024 LinkedIn SWE postings; within-company comparisons inherit this concentration. A cap-50 per company sensitivity would be the natural follow-up but was not run.

6. **The validated regex bundle is saved at `exploration/artifacts/shared/validated_mgmt_patterns.json`** with the full per-pattern precision table and kept/dropped lists. Agent M (T22/T23) should load this bundle rather than re-deriving patterns.

## 2. Surprise inventory — 14 findings the original research design did not anticipate

Listed in rough order of importance for the paper.

### 2.1 RQ3 direction inverted — posting language LAGS worker AI adoption

**Surprise magnitude: high.** The research design hypothesized that employer AI requirements would outpace worker adoption (anticipatory restructuring). The data says the opposite at every benchmark: worker 80.8%, posting 28.6% broad / 6.0% hard requirement. **This finding is opposite both to the original hypothesis AND to the popular "employers demand impossible AI skills" framing.** It reframes the paper around a new mechanism — posting-language update lag — that is more novel than the original anticipatory-restructuring framing. Citations: T23, V2 section 2.

### 2.2 Director-level role reconfiguration +156% technical orchestration

**Surprise magnitude: high.** The research design expected a senior archetype shift at mid-senior; nobody anticipated that directors would show the largest single profile shift in the exploration (+156% technical-orchestration density, −21% people-management density, tech_count coefficient flipping from −0.48 to +0.35). Directors recast from "people bosses" to "hands-on tech leads." This explains the T20 mid-senior↔director boundary blur as a structural consequence of the director role absorbing mid-senior-like features. Citations: T21 (primary), T20 (feature flip), V2 Alt 2 (holds at +120% after top-10 exclusion).

### 2.3 74.6% of overlap-panel companies pivoted their dominant archetype in two years

**Surprise magnitude: high.** No prior work suggests within-company archetype pivot rates this high at this frequency. Median total-variation-distance per company is 0.629 — half the companies redistribute more than 63% of their archetype mass between 2024 and 2026. Citation: T16. Potential measurement artifacts: 30.5% scraped-side archetype label coverage means the pivot is computed on potentially non-random subsets of the 2026 postings.

### 2.4 Entry-level credential-stack convergence within archetypes

**Surprise magnitude: moderate-high.** The T15 corpus-level seniority-convergence null hid this clean within-domain pattern. Entry postings in 2026 catch up to mid-senior postings on credential-stack breadth in 10/10 large archetypes. In several archetypes (.NET, Java, Data, JS frontend, LLM/GenAI, DevOps, AWS cloud SDE, Defense) the gap FLIPS sign — entry now asks for more credential categories than mid-senior. Citation: T28. V2 narrowing: cite "2-7 flip" rather than "7/10."

### 2.5 Authorship-style shift explains the length growth (and ~1/3 of the breadth rise)

**Surprise magnitude: high.** T29 authorship score shifted +0.33 std between periods. 88.7% of 2026 postings score above the 2024 median. **Under style-matched nearest-neighbor analysis, the char_len delta attenuates by at least 23% and can flip sign depending on which features enter the matcher. The AI prevalence delta attenuates only 0-7%.** Length growth is mostly style migration; AI explosion is real content. This unifies the T13 length-in-responsibilities finding with the T15 within-group homogenization finding via a single mechanism — recruiter-LLM drafting adoption. Citation: T29; Gate 3 narrowing 1 (cite attenuation not sign-flip).

### 2.6 AI explosion is NOT SWE-specific — SWE-vs-adjacent DiD trivial

**Surprise magnitude: moderate-high.** The research design was SWE-framed. T18 DiD shows SWE-vs-control +29.6 pp broad AI, adjacent-vs-control +27.5 pp, **SWE-vs-adjacent +2.1 pp [0.77, 3.38]** (V2 verified). Network engineer broad AI quadrupled 4.2% → 16.6% with zero SWE reclassification. **The paper is no longer about "SWE restructuring" — it is about "information-tech restructuring."** This is a broadening not a weakening: more occupations in scope, cleaner parallel-trends story.

### 2.7 Seniority boundaries SHARPENED at 3 of 4 levels

**Surprise magnitude: moderate-high.** The research design's RQ1 junior-narrowing thread predicted boundary blurring between seniority levels. T20's structured 8-feature classifier finds: entry↔mid-senior +0.040 AUC, entry↔associate +0.093, associate↔mid-senior +0.100. **Only mid-senior↔director blurred (−0.061), and the mechanism is that directors are becoming more tech-heavy, not that the boundary dissolved.** The junior-through-mid-senior part of the ladder is MORE discriminable in 2026, not less.

### 2.8 Tool-stack adopter cluster is consulting / SI / enterprise, not FAANG

**Surprise magnitude: moderate.** The T16 cluster 3 — 46 companies, 19% of the overlap panel, +0.52 AI rise — is dominated by consulting firms, system integrators, and enterprise software employers (AT&T, Deloitte, American Express, Aditi Consulting, Aveva, Macquarie Group). **Adobe is the most tech-native member. No FAANG.** The companies rewriting their templates hardest are the downstream adopters signaling AI capability to clients, not the ones building AI systems. This is unanticipated — Gate 1 speculated adopters would be FAANG-adjacent.

### 2.9 LLM/GenAI archetype is 68% new entrants, not existing-company pivot

**Surprise magnitude: moderate.** 2024 LLM/GenAI cluster: 616 companies. 2026 cluster: 1,174 companies. **Only 138 companies overlap.** 68.2% of 2026 LLM/GenAI volume is from companies NOT in the 2024 cluster. Top 2026 employers: Anthropic, Microsoft AI, Intel, Alignerr, Harvey, Cognizant — many NOT in 2024. **The AI market is bifurcated: an existing-company tool-stack adoption wave PLUS a much larger new-entrant wave.** Citation: T28.

### 2.10 Entry-specialist intermediaries are invisible to `is_aggregator`

**Surprise magnitude: moderate.** SynergisticIT, WayUp, Jobs via Dice, Lensa, Emonics, Leidos, IBM — all 100% entry-post specialists driving ~15-20% of the 2026 entry pool. **None are flagged by the current `is_aggregator` field.** Every single-operationalization entry-share aggregate over the current `is_aggregator` is structurally miscalibrated. Citation: T08, T06.

### 2.11 Tech network modularity ROSE

**Surprise magnitude: moderate.** Prior intuition: AI would blur technology bundles. The data shows the opposite — Louvain modularity 0.56 → 0.66 with two new first-class communities (LLM/RAG, AI-tools triad). **AI is a new specialty layer being stacked on top of existing stacks, not a diffused horizontal skill.** This is the structural dual of the Finding-1 finding that AI is ADDED to tech stacks (density rose among AI-mentioning postings), not replacing them. Citation: T14.

### 2.12 2026 postings are more internally HOMOGENEOUS than 2024

**Surprise magnitude: moderate.** Against the "role blurring" prior. Within-group average pairwise cosine rose +0.017 (junior), +0.027 (senior), +0.040 (unknown) between periods. Citation: T15. T29 provides the mechanism candidate — recruiter-LLM drafting tools produce more uniform templates.

### 2.13 AI vocabulary spread into non-AI archetypes

**Surprise magnitude: moderate.** JS frontend broad AI +14.7 pp, .NET +13.6, Java enterprise +11.6, DevOps +10.2, Data engineering +9.2. This is not an "AI archetype grew" story — this is an "AI vocabulary layer stacked onto every archetype" story. Citation: T28.

### 2.14 Austin's JS-frontend share collapsed 25.3% → 9.1%

**Surprise magnitude: moderate (single-metro).** The sharpest single metro × archetype shift in the data. Austin's JS frontend share collapsed while LLM/GenAI rose 3.2% → 15.9% in the same metro. A single data point, not a statistical pattern — but interview-worthy. Citation: T17.

## 3. New testable hypotheses for the analysis phase (10)

Each hypothesis has: (a) a precise statement, (b) exploration evidence that supports it, (c) what additional analysis would test it formally, and (d) a novelty / publishability assessment.

### H1. Posting language is a lagging indicator of workplace AI tool adoption with a ~2-3 year lag

**Statement.** Worker-side AI tool adoption crossing X% precedes employer-side posting-language prevalence crossing X% by approximately 2-3 years. The lag is driven by HR/recruiter template update cycles, not by employer ignorance of AI use.

**Exploration evidence.** Worker any-use 63.2% (SO 2024) → 80.8% (SO 2025), posting broad AI 5.13% (2024) → 28.6% (2026). Posting-side started from nearly zero when worker-side was already majority. The 2026 hard-AI-requirement rate of 6.0% is still below the 2024 worker rate of 63.2%. (T23, T22.)

**Formal test.** Interrupted time-series on tool-specific mentions (Copilot, Claude Code, ChatGPT, Cursor) with known release dates as break points. Compare implied posting-side adoption time to worker-side adoption time from contemporaneous survey data. Test whether the lag is constant across tools or varies with tool maturity. Needs longer time window than the current 2024-2026 bracket — an analysis-phase ask for continued daily scraping.

**Novelty / publishability.** High. This is a new angle on posting-based labor economics and contradicts both the popular "employers demand impossible AI skills" narrative and the anticipatory-restructuring framing. Publishable as the paper's lead.

### H2. Director-level role reconfiguration is the single largest role-redefinition signal in SWE postings

**Statement.** Between 2024 and 2026, director-titled SWE postings shifted from a people-management-dominant profile to a technical-orchestration-dominant profile more sharply than any other cell (seniority × period × archetype). The tech_count coefficient in an entry↔director boundary classifier flips sign in both 2024 and 2026.

**Exploration evidence.** Director tech_count 2.93 → 8.03 (+173%); director orchestration density +156%; director people-management −21%; director strategic +117%; `tech_count` coefficient −0.48 → +0.35 in mid-senior↔director boundary classifier. (T20, T21.)

**Formal test.** (a) Cluster-robust SE on director-only regression of orchestration density on period × AI mention. (b) Pre-registered test on director-cell n (99 / 112) with bootstrap CIs. (c) Comparison with LinkedIn self-title search (director titles on LinkedIn over time) to cross-validate that the role change is in real postings, not a data-source artifact.

**Novelty / publishability.** High-moderate. The director recasting is unanticipated by the research design and has direct implications for how career ladders are communicated. Publishable as a sub-headline.

### H3. The tool-stack adopter cluster is motivated by client signaling, not internal workflow change

**Statement.** The 46-company "tool-stack adopter" cluster (Deloitte, AT&T, Amex, Aditi, Aveva, Adobe, Macquarie) rewrote their SWE postings toward AI vocabulary primarily to signal AI capability to clients (consulting, system integration, enterprise software customers), not because their internal engineering workflows actually changed proportionally.

**Exploration evidence.** Cluster composition skews consulting/SI/enterprise. Adobe is the only arguably tech-native member. No FAANG. ΔAI +0.52 vs cluster-0 moderate adopters +0.10 despite comparable engineering-workforce profiles. (T16.)

**Formal test.** (a) Compare the cluster's SWE posting AI language against their client-facing marketing language (extracted from company websites and press releases). If posting-side and marketing-side shifts are tightly coupled, client signaling is the driver. (b) Interview probe. (c) Regression: predict ΔAI from client-facing AI language strength, controlling for engineering-team AI adoption proxies.

**Novelty / publishability.** Moderate-high. Introduces "posting-as-signal-to-clients" as an alternative mechanism to "posting-as-truthful-description-of-work." Could generate a methodological contribution on disentangling the two.

### H4. Within-company archetype pivot reflects portfolio reconfiguration, not posting-mix drift

**Statement.** The 74.6% archetype pivot rate among overlap-panel companies reflects deliberate portfolio reconfiguration — companies making explicit decisions to hire for different kinds of SWE work — rather than random drift in which postings happen to be on the posting board at scrape time.

**Exploration evidence.** Rate holds at 73.2% under ≥5-per-period threshold. Median TVD 0.629. (T16.) Companies in cluster 3 show the largest pivots; their description content changes are consistent with AI-forward repositioning.

**Formal test.** (a) Compare within-company archetype distributions across short time windows (within-month or within-quarter) vs across the 2-year 2024-2026 window. If short-window variation is >> long-window variation, the "pivot" is noise. (b) Panel regression of within-company archetype TVD on observable portfolio-change signals (new products launched, organizational restructurings from press releases, M&A activity). (c) Test whether the pivot is concentrated in companies with public AI strategy announcements.

**Novelty / publishability.** Moderate-high. Very few papers estimate within-company archetype pivot rates at this frequency.

### H5. The within-archetype credential-stack convergence is driven by entry postings adding an AI-category credential

**Statement.** The 10/10 large-archetype credential-stack convergence (entry catches up to mid-senior on credential breadth) is largely explained by 2026 entry postings adding an AI-tool/ML credential category that was absent from 2024 entry postings, while mid-senior postings have always had that category implicitly.

**Exploration evidence.** T28 shows the convergence survives under the narrower T28 pattern and under V2's 6-category rebuild. T23 shows entry-level AI mention rate rose 7.2% → 31.8% (narrow) / 6.3% → 29.1% (broad). LLM/GenAI grad-internship pipelines (Visa, GM, Adobe, TikTok — the T22 top ghost-entry postings) are real AI-domain postings. (T28, T23, T22.)

**Formal test.** Decompose the credential-stack delta into per-category components. If the AI-credential component explains >50% of the convergence, the hypothesis is supported. Test whether the convergence disappears when the AI-category credential is excluded from the stack definition.

**Novelty / publishability.** Moderate. Provides a mechanism for what would otherwise be a puzzling within-domain finding. Sharpens the narrow RQ1b claim.

### H6. Entry-specialist intermediaries are a growing structural component of the SWE entry pool

**Statement.** The fraction of SWE entry postings coming from a small set of specialist intermediary employers (staffing firms, college-jobsite intermediaries, tech-giant intern pipelines) grew between 2024 and 2026 and is not captured by the current `is_aggregator` flag.

**Exploration evidence.** T06/T08/T16 identify SynergisticIT, WayUp, Jobs via Dice, Lensa, Emonics, Leidos, IBM as drivers. None flagged by `is_aggregator`. Cluster-2 ("length-stagnant entry-riser") in T16 typology is dominated by staffing firms (Anduril, BAE, Ascendion, Amazon Web Services, AMERICAN SYSTEMS) with entry rising under both operationalizations but length and scope flat.

**Formal test.** Build a dedicated intermediary classifier (title lookup + company metadata + description templating signature) and apply across a longer time window than the current 2024-2026 bracket. Test whether the intermediary share is growing as a fraction of the entry pool.

**Novelty / publishability.** Moderate. Methodological contribution to entry-level SWE posting research.

### H7. Specification dependence of junior metrics is a first-class threat to validity — publishable as methodological contribution

**Statement.** For longitudinal SWE posting research, `seniority_final` and a label-independent YOE proxy have <10% row overlap and disagree in direction on within-company overlap panels. Single-operationalization junior-share claims are structurally unreliable.

**Exploration evidence.** T06 <10% overlap, 115-company overlap panel direction flip. T16 n=240 panel: `seniority_final` within −0.032, YOE ≤ 2 within +0.015. T08 <10% row overlap. (T06, T16, T08.)

**Formal test.** Replicate the direction flip across independent posting datasets — Burning Glass / Lightcast, Indeed, Revelio, LinkedIn-full. If the direction flip is specific to our dataset, it's an instrument issue. If it replicates, it's a publishable field-wide methodological contribution.

**Novelty / publishability.** High, as a methods paper or companion piece. This is the strongest RQ5 candidate.

### H8. Recruiter-LLM drafting adoption is the mechanism behind length growth, template homogenization, AND the mentor-language rise

**Statement.** The style-shift detected by T29 is driven by recruiter-LLM drafting tool adoption, not by generic text drift. Companies that adopted recruiter-LLM tools between 2024 and 2026 show (a) larger authorship-score shifts, (b) larger length growth, (c) larger within-company posting homogenization, and (d) larger mentor-language insertion.

**Exploration evidence.** T29 median shift +0.33 std, 88.7% of 2026 above 2024 median. Top 2026 LLM-score companies include Alignerr (0.826), Walmart (0.505), LinkedIn (0.499), Intuit (0.467). Walmart scored high already in 2024 (+0.097) and rose to +0.505. (T29.)

**Formal test.** Build a corpus-level authorship classifier calibrated on labeled pre-2023 postings (definitely human) and post-2024 postings (likely LLM). Identify company-level adopters. Test whether top-LLM-score companies correlate with known recruiter-tool adopter lists (Gem, Beamery, HireEZ, Paradox, etc.). Run the style-matched unifying test at the company level rather than the posting level.

**Novelty / publishability.** Moderate-high. T29 as currently executed is partially instrument-confounded (bullet density, raw-vs-cleaned text). A company-level authorship analysis would resolve the ambiguity and produce a publishable methodological diagnostic.

### H9. LLM/GenAI is a separate labor market segment, not a continuation of the ML/AI archetype

**Statement.** The 2024 ML/AI cluster and the 2026 LLM/GenAI cluster are structurally different labor market segments — different employer bases, different technology stacks, different entry profiles — and should be modeled as two segments rather than one trend.

**Exploration evidence.** 138 / 1,174 overlap between 2024 and 2026 LLM/GenAI clusters. 68.2% new entrants. Different senior-junior content pattern (LLM/GenAI senior-junior gap WIDENS +1.4 on requirement_breadth while every other archetype converges). Different top employers (Anthropic, Microsoft AI, Alignerr, Harvey in 2026 vs the classic 2024 ML companies). (T28, T16.)

**Formal test.** (a) Propensity-score balance test across observables — are 2024 ML and 2026 LLM/GenAI companies drawn from the same distribution? (b) Out-of-sample prediction — does a 2024-trained archetype classifier predict 2026 LLM/GenAI correctly, or does it need new features? (c) Panel regression with separate segment dummies.

**Novelty / publishability.** Moderate. The bifurcation finding (existing + new-entrant) is already in the paper; this hypothesis sharpens the "new entrant" reading.

### H10. The AI × senior interaction is localized to tech orchestration, NOT to people management — and it is AI-driven not template-driven

**Statement.** In 2026, AI-mentioning senior postings have identical people-management density to non-AI senior postings, but +76% technical-orchestration density. This uplift is driven by real role-content shift, not by template insertion, because the cross-seniority pattern (entry −13% people-management, mid-senior +25%, director −21%) is non-monotonic — templates would produce uniform uplift across levels.

**Exploration evidence.** T21 AI × senior interaction table, cross-seniority comparison, validated orchestration pattern at 100% precision on V2 audit. (T21.)

**Formal test.** (a) Company fixed-effects regression of orchestration density on period × AI mention × seniority, clustering on company. The AI × period × seniority (mid-senior, director) interaction should be significant. (b) Within-company pattern: among companies with both AI and non-AI senior postings, compare orchestration density. (c) Style-matched test on the orchestration density delta specifically.

**Novelty / publishability.** High. This is the cleanest causal-looking link between AI adoption and senior-role content in the exploration.

## 5. Key tensions the analysis phase must resolve

Five tensions or puzzles worth explicit attention. These must be addressed in the paper draft or the analysis will be one reviewer question away from a revision.

### Tension 1 — T21 non-monotonic people-management pattern vs T29 mentor style correlation

**The puzzle.** T21 finds people-management density rose +25% at mid-senior but FELL at entry (−13%) and director (−21%). This non-monotonic pattern rules out a field-wide template-shift explanation — if recruiter-LLM drafting were inserting mentoring language uniformly, entry and director would have risen too. BUT T29 separately finds that the `mentor` sub-pattern specifically is correlated with LLM-authorship score. Both findings are sound.

**Reconciliation.** The honest reading is that (a) some fraction of the mid-senior people-management rise is style-driven (the mentor component), and (b) some fraction is real — driven by the non-monotonic level pattern. The orchestration rise (+98% mid-senior, +156% director) is far less style-correlated and is the cleaner senior-redefinition signal.

**What the analysis phase must do.** Run a style-matched test specifically on the narrow people-management pattern set by level. If the mid-senior +25% attenuates under style matching but the director −21% and entry −13% hold, the honest story is "style drove the mentor component; real role redefinition drove the orchestration component." Cite that decomposition explicitly in the paper rather than presenting the +25% as pristine.

### Tension 2 — T28 credential-stack flip count is definition-dependent

**The puzzle.** T28 reports the entry-vs-mid-senior credential-stack gap flips sign in 7 of 10 large archetypes. V2's independent 6-category credential-stack finds only 2 of 10 flip. Both re-derivations find 10/10 convergence in the expected direction; only the flip count differs.

**Reconciliation.** The convergence direction is robust; the sign-flip count is pattern-dependent. Cite 10/10 convergence as the robust claim. Flip count should be a secondary observation with explicit definition dependence: "2-7 depending on credential pattern set." (Gate 3 narrowing 2 applied.)

**What the analysis phase must do.** Fix one credential-stack definition before running the primary; report the per-archetype gap (not just the flip count) under that definition. Report robustness across at least two definitions (T28 + V2 6-category) in the appendix. Avoid the "7/10" framing entirely.

### Tension 3 — T23 per-tool worker rates are extrapolated, not surveyed

**The puzzle.** SO 2025 publishes per-tool shares only among the "AI agent user" subset (17% of respondents). T23 derives per-tool unconditional rates by multiplying these shares by 80.8% any-use. The 54.9% Copilot / 66.0% ChatGPT / 33.0% Claude Code numbers are extrapolations with an implicit assumption that tool-share is uniform between agent users and non-agent users.

**Reconciliation.** The broad-AI ratio (0.35) is computed directly from unconditional rates (28.6% posting vs 80.8% worker) and does not have this problem. Use broad-AI as the lead divergence number. Per-tool claims should be softened to "approximately 10-15× worker-to-posting ratio" rather than specific figures. Do not cite 108× ChatGPT. (Gate 3 narrowing 3 applied.)

**What the analysis phase must do.** Either (a) find a pro-dev unconditional Copilot benchmark (GitHub publishes 20M total users but not pro-dev share), (b) cite the lower-bound "at least ~35% Copilot among pro-devs" from 67.9% conditional × 50% most-conservative any-use, or (c) re-anchor the lead on broad AI alone. Present the per-tool claims with explicit extrapolation disclaimers.

### Tension 4 — T29 length-flip direction is feature-set-dependent

**The puzzle.** T29 reports the char_len delta flips from +377 to −411 under style matching. V2 shows this depends on the authorship-feature composite: +473 under a 3-feature equal-weight composite, −264 under em-dash only, +704 under bullet only, −411 under T29's 7-feature composite. The authorship score correlates r = 0.59 with char_len, so matching on it mechanically flattens length. The sign-flip is a specification artifact.

**Reconciliation.** Cite the attenuation story — "length growth is mostly style migration, not primarily content expansion" — supported by 23-62% attenuation on tech_count, scope_density, and requirement_breadth under ANY matching specification. The 0-7% attenuation on AI is robust. The sharp −411 flip is not. (Gate 3 narrowing 1 applied.)

**What the analysis phase must do.** Drop the "length flips sign" headline. Report attenuation across multiple specifications. Note that char_len is too tightly coupled to the authorship composite to style-match directly. If the paper wants a defensible length finding, it should use section-stratified T13 decomposition (responsibilities + role_summary + preferred, not requirements) as the substantive claim — that is not style-dependent.

### Tension 5 — Director-level boundary blur: real role change or thin-sample noise?

**The puzzle.** Director cells are small (99 / 112). T20's mid-senior↔director AUC blur (−0.061) and T21's director orchestration +156% and people-management −21% depend on these cells. V2 confirms that excluding top-10 AI-heavy director companies still yields +120% orchestration — but that still leaves director findings vulnerable to small-n noise.

**Reconciliation.** The convergent evidence (T20 feature coefficient flip, T21 density shifts, V2 top-10 exclusion robustness) is strong enough to assert directionally but too thin to assert with tight precision. Report CIs on every director finding. Treat the director recasting as a leading hypothesis for analysis-phase follow-up, not a settled fact.

**What the analysis phase must do.** (a) Bootstrap CIs on director cells explicitly. (b) Test the director finding on independent data sources if available. (c) Build a director-specific follow-up analysis that uses T09 + T14 + T21 + T20 outputs jointly. (d) Flag the finding as an analysis-phase hypothesis, not an exploration conclusion, in the paper draft.

## 6. Data gaps — what we could not answer that we wish we could

1. **Tool-specific pro-dev unconditional adoption rates over time.** Stack Overflow publishes aggregates; we need per-tool rates without the agent-user subset extrapolation. Anthropic / Accenture / GitClear cross-checks help but are not directly comparable. An analysis-phase data acquisition task.

2. **Within-company longitudinal signal at a finer temporal resolution than 2 years.** The 2024 and 2026 snapshots are points in time; we cannot tell whether the archetype pivot rate (74.6%) is smooth drift or abrupt restructuring. Continued daily scraping will close this gap for future work.

3. **Company-level authorship adoption events.** When did Alignerr, Walmart, LinkedIn, Intuit start using recruiter-LLM drafting tools? Without this, the T29 mechanism hypothesis can only be tested correlationally.

4. **A director-specific panel with sufficient sample.** The exploration has 99 / 112 director-labeled rows. Any director-specific analysis is underpowered at the current sample. A dedicated director-title extraction would expand this.

5. **Indeed cross-validation on the senior-orchestration and RQ3 findings.** T18 is LinkedIn-only, T23 is LinkedIn-only. Indeed has thin seniority coverage; sensitivity checks on the AI posting rate would strengthen the field-wide claim.

6. **Pre-2024 baselines.** The 2024 snapshots (asaniczka Jan, arshkon Apr) are close together in time. A longer 2022-2024 baseline would calibrate the "authorship shift" direction more credibly — how much of the 2024-2026 +0.33 std is a genuine acceleration vs a continuation of pre-existing drift?

7. **Worker self-reported posting-language-comprehension data.** Do developers read the posting, and how closely do they match its requirements against their actual workflow? This is a qualitative-phase ask but worth flagging.

8. **Industry-stratified cross-period pooling.** Asaniczka has 0% industry coverage, so all industry findings are arshkon-vs-scraped only. Any industry-specific claim has this constraint.

9. **Scraped archetype coverage on raw text.** 30.5% scraped-side T09 label coverage is the binding constraint on every within-archetype 2026 claim. A lightweight classifier that labels on raw text rather than LLM-cleaned text would broaden this substantially.

10. **Cross-platform posting-to-application conversion data.** Do applicants disproportionately apply to AI-forward postings? If yes, the RQ3 divergence has a selection interpretation (employers are writing what applicants respond to); if no, the posting-as-signal hypothesis is weakened.

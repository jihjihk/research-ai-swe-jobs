# T10. Title taxonomy evolution

Agent G (Wave 2). Run: 2026-04-15.
Data: shared `swe_cleaned_text.parquet` (63,701 SWE LinkedIn rows) joined to `title_normalized` from `data/unified.parquet`. Text-based cosine step uses `text_source='llm'` subset (34,258 rows).
Script: `exploration/scripts/T10_title_taxonomy.py`.

---

## Headline finding

**Two simultaneous structural shifts in the SWE title landscape.** (1) The share of titles containing AI/ML/LLM/agent/data terms more than doubled, from 9.5% in 2024 to 23.1% in 2026, driven primarily by the `ai` token itself (1.0% → 9.8%, 9.4×) and `agent` (0.03% → 1.1%, 41×). (2) On the same `title` field, the `senior` marker dropped from 41.7% to 28.9% of postings (−12.8 pp), while `staff` rose from 2.7% to 5.8% (+3.1 pp) and `principal` fell 5.2% → 3.7%. The title space is also becoming **more fragmented**, not more standardized: unique titles per 1,000 postings rose 407 → 448, and the top-50 title concentration dropped 36.4% → 31.6%. The TF-IDF-cosine drift on the top 10 shared titles is moderate — `system engineer` (cos 0.75) and `test engineer` (0.77) drift most, `software engineer` (0.91) least — consistent with "roles whose content was reworked most" being systems/QA-adjacent rather than core SWE.

The Gate 1 RQ1 junior-narrowing framing is not visible in titles: explicit junior/entry markers are near zero in both periods (`junior` 0.6% → 0.8%, `entry_level` 0.07% → 0.24%), so titles are not doing the signaling work that the `seniority_final` or YOE-based proxies do. The vocabulary shift is happening in the **senior/staff/principal band** and in the **AI compound band**, not in titles labeled "junior."

---

## Methodology

1. **Title vocabulary comparison** uses `title_normalized` (lowercased, pipeline-normalized). Note: the preprocessing pipeline strips seniority prefixes from `title_normalized`. For seniority-marker analysis only, I fall back to the raw `title` column (lowercased). Everything else uses `title_normalized`.
2. **Concentration**: count of distinct `title_normalized` values per 1,000 SWE postings; HHI across title shares; top-10 and top-50 title shares.
3. **AI compound shares**: 8 regex patterns (`ai`, `ml`, `llm`, `agent`, `data`, `genai`, `nlp`, `deep learning`) applied to `title_normalized`. A title is counted once if any pattern matches.
4. **Title-to-content cosine**: for each of the top 10 shared titles (`n_2024 ≥ 20` and `n_2026 ≥ 20` in the LLM-text subset), fit a TF-IDF vectorizer on the pooled 2024+2026 descriptions, average the 2024 rows into one vector and the 2026 rows into another, compute cosine.
5. **Seniority marker shares** on raw `title` for 12 markers.
6. **New 2026 title thematic grouping**: 12 theme regex patterns; titles matching any are tagged; "other" for unmatched.
7. **Essential sensitivity (a)**: aggregator exclusion. Reran the new-titles list with `is_aggregator = false`. Top 20 barely changes (see `new_titles_top100_no_aggregator.csv`).
8. **Recommended sensitivity (c)**: seniority operationalization is not directly applicable to title-taxonomy metrics (titles are structural, not a seniority output); we instead report seniority-marker shares in titles as a complement.

|---|---|---|---|

| principal | 5.2% | 3.7% | −1.6 |
| manager | 1.3% | 0.6% | −0.7 |
| **staff** | 2.7% | 5.8% | **+3.1** |
| mid_ii_iii | 4.7% | 5.9% | +1.2 |
| associate | 0.5% | 0.8% | +0.3 |
| junior | 0.6% | 0.8% | +0.2 |
| entry_level | 0.07% | 0.24% | +0.2 |

**The "senior title share" dropped 12.8 pp** between 2024 and 2026. This is compatible with three mechanisms:
- real senior-role decline (composition),
- relabeling of senior work under "staff" (staff rose +3.1 pp) or mid-level markers (+1.2 pp),
- different title-writing norms in the scraped source.

The simultaneous staff rise, combined with the Gate 1 finding that cross-period junior-share signals are below instrument noise, favors **relabeling**: the same band of work that used to be "Senior SWE" is increasingly being posted as "Staff SWE" or as bare "SWE." Independently, `architect` fell 2% → 0.4%, and `principal` fell, suggesting the "above senior" band is actually shrinking, not growing. This is a T21 follow-up.

## Surprises & unexpected patterns

1. **The title space fragmented, not standardized.** I expected unique-titles-per-1K to hold steady or drop as posters converge on canonical names ("Software Engineer" as default). Instead, unique-per-1K rose 10% and top-50 share fell 5 pp. Employers are coining more bespoke variants, especially compound AI titles.
2. **"Senior" as a title marker fell 12.8 pp.** None of the Wave 1 reports set this expectation — Gate 1's focus was on entry share. A ~30% relative drop in a title marker that big is potentially a headline-adjacent finding: the senior rung may be relabeling rather than narrowing from below.
3. **`agent` in titles grew 41×.** From 6 postings in 2024 to 441 in 2026. The `agent`/`ai agent`/`agentic ai` band is a distinct emerging role type, not a rebranding of ML engineer. This is new structural content that T09 and T14 should validate.
4. **Disappeared titles are dominated by formatting shifts.** The "top 5 disappeared" list has 4 of 5 entries that are punctuation/parenthetical variants of still-common roles. This degrades the naive interpretation of "truly disappeared" and suggests future work should use a more aggressive title canonicalizer (`(sre) → sre`, `- remote → ""`, etc.) before this step.
5. **Title-to-content drift is moderate even for the biggest drifters (cos 0.75).** I expected either near-identity (>0.9) or dramatic divergence (<0.5). The 0.75-0.90 band suggests "same job family, thickened/reworked requirements" rather than "same title, different role."

## Data quality caveats

1. **Cosine denominator is thin on the 2026 side for several top-10 titles.** `system engineer` has n=46, `java developer` n=42, `systems engineer` n=74. These cosines are stable enough to rank but not precise enough to tightly interval-estimate. Flagged in the table.
2. **`title_normalized` strips seniority prefixes.** All seniority-marker analysis uses raw `title` to recover that signal. The two are not fully comparable — `title_normalized` is cleaner for title-content analysis, raw `title` is required for seniority-marker detection.
3. **Disappeared-titles list is sensitive to title canonicalization.** Pipeline-level title canonicalization is modest (lowercase + whitespace); more aggressive canonicalization would shrink the "disappeared" list substantially.
4. **Text subset for cosine step is `text_source='llm'` only.** On the 2026 side this is ~30% of scraped SWE rows; on 2024 it's ~94-100%. Per-title n is effectively capped by the 2026 LLM subset. Thin cells have been flagged in the table.
5. **"Other" theme in new titles is 34% of new postings.** The theme classifier is rule-based and rough; 5,523 new titles fell outside the 12 theme regexes. A more thorough theme taxonomy would shrink "other," but the dominant named themes (AI/ML, platform, fullstack, data) are robust to the threshold.

## Sensitivity summary

| Dimension | Applied? | Result |
|---|---|---|
| (a) aggregator exclusion | **yes** | `new_titles_top100_no_aggregator.csv`; the top-20 new titles barely change (AI Engineer, AI Solutions Architect, etc. still top). Aggregator exclusion is not the story. |
| (b) company cap 50 | N/A | title vocabulary is a per-posting unit, not a corpus-level metric. Cap-50 would deflate counts without changing the comparison. |
| (c) seniority operationalization | **complement** | Reported via title seniority markers (on raw `title`) rather than applied to the new/disappeared title cut. |
| (d) description text source | partial | The cosine step uses `text_source='llm'` only. Raw-text fallback would be a second run; not essential since cosine is a relative ranking, not a level claim. |
| (e) source restriction | deferred | arshkon vs scraped vs pooled-2024 split is applied in T11 for complexity metrics; for title vocabulary, the aggregate view is what the question demands. |
| (f) within-2024 calibration | deferred to T11 | title vocabulary does not have a natural "metric" to calibrate. |

## Action items for downstream tasks

1. **T11 (this agent)** already incorporates the AI-term explosion in its `ai_term_count` metric — T10 results corroborate.
2. **T21 (senior role evolution)** should take the 12.8 pp senior-title drop as a primary diagnostic and ask whether this is relabeling (staff rose, architect fell) or real compositional change. The two-ply `senior→staff` relabeling hypothesis is testable on the overlap panel: same companies, same employers, did "senior" postings become "staff" postings?
3. **T09 (archetype discovery)** should test whether the `ai_agent`/`agentic` cluster is a distinct archetype or embedded within a broader AI/ML domain. If distinct, this is the strongest candidate for a truly new 2026 archetype.
4. **T22 (ghost forensics)** should spot-check the outlier new titles (e.g., `founding ai engineer`, `forward deployed ai engineer`) for aspirational/ghost patterns.
5. **Preprocessing owner** should consider adding stronger title canonicalization (strip parentheticals, normalize `-remote`/`, remote`, etc.) in a Stage 3+ title step. It would improve T10-style analyses without affecting downstream.

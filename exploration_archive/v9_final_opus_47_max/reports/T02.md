## Interpretation

Asaniczka's `associate` label is a broad LinkedIn platform label that includes meaningful mid-level representation, not a "junior only" bucket. The YOE distribution (mean 4.44, median 4) and the LLM re-read (35% mid-senior) both point to a label universe that spans roughly 2â€“7 years YOE â€” somewhere between arshkon-entry (mean 4.21) and arshkon-mid-senior (mean 5.92), but leaning senior.

Importantly, **asaniczka `associate` is also not equivalent to arshkon `associate`** â€” the arshkon `associate` label has a lower mean YOE (3.90 vs 4.44) and a lower senior-tail share (`â‰Ą5` = 34.3% vs 49.5%). So even restricting to "label-matched associate" does not give a consistent 2024 2024 operationalization across the two Kaggle snapshots.

Under the `seniority_final` schema, asaniczka still has 277 entry + 39 associate SWE rows in the LLM-labeled default frame. That's an n for entry-level of 277 in asaniczka and 177 + 15 = 192 in arshkon and 1,650 + 190 = 1,840 in scraped. The LLM does the entry-labeling work on asaniczka; the native label is just too coarse to substitute.

## Why the title cue signals are weak on Kaggle

The extreme sparsity of junior/senior title cues in Kaggle-source data (0.22% junior in arshkon-mid-senior, 0.95% in arshkon-entry) was itself a surprise. Looking at the top titles:

- arshkon `seniority_native='entry'` top 15: "software engineer" (52), "data engineer" (24), "developer" (23), "systems engineer" (19), "java developer" (18). Almost no explicit "junior" or "entry-level" in title.
- arshkon `seniority_native='mid-senior'` top 15: "software engineer" (244), "data engineer" (82), "devops engineer" (53). Also no explicit "senior" in many titles.

Kaggle snapshots use LinkedIn's platform-level seniority label (applied by employer selection in the posting flow), but titles are generic. This is different from explicit-title conventions in tech industry (Google, Amazon, etc.) and may reflect either smaller employers or different employer norms in January 2024. In scraped 2026 data, explicit-title share is much higher (e.g., `seniority_native='entry'` SWE in scraped has 226 junior-cue hits out of 5,247 = 4.3%, vs 0.95% in arshkon 2024).

Implication for Wave 2+: **title-based seniority heuristics are much more useful in 2026 scraped data than in 2024 Kaggle data**, which could confound period-comparison findings. Be skeptical of any "explicit junior title % rose between 2024 and 2026" claim â€” it may be a source-selection artifact rather than a real market signal.

## Surprises & unexpected patterns

1. **Asaniczka `associate` is MORE senior-skewed than arshkon `associate`** on YOE (`â‰Ą5` share 49.5% vs 34.3%). This was unexpected â€” the expectation was that the two sources' `associate` labels would be comparable. They are not. Asaniczka's `associate` is a broader bucket spanning mid-level.

2. **35% of asaniczka `associate` SWE descriptions are labeled mid-senior by the LLM**, vs 21% of arshkon `associate`. Either asaniczka's platform labels are noisier in the juniorâ†’mid boundary or asaniczka's descriptions use more senior-signaling language in practice.

3. **Kaggle SWE titles are surprisingly generic.** Only 0.8% of arshkon `seniority_native='mid-senior'` SWE titles contain `senior` / `lead` / etc. This is much lower than expected and much lower than 2026 scraped data. Either the Kaggle snapshots are drawing from a different employer universe (smaller companies?) or employers in 2024 labeled "mid-senior" without using senior words in the title. A direct check of same-title Ă— same-source across 2024 â†’ 2026 in Wave 3 would help disambiguate.

4. **Top-title Jaccard is low across the board.** Even within-source same-seniority groups have Jaccard ~0.4â€“0.5; cross-source or cross-seniority drops to 0.28â€“0.43. The SWE title universe is fragmented â€” there is no dominant "top 50" that both sources agree on. This has implications for same-title cohort designs (T31): truly overlapping same-title cohorts may be small.

5. **Arshkon `associate` â†’ `seniority_final='entry'` at 8%**, which is higher than arshkon `entry` â†’ `seniority_final='entry'` at 7%. In other words, the LLM re-reads arshkon descriptions and produces similar entry rates regardless of whether the native label was `entry` or `associate`. The native `entry` label is thus only weakly informative relative to what the description says â€” another argument against over-weighting `seniority_native` in cross-period comparisons.

6. **Asaniczka `associate` senior-cue rate (1.65%) is above arshkon-associate (1.14%) and about half of arshkon mid-senior (2.91%).** This is consistent with asaniczka-associate being a broader bucket than arshkon-associate.

## Data quality caveats

- **All LLM-based signals are restricted to `llm_classification_coverage='labeled'`** â€” deferred / not_selected rows are out-of-frame. For arshkon and asaniczka this is 99%+ of SWE, so the bias is minimal, but it does exclude a tiny fraction.
- **Top-title Jaccard uses top-50 by count**. Different top-k choices would shift the values slightly but should not change the rank-order conclusion.
- **"Entry"-rate fractions should be interpreted as rates of LLM matching an explicit description signal**, not actual entry-level prevalence. These are label-independent to the extent that they use raw text input to the LLM.

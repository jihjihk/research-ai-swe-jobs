# Critic: 03_atlanta_not_san_francisco

**Verdict:** Structurally strong thesis, but conflates three distinct "AI geographies" and over-reads a statistically weak correlation. Needs scoping fixes before publication.

## Revisions required

- **Apples-to-oranges sources (para 2).** Brookings measures AI *job posts/employment*, CBRE measures AI *talent stock*, PitchBook measures AI *investment dollars*. The piece treats all three as one "consolidation" claim and then refutes it with its own *posting-rate-of-change* data — a fourth construct. At minimum, rewrite para 2 to name the three metrics separately, and acknowledge that our rebuttal only directly engages the Brookings posting layer. The PitchBook/CBRE points are not actually contradicted by our data.

- **Quantitative claims not in the evidence block:**
  - "six times the national rate" (CBRE) — cite-only, fine but flag as source claim.
  - "52% of American AI investment" (PitchBook) — cite-only, fine but flag.
  - "grew some fifteen-fold" for the applied-AI senior archetype — *not* in evidence block. Must add a T34 row or delete.
  - "The collective premium on SF, Seattle, NY, Austin, Boston over the rest sits below 2 pp" — evidence block says "tech-hub premium <2 pp" but does not list the five metros; add explicit T17 row.

- **Missing denominators:** 10.5% metro vs 11.1% remote AI rate — of what base? Give N postings for each. The "26 metros with ≥50 postings" floor should appear in-body, not only the evidence block.

- **r = −0.22 at n=26 (p ≈ 0.28)** is not statistically distinguishable from zero. The piece leans on this correlation to argue AI-change and junior-change "move along different axes." Soften: say the two series are *uncorrelated in our sample* and drop any causal phrasing. The current "Neither pattern is predicted by either metro's AI-language growth" overclaims.

- **Atlanta-leads vs Bay-Area-26% tension (para 6).** The resolution — rate vs. level — is stated but buried. Promote it earlier; a reader could otherwise reasonably conclude the piece contradicts itself.

- **"Diffusion" vs flat profile (para 4 and closing).** The data show a *uniform rise*, not diffusion *from* a hub *to* peripheries. "The Bay Area is where the diffusion started and moved on from" is narrative, not evidence. Either rewrite as "adoption is already broadly distributed" or add a time-series showing Bay-Area leadership in an earlier era.

- **n=26 heterogeneity caveat.** Evidence block admits junior-share SNR <2 on 13/18 metros. The San Diego +15 / Detroit −5 contrast in para 3 should carry that caveat in-body.

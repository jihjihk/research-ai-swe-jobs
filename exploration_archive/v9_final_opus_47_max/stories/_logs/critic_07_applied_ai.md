# Critic: 07_applied_ai_older

## Revisions

- **Unsourced quantitative claim — Fastly "2.5× more AI-assisted code per day."** Not in the evidence block, not in the sensitivity verdict, and the evidence block itself says Fastly measures productivity — not YOE asks. Either pull from a cited Fastly figure or cut the number and paraphrase directionally. As written, it's a smuggled statistic.

- **"Roughly twice that of the nearest comparator" (director share) overstates a 0.9 pp gap on a ~2% base.** The evidence notes 1.9% of 2,251 = 43 directors — small-n. "2×" is technically true but rhetorically load-bearing for a thin margin. Downgrade to "modestly higher" or report the raw counts ("43 vs ~10-ish") and let the reader judge. Do not lead the paragraph with it.

- **Median 6 vs 5 is a 1-year gap — the piece needs to own this, not bury it.** The sensitivity verdict concedes "could shift by 0.3 under alternative cluster-boundary definitions." A 1.0-year gap that could move ±0.3 is material but not dramatic. The current phrasing ("more years of experience, not fewer") is defensible; the closing ("6 years, architecturally senior, and expensive" vs a hypothetical "3-to-4") inflates the gap into a 3-year gulf. Tighten the counterfactual or lose it.

- **Strawman risk on Karpathy/Huang/Altman/Welsh.** Their claim is "programming ends / English is the language" — about the act of coding, not about the hiring bar for AI-specialist roles. The piece's evidence addresses the narrower "Applied-AI requisitions want seniors." One paragraph should concede that the piece tests a sub-claim of the democratisation thesis, not its main trunk. Otherwise it reads as rebutting an argument the opponents didn't quite make.

- **"Democratise" is doing pejorative work.** The thesis is about access to programming capability; the evidence is about hiring-bar for a specific frontier role. Separate these explicitly — maybe one sentence: "Access to code-writing is one question; the credential gate on building production LLM systems is another." This protects the piece from the charge of equivocation.

- **Counter-voices (Fastly, Orosz, Harvard 62M) are flagged in evidence but absent from the body.** At minimum, Orosz's "Seniority Rollercoaster" deserves a nod — it's the strongest adjacent finding. One sentence engaging the best counter makes the piece harder to dismiss.

- **Closing sentence overreaches.** "Not collapsing the skill ceiling on the work that most closely resembles it" generalises from requisition-YOE to *actual work performed*. The evidence is about what employers ask for, not what the job contains. Soften to "the credential bar on that work" or "what employers are willing to pay for."

## Verdict

Central claim holds; presentation inflates a modest gap, smuggles an uncited stat, and fights a broader strawman than the evidence supports — revise for calibration, not for retraction.

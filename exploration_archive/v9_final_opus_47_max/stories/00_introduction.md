# Corners of the Screen

*What the software-engineering job-postings data says when no one is listening.*

The public argument about artificial intelligence and software-engineering work is loud, anecdotal and curiously under-tested. From Substack to the Financial Times, one version of the story predominates: AI will eat the junior rung, redistribute work to the senior, collapse the skill premium and concentrate the whole thing in San Francisco. Each of those four claims has been repeated, by named commentators in reputable outlets, through the twenty-four months during which the data for this collection were gathered. Three of them — and part of the fourth — the data do not support.

This collection comprises short pieces drawn from an unusually deep exploration of the period. The underlying corpus is 68,000 software-engineering job advertisements collected from LinkedIn between 2024 and 2026, across three data sources; the exploration that produced the pieces ran to eight phases, thirty-eight analytical tasks, two adversarial verification gates, four research memos, and a nine-thousand-word synthesis. Every claim in every piece is anchored to a specific number, and every number has been independently re-derived by a second investigator before publication.

What the collection contributes to the public argument is not more opinion, of which there is already a surplus. It is a small number of sharp, quantitative findings that contradict confident public predictions. The junior rung is not where software hiring thinned. Regulated finance is not an AI laggard in what it asks of engineers. The most-adopted AI tool is the least-codified requirement. The emergent AI-specialist role asks for more years of experience, not fewer. Employers' ranking of which occupations AI has changed is essentially identical to the workers'. The geography of AI in ordinary job descriptions is not Silicon Valley.

A handful of the pieces are methodological. One, in particular — on a "management declined" finding that collapsed under semantic-precision testing — argues that a small but growing share of the published labour-economics literature on job postings may be vulnerable to the same treatment, though nobody has performed it.

None of these findings ends the argument. That is not a news magazine's job. What the pieces do is insist that the argument be conducted in light of the evidence — and provide, for the reader willing to read beyond the headlines, the evidence.

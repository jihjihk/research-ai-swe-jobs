# Findings

Six headline findings from this study. Each links to the analysis that produced it and to the robustness checks that tested it.

## Lead findings

| # | Finding | Page |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Employers describe AI work at a fraction of the rate workers report using it | [A1](a1-cross-occupation-divergence.md) |
| 2 | When firms add AI language to postings, they rewrite the same titles rather than invent new ones | [A2](a2-within-firm-rewriting.md) |
| 3 | Junior and senior job descriptions moved apart between 2024 and 2026, not together | [A3](a3-seniority-sharpening.md) |
| 4 | Every tier asks for broader skills than in 2024; senior roles gained more breadth than junior | [A4](a4-scope-inflation.md) |
| 5 | Two new senior archetypes stand out in 2026: applied-AI/LLM engineer and data-platform/DevOps engineer | [A5](a5-archetypes.md) |
| 6 | The software-engineering vs. control gap doesn't depend on which controls we picked | [A6](a6-did-robustness.md) |

## What we rejected

Three alternative explanations for the AI rise were formally rejected; two more were rejected as dominant mediators. Ruling these out is part of the argument. See [What we rejected](rejections.md).

## Measurement corrections

One earlier finding was corrected under adversarial verification; a second was demoted to a flagged claim. See [Measurement corrections](corrections.md).

## How each finding page is organized

1. **Claim** — one sentence.
2. **Key figure** — the single most important visual.
3. **Evidence** — the analyses behind the claim, with magnitudes.
4. **Sensitivity verdict** — how the claim held up under robustness checks.
5. **Dig deeper** — links to the underlying task reports.

    T07 — External benchmarks & power analysis¶

    Agent: D (Wave 1) Date: 2026-04-20 Inputs: data/unified.parquet; BLS OES state file state_M2024_dl.xlsx (May 2024 release, fetched via Wayback); FRED JTU5100JOL (JOLTS Information, NSA) and JTSJOL (JOLTS total nonfarm, SA). T30 panel CSV status at run time: not yet published — definitions computed locally per spec.

    Headline¶

    Cross-period comparisons are well-powered for total SWE volume, most senior definitions, and the YOE-based junior definitions (J3, J4), but underpowered for native-label-only entry (J1) in the arshkon-vs-scraped comparison. Pooling arshkon + asaniczka as the 2024 baseline rescues J3/J4 into "strong" territory. S2 (director-only) is severely underpowered across all comparisons. State-level geographic correlation with BLS OES is r = 0.97 linear, 0.94 log-log for pooled 2024 — well above the r > 0.80 target. JOLTS Information openings in 2026 YTD (97k NSA avg) are 0.71× the 2023 average (136.8k), confirming we are comparing a mid/late-cycle 2024 snapshot to a weak 2026 scraped window — any "fewer postings" finding must control for aggregate hiring weakness.

    Recommended Wave 2 primaries (orchestrator input):

    1. Primary junior definition = J3 (YOE ≤ 2), with pooled 2024 baseline. MDE = 4.3 pp (p=0.5). This lets us detect a shift from e.g. 14 → 10 pp with 80% power.
    2. Sensitivity junior definitions = J4 (YOE ≤ 3) and J2 (entry+associate, arshkon-only). J1 is reserved for descriptive only — it cannot support a claim in the arshkon-vs-scraped comparison.
    3. Primary senior definition = S4 (YOE ≥ 5) with pooled 2024 baseline. MDE = 1.7 pp.
    4. Sensitivity senior definitions = S1 and S3. S2 (director-only) is underpowered everywhere — descriptive only.

    Aggregator-exclusion robustness. Aggregator exclusion drops pooled 2024 SWE from 22,820 → 19,447 (−15%) and scraped SWE from 45,317 → 37,609 (−17%). Verdicts flip in one place: J4 for arshkon-vs-scraped downgrades from strong to ok (0.049 → 0.051 MDE p=0.5) — still usable. J3 stays underpowered in arshkon-only. No definition that was "ok" in included becomes underpowered in excluded. The feasibility claims are robust to aggregator treatment.

    Metro-level feasibility¶

    Multi-location SWE postings excluded from per-metro rollups (correct by design):

    source multi-location unresolved metro with metro total SWE
    arshkon 116 1,805 2,770 4,691
    asaniczka 894 4,343 12,892 18,129
    scraped 3,344 8,036 33,937 45,317

    So for the per-metro analyses, 62% of arshkon SWE, 71% of asaniczka SWE, and 75% of scraped SWE are assigned to a metro. The remainder split roughly 50/50 between multi-location representatives and unresolved metros. Multi-location exclusion is ~2-7% of SWE by source.

    Metro-level qualification (≥50 SWE in both 2024 baseline and scraped):

    • Pooled 2024 baseline — 26 metros qualify at ≥50, 25 metros at ≥100. This covers the full 26-metro study frame except New York City Metro (which only has 67 SWE in pooled 2024 despite 2,220 in scraped — a huge asymmetry).
    • Arshkon-only baseline — 18 metros qualify at ≥50, only 8 at ≥100. Arshkon is too small to support metro-level arshkon-only claims for most markets.

    Conclusion: metro-level analyses should use the pooled 2024 baseline. NYC is a notable asymmetry — arshkon has only 7 SWE rows there and asaniczka has 60, versus 2,220 scraped. NYC's 2024 baseline is compositionally different from its 2026 scraped, likely because arshkon underrepresents NYC in 2024 (this is a surprise — see below).

    Tables: exploration/tables/T07/metro_counts.csv, metro_exclusion_summary.csv, metro_qualification_summary.csv.

    Part B. External benchmarks¶

    B1. BLS OES state-level correlation (geographic validation)¶

    Fetched May 2024 OEWS state file (state_M2024_dl.xlsx) from Wayback Machine (direct BLS fetch blocked 403). Filtered to SOC 15-1252 (Software Developers) and 15-1253 (Software QA Analysts and Testers).

    Note on SOC codes: The dispatch referenced SOC 15-1256 for QA/testers; the correct current SOC code is 15-1253. SOC 15-1256 does not appear in the May 2024 OES state file. Used 15-1253 throughout.

    National totals (50-state sum): 15-1252 = 1,601,380; 15-1253 = ~185k (only states with enough sample size publish). Combined ≈ 1.79M, consistent with the BLS-reported ~1.7M software developer national employment for 2024.

    Pearson correlations — state-level count vs BLS OES total (15-1252 + 15-1253):

    our_source linear r log-log r (log1p)
    arshkon 0.975 0.935
    asaniczka 0.964 0.933
    pooled 2024 0.974 0.940
    scraped 0.974 0.793

    All exceed r > 0.80 target. The log-log correlation is the more honest metric (linear correlation is driven almost entirely by the California-Texas-New York anchor points). The scraped data's lower log-log r (0.79, just below target) reflects the scraper's metro-centric coverage — many small states have zero scraped postings because no metro in those states was searched; BLS shows small but nonzero employment there. This is not a validity concern but a known coverage artifact.

    State-share comparison (share of our SWE vs share of BLS employment): r = 0.974 for pooled 2024 and 0.974 for scraped, essentially identical — our geographic distribution closely matches BLS.

    Table: bls_oes_state_counts_merged.csv, bls_oes_correlation_summary.csv. Figure: bls_oes_scatter.png.

    B2. Industry distribution¶

    Only arshkon (99.2% coverage) and scraped (100%) have company_industry. Asaniczka has none. BLS does not publish an industry × state × occupation cross for the state file, so no direct BLS industry benchmark.

    Between-period industry distribution shift (arshkon 2024 → scraped 2026):

    industry arshkon share scraped share pp change
    Software Development 13.1% 26.2% +13.1
    IT Services and IT Consulting 25.8% 12.6% −13.2
    Technology, Information and Internet 2.3% 4.9% +2.6
    Information Services 3.2% 0.4% −2.8
    Staffing and Recruiting 5.8% 2.7% −3.1
    Defense and Space Manufacturing 2.6% 3.8% +1.2
    Financial Services 8.4% 7.0% −1.4

    The "Software Development ↔ IT Services" swap is suspiciously symmetrical (+13.1 vs −13.2 pp) and combined with several new multi-label industries appearing in scraped only ("Information Services and Technology, Information and Internet", multi-industry combos), this is very likely a LinkedIn industry-taxonomy change between 2024 and 2026, not a real market shift. Industry-based analyses must therefore treat industry labels as potentially non-comparable between periods. Downstream tasks should either (a) pool Software Development + IT Services into a single "software industry" bucket, or (b) avoid industry-stratified comparisons entirely.

    Table: industry_distribution_ours.csv, industry_shift_arshkon_vs_scraped.csv, industry_summary.csv.

    B3. JOLTS Information-sector context¶

    Fetched JTU5100JOL (Information Job Openings, NSA, thousands) via Wayback — data through 2026-02.

    period avg openings (NSA, thousands) ratio vs 2023
    2023 avg 136.8 1.000
    2024 avg 122.5 0.896
    2025 avg 121.2 0.886
    2026 YTD (Jan–Feb) avg 97.0 0.709
    2026-02 (latest) 92 0.672

    Dispatch note check: The dispatch estimated 2026 Info openings at ~0.66× the 2023 average. Current FRED data through 2026-02 shows 0.71× YTD, with the single most recent month at 0.67×. The estimate was approximately right, slightly pessimistic on the YTD but well aligned with the latest month.

    Total nonfarm (JTSJOL, SA): 2023 avg 9,268k → 2026 YTD 7,061k (0.762× of 2023). So the Information sector has fallen ~30% from its 2023 peak while total nonfarm has fallen ~24% — Information sector is contracting faster than the overall economy.

    Implications for the analysis:

    1. Our 2024 baseline (arshkon ≈ April 2024; asaniczka ≈ January 2024) sits at 0.90× the 2023 peak — the 2024 market was already softer than 2023 but still well above the 2026 trough.
    2. Our 2026 scraped window (March–April 2026) is in the weakest hiring environment of the whole series since 2020.
    3. Any "fewer junior postings" finding MUST control for aggregate openings. The information-sector opening count fell ~30% from 2024 (122.5k avg) to 2026 YTD (97k) — a 21% volume decline. If our SWE posting count falls by 21%, that's the macro baseline, not entry-level-specific. Entry-level claims must be expressed as share of SWE postings, not absolute count.
    4. The weak hiring environment means composition effects dominate: if employers post fewer jobs overall, they may disproportionately keep the senior openings, shifting the composition in favor of senior roles. Interpretation of any senior-share increase needs to account for this.

    Figures: jolts_information_series.png, jolts_total_nonfarm.png. Tables: jolts_annual.csv, jolts_context_summary.csv.

    Framing: what population does our sample represent?¶

    What we sample: U.S. LinkedIn software-engineering job postings, English-language, successfully date-validated, in the default-filtered analysis frame.

    What we can generalize to: - The LinkedIn SWE job-posting market. BLS OES state-level correlation of 0.94 log-log (pooled 2024) confirms our geographic distribution is consistent with the full BLS employment footprint — we are not sampling a geographically idiosyncratic subset. - The 26-metro study frame is well-populated in both periods at the pooled 2024 baseline.

    What we cannot generalize to: - Non-LinkedIn posting channels (Indeed is available as sensitivity only; other channels absent). - Actual hires, only postings. JOLTS is openings data, not hires — our posting counts are a leading signal, not an outcome. - Seasonal comparisons. Arshkon snapshot ≈ April 2024; asaniczka ≈ January 2024; scraped ≈ March–April 2026. There is a mild seasonality effect but 2024 and 2026 are both early-year, minimizing seasonal confound. - Non-English markets.

    Surprises & unexpected patterns¶

    1. SOC 15-1256 does not exist in the May 2024 OES file; the correct code is 15-1253 (Software QA Analysts and Testers). The dispatch's referenced code appears to be from an older or different SOC edition. Used 15-1253 without material change to the conclusion (r > 0.94 pooled 2024).
    2. "Software Development" and "IT Services and IT Consulting" almost exactly swapped their shares (−13.2 pp and +13.1 pp) between arshkon and scraped. Combined with new multi-label industry combinations appearing only in scraped, this strongly suggests a LinkedIn taxonomy change rather than a real industry shift. Industry-based analysis is therefore unreliable across the 2024↔2026 comparison at the raw-label level.
    3. New York City Metro is extremely underrepresented in arshkon (7 SWE rows) — 312× smaller than scraped (2,220). This is not a multi-location or metro-assignment issue but appears to be a genuine arshkon sampling gap. NYC analyses should use the pooled 2024 baseline (67 SWE) or be reported arshkon-only with this caveat stamped on them.
    4. S2 (director-only) is underpowered in every comparison — n=17 in arshkon, n=91 pooled. Director-level claims based on the native seniority enum cannot be supported by this dataset. Senior-role drift analyses should use S1/S3/S4, not S2.
    5. Scraped not_selected rows are large (19,494 of 45,317 SWE) — 43% of the scraped SWE sample is outside the sticky LLM frame. Analyses that require LLM labels (J3, J4, S4, and anything using description_core_llm) drop to 25,816 scraped SWE. Still workable, but substantially smaller than the full 45,317.
    6. Asaniczka's SWE count after default filters is 18,129 — more than 3× arshkon's 4,691. If pooled 2024 is accepted as the baseline, asaniczka dominates it 4:1. This means "pooled 2024" conclusions are really "asaniczka conclusions, lightly augmented by arshkon." If the two sources disagree on direction (which the native entry-label asymmetry guarantees for entry share), pooled results will lean asaniczka-heavy.
    7. Scraped log-log correlation with BLS OES is 0.79 — just below the 0.80 target. The linear correlation is well above, but the log-log reflects that scraped has zero postings in 15+ small states because the scraper's metro frame doesn't cover them. This is a known coverage design choice, not an instrument failure — but analyses that describe "the SWE market nationally" should flag that the scraped data describes the 26-metro subset, not the long tail.
    8. The 2026 hiring environment is unusually weak (Information openings at ~0.71× of 2023 average; total nonfarm at ~0.76×). Pre-registering that our "decline in X" findings will be interpretable only as share-of-SWE, not volume, is essential. The dispatch's 0.66× estimate for Information openings was approximately correct.
    9. J3 (YOE ≤ 2) arshkon-only has just 449 rows and fails the MDE threshold. Interpret "YOE ≤ 2" findings cautiously in the arshkon-vs-scraped comparison — they require the pooled baseline to be statistically resolvable.

    T23. Employer-requirement / worker-usage AI divergence¶

    Agent: M (Wave 3) Date: 2026-04-20 Cohort: SWE LinkedIn English date_flag='ok', is_swe=true, from data/unified_core.parquet. n=48,634 (arshkon 4,687 + asaniczka 18,125 + scraped 25,822). Default filter: source_platform='linkedin' AND is_english=true AND date_flag='ok'. AI patterns: V1-validated ai_strict_v1_rebuilt as PRIMARY; ai_any = union of ai_tool / ai_domain / ai_general / ai_strict as the broader envelope.

    Headline findings¶

    1. Employer-side AI requirement rate rose 10× in 2 years, from a very-low base. ai_strict (V1-validated, 0.96 precision after T22 re-val) went 1.03% (2024 pooled) → 10.61% (2026 scraped). ai_any broader envelope: 5.07% → 29.36%. Even at its new 2026 level, the employer-side rate is a FRACTION of every plausible worker-side benchmark.

    2. Worker-side AI usage was ALREADY HIGH in 2024. Stack Overflow 2024 Developer Survey reported 63% of professional developers currently use AI; 2025 survey reports 84% (50.6% daily). DORA 2025 reports 90% of SWE professionals use AI at work. Anthropic Economic Index (March 2026) measures 75% task coverage for Computer Programmers. Under every band {50%, 65%, 75%, 85%}, 2026 employer AI-strict rate is 40-75 pp BELOW worker-side usage.

    3. The divergence DIRECTION is robust across all benchmark bands. Under 50% (most conservative worker-usage assumption), the 2026 gap is still +39.4 pp (worker − employer ai_strict). Under 85% it is +74.4 pp. The qualitative finding "employer requirements are much LOWER than worker usage" is not sensitive to benchmark choice.

    4. The divergence WIDENED with period. 2024 gap at 75% band = 73.97 pp; 2026 gap at 75% band = 64.39 pp. Numerically, the gap NARROWED slightly because employer-side grew 10× while worker-side grew ~1.33×. But both periods' gaps are massively positive — this is a direction-robust but magnitude-sensitive finding.

    5. Tool-specificity: employers rarely ask for specific AI tools, but when they do, those asks grew from ~0%. 2024 pooled: copilot 0.004%, cursor 0%, claude 0.013%, rag 0.088%. 2026 scraped: copilot 0.10%, cursor 1.98%, claude 3.49%, rag 5.20%. The most-asked tool-specific AI capability in 2026 is RAG (5.2%), then Claude (3.5%), then Cursor (2.0%). GitHub Copilot is the most-adopted AI tool by developers (4.7M paid subs globally) but appears in only 0.1% of postings — a massive usage-vs-requirement gap for the specifically most-adopted tool.

    6. Seniority-level pattern in employer AI asks: S4 ai_strict rate (10.99% 2026) > J3 (8.52%) — seniors are asked for AI more than entry, but the difference is modest (+2.5 pp). This CONTRADICTS a "AI is squeezing out entry roles" narrative: entry postings now mention AI at 8.5%, comparable to senior's 11%. The copilot / cursor specific rates are near-identical across J3 and S4 (1.7% J3 copilot / 2.0% S4 cursor) — AI tooling requirements are NOT more senior-gated than generic AI language.

    7. Aggregator vs direct: direct employers have SLIGHTLY higher AI-strict rates than aggregators in 2026 (10.87% direct vs 9.35% aggregator). In 2024 both were near-zero. The divergence pattern is not confounded by aggregator composition.

    Temporal divergence — how fast is each side growing?¶

    Full table at tables/T23/temporal_divergence.csv.

    Measure 2024 2025-2026 Change (pp) Ratio
    Employer ai_strict (ours) 1.03% 10.61% +9.58 pp 10.30×
    Worker SO currently use AI 63.0% 84.0% +21.0 pp 1.33×

    Both sides grew, but the ratios are starkly different: employer-side multiplied 10× from a tiny base while worker-side grew 1.33× from a large base. In absolute pp, worker-side grew MORE (21 pp vs 9.6 pp), but from a much higher starting point. The gap in absolute terms narrowed modestly (73.97 pp → 64.39 pp under 75% band), but remains multiplicatively massive — employers are ~6× slower to codify AI into formal requirements than developers are to adopt AI in practice.

    Surprises and unexpected patterns¶

    1. 2024 employer ai_strict was 1.03%. Given 2024 Stack Overflow data says 63% of developers were already using AI, employer postings in 2024 essentially IGNORED AI as a formal requirement. The divergence was already massive in 2024 — 63 pp / 60× multiplicative. This means the divergence story is NOT a 2026 phenomenon; it has been present since at least 2024.

    2. The multiplicative growth gap is ~10×. Employer-side grew 10.3× (1% → 10.6%) vs worker-side 1.33× (63% → 84%). Employer requirements are MULTIPLYING faster but STARTED SO LOW they're still far behind in absolute terms. Framing: employer formalization of AI is playing catch-up to developer adoption, not driving it.

    3. Copilot at 0.10% is astonishing. GitHub Copilot has been the #1 AI coding tool since 2023. Microsoft reports 4.7M paid subs and enterprise penetration at 90% of Fortune 100. That only 0.10% of SWE postings mention "copilot" means employers are NOT formalizing AI tool requirements even for the tool most of their employees actually use. The "copilot as entry-level requirement" narrative is false in the data.

    4. RAG at 5.20% is leading the tool-specific rise. RAG (retrieval-augmented generation) is an ARCHITECTURAL pattern, not a discrete product. Its presence in 5% of 2026 SWE postings (up from 0.09% in 2024) indicates employers are asking for AI-pipeline / vector-db competencies more than tool-specific skills. This matches the "AI-as-domain" expansion found in T09 (ML/LLM archetype emergence).

    5. Entry-level copilot rate (0.17% in 2026) is HIGHER than senior copilot rate (0.11%) — consistent with T11's finding that "copilot rate J3 4.6% > S4 4.1%" under broader patterns. AI tools are not being gated to senior levels.

    6. Anthropic's 75% task coverage is NOT a worker-adoption rate. It measures task-level usage if a programmer uses Claude. It's methodologically different from SO's "percent of developers currently use AI." The chart caveats both series to avoid false equivalence.

    7. DORA 2025's 90% is likely an upper-envelope bias (their sample is 5k tech-involved professionals, self-selected via Google-networked channels). 50% SO-daily is a more conservative worker-side point estimate. Using it still gives a 39+ pp gap in 2026.

    8. Aggregator-direct gap is small in 2026 for AI rates but in opposite direction than for ghost-indicators (T22). Aggregators post LESS AI (9.35% vs 10.87% direct on ai_strict) but MORE tech_count (7.63 vs 7.33). Aggregators seem to strip scope/aspiration language but preserve (and possibly over-emphasize) technology lists. They are a separate posting style.

    What RQ3 should carry forward¶

    The divergence is definitively direction-consistent across all reasonable worker-side benchmarks (50-85% band), cross-period robust (positive in both 2024 and 2026), and magnitude-large (20+ pp even under most charitable pairing). The qualitative finding "employers under-codify AI skills relative to worker adoption" is not sensitive to methodological choices.

    The NOVEL contribution of T23 is a DOCUMENTED DIVERGENCE PATTERN rather than an exact gap. Combined with T22's finding that 50% of near-AI-token windows contain hedging language, the emerging mechanism is: employers formally require AI in only 10% of SWE postings but WHEN they mention AI, they frame it as "preferred / bonus / nice-to-have" half the time. This aligns with RQ3: employers are expressing AI skill preferences, not codifying them as firm requirements, even though the underlying workforce already uses AI at 63-90% rates.

    For Wave 3.5 T32 (cross-occupation AI divergence), this T23 pattern is the SWE-specific baseline. If SWE-adjacent and control occupations show similar divergence patterns, RQ3 becomes a generic labor-market finding. If SWE is uniquely divergent, RQ3 becomes SWE-specific and may strengthen into a paper-lead finding.

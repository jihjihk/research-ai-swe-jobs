    T32. Cross-occupation employer/worker AI divergence (H_A)¶

    Agent: Q (Wave 3.5) Date: 2026-04-20 Cohort: All LinkedIn English date_flag='ok' across three analysis groups (SWE, SWE-adjacent, control) from data/unified_core.parquet. n=110,000 pooled (SWE 48,634; adjacent 24,700; control 36,666). 2024 = arshkon+asaniczka, 2026 = scraped. Patterns: V1-validated ai_strict_v1_rebuilt (0.96 precision) as PRIMARY employer measure. ai_broad_no_mcp drops MCP for 2024 baseline.

    Headline findings¶

    1. The SWE divergence pattern (T23) holds for EVERY cross-occupation subgroup with a benchmark (16/16 direction-universal in BOTH 2024 AND 2026). Under any-AI worker benchmark (curated 2024 external sources), every subgroup with a benchmark has employer_rate < worker_rate in BOTH periods. The divergence direction is OCCUPATION-UNIVERSAL — it is a GENERAL labor-market property of posting requirements, not SWE-specific.

    2. The divergence MAGNITUDE scales with worker exposure: Spearman(worker_any_mid, gap_2026) = +0.71, p=0.002. Occupations where workers have adopted AI more (SWE, data-scientist, ml-engineer) have the LARGEST employer-worker gaps. Occupations with low worker adoption (nurse, civil engineer) have smaller absolute gaps (0.15 − 0 = 0.15 pp vs SWE 0.84 − 0.11 = 0.73 pp).

    3. Employer AI-strict rate in 2026 correlates EXTREMELY strongly with worker benchmark: Spearman(worker_mid, employer_2026) = +0.92. Employers RANK occupations similarly to worker adoption; they just codify at FAR lower absolute rates. This is the universality-of-divergence claim: the ordering is preserved, the level is compressed.

    4. ONE subgroup breaks the rule at the 50% band: ml_engineer (adjacent). Employer 2026 ai_strict = 63.1% (n=187, thin) vs worker any-AI mid = 85%. Under 50% band gap = −13 pp (employer EXCEEDS 50% band). Under the 85% curated midpoint, gap is still +22 pp. ML Engineer is the ONLY occupation where employer AI-formalization rate approaches worker adoption, and even there it sits below the worker mid.

    5. Adjacent occupation sub-group variation spans 0.6%-63%. Within SWE-adjacent, 2026 ai_strict ranges from 0.56% (network_engineer) to 63.1% (ml_engineer, thin n=187) — a 100× spread. Data_scientist 17.4%, solutions_architect 15.6%, data_engineer 12.4%, security_engineer 5.1%. The rank-order tracks how AI-native the role is perceived to be.

    6. Control occupations have near-ZERO formalization of AI even at the 2026 benchmark. Accountant 0.69%, financial_analyst 1.37%, civil_engineer 0.47%, electrical_engineer 0.09%, mechanical_engineer 0.10%, nurse 0.00%. Nurse ai_strict is literally 0/6,801 — ZERO strict-AI-mentions in 6,801 2026 nursing job posts. Employers have not begun to codify AI skills in control occupations at ALL, despite external benchmarks showing 12-50% worker adoption in some of these fields (accounting especially: Thomson Reuters 2024 reports 44% daily-use among accountants using AI).

    7. DiD-style SWE-specificity is PRESERVED. SWE ai_strict Δ 2024→2026 = +10.6 pp (1.03% → 11.6% weighted with ml_engineer + other_swe). Adjacent Δ = +11.4 pp (but CONTAINS ml_engineer which drives half; other_adjacent Δ only +12.9 pp). Control Δ = +0.33 pp (practically zero growth). SWE employer AI rise is 30× larger than control employer AI rise (10.6 pp vs 0.33 pp). The AI-in-postings story is specific to SWE and knowledge-work-adjacent; it has NOT crossed into mature control occupations.

    8. Alternative framing (daily-use vs any-use): direction still universal but compressed. Under worker daily-use benchmarks (8-60% curated), gap stays positive across all subgroups in both periods — the qualitative finding is band-independent.

    The positive Spearman (worker-vs-gap) means occupations with higher worker adoption have LARGER gaps — not simply "everyone has the same gap". The +0.92 Spearman worker-vs-employer means employers DO differentiate by occupation in the same direction as workers, just at far lower absolute levels.

    This is the key universality result: employer AI formalization rank-tracks worker AI adoption across 16 occupations in 2026, with an ~80-90 percentile compression. If worker adoption is the ceiling, employer codification is at ~10-30% of that ceiling across the board.

    Full table at tables/T32/subgroup_divergence_daily.csv.

    Under the daily-use midpoint benchmark: - 15/16 subgroups show positive gap (ml_engineer adjacent is the exception at −3 pp). - Universality is preserved qualitatively; magnitudes compress (e.g., SWE-adjacent data_scientist gap drops from 58 pp (any-mid) to 28 pp (daily-mid)). - The robustness of the direction under the daily-use framing strengthens the divergence claim: even if we generously interpret "use AI" as "use it daily", employers still under-formalize relative to worker behavior.

    Essential sensitivities¶

    (a) Aggregator exclusion¶

    Not separately stratified in this run (T23 confirmed aggregator exclusion does not flip SWE direction; subgroup rates here pool direct+aggregator). Given the universality direction is robust across all 16 subgroups at 11× or greater gap, excluding aggregators would tighten (not flip) the direction.

    (f) Within-2024 calibration per occupation¶

    Within-2024 arshkon-vs-asaniczka source comparison for ai_strict is SNR 32.9 pooled SWE (from calibration table). For control occupations, 2024 ai_strict is near zero in both sources (electrical_engineer 0%, civil_engineer 0%, mechanical_engineer 0%, nurse 0.3%) — not calibration-noise-sensitive. For SWE-adjacent, 2024 cell rates range 0%-3% with source-source agreement within 2 pp on data_scientist (within SNR).

    (g) SWE tier¶

    SWE-strict (tier-½ regex) vs SWE-broad (including embedding-adjacent) not separately run here; core finding (SWE 11.16% 2026 ai_strict on other_swe subgroup) is on the full SWE classifier. The rate within SWE ml_engineer (47.5%) demonstrates that WITHIN SWE, sub-role matters enormously, consistent with the cross-occupation pattern.

    Surprises¶

    1. Universality is cleaner than expected. All 16 subgroups with benchmark show the same direction in both periods. Gate 2's T18 finding that SWE is DiD-specific (+14 pp vs control +0.17) was framed as an "SWE-specific labor-market" claim. T32 reframes: employer under-codification of AI is UNIVERSAL; the MAGNITUDE of under-codification is SWE-concentrated, but the DIRECTION is occupation-universal. Paper should carry BOTH framings.

    2. Nurse ai_strict = 0.00% on 6,801 2026 postings is astonishing. 31.5% of US hospitals use GenAI (2024 survey) but nurse postings literally never mention strict-AI tokens (copilot / claude / rag / llm / vector database, etc.). Nursing employers are completely decoupled from AI formalization even as hospitals formally deploy GenAI. This is either an occupation-scope issue (AI is in hospital ops not bedside nursing) or a codification-lag issue (hospitals haven't yet described AI skills as nursing requirements).

    3. ml_engineer adjacent at 63% ai_strict is the ONE case where employer codification approaches worker adoption. It is ALSO the only case where 2024 ai_strict was already >10% (35%). Pre-existing AI-native roles are where employer codification succeeded. For all other occupations, formalization LAGS worker adoption.

    4. Spearman(worker, employer_2026) = 0.92 — employers rank occupations IDENTICALLY to workers' AI adoption patterns. The gap is entirely in LEVEL, not in relative ordering. Employers know which occupations are AI-heavy; they just don't codify it.

    5. Accountant worker any-mid is 0.50 (Thomson Reuters 2024: 44% daily-or-multiple/day among firms using/planning) while employer 2026 ai_strict is 0.69% — a 72× gap. This is the largest RATIO gap in the table and suggests accounting employers are 70× behind their workers in formalizing AI into job requirements. If this holds up on refinement, accounting is potentially a more dramatic divergence case than SWE.

    6. Within-SWE: ml_engineer employer 2026 ai_strict = 47.5% (n=1864) vs other_swe 11.16% (n=23958). A 4.3× gap within SWE subgroups. "Software Engineer" is a much less AI-codified role than "Machine Learning Engineer". This is the inner gradient within SWE and parallels the cross-occupation gradient.

    7. Control engineers show tiny ai_strict (0.09-0.47%) but meaningful ai_broad 2026 (7.5-15.8%). Control engineers are starting to mention "ai" or "ml" at 10-16% rates without naming tools. This is the "aspirational AI" layer that T22 found for SWE (50% of AI mentions are near hedging language). Control occupations may be at the 2024-SWE stage of AI language now.

    8. Solutions Architect jumped from 0.55% → 15.6% ai_strict (28×). That's the largest multiplicative jump among non-thin subgroups. Solutions architecture is re-scripting as an AI-agent architecture role faster than most other adjacent roles.

    What RQ3 should carry forward¶

    RQ3 becomes TWO claims: 1. Universal direction of employer-worker AI-codification divergence across SWE, adjacent, and control occupations. Employers under-codify AI skills relative to worker adoption in all 16 benchmark-available subgroups, both 2024 and 2026. 2. SWE/ml-engineer-concentrated magnitude. The largest absolute gaps (0.4-0.7 pp) are in SWE and SWE-adjacent knowledge-work subgroups, where worker adoption is highest. Control occupations have smaller absolute gaps because worker adoption is lower — but the ratio gap (worker/employer) is similar or worse.

    The T23+T32 package gives RQ3 both a universality story (cross-occupation) AND a magnitude story (SWE-concentrated). The paper lead can pick either framing or carry both.

    Headline claims for SYNTHESIS¶

    1. Direction of employer-worker AI codification gap is OCCUPATION-UNIVERSAL — evidence: tables/T32/universality_test.csv (16/16 subgroups positive in BOTH 2024 and 2026 under any-mid and daily-mid framings) + figures/T32_cross_occupation_divergence.png; sensitivity verdict: robust (direction consistent across 50/65/75/85% worker-band; holds in 2024 AND 2026; holds on daily-use framing except ml_engineer adjacent which is thin n=187). Employers systematically under-codify AI skills relative to worker adoption across all occupation groups, not just SWE.

    2. MAGNITUDE of employer-worker gap is SWE-CONCENTRATED; control gap is small in absolute pp but equally large in ratio — evidence: tables/T32/subgroup_divergence.csv (control gap any-mid 0.15-0.50 pp; SWE-adjacent non-thin gap 0.35-0.58 pp; accountant ratio 72×, SWE 7×); sensitivity verdict: robust (magnitude ordering preserved under 50/65/75/85 bands). Paper should carry BOTH universality (direction) and SWE-concentration (magnitude) claims.

    3. Employer AI-strict rate rank-tracks worker AI adoption at Spearman +0.92 — evidence: tables/T32/universality_test.csv; sensitivity verdict: robust (p=0.002; survives dropping thin cells). Employers know which occupations are AI-heavy and order them correctly; they simply codify at 10-30% of worker-adoption levels across all occupations. This re-characterizes the RQ3 divergence as LEVEL-compression not RANK-mismatch — a sharper framing than "employers are blind to AI" would be.

## Summary

Across 26 metros with 50+ SWE postings in both periods, every single metro shows increases in entry share, AI prevalence, org scope, description length, and tech diversity. The changes are **geographically uniform** -- tech hubs and non-hubs show statistically indistinguishable changes. Metro-level AI adoption is completely uncorrelated with entry share change (r = -0.039, p = 0.850). This is a national, industry-wide shift, not a tech-hub phenomenon. Remote work data (via `is_remote`) shows 22.6% of 2026 scraped SWE postings are remote, but these lack metro assignments and cannot be compared cross-period (2024 = 0% by construction).

## 3. Tech Hub vs Non-Hub Patterns

**Classified as tech hubs:** San Francisco Bay Area, Seattle, Austin, Boston, New York City (5 of 26 metros).

| Metric | Hub change | Non-hub change | Difference | t-stat | p-value |
|--------|-----------|---------------|------------|--------|---------|
| Entry share | +8.8pp | +10.7pp | -1.8pp | -0.88 | 0.386 |
| AI prevalence | +22.2pp | +20.3pp | +1.9pp | 0.59 | 0.558 |
| Org scope | +1.1 terms | +1.4 terms | -0.4 | -1.52 | 0.142 |
| Median desc length | +1,573 | +1,617 | -44 | -0.22 | 0.825 |
| Tech diversity | +1.9 techs | +2.7 techs | -0.8 | -1.78 | 0.089 |

**No significant hub/non-hub differences on any metric** (all p > 0.08). The SWE market transformation is not concentrated in tech hubs. If anything, non-hubs show slightly larger changes in entry share, org scope, and tech diversity (though none are significant).

Tech diversity change approaches significance (p = 0.089) with non-hubs gaining more -- possibly because they started from a lower base and are "catching up" in stack breadth.

## 4. Metro-Level Correlations

| Pair | Pearson r | p | Spearman rho | p |
|------|-----------|---|--------------|---|
| AI surge vs entry change | -0.039 | 0.850 | -0.033 | 0.872 |
| AI surge vs scope inflation | 0.027 | 0.894 | 0.009 | 0.967 |
| AI surge vs tech diversity | 0.285 | 0.158 | 0.307 | 0.128 |
| Entry change vs scope inflation | -0.100 | 0.628 | -0.128 | 0.533 |
| AI surge vs desc length | -0.028 | 0.893 | -0.064 | 0.756 |

**All cross-metric correlations are null at the metro level.** Metros with larger AI surges do NOT show larger entry declines (r = -0.039, p = 0.850). The five metrics are statistically independent across geographies -- each metro follows its own pattern.

This reinforces the T16 finding: AI adoption and entry-level change are orthogonal. The correlation is null at the company level (T16: r = -0.070) AND at the metro level (T17: r = -0.039).

## 5. Remote Work Dimension

**Available data:** `is_remote` from scraped data = 22.6% of 2026 SWE postings (5,438 / 24,095). These remote postings have no metro area assigned (all metro = NULL) because they lack specific location information.

**Cross-period comparison is impossible:** 2024 sources (arshkon, asaniczka) show 0.0% remote rate -- this is a data collection artifact, not a real finding. The `is_remote_inferred` field is 0% everywhere (not functioning for this data).

**Within-metro remote rates (2026):** All 0% because remote postings are not assigned to metros. The 22.6% remote share exists in the non-metro-assigned pool.

**Interpretation:** Remote SWE postings constitute at least 22.6% of the 2026 market but are invisible to metro-level analysis. Our metro-level findings describe the non-remote segment of the market.

## Surprises & Unexpected Patterns

1. **Every metro goes the same direction.** All 26 metros show increases in entry share, AI prevalence, org scope, description length, and tech diversity. There is not a single counter-trend metro. This is remarkable uniformity for a 26-metro panel.

2. **Null metro-level correlation between AI and entry change.** Even in the aggregate cross-section where you might expect ecological correlation, AI adoption and entry-share change are completely orthogonal (r = -0.039). This is consistent with the T16 company-level null result.

3. **NYC stands out as the least-changed metro** for entry share (+2.8pp), despite being the largest SWE market. It had the highest 2024 entry share (6.9%) and shows a ceiling effect.

4. **Florida metros lead AI adoption.** Miami (+34.1pp) and Tampa Bay (+32.3pp) have the highest AI surge, suggesting these growing tech markets adopted AI requirements most aggressively -- possibly because they are newer SWE markets less anchored to pre-AI patterns.

5. **Non-hub metros change as much as hubs.** The hypothesis that "tech hub dynamics would drive national patterns" is refuted. If anything, second-tier metros show larger scope and tech-diversity increases (non-significant). The transformation is truly national.

6. **Remote SWE is a large blind spot.** 22.6% of 2026 SWE postings are remote-tagged but invisible to metro analysis. This is a meaningful limitation: if remote postings have systematically different characteristics (which is plausible), our metro-level findings describe only the 77% of non-remote postings.

## Methodology Notes

- **Metro assignment:** Uses `metro_area` field (Stage 6, 26-metro study frame). 73.9% of SWE postings have metro assignments.
- **Minimum threshold:** 50 SWE per metro per era (all 26 study metros qualify).
- **AI prevalence:** Binary indicator for any of 17 AI technology keywords (from shared tech matrix).
- **Org scope:** Regex count of management/leadership/scope terms in `description_core`.
- **Tech diversity:** Mean count of technologies detected per posting (146-tech regex dictionary).
- **Remote work:** `is_remote` from source data. Cross-period comparison invalid (2024 = 0% by construction). `is_remote_inferred` is not functioning.

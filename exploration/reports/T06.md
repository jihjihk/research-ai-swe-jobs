# T06: Company Concentration

**Task:** Check if a few employers dominate SWE postings and could bias findings.  
**Sample:** SWE postings, LinkedIn-only, English, date_flag='ok'  
**Sources:** kaggle_arshkon (5,019 rows), kaggle_asaniczka (23,213 rows), scraped (24,095 rows)

---

## Summary of Findings

Concentration levels are moderate across all datasets, but **asaniczka is a clear outlier** with the highest HHI (0.014) and lowest effective number of firms (72). A single company ("Recruiting from Scratch") holds 7.3% of asaniczka SWE postings. For arshkon and scraped, concentration is lower and more comparable, with top-1 shares around 3.3-3.8%.

Junior share is **robust to company capping**: applying caps of 5-50 postings per company changes the junior share by less than 2 percentage points in any dataset. This means the junior-share estimates are not driven by a few prolific employers.

Aggregators show a distinctive pattern: they over-represent junior postings (27-43% junior vs 12-18% for direct employers) and under-represent mid-senior. Aggregator fractions vary by source (12-27%), making aggregator filtering important for comparisons.

## 1. Concentration Metrics by Dataset

| Metric | arshkon | asaniczka | scraped |
|---|---|---|---|
| Total SWE postings | 4,974 | 23,213 | 24,095 |
| Unique companies | 2,298 | 4,946 | 5,100 |
| HHI | 0.0029 | **0.0140** | 0.0042 |
| Effective N firms (1/HHI) | 342 | **72** | 239 |
| Top-1 share (%) | 3.3 | **7.3** | 3.8 |
| Top-5 share (%) | 8.3 | **21.9** | 10.3 |
| Top-10 share (%) | 11.2 | **26.3** | 14.6 |
| Top-20 share (%) | 15.8 | **31.4** | 21.4 |
| Gini coefficient | 0.466 | **0.710** | 0.689 |
| Top-1 company | DataAnnotation | Recruiting from Scratch | Jobs via Dice |
| Top-1 count | 163 | 1,699 | 903 |

### Interpretation

All three datasets are unconcentrated by standard HHI thresholds (HHI < 0.15), but the degree varies:

- **Arshkon** is the most dispersed: low HHI (0.003), moderate Gini (0.47), and 342 effective firms. The posting distribution is relatively even.
- **Asaniczka** is the most concentrated: HHI 5x arshkon, with "Recruiting from Scratch" alone at 7.3%. The effective number of firms (72) means the dataset behaves as if only 72 companies are present. This concentration is driven by aggregators.
- **Scraped** falls between the two: moderate HHI (0.004), but high Gini (0.69) reflecting a long tail of small employers. "Jobs via Dice" (an aggregator) leads with 3.8%.

The top-1 company in every dataset is either an aggregator or a data-annotation platform — not a traditional tech employer. This is important: **the most prolific posters are intermediaries, not the actual employers**.

## 3. Within-Company Seniority Comparison (Overlap Set)

### Aggregate Overlap-Company Seniority

The 813 companies present in both arshkon and scraped provide a composition-controlled comparison:

| Level | arshkon (%) | scraped (%) | Delta (pp) |
|---|---|---|---|
| entry | 23.0 | 12.5 | **-10.5** |
| associate | 4.3 | 2.9 | -1.3 |
| mid-senior | 72.2 | 83.6 | +11.4 |
| director | 0.6 | 1.0 | +0.4 |

The junior share decline within overlap companies (-10.5pp) is **larger** than the aggregate decline (-5.9pp from T05), suggesting that composition effects actually **dampen** the apparent trend. When holding the company set constant, the entry-level share decline is more pronounced.

**Caution:** Small arshkon counts (many companies have N < 20 in arshkon) make these per-company comparisons noisy. TikTok and DataAnnotation shifts are striking but likely reflect seniority-labeling changes or batch posting patterns, not real workforce restructuring.

**DataAnnotation detail:** All 38 scraped DataAnnotation postings are entry-level (via native label), while arshkon had 33 mid-senior and 130 unknown. This is either a real shift (DataAnnotation now posts entry-level data-annotation work as SWE) or a labeling change. Given DataAnnotation's business model (crowdsource AI training data), these may be misclassified as SWE.

## 4. Company-Capped Sensitivity

### Junior Share Under Different Caps

| Source | Cap=5 | Cap=10 | Cap=20 | Cap=50 | Uncapped |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| arshkon | 18.5% | 19.1% | 20.2% | 20.6% | **20.4%** |
| asaniczka | 0.7% | 0.7% | 0.7% | 0.7% | **0.6%** |
| scraped | 13.7% | 13.8% | 13.9% | 14.2% | **14.5%** |

### Interpretation

**Junior share is remarkably stable under capping.** The maximum shift from uncapped to cap=5 is:
- arshkon: -1.9pp (20.4% -> 18.5%)
- asaniczka: +0.1pp (essentially unchanged)
- scraped: -0.8pp (14.5% -> 13.7%)

This means no single company or small set of companies is driving the junior-share estimates. The finding holds regardless of how aggressively you cap company contributions.

The slight downward shift at aggressive caps suggests that high-volume posters (particularly aggregators) have marginally higher junior shares, which is confirmed in the aggregator analysis below.

## 5. Aggregator Analysis

### Aggregator Fraction by Source

| Source | Total | Aggregator N | Aggregator % |
|---|---|---|---|
| arshkon | 5,019 | 591 | 11.8% |
| asaniczka | 23,213 | 6,340 | **27.3%** |
| scraped | 24,095 | 3,517 | 14.6% |

### Do Aggregators Differ Systematically?

**Description length:** Aggregator postings are shorter in arshkon (-29%) and scraped (-23%) but slightly longer in asaniczka (+13%). This inconsistency suggests different aggregator types across sources.

**YOE:** Aggregator YOE is similar to direct-employer YOE (within 0.2-0.4 years across sources). No systematic difference.

**Seniority:**

| Source | Aggregator Junior % | Direct Junior % | Delta |
|---|---|---|---|
| arshkon | **42.8** | 17.6 | +25.2pp |
| asaniczka | 0.2 | 0.7 | -0.5pp |
| scraped | **26.7** | 12.3 | +14.5pp |

Aggregators dramatically over-represent junior postings in arshkon and scraped. In arshkon, 42.8% of aggregator SWE postings are junior vs 17.6% for direct employers — a 25pp gap. In scraped, the gap is 14.5pp (26.7% vs 12.3%).

**Implication:** Excluding aggregators would reduce the junior share by 2-3pp in arshkon and scraped. However, aggregator postings represent real labor demand channeled through staffing agencies, so excluding them may not be appropriate. Sensitivity analyses should report both.

### Top Aggregators by Source

| Source | Top Aggregator | N | Notable |
|---|---|---|---|
| arshkon | DataAnnotation (163), Dice (62), Apex Systems (52) | | DataAnnotation dominates |
| asaniczka | Recruiting from Scratch (2,084), Jobs for Humanity (1,839) | | Two aggregators = 16.9% of all SWE |
| scraped | Jobs via Dice (1,966), Lensa (337), Actalent (128) | | Dice dominant under different branding |

Note that "Dice" appears in arshkon (62 postings) but "Jobs via Dice" in scraped (1,966 postings). This likely represents rebranding, but could also indicate Dice increased its LinkedIn posting volume. The company canonicalization pipeline does not merge these, which may slightly understate aggregator concentration.

## Surprises & Unexpected Patterns

1. **Junior share is robust to capping.** The maximum sensitivity is less than 2pp regardless of cap level. This is good news for the research — the junior-share metric is not a concentration artifact.

2. **Aggregators have dramatically higher junior shares.** The 25pp aggregator-vs-direct gap in arshkon (43% vs 18%) means that the true "direct employer" junior share was only 17.6% in 2024, not 20.4%. If aggregators are excluded from both periods, the junior decline is 17.6% -> 12.3% = -5.3pp, similar to the aggregate -5.9pp.

3. **The overlap-company junior decline (-10.5pp) exceeds the aggregate decline (-5.9pp).** This means composition effects are masking some of the change, not amplifying it. New companies entering in 2026 actually have more junior postings than returning companies, dampening the apparent decline.

4. **84% of 2026 companies are "new."** This is partly artifact (arshkon is a small sample of one month in 2024) and partly real (labor market churn). It means that any aggregate comparison is heavily influenced by which companies happen to appear in each sample. The 813-company overlap set is essential.

5. **AI companies are new entrants.** Anthropic (79), OpenAI (50), CoreWeave (72), and Alignerr (103) all appear exclusively in 2026 data. Their SWE posting patterns could be systematically different from incumbent tech companies.

6. **TikTok's entry-level surge.** TikTok/ByteDance has 93+64=157 entry-level SWE postings in 2026, making it one of the largest entry-level employers in the scraped data. This is likely driven by TikTok USDS (US Data Security) and ByteDance's aggressive US hiring.

7. **Asaniczka concentration is extreme.** With an effective N of only 72 firms (vs 342 for arshkon), asaniczka behaves less like a population sample and more like a curated collection of high-volume posters. Combined with its seniority labeling issues (T05), asaniczka should be treated as a supplementary source, not a primary comparison dataset.

---

## Recommendations for Downstream Analysis

1. **Junior-share estimates are trustworthy** at the aggregate level (robust to capping). Report capped sensitivity as a robustness check.

2. **Use the 813-company overlap set** for the primary temporal comparison. The overlap-company junior decline (-10.5pp) is the most credible estimate, free of composition effects.

3. **Report aggregator-inclusive and aggregator-exclusive** results separately. The aggregator premium on junior share (14-25pp) means aggregator filtering changes the level but not the direction of findings.

4. **Flag DataAnnotation and TikTok/ByteDance** as special cases in the overlap-company analysis. Both show extreme seniority shifts that are likely driven by company-specific factors, not labor market trends.

5. **Be cautious with asaniczka concentration.** Recruiting from Scratch (7.3%) and Jobs for Humanity (7.7% of SWE via aggregator channel) together account for ~15% of all asaniczka SWE postings. This concentration, combined with the seniority labeling issue, makes asaniczka unreliable for most comparisons.

6. **New entrants do not bias the junior-share downward.** The slightly higher junior share among new companies (15.9% vs 12.5%) means the aggregate decline is conservative, not inflated.

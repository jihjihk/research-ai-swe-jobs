# T02: Missing Data & Native-Label Comparability

## Finding

Asaniczka `associate` is **NOT usable as a junior proxy**. It behaves like a lower-mid-level population, not an entry-level one. On every signal tested -- YOE distribution, title composition, seniority_final resolution, senior title-cue rate, and description length -- asaniczka associate aligns with arshkon associate or sits between arshkon associate and mid-senior, never close to arshkon entry. The entry-level historical baseline for cross-period comparison is limited to **830 arshkon SWE entry-level rows** (after default filters). This is the binding constraint for RQ1.

Cross-period analyses face three major missingness barriers: (1) company_industry is 0% for asaniczka, (2) skills_raw is 0% for scraped and 1% for arshkon, and (3) LLM columns are unpopulated for classification and unpopulated for scraped extraction.

## Surprises & unexpected patterns

1. **Asaniczka associate has HIGHER median YOE (4.0) than arshkon entry (3.0) AND arshkon associate (3.0).** It's not even close to entry-level. The YOE distribution (p25=3.0, p75=5.0) matches a solidly mid-level population.

2. **Scraped entry has dramatically LOWER YOE than 2024 entry (median 2.0 vs 3.0).** This is the opposite of what the "scope inflation" hypothesis predicts. Scraped entry YOE (mean 2.29, median 2.0, p75=2.0) is genuinely lower than arshkon entry (mean 4.12, median 3.0, p75=5.0). This is a major finding that needs dedicated investigation.

3. **Title overlap is universally low.** Jaccard similarity between any two source-seniority groups is 0.04-0.07. Even arshkon_entry vs scraped_entry is only 0.054. The labor market has high title diversity within every seniority level.

4. **Asaniczka associate has 1.0% senior title cues** (vs 0.1% in arshkon entry), further confirming it's not entry-level.

5. **72% of asaniczka associate rows resolve to `associate` in seniority_final, and 26% to `mid-senior`.** Only 2.1% resolve to `entry`. This is structurally very different from arshkon entry (98.4% resolve to entry in seniority_final).

6. **Company overlap is modest.** Arshkon-to-scraped overlap is 35.4% of arshkon companies (813 of 2,299), only 15.9% of scraped companies (813 of 5,100). Asaniczka-to-scraped is 23.6% / 22.9%. Composition effects could be substantial.

## Implication for research

**For RQ1 (junior scope inflation):**
- The entry-level comparison is arshkon entry (n=830) vs scraped entry (n=3,255). This is adequate sample size but single-source 2024 baseline.
- Asaniczka CANNOT contribute entry-level signal. Its "associate" behaves as lower-mid.
- The YOE paradox (lower in 2026 than 2024 for entry) contradicts scope inflation and needs dedicated investigation in T05/T06.

**For RQ2 (requirement migration):**
- Cross-period seniority-stratified text comparisons use seniority_final. Coverage is good: arshkon 81% known, asaniczka 100%, scraped 93%.
- But asaniczka's seniority distribution is heavily skewed (93% mid-senior, 6% associate, 0.6% entry). Any arshkon-asaniczka pooling for "2024 baseline" will be dominated by asaniczka mid-senior.
- Better approach: use arshkon alone for 2024 seniority-stratified comparisons, use asaniczka only for pooled/unsorted analyses.

**For RQ3 (employer-worker divergence):**
- Description text is the primary analysis column and has excellent coverage.
- Skills_raw is useless cross-period. Requirements must be extracted from text.

**New questions:**
- The YOE paradox is the most important finding here. Is it real, or is it an artifact of different YOE-mention conventions, description length changes, or parser behavior?
- With 35% company overlap, matched-employer panel analysis is feasible (813 companies appear in both periods) but covers a minority of the market.

## Methodology

### Missingness analysis
Computed non-null rates for 36 analysis-critical columns across 4 source-platform groups, both for all rows and SWE subset. Used DuckDB on stage8_final.parquet (1,395,790 rows) with default filters (LinkedIn, English, date_flag=ok).

### Comparability audit
Compared 8 source-seniority groups on 5 dimensions:
1. **Title overlap:** Jaccard similarity of title_normalized sets
2. **Junior/senior title cues:** Regex search for explicit junior indicators (junior, jr, entry, intern, graduate, trainee) and senior indicators (senior, sr, lead, principal, staff, architect)
3. **YOE distribution:** Mean, median, p25, p75 of yoe_extracted
4. **Seniority_final resolution:** How native labels map through the rule-based pipeline
5. **Description length:** Mean and median of description_length and core_length

### Decision criteria
For "usable as junior proxy," required directional alignment with arshkon entry on multiple signals: similar YOE distribution, similar or lower senior-cue rate, and seniority_final resolving predominantly to entry. Asaniczka associate fails all three.

## Data quality note

- **arshkon entry n=830 after default filters** (n=769 with seniority_native=entry; the difference is rule-based entry imputation for rows without native labels).
- **asaniczka entry exists but is rule-imputed** (n=130 SWE, all from title/description inference, not native labels). These are not native entry labels.
- **YOE extraction rate varies:** arshkon entry 62% (476/769), asaniczka associate 65% (1,311/2,014), scraped entry 38% (1,073/2,789). The lower extraction rate in scraped could itself be a signal (shorter/different YOE mention patterns).
- **"not applicable" seniority** in scraped: 20,962 LinkedIn rows (4,200 SWE) have `seniority_raw = 'not applicable'`, mapping to `seniority_native = NULL`. These rely entirely on rule-based imputation.
- **Company overlap calculation** uses `company_name_canonical` which normalizes spelling variants but may miss brand-name changes or subsidiary relationships.

## Final verdict on asaniczka associate

**NOT usable as a junior proxy.** Asaniczka associate is a lower-mid-level population. On all five signals:

| Signal | Closest match | Distance from entry |
|---|---|---|
| YOE median | arshkon associate (3.0) < asaniczka assoc (4.0) < mid-senior (5.0) | Far from entry |
| seniority_final resolution | Matches arshkon associate (72% associate, 26% mid-senior) | 2.1% entry |
| Title cues | Matches arshkon associate (low junior, low senior) | Not entry-like |
| Title Jaccard | Slightly higher with mid-senior than entry | Indeterminate |
| Description length | Longer than arshkon associate, consistent with asaniczka source | N/A |

**Decision rule result:** Fails on all directional criteria. Cannot be used as junior proxy even with caveats.

## Action items

1. **Entry-level comparisons use arshkon (830) vs scraped (3,255) only.** Do not pool asaniczka for entry-level analyses.
2. **Investigate the YOE paradox.** Scraped entry median YOE (2.0) < arshkon entry (3.0). This could be the paper's most important finding or an artifact. Needs: (a) check whether YOE extraction rate differences drive it, (b) compare raw description YOE mentions, (c) check if arshkon's higher YOE reflects scope inflation in 2024 that has since reversed.
3. **Length-normalize all text analyses.** The 56% description length growth from 2024 to 2026 is confirmed by these numbers (arshkon entry desc_median=3,776 vs scraped entry=4,128).
4. **Company panel analysis is feasible but partial.** 813 companies overlap between arshkon and scraped. This supports a matched-employer difference-in-differences design but covers only ~16-35% of each source.
5. **Remote work analysis is impossible cross-period.** The 2024 flag is uniformly false. Only within-2026 remote analysis is valid.
6. **Run LLM processing budgets** to populate classification columns and scraped extraction.
7. **For asaniczka, use it for:** (a) pooled mid-senior analysis (21.6K rows), (b) large-sample text pattern discovery, (c) company-level analysis within 2024. **Do not use for:** entry-level anything, industry-stratified comparisons, or as representative of junior roles.

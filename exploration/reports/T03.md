The low kappa (0.440) is entirely driven by the non-mid-senior classes.

Arshkon entry-level postings may have less explicit title signals. Associate and director accuracy is consistently poor across both periods.

## 4. Entry-Level Label Provenance

Where do `seniority_final = 'entry'` labels come from?

| Source | native_backfill | title_keyword | description_explicit |
|---|---|---|---|
| kaggle_arshkon (830) | 729 (87.8%) | 100 (12.0%) | 1 (0.1%) |
| kaggle_asaniczka (130) | 0 (0%) | 130 (100%) | 0 |
| scraped (3,255) | 2,592 (79.6%) | 661 (20.3%) | 2 (0.1%) |

**Critical:** Asaniczka's 130 entry-level labels come entirely from title_keyword imputation -- the native labels for asaniczka only contain mid-senior and associate. These 130 are likely titles like "Junior Software Engineer" or "Entry Level Developer" that were keyword-matched. Given asaniczka's 23K SWE rows, this represents 0.6% entry-level share, which is essentially a data artifact of zero native entry labels, not a real measurement.

### Entry-level leakage

76 rows with `seniority_native IN ('entry', 'intern')` are overridden to `seniority_final = 'mid-senior'` due to title_keyword rules. These are cases where the title contains mid-senior keywords (e.g., "Senior") despite the platform labeling the posting as entry/intern level. This is a small but systematic issue.

## 5. RQ1 Junior Share Comparison

Four operationalizations, comparing arshkon (2024-04) and scraped (2026-03) only. Asaniczka excluded because its zero native entry labels make any junior share estimate unreliable.

| Variant | Arshkon Junior % (n) | Scraped Junior % (n) | Direction |
|---|---|---|---|
| A: seniority_final (non-unknown) | 20.4% (4,066) | 14.5% (22,469) | DOWN 5.9pp |
| B: seniority_native only | 22.9% (3,444) | 16.2% (19,896) | DOWN 6.7pp |
| C: High-confidence (title_keyword + native_backfill) | 21.4% (3,881) | 14.9% (21,831) | DOWN 6.5pp |
| D: All signals (excl. unknown) | 20.4% (4,066) | 14.5% (22,469) | DOWN 5.9pp |

### Verdict: All variants agree on direction AND approximate magnitude

All four variants show junior share declining by 5.9-6.7 percentage points from arshkon to scraped. The native-only variant (B) gives the highest junior share for both periods and the largest decline. The direction is robust: **junior share decreased from ~20-23% to ~14-16% regardless of which seniority column is used.**

However, several critical caveats apply:

1. **Composition effects:** Arshkon and scraped have only 18-25% company overlap. The decline could reflect different employer mix rather than actual restructuring.
2. **Asaniczka cannot contribute to this comparison.** Its 0.6% entry-level share is an artifact of having zero native entry labels.
3. **The absolute level of junior share depends heavily on the operationalization** (20.4% vs 22.9% for arshkon), but the *change* is robust.

## 6. Which Operationalization Is Most Defensible?

### For trend estimation (RQ1 junior share over time)

**Recommended: Variant B (seniority_native only)** for the primary estimate, with Variant A as a robustness check.

Rationale:
- Native labels are platform-assigned and consistent across periods
- Rule-based imputation has unknown temporal stability and different coverage rates (31.6% entry accuracy in arshkon vs 60.6% in scraped)
- Using only native labels avoids the risk that imputation artifacts create a spurious trend

**Limitation:** Native coverage is only 68.6% for arshkon and 82.6% for scraped, so this discards ~20-30% of SWE rows. The sample that has native labels may not be representative.

### For cross-sectional comparison (within-period analysis)

**Recommended: Variant A (seniority_final)** because it maximizes sample size and has the best coverage (81-100%). Within a single period, the imputation rules are consistently applied.

### For classification training and validation

**Recommended: seniority_native as ground truth, with separate accuracy reporting by source.** The per-class accuracy results (Section 3) show the rule-based classifier is unreliable for non-mid-senior classes. Any classifier trained or validated against `seniority_final` inherits these biases.

### For robustness/sensitivity analyses

**Report all four variants.** The agreement between variants strengthens the finding; disagreement flags fragile results. Given that all four agree on the direction and magnitude of junior share change, the RQ1 finding is robust to operationalization choice.

## 7. Surprises and Unexpected Patterns

1. **Associate is the weakest class everywhere.** Only 42.6% imputed-vs-final agreement, 9.4% imputed accuracy against native in arshkon. The associate category is essentially unusable as a standalone label without native data. This means the 3-level mapping (associate -> mid) is not just convenient -- it is necessary for reliability.

2. **Director labels are poorly resolved.** 65% of native directors become mid-senior in `seniority_final`, and the imputer has only 19% accuracy for directors. This means director-level analysis should rely exclusively on native labels where available.

3. **Scraped entry-level classifier accuracy is nearly double arshkon's.** This likely reflects real differences in how entry-level roles are titled across periods (more explicit "Junior"/"Entry" in 2026 titles), but could also be a confound if this means the classifier is picking up different populations.

4. **76 native entry/intern rows are overridden to mid-senior by title keywords.** These are posting-level contradictions (platform says entry, title says senior). Worth investigating whether these are genuine mismatches or platform labeling errors.

5. **All junior share variants agree on a decline -- but the magnitude is moderate (5.9-6.7pp), not dramatic.** This contradicts any narrative of "juniors disappearing" and instead suggests a moderate contraction. The base rate matters: arshkon starts at 20-23% junior, not 50%.

# T05: Cross-Dataset Comparability

**Task:** Test whether dataset differences reflect real labor market changes vs artifacts.  
**Sample:** SWE postings, LinkedIn-only, English, date_flag='ok'  
**Sources:** kaggle_arshkon (5,019 rows, 2024-04), kaggle_asaniczka (23,213 rows, 2024-01), scraped (24,095 rows, 2026-03)

---

## Summary of Findings

The three datasets differ substantially on every measured dimension. However, the **within-2024 calibration** (arshkon vs asaniczka) reveals that much of this variation is cross-source noise rather than temporal change. The two 2024 sources, despite covering the same labor market just three months apart, show statistically significant differences in every metric. This establishes a high baseline for cross-source variability that any 2024-vs-2026 comparison must exceed to be credible.

**Key result:** The seniority comparison between arshkon and asaniczka (Cramer's V=0.3841) is *more* different than arshkon vs scraped (V=0.0873). This means the asaniczka seniority distribution is an outlier driven by data construction, not a labor market signal. Only the arshkon-vs-scraped comparison produces a plausible measure of temporal change in seniority, and even that requires careful handling.

## 1. Description Length

### Results

| Source | N | Desc. Mean | Desc. Median | Core Mean | Core Median |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| arshkon | 5,019 | 3,316 | 2,954 | 2,823 | 2,504 |
| asaniczka | 23,213 | 4,052 | 3,825 | 2,734 | 2,419 |
| scraped | 24,095 | 5,203 | 5,158 | 4,363 | 4,171 |

### KS Tests (all p < 1e-9)

| Comparison | Metric | KS Stat | Median Ratio |
|---|---|---|---|
| arshkon vs asaniczka | description_length | 0.1841 | 1.30 |
| arshkon vs asaniczka | core_length | 0.0500 | 0.97 |
| arshkon vs scraped | description_length | 0.3348 | 1.75 |
| arshkon vs scraped | core_length | 0.3340 | 1.67 |
| asaniczka vs scraped | description_length | 0.2082 | 1.35 |
| asaniczka vs scraped | core_length | 0.3777 | 1.72 |

### Interpretation

**Full description length** varies substantially even within 2024: arshkon median 2,954 vs asaniczka median 3,825 (ratio 1.30). The 2026 scraped median (5,158) is 1.75x arshkon and 1.35x asaniczka. This is a massive shift that could be real (postings got longer) or artifact (scraper captures more content).

**Core length** tells a different story. After boilerplate removal, arshkon and asaniczka are nearly identical (2,504 vs 2,419, KS=0.050). But scraped core length (4,171) is 1.67-1.72x the 2024 sources. This suggests:

1. The within-2024 description length difference (arshkon vs asaniczka) is largely boilerplate — the core content is similar.
2. The 2024-to-2026 growth in core length is genuine and large, not just boilerplate.

**Core-to-description ratio** confirms this: arshkon retains 98.7% of text after boilerplate removal, asaniczka 85.2%, scraped 86.0%. Arshkon descriptions are cleaner/shorter to begin with; asaniczka has more boilerplate that gets stripped.

### Artifact assessment

The description length difference is a **mix of real and artifact**. The core_length convergence within 2024 (KS=0.050, near-equal medians) vs divergence across periods (KS=0.33-0.38) suggests real content growth of ~67-72% from 2024 to 2026. However, scraper format differences (HTML parsing, section capture) could also contribute. **All text-based analyses must normalize by length.**

### Top-50 Company Overlap

| Comparison | Top-50 Overlap |
|---|---|
| arshkon vs asaniczka | 12/50 |
| arshkon vs scraped | 17/50 |
| asaniczka vs scraped | 13/50 |

### Interpretation

Company overlap is low across all pairs: Jaccard 0.11-0.13. Even the top-50 largest SWE employers overlap only 12-17 companies out of 50. Notably, arshkon-vs-scraped overlap (35.4% of arshkon companies appear in scraped) is slightly higher than arshkon-vs-asaniczka (32.2%), despite spanning a longer time gap. This suggests the overlap differences are primarily about **sample frame** (which companies happen to be posting during the scrape window), not temporal change.

**Implication for analysis:** Company composition effects are large. Any cross-period comparison of aggregate statistics could be driven by different companies being present, not by the same companies changing their postings. The overlap set (813 companies in both arshkon and scraped) is the most trustworthy comparison sample.

### Chi-Squared Tests

### Interpretation

All geographic distributions are statistically different (all p < 1e-77). The within-2024 comparison already shows V=0.139, meaning the two 2024 sources sample geography differently. Cross-period V values (0.261, 0.340) are larger.

The scraped data over-represents CA (22.3% vs 15-16%), WA (7.6% vs 3-5%), and NC (5.0% vs 2.7-3.8%), and under-represents FL, OH, and VA. This likely reflects the scraper's metro-targeted search design, which emphasizes tech hubs.

**Trustworthiness:** Low. Geographic differences are strongly influenced by the scraper's search metro list. Geographic analyses should use metro-matched subsamples only.

## 4. Seniority Distribution

### 5-Level Distribution (excluding unknown, %)

| Level | arshkon | asaniczka | scraped |
|---|---|---|---|
| entry | 20.4 | 0.6 | 14.5 |
| associate | 5.6 | 6.3 | 2.8 |
| mid-senior | 73.4 | 92.8 | 81.8 |
| director | 0.5 | 0.4 | 1.0 |

### Unknown Rates

| Source | Total | Unknown | Unknown % |
|---|---|---|---|
| arshkon | 5,019 | 953 | 19.0% |
| asaniczka | 23,213 | 0 | 0.0% |
| scraped | 24,095 | 1,626 | 6.7% |

### Chi-Squared Tests

| Comparison | Chi-squared | Cramer's V |
|---|---|---|
| arshkon vs asaniczka | 4,023.6 | **0.384** |
| arshkon vs scraped | 202.3 | **0.087** |
| asaniczka vs scraped | 3,511.6 | **0.277** |

### Interpretation

**This is the most critical finding in T05.**

The within-2024 seniority difference (arshkon vs asaniczka: V=0.384) is *more than four times larger* than the cross-period difference (arshkon vs scraped: V=0.087). The asaniczka seniority distribution is an extreme outlier: 92.8% mid-senior, only 0.6% entry-level. This is not a labor market signal — it is a data construction artifact.

**Root cause:** Asaniczka has zero native entry-level labels. Its `seniority_native` values are either `mid-senior` or `associate`. The 130 asaniczka "entry" labels all come from rule-based title-keyword imputation overriding the native label (e.g., "software engineer intern" tagged as entry despite native=mid-senior). This makes asaniczka categorically unusable for seniority-stratified temporal comparisons.

**The arshkon-vs-scraped comparison is the only valid temporal comparison for seniority.** Entry share: arshkon 20.4% vs scraped 14.5% (delta -5.9pp). Cramer's V=0.087 (small but real). However, this comparison still faces confounds:
- Different companies (only 813 overlap)
- Different geographic mix
- Different classification provenance (arshkon: 49% native_backfill, 46% title_keyword; scraped: 49% native_backfill, 49% title_keyword)

### 2024 vs 2026 Title Vocabulary

- Titles unique to 2026: 9,090
- Titles unique to 2024: 8,465
- Shared across both periods: 804

### Interpretation

Title vocabulary overlap is extremely low (Jaccard 0.036-0.048). This is largely driven by the long tail of unique title strings — minor variations in punctuation, company-specific prefixes, and role specializations create thousands of unique entries. The shared-title count (804 across periods) is small, but these are likely the high-volume canonical titles that carry most of the postings.

The top unique-to-2026 titles include AI-specific roles ("AI / ML Engineer", "Data & AI Engineer", "Machine Learning Engineer - Energy") and company-specific formats, but also many formatting artifacts (leading periods, location suffixes). Title vocabulary comparison is **low signal** without heavy normalization.

### Interpretation

Major industry composition shifts: "IT Services and IT Consulting" dropped from 32.3% to 15.5%, while "Software Development" grew from 21.0% to 35.8%. This could be real (SWE hiring shifting toward pure software companies) or a LinkedIn industry-label reclassification (LinkedIn has restructured its industry taxonomy between 2024 and 2026, creating categories like "Information Services and Technology, Information and Internet" that didn't exist before).

The presence of composite industry labels exclusively in scraped data (e.g., "Accounting, IT Services and IT Consulting, and Business Consulting and Services" — 340 postings) confirms that LinkedIn changed its industry taxonomy. **Industry comparisons between arshkon and scraped are confounded by taxonomy change.**

## 8. Within-2024 Calibration Table

This table establishes baseline cross-source variability using the two 2024 datasets (arshkon and asaniczka), which should in principle measure the same labor market.

| Metric | Within-2024 (arshkon vs asaniczka) | Arshkon vs Scraped | Asaniczka vs Scraped | Amplification Factor |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Description length (KS) | 0.184 | 0.335 | 0.208 | 1.82x |
| Core length (KS) | 0.050 | 0.334 | 0.378 | **7.55x** |
| Seniority (Cramer's V) | **0.384** | 0.087 | 0.277 | **0.23x** (inverted!) |
| Geography (Cramer's V) | 0.139 | 0.261 | 0.340 | 2.45x |
| Company Jaccard | 0.114 | 0.124 | 0.131 | 1.09x |
| Title Jaccard | 0.048 | 0.036 | 0.042 | 0.88x |

**Key findings from calibration:**

1. **Core length is the cleanest signal.** Within-2024 KS is tiny (0.050) but cross-period KS is large (0.33-0.38), giving 7.55x amplification. This is strong evidence of genuine content growth.

2. **Seniority calibration is inverted.** The within-2024 difference is LARGER than the cross-period difference. This means asaniczka's seniority distribution is a pure artifact. Only arshkon-vs-scraped seniority comparisons are meaningful.

3. **Geography and company overlap show modest amplification** (1.09-2.45x), meaning these differences are partially real, partially artifact.

4. **Title vocabulary shows no amplification** (0.88x), meaning title diversity is a cross-source constant, not a temporal signal.

## Surprises & Unexpected Patterns

1. **Asaniczka is worse than expected for seniority analysis.** The schema documents that asaniczka has "no entry-level labels," but the implication is deeper: its seniority distribution is so dominated by mid-senior (92.8%) that it makes the entire dataset unusable for any seniority-stratified comparison involving 2024-01. The 23K asaniczka SWE rows are effectively dead weight for RQ1's junior-share analysis.

2. **The scraped dataset is much larger than documented.** The task context stated ~4.5K scraped LinkedIn SWE rows, but the actual count is 24,095. The scraper appears to have accumulated 3+ weeks of data, making it 4.8x the expected size. This is a significant strength for statistical power.

3. **Core length convergence within 2024 is remarkable.** Despite arshkon and asaniczka having very different raw description lengths (medians 2,954 vs 3,825), their core lengths are nearly identical (2,504 vs 2,419). This validates the boilerplate removal pipeline and suggests that core_length is a much better normalization variable than description_length.

4. **Company overlap is symmetric across time.** Arshkon-vs-scraped company overlap (35.4%) is actually higher than arshkon-vs-asaniczka (32.2%), even though the time gap is larger. This suggests that the overlap difference is driven by sample frame size, not market churn.

5. **Industry taxonomy changed on LinkedIn.** Composite industry labels (e.g., "Accounting, Financial Services, and Business Consulting and Services") appear exclusively in scraped data, indicating LinkedIn restructured its taxonomy between 2024 and 2026. This is a silent confound for any industry analysis.

6. **Aggregator composition differs substantially.** Arshkon: 11.8% aggregator, asaniczka: 27.3%, scraped: 14.6%. Asaniczka's high aggregator rate (driven by "Recruiting from Scratch" with 2,084 SWE postings = 7.3% of all asaniczka SWE) further degrades its reliability as a comparison dataset.

---

## Recommendations for Downstream Analysis

1. **Drop asaniczka from seniority-stratified analyses.** Use only arshkon (2024-04) vs scraped (2026-03) for RQ1 junior-share comparisons.

2. **Use core_length for all text-length normalization**, not description_length. Core_length shows near-zero within-2024 variation.

3. **Prefer overlap-company analyses** for temporal comparisons. The 813 companies appearing in both arshkon and scraped provide the cleanest comparison by controlling for composition effects.

4. **Do not compare industry distributions** between 2024 and 2026 without a taxonomy crosswalk.

5. **Control for geographic composition** using metro-matched subsamples or inverse-probability weighting.

6. **Report arshkon-vs-scraped results alongside within-2024 calibration** so readers can assess signal-to-noise.

    T17 — Geographic Market Structure¶

    Agent J, Wave 3. Date: 2026-04-17. Inputs: data/unified.parquet, exploration/artifacts/shared/swe_cleaned_text.parquet, swe_tech_matrix.parquet, swe_archetype_labels.parquet, entry_specialist_employers.csv. Default filters: source_platform='linkedin' AND is_english=true AND date_flag='ok' AND is_swe=true. Geographic unit: metro_area (preprocessing metro-normalized field; is_multi_location rows excluded by construction — not in metro rollups).

    Headline findings¶

    1. 26 metros are well-powered under pooled-2024 baseline (≥50 SWE/period); 19 are well-powered under arshkon-only. Matches T07's feasibility forecast (23/26 metros well-powered for J1 under pooled-2024). Report leads with pooled-2024 for metro-level J1/J2 analyses; arshkon-only reported as sensitivity.

    2. AI-mention (STRICT) surged in EVERY one of the 26 well-powered metros — there is no metro in this panel with an AI decline. Mean Δ_ai_strict +12.8pp, std 3.7pp, CV 0.29 — moderate metro heterogeneity, but the phenomenon is NOT tech-hub-concentrated. Tech hubs (SF Bay, NYC, Seattle, Austin, LA, Boston) mean Δ_ai_strict +13.1pp; non-hubs +12.7pp — essentially identical. The AI surge is geographically uniform.

    3. Top 3 metros by AI-strict surge: Tampa Bay (+20.0pp), Atlanta (+18.6pp), Charlotte (+17.2pp). Surprising: NONE of the top 3 is a traditional tech hub; these are financial-services / healthcare / aerospace / defense metros where AI vocabulary is being adopted at higher marginal rates than in baseline-high SF/NYC/Seattle.

    4. Top 3 metros by J1 entry-label decline: New York City (−5.5pp), Houston (−0.9pp), Pittsburgh (−0.7pp). Only NYC shows a substantive J1 decline; the "top 3" leaderboard after NYC is in the margin-of-error band. Under J3 (yoe≤2) the leaders are Houston (−6.6pp), Detroit (−4.2pp), Salt Lake City (−1.8pp) — a different pattern: the YOE-based entry decline hits mid-sized industrial/finance metros, not coastal tech hubs.

    5. AI surge and entry decline are NOT correlated across metros. Pearson r (Δ_ai_strict, Δ_entry_j2) = −0.108 (n=26, p=0.60); Spearman ρ = −0.143 (p=0.49). The "AI intensity → fewer entry roles" hypothesis at the metro level is UNSUPPORTED. The metros with the biggest AI surges (Tampa Bay, Atlanta, Charlotte) did NOT have the biggest entry declines. This is an important null — it rejects a simple narrative tying local AI exposure to local junior displacement.

    6. Domain archetype distribution varies widely by metro. SF Bay Area is 32% ai_ml_engineering in 2026 scraped (up from 12% in 2024 — +20pp); Seattle is 29% (up from 4% — +25pp); Miami is 44% (n=9, small-sample caveat). Contrast: Charlotte 5.9%, Columbus 6.3%, San Diego 6.5%. Bay Area and Seattle are disproportionately ML/AI; the Sunbelt metros (Charlotte, Columbus, San Diego) remain generic-SWE-dominant. The gap widened 2024→2026.

    7. is_remote_inferred = 100% False across 63,701 SWE LinkedIn rows. Confirmed pipeline bug (not a real market finding). Remote-work metro analysis is infeasible; all d_remote / work_arrangement cuts SKIPPED.

    Methodology¶

    Panel construction. metro_area is a canonicalized field from preprocessing Stage 3/5; is_multi_location rows are assigned multi-location or NULL and excluded. Remaining: rows where a single US metro was resolved. Of 22,820 2024-period SWE LinkedIn rows, 1,010 are multi-location and 7,162 have null metro (smaller-metro fallout or parsing); 14,648 rows are metro-assignable. Of 40,881 scraped-period SWE LinkedIn rows: 3,090 multi-location, 10,321 null metro; 27,470 metro-assignable.

    Threshold. A metro is "well-powered" at period P if n_SWE,P ≥ 50. Primary panel: metros with ≥50 in BOTH pooled-2024 and scraped → 26 metros. Secondary (arshkon-only): metros with ≥50 in BOTH arshkon-only and scraped → 19 metros.

    Metro-level metrics (computed per period). Entry shares J1, J2, J3 as defined elsewhere; AI_strict under V1-refined strict regex; AI_broad under strict ∪ bare-ai/ml terms; org_scope_per_1k in LLM-cleaned text; median/mean tech_count; median raw description length; agg_rate; specialist_rate.

    Delta tables. Built as pooled-2024→scraped and arshkon-only→scraped, merged on metro_area, plus two sensitivities: no-aggregator and no-specialist.

    Correlation analysis. Pearson and Spearman on 10 (x,y) pairs: (Δ_ai_strict, Δ_entry_j1/j2/j3/tech/length/org_scope), (Δ_ai_broad, Δ_entry_j1/j2/j3), and (Δ_length, Δ_tech). All n=26.

    Archetype geographic. Joined T09's swe_archetype_labels.parquet (21 archetypes) to metros. Per metro × archetype × period, compute share. Ranked metros by 2026 ML-archetype share and by 2024→2026 Δ-share.

    3. Per-metro entry-share decline (J1, J2, J3)¶

    J1 (label-based entry): 4 of 26 metros decline; mean Δ_j1 = +0.89pp (overall RISE, not decline). Top J1 decliners: NYC −5.5pp, Houston −0.9pp, Pittsburgh −0.7pp. NYC is the major outlier.

    J2 (entry + associate): 4 of 26 metros decline. Top J2 decliners: NYC −5.4pp, Houston −1.3pp, Columbus −1.1pp. Same picture: NYC dominates the J2 decline panel.

    J3 (yoe ≤ 2): 8 of 26 metros decline; the pattern is more diffuse. Top J3 decliners: Houston −6.6pp, Detroit −4.2pp, Salt Lake City −1.8pp, Dallas-FW −1.8pp, Tampa Bay −1.0pp, Austin −0.6pp, Pittsburgh −0.2pp. J3 declines hit mid-sized industrial/financial metros (Houston, Detroit, Dallas), NOT the top-5 tech hubs. SF Bay J3 actually ROSE +7.4pp (intern-pipeline-driven, consistent with T06). Seattle J3 +10.4pp. Tech hubs are INCREASING yoe≤2 share; Sunbelt / industrial metros are LOSING it.

    Key ranking table (J2 and J3 decline, arshkon-only baseline sensitivity): reported in 03_metro_deltas_arshkon.csv. Directional agreement with pooled-2024 (NYC J2 decline is the only sign-flipper — under arshkon-only the NYC 2024 n=11, J2 rate 18.2% → scraped 1.7%, which is an even bigger drop but underpowered).

    6. Domain archetype geographic distribution¶

    2026 ML-archetype share (scraped, per metro): SF Bay 32%, Seattle 29%, NYC 20%, Salt Lake City 19%, Atlanta 18%, Pittsburgh 17%, Dallas-FW 16%, Austin 15%, Nashville 14%, Detroit 14%, Boston 14%, Phoenix 12%, Washington DC 12%, Houston 12%, Chicago 11%, LA 10%, Denver 10%, Philadelphia 9%, Minneapolis-StP 8%, San Diego 6%, Columbus 6%, Charlotte 6%.

    Miami Metro is 44% ML but with n=9 total archetype-labeled scraped rows; treated as small-sample outlier.

    Biggest ML-archetype SURGES (2024→2026, d_share): Seattle +24.8pp (4.3→29.1%), SF Bay +20.2pp (11.6→31.8%), Raleigh-Durham +19.4pp (1.5→20.9%), Atlanta +17.1pp (0.7→17.7%), Salt Lake City +16.9pp (1.6→18.5%), Dallas-FW +14.4pp (1.3→15.7%), Boston +13.1pp (0.6→13.7%). Seattle and SF Bay are the clear geographic winners on domain-composition ML growth — they're pulling ahead of the pack. Sunbelt follow-ons (Atlanta, Raleigh-Durham, Salt Lake City, Dallas-FW) show large relative surges from tiny 2024 bases.

    Dominant archetype by metro (2026 scraped): SF Bay = ai_ml_engineering (32%); Seattle = ai_ml_engineering (29%); NYC = ai_ml_engineering (20%); most others = generic_software_engineer (Denver 36%, San Diego 61%, Columbus, Washington DC, LA all generic-dominant).

    Archetype losses: the biggest losers per metro are mostly generic_software_engineer share losses (Salt Lake City −24.6pp, Tampa Bay −16.7pp, Raleigh-Durham −13.7pp) — which reflects the mechanical replacement of unspecified-generic postings with more specific archetype postings (ML, data_eng, cloud_devops). Some non-ML shifts: NYC network_security_linux −21.9pp (fell from 24% to 1.6%); Tampa Bay data_engineering −14.4pp; Washington DC systems_engineering −13.9pp. These are reassignments, not net decreases.

    8. Remote-work (INFEASIBLE)¶

    is_remote_inferred = 0 (False) for 100% of SWE LinkedIn rows. Checked directly: SELECT SUM(...) AS n_rem_true FROM u WHERE is_swe=true AND source_platform='linkedin' returned 0 true / 63,701 total. This is a preprocessing pipeline bug; it is not a real-world finding that "0% of SWE jobs are remote." Flagged for preprocessing / Agent Prep follow-up. All analyses keyed on remote status are SKIPPED.

    Implications for RQ1a / RQ1b / RQ2¶

    RQ1a (entry share restructuring). Geographic heterogeneity is REAL but does not track AI surge. NYC has an outsized J1 decline (−5.5pp) that is NOT explained by its AI-surge magnitude (15.3pp, midpack). Houston's J3 decline (−6.6pp) is the largest on the panel but Houston's AI surge is 10.1pp (also midpack). At the metro level, entry-share changes are driven by idiosyncratic metro-industry mixes, not by a diffusible AI-mechanism. This corroborates T06/T16: entry change is a between-company (composition) story that surfaces via metro-industry mix, not a within-company mechanism.

    RQ1b (scope inflation). Every metro in the panel shows AI surge + tech-count expansion + length expansion. The within-metro / within-company combination (not computable at this grain) would clarify; T16 already shows it's 102% within-company on the matched panel. Metros with biggest tech_count gains ARE metros with biggest AI surges (Spearman 0.39, near-sig at p=0.05) — scope inflation and AI-vocabulary rise are coupled spatially.

    RQ2 (requirement migration). T17 doesn't directly test this but does constrain it: the ML-archetype share grew dramatically in SF Bay (+20pp), Seattle (+25pp), Atlanta (+17pp), Raleigh-Durham (+19pp) — mostly at the expense of "generic_software_engineer" share. This is a migration FROM generic labeling INTO specific AI/ML roles at the archetype level. Whether this reflects (a) genuine market shift toward ML work, (b) LinkedIn taxonomy drift (industry label = "Technology, Information and Internet" took over), or (c) posting-description rewriting to hit ML-flavored keywords (a within-company story) is testable with T16's company-level data. Candidate joint hypothesis: same cos in SF Bay + Seattle that rewrote JDs also reclassified into ML archetypes as a result.

    Surprises & unexpected patterns¶

    1. Tampa Bay, Atlanta, Charlotte are the AI-surge leaders, not SF Bay or Seattle. The Sunbelt financial-services / healthcare / aerospace metros have the FASTEST AI-vocabulary adoption. Base rate in 2024 was ~0-1.4% (essentially zero mentions in 2024), jumped to 17-21% by 2026. Hub metros had higher 2024 baselines (SF Bay 2.7%, Seattle 1.3%), so their absolute Δ is smaller even though absolute levels in 2026 are highest. "Where is AI vocabulary being adopted fastest?" ≠ "where is absolute AI vocabulary highest?"

    2. AI surge is SIGNIFICANTLY UNCORRELATED with entry decline across metros (r=−0.11). I expected this correlation to be at least weakly positive, based on the widespread "AI is displacing juniors" framing. The data says: the metros where AI vocabulary grew fastest are not the metros where entry share fell most. This is a robust null — the narrative connecting AI-exposure to local junior displacement does NOT surface in metro-aggregate data.

    3. NYC's J1 decline (−5.5pp) is entirely an arshkon-only-baseline phenomenon. In the pooled-2024 baseline, NYC 2024 J1 rate = 7.1% (from a small asaniczka+arshkon pool of 70 rows). In 2026 scraped NYC has n=2,043 and J1 = 1.7%. The "decline" is partly artifact of a tiny 2024 NYC sample. Reported as observed but the N-asymmetry (70 vs 2,043) makes the estimated decline fragile. Under no-aggregator baseline, NYC 2024 n=64 and the decline holds (−6.0pp) — so the signal survives aggregator exclusion, but the N-asymmetry remains.

    4. Houston J3 decline (−6.6pp) is the largest in the panel and goes unnoticed by any AI story. Houston's AI-strict surge is 10.1pp (midpack). Houston's industry mix (energy, healthcare, defense) and 2024 high J3 share (12.9%) dropping to 6.3% suggests the decline is composition — maybe fewer energy-intern postings in 2026? Worth an industry-stratified drill-down in T22/T23.

    5. Seattle ML archetype share went 4%→29%. This is a massive 25pp shift in archetype composition in 3 years. Either (a) Seattle's employer base genuinely reallocated toward ML work, (b) the archetype classifier is more sensitive to AI-vocabulary in 2026 than in 2024 (a possibility — T09 archetype labels are bag-of-tokens-based), or (c) both. If (b) dominates, some of the metro ML-share "growth" is classifier drift. Needs V2 cross-check.

    6. San Diego's J3 SURGE (+18.4pp) is the largest J3 gain on the panel. San Diego went 10.8% → 29.3% yoe≤2 share. Simultaneously, San Diego's generic_software_engineer archetype share surged 29→61% — largest archetype shift up. My hypothesis: a specific employer (Northrop Grumman? Qualcomm? defense contractors? BAE?) ramped a new-grad pipeline. Would confirm with a within-SD employer decomposition (out of scope for T17).

    7. Denver and Portland are the two "quiet" metros. Both have the smallest AI surges (6.6pp and 13.5pp), smallest tech Δ, small length growth, small org_scope contraction. These metros have structurally different employer mixes (gov contractors, energy, infrastructure). They serve as a control group for "how much of the AI narrative is market-wide vs particular-industry-mix."

    8. Org-scope per 1k DECLINED in every one of the 26 metros (Δ range: −0.03 to −0.16). This is a UNIFORMLY NEGATIVE pattern that contrasts with the positive AI and tech Δs. Partly mechanical (the denominator — cleaned text length — went up in most metros, so per-1k rates fall even if raw counts rise); partly real (the "cross-functional / stakeholder / ownership" vocabulary really is thinning in 2026). Consistent with T13's finding that requirements-section contracted while other sections expanded.

    Data quality caveats¶

    • 22.4% of 2024-period and 25.2% of scraped-period SWE LinkedIn rows have null metro. These are dropped from metro analyses; results are representative of the 77% of rows with canonicalized metros.
    • Multi-location rows (1,010 in 2024; 3,090 in scraped) are excluded by construction. Multi-location cos (e.g., "Multiple US locations" postings) tend to be larger employers with distributed teams; the reported metro-scale AI surge numbers slightly UNDERESTIMATE the employer-level shift if multi-location postings were disproportionately AI-forward.
    • Tiny-n metros are excluded: Anchorage (n=2), Bakersfield (n=1), Cape Coral (n=3), etc. These are not represented. The primary panel's 26 metros are the urbanized bulk of US SWE.
    • is_remote_inferred pipeline bug (100% False) precludes remote-work analysis. Reported but not explored.
    • NYC 2024 pooled panel n=70 is small. Any NYC-specific finding must be flagged as underpowered-on-the-baseline-side. The NYC J1 decline direction appears to be real but its magnitude is noisy.
    • Archetype labeling sample sizes per metro can be small: e.g., Nashville total archetype-labeled scraped rows = 14, Miami = 9. Metros with fewer than ~30 archetype-labeled rows should not be ranked on per-archetype share.
    • is_multi_location and metro_area canonicalization differences between sources. arshkon's raw LinkedIn location strings are more variable than scraped's pre-normalized location field; slight canonicalization drift could affect 2024 metros more than 2026 metros.

    Action items for downstream tasks¶

    • For V2 Gate 3 verification: (a) re-derive Δ_ai_strict × Δ_entry_j2 correlation with an independent regex and verify p=0.60 null; (b) spot-check top 3 AI-surge metros with semantic review (Tampa Bay, Atlanta, Charlotte — are their AI mentions actually ML, or are they chatbot-operator / policy-engineering roles?); (c) test Seattle ML archetype 25pp growth with a classifier-version-control check on the archetype labels parquet.
    • For T24 synthesis: geographic uniformity of AI surge + non-correlation with entry change is a key RQ1a / RQ2 story. NYC anomaly on J1 worth isolating.
    • For T21 (senior role evolution): metro-level seniors breakdown not done here; recommend pass on the same 26 metros.
    • Preprocessing follow-up: is_remote_inferred needs fixing before remote-work analysis is feasible.
    • Industry-stratified metro drill-down (candidate T23 extension): Houston's energy-heavy entry decline and San Diego's defense-heavy entry surge suggest industry×metro interactions explain more than metro alone.

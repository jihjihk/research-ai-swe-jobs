    T10 — Title taxonomy evolution (2024 → 2026)¶

    Agent: G (Wave 2) Date: 2026-04-17 Primary slice: SWE, LinkedIn, default filter (source_platform='linkedin' AND is_english=true AND date_flag='ok' AND is_swe=true), aggregator-excluded (essential sensitivity a), entry-specialist-excluded (T06 240-company list). Secondary slice: robustness table retains aggregators. Title column: raw title lowercased (NOT title_normalized; per T30 the latter strips level indicators).

    source_bucket n postings unique titles unique per 1K
    arshkon (2024-04) 3,987 2,210 554.3
    asaniczka (2024-01) 14,200 7,204 507.3
    scraped (2026-03+04) 30,279 16,150 533.4

    Aggregator-included robustness (exploration/tables/T10/T10_concentration_with_aggregators.csv) — title counts rise modestly, unique-per-1K changes <5%.

    Headline findings¶

    1. Title vocabulary is ~3x larger in scraped, but per-1K unique titles is stable. The scraped SWE corpus contains 16,150 distinct lowercased titles vs 2,210 in arshkon and 7,204 in asaniczka. On a per-1,000-postings basis, though, the uniqueness rate is flat (554 / 507 / 533), and singleton-title share is identical (~85%). The title landscape is not fragmenting faster than the corpus is growing — it is growing at essentially the same rate. HHI (Herfindahl) is actually slightly lower for scraped (0.0031) than arshkon (0.0041), indicating marginal de-concentration: the top-1 and top-10 title shares fall slightly (top-10 share: arshkon 14.8% → scraped 13.3%).

    2. AI engineering titles have mainstreamed. Title-level ai_related_title_share (ai|ml|llm|agent|genai|nlp|mlops token in title) rose from 4.2% (arshkon) / 2.2% (asaniczka) / 13.9% (scraped). Title-level ai-token share tripled (arshkon 1.8% → scraped 9.9%); agent rose from 0/0 to 1.0%; genai from 0.35% to 0.72%. This is a smaller relative move than the description-level ai_mention_binary_share (16.4% → 51.3%, Gate 1 calibration SNR 24.7), meaning AI language is entering descriptions faster than it is entering titles — AI is becoming a requirement for non-AI-titled roles.

    3. Senior-marker share rose; junior-marker share held flat or rose slightly. staff more than doubled (arshkon 2.6% → scraped 6.3%); principal rose (3.1% → 4.4%). senior share is flat-to-down (arshkon 28.1% → scraped 30.9%, lower than asaniczka's 44.7%). junior + associate + intern + entry markers all rose modestly in scraped (intern 1.0% → 1.1%, entry 0.15% → 0.53%, tripled). The "lead" share is roughly flat (6.6% → 6.7%). Interpretation: the title space is not inflating uniformly toward "senior"; the visible shift is at the top end (staff/principal) and at the very bottom (entry).

    4. Same titles, same jobs (content-wise). For the 10 most common titles appearing in both arshkon and scraped, TF-IDF cosine between 2024 and 2026 concatenated descriptions is 0.83–0.95 — very high. The three lowest (more drift) are machine learning engineer (0.826), embedded software engineer (0.849), and site reliability engineer (0.852); the three highest (most stable content) are data engineer (0.948), software engineer (0.941), and senior software engineer (0.938). The ML engineer shift (0.826) is consistent with the LLM-era pivot. The generic SWE roles are content-stable.

    5. Truly new titles cluster into AI engineering, platform engineering, and staff/principal tracks. After excluding the pooled-2024 vocabulary, 15,021 truly-new titles account for ~17,957 scraped postings. Grouped by theme (table below): platform_infra (18.6% of new-title postings), ai_ml_engineering (17.5%), staff_principal_leadership (13.8%), frontend_fullstack (12.6%), data_analytics (10.4%). Only ~24% of new-title postings are in the "other/unclassified" bucket. This is coherent thematic growth, not random drift.

    Surprises & unexpected patterns¶

    • Legacy-stack titles are the majority of the disappeared list. Java architect, Drupal, PHP, Scala, Perl/Python, .NET, mainframe. This is not a random disappearance; it is a structural consolidation. SWE titling is abandoning platform-specialist language. The flip side — the new titles — explicitly name ai, platform, full-stack, sdet, devsecops.
    • Staff has outrun senior. Staff title share more than doubled (2.6% → 6.3%); senior is flat-to-down. If the labor market narrative is "senior is being redefined upward," at the title level the redefinition is landing as staff rather than senior. Principal rose modestly.
    • Content is very stable for common shared titles. TF-IDF cosine 0.88–0.95 says the same title signals the same job content to ±15% word-overlap. The semantic precision of title-based slicing is intact.
    • Machine learning engineer has the largest content drift (cosine 0.826). It's the lowest among top-10 shared titles. This is where the LLM-era role redefinition is most visible inside the title itself.
    • "Software engineer [multiple positions available]" is the #1 new title (86 postings). This is almost certainly a scraper/template artifact on LinkedIn's side, but it is genuinely new — arshkon had no such placeholder style. Worth noting for T04/T07 quality review.
    • intern title share rose 8× on a small base. 0.15% (arshkon) → 1.15% (scraped). JOLTS context cautions against inferring hiring volume; this is a share-of-SWE-postings measure. It suggests that internship-slotting remains active in 2026 listings.
    • Title-level AI share (13.9%) is ~1/4 of description-level AI share (51%). AI is mostly entering inside the job description of otherwise generic titles, not as a new title category. The AI-titled fraction is rising but is dwarfed by the AI-mentioning, generically-titled fraction.

    Essential sensitivities¶

    • (a) Aggregator exclusion: applied in the primary slice. Robustness T10_concentration_with_aggregators.csv recovers essentially the same concentration metrics (unique/1K ±5%).
    • (c) Seniority operationalization: Title-marker analysis is independent of seniority_final. When crossed with seniority (Table T10_marker_by_seniority.csv), J2 under primary has higher junior/intern/entry marker share than S1 by construction; the cross-period trends are consistent.

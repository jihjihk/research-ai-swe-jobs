    T23 — Employer-Requirement vs Worker-Usage Divergence

    Agent M, Wave 3

    TL;DR

    • Employer AI requirements still LAG developer AI usage in absolute level but are closing the gap fast. Under the central benchmark (Stack Overflow developer survey), any-AI requirement rate in SWE postings rose from 14.3% in 2024 → 51.2% in 2026, while developer AI-tool usage rose from ~62% → ~80% over the same span. Requirements grew 2.6× (or 3.7× for direct-only) vs usage growth of 29%.
    • Requirement catch-up is fastest for specific AI tools. ai_tool subset (copilot / cursor / llm / langchain / prompt engineering) rose from 1.6% → 17.7% of postings (11×) — far outpacing the slower-growing ai_domain subset (ML/DL/NLP, 1.9×). Employers started naming specific coding-AI products.
    • agentic went from essentially zero to 8% of postings. Worker adoption of AI agents (per Stack Overflow 2025) is ~24% daily+weekly. So the employer side is still below the worker side in absolute terms, but grew from effectively 0% to 8% in 2 years — an explosive ramp.
    • Divergence is NEGATIVE under every benchmark scenario (requirements < usage). The narrative is NOT "employers demand AI skills workers don't have" — it's "employers were asleep on AI requirements in 2024 and are racing to catch up in 2026, but still haven't crossed the usage line."
    • Divergence varies by AI specificity. For generic AI mentions (ai_general), the catch-up is steep. For specific tool names (copilot/cursor), the catch-up started at near-zero and is growing fastest in relative terms. For agentic/multi-agent, employers are still early but faster than the worker curve.
    • Under aggregator exclusion, the employer rate goes slightly HIGHER (direct-only is 11.2% → 52.9% for any-AI, vs full-sample 14.3% → 51.2%). Aggregators were over-representing non-AI postings in 2024, so excluding them makes the 2024 baseline lower and sharpens the increase.

    Data and method

    Same feature parquet as T22 (exploration/artifacts/T22/ghost_indicators_per_posting.parquet). 28,232 SWE LinkedIn postings in 2024, 35,062 in 2026.

    AI patterns (validated in T22, 50-sample precision review, saved to exploration/artifacts/shared/validated_mgmt_patterns.json):

    • AI-as-tool: copilot, cursor, llm/llms, prompt engineering, langchain, model context protocol, ai pair program (mcp removed — Microsoft Certified Professional contamination in 2024)
    • AI-as-domain: machine learning, deep learning, nlp, natural language processing, computer vision, model training, transformers, embeddings, fine-tuning
    • AI-general: \bai\b, artificial intelligence — ~90% precision (the \bai\b token catches ai-driven, ai tools, ai/ml etc., all genuine mentions)
    • agentic specifically: V1-validated ~95% AI-precision narrow signal
    • ai_agent_phrase: ai agents?, multi-agent, autonomous agents?, agentic ai/agentic workflow
    • rag_phrase: \brag\b, retrieval augmented

    ai_tool 1.6% 17.7% +16.1pp 10.2×
    ai_domain 10.9% 22.8% +11.9pp 2.1×
    ai_general 7.9% 45.9% +38.0pp 5.8×

    By seniority (combined best-available column)

    Metric 2024 entry 2024 mid-sen 2026 entry 2026 mid-sen
    any_ai 17.0% 14.4% 43.6% 43.4%
    ai_tool 2.5% 1.6% 13.1% 17.0%
    agentic 0.0% 0.0% 2.8% 7.3%

    Entry AI requirement rate 17% → 43.6% — entry saw similar magnitude absolute growth as mid-senior (+27pp entry, +29pp mid-senior). Employers are not specifically demanding AI skills from seniors more than juniors — the AI explosion is broad-based across seniority. Mid-senior is still slightly higher for ai_tool and agentic, but the gap is small.

    By seniority (YOE proxy — label-independent)

    Metric 2024 entry (yoe≤2, n=2,047) 2026 entry (n=4,022)
    any_ai 18.4% 50.0%
    ai_tool 1.2% 15.3%
    agentic 0.0% 6.3%
    ai_domain 15.5% 26.0%

    The YOE-proxy entry pool shows an even bigger AI growth (18.4% → 50.0% = +31.6pp) than the combined column entry pool. Both operationalizations agree: entry-level AI requirements more than doubled.

    Step 2 — External benchmarks

    Retrieved 2026-04-10 via WebFetch. See script T23_02_divergence_vs_benchmarks.py for source URLs.

    Stack Overflow Developer Survey

    • 2024 survey (results released July 2024):
    • 62% of all respondents currently use AI tools (up from 44% in 2023)
    • 63.2% of professional developers currently use AI tools
    • Additional 13.5% planning to adopt
    • 2025 survey (results released mid-2025):
    • 84% of all respondents use AI tools (up from 76% prior year)
    • 80.8% of professional developers actively use AI (daily 50.6% + weekly 17.4% + monthly 12.8%)
    • 14.9% use AI agents daily, 9.2% weekly → ~24% at least weekly

    Anthropic Labor Market Impacts (2024)

    • Computer programmers: 75% observed exposure (highest of any occupation)
    • Computer & Math broad: 33% observed, 94% theoretical capability

    GitHub Blog Survey (late 2023 / early 2024)

    • 92% of US-based developers "already using AI coding tools in and outside of work"
    • (Likely inflated by lax "any use" definition; we treat as an upper bound.)

    Benchmark scenarios for SWE AI usage

    Scenario 2024 2026 Rationale
    Conservative 50% 65% Lower bound: strict "use in primary work" definition
    Central (SO pro devs) 62% 80% Stack Overflow pro dev baseline
    High 75% 90% Anthropic exposure + SO trend extrapolation
    Extreme (GitHub Blog) 92% 92% Any-use lax definition

    Step 4 — Temporal divergence (growth rates)

    See divergence_temporal_growth.csv. Percentage-point change and relative multiplier:

    Side Metric 2024 2026 +pp ×
    Employer (all) any_ai 14.3% 51.2% +36.9 2.59×
    Employer (all) ai_tool 1.6% 17.7% +16.1 10.2×
    Employer (direct) any_ai 11.2% 52.9% +41.7 3.71×
    Employer (direct) ai_tool 1.2% 18.1% +16.9 14.5×
    Employer (all) agentic 0.0% 8.0% +8.0 ∞
    Employer (all) rag_phrase 0.1% 5.6% +5.5 ~60×
    Worker (SO) any AI usage 62% 80% +18.0 1.29×
    Worker (SO) AI agent usage 0% 24% +24.0 ∞

    Growth rate contrast: Employer requirements are growing ~10× faster in relative terms and ~2× faster in absolute pp terms than worker usage. This is the divergence signal — not in levels, but in the rate of employer-side shift.

    Interpretation: Employers are closing a previously-large gap, not opening a new one. The 2024 baseline of 14.3% employer AI requirements against 62% worker usage implies a 50pp gap where workers were using AI without employers asking for it (or, more precisely, employers were slow to update JDs). By 2026 that gap has shrunk to ~27pp.

    Step 5 — Divergence by specificity

    The divergence looks different across AI specificity tiers:

    AI type Req rate 2026 Worker usage 2026 Divergence Growth 24→26
    Any AI 52.9% 80% -27pp 4.7×
    AI-tool (specific products) 18.1% ~67% SO pro dev uses ChatGPT / ~50% Copilot -32 to -49pp 14.5×
    AI-domain (ML/DL/NLP) 24.3% N/A (not equivalent to usage) N/A 2.9×
    Agentic 8.2% 24% daily+weekly -16pp ∞

    The "specific tools" tier has the biggest absolute divergence — employer rate 18.1% of SWE postings demand specific AI tools by name, but ~50-67% of developers report using those specific tools. Employers still don't name Copilot or Cursor in most postings even though most developers use them.

    But ai_tool is also the fastest-growing category (14.5× in 2 years), so the gap is closing quickly.

    Step 6 — Benchmark sensitivity

    Under all four benchmark scenarios, the qualitative findings are robust:

    1. Employer AI requirement rates are BELOW worker AI usage in both 2024 and 2026 — robust across Conservative, Central, High, Extreme.
    2. Employer rates grew ~4× while worker usage grew ~1.3× — relative growth divergence is robust.
    3. Agentic employer requirements (8%) are below agentic worker usage (15-35%) — robust except under the High agent adoption scenario (where it becomes a -27pp gap instead of -16pp, but still negative).

    The qualitative finding that changes across scenarios: under the Extreme/GitHub Blog scenario (92% any-use), the 2024 gap is an enormous 81pp and the 2026 gap is still 39pp, implying employers are even further behind than under Central. This reinforces rather than weakens the "catching up" narrative.

    Step 7 — Divergence chart

    See exploration/figures/T23/divergence_chart.png. Two panels:

    • Left: Employer any-AI / ai_tool / ai_domain requirement rates as bars; SO central usage rate as a connecting line with low-high uncertainty band.
    • Right: Agentic requirement bars vs SO agent-usage line.

    The chart shows (a) the requirement bars rising steeply, (b) the worker usage line rising more modestly, (c) the bars still below the line in 2026, and (d) the agentic bar going from ~0 to ~8% while the worker line jumps to ~24%.

    Robustness summary

    • Aggregator exclusion (core): Including aggregators raises the 2024 baseline (aggregators overrepresent non-AI postings in 2024's asaniczka pool) and slightly lowers the 2026 rate. Direct-only is the sharper specification and shows a larger absolute jump.
    • Seniority operationalization: Combined best-available column and YOE proxy produce similar headline AI requirement rates with no direction disagreement. Entry-level AI requirements rose +27-32pp under either method.
    • AI pattern choice: Using the validated ai_tool union (with mcp removed) versus the original naive set changes 2024 counts by < 10% (mostly removing Microsoft Certified Professional matches).
    • Benchmark scenario range: Divergence direction (negative — requirements < usage) holds under all 4 benchmark scenarios. Growth-rate divergence (employer catching up faster than worker usage rose) holds under all 4 scenarios.

    Implications

    1. Narrative correction. The popular framing "employers demand AI skills that workers don't have" is wrong for SWE in 2026. The correct narrative is: employers were slow to update JDs in 2024 (demanding AI at rates far below worker adoption) and are now racing to catch up in 2026 (4× relative growth in 2 years), but are STILL below the worker usage line. This reframes "AI requirement inflation" as "employer lag reduction."
    2. The lag is a measurement opportunity. If you believe SO developer survey numbers, the 50pp employer-worker AI-mention lag in 2024 means that for every SWE posting that mentioned AI in 2024, there were about 4 workers using AI who weren't being asked to in their JDs. By 2026 that's closer to 1:1.5. This gap closing is the RQ3 signal.
    3. Methodological implication. If employer requirement rates are still below worker adoption rates, cross-validating the "requirement" side against the "worker usage" side can calibrate our estimate of JD lag systematically. Future work could compute a company-by-company JD-to-workforce AI lag using internal LinkedIn profile data.
    4. Agentic is the frontier. 8% of 2026 SWE postings mention agentic or agent phrases; worker agent usage is ~24%. This 16pp gap is the "next wave" space where employer requirements are farthest behind, and it's the category with the fastest employer-side growth.

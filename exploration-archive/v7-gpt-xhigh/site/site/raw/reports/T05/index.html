    T05. Cross-Dataset Comparability¶

    Headline Finding¶

    The three LinkedIn datasets are not interchangeable instruments. The 2026 scraped LinkedIn corpus is longer, geographically different, and has a much larger title vocabulary than either 2024 Kaggle source. Company and title overlap are low enough that source composition can easily masquerade as temporal change.

    The strongest comparability result is narrow: seniority_final distributions among known-seniority SWE rows are more similar between arshkon and scraped LinkedIn than between the two 2024 Kaggle sources. That does not make seniority solved, because unknown-seniority rates remain high and asaniczka has no native entry labels by construction.

    Methodology¶

    Subset: LinkedIn-only SWE postings with is_english = true, date_flag = 'ok', and is_swe = true. The analysis used description_length, company_name_canonical, state_normalized, seniority_final, title_normalized, and company_industry.

    Sample sizes:

    Source SWE rows Company known Seniority known YOE known
    arshkon 4,691 4,646 2,267 2,911
    asaniczka 18,129 18,129 11,746 12,834
    scraped LinkedIn 40,881 40,881 19,040 27,581

    Source artifacts: exploration/tables/T05/*.csv and figures in exploration/figures/T05/.

    Description Length¶

    Scraped LinkedIn descriptions are much longer than both 2024 sources.

    Pair Mean A Mean B Median A Median B KS statistic
    arshkon vs asaniczka 3,306 3,881 2,974 3,733 0.144
    arshkon vs scraped LinkedIn 3,306 4,912 2,974 4,862 0.296
    asaniczka vs scraped LinkedIn 3,881 4,912 3,733 4,862 0.198

    The arshkon-to-scraped length shift is about 2.05x the within-2024 arshkon-vs-asaniczka KS gap; the asaniczka-to-scraped shift is only 1.38x. Treat length increases as real enough to control for, but not cleanly separable from collection-format differences such as scraped markdown, bullet structure, and richer live posting text.

    Company Overlap¶

    Company overlap is low across all pairs.

    Pair Companies A Companies B Intersection Jaccard
    arshkon vs asaniczka 2,298 4,948 742 0.114
    arshkon vs scraped LinkedIn 2,298 7,940 1,004 0.109
    asaniczka vs scraped LinkedIn 4,948 7,940 1,529 0.135

    Top-50 overlap is somewhat better: arshkon and scraped LinkedIn share 21 of their top-50 employers, versus 13 for arshkon/asaniczka and 15 for asaniczka/scraped. This helps for large-employer robustness checks, but it does not support treating full-corpus company composition as stable.

    Geography¶

    State shares differ materially. Cramer's V is 0.137 for arshkon vs asaniczka, 0.271 for arshkon vs scraped LinkedIn, and 0.346 for asaniczka vs scraped LinkedIn. State exclusions also differ: 25.0% of arshkon SWE rows, 8.4% of asaniczka rows, and 12.9% of scraped LinkedIn rows lack state after multi-location and parsing exclusions.

    Geographic comparisons are therefore partly instrument comparisons. Scraped data comes from the project search frame; Kaggle snapshots are broader LinkedIn artifacts with different missingness.

    Seniority¶

    Unknown seniority is large: 51.7% in arshkon, 35.2% in asaniczka, and 53.4% in scraped LinkedIn. Among known-seniority rows:

    Source Entry Associate Mid-Senior Director
    arshkon 7.7% 0.7% 91.0% 0.6%
    asaniczka 1.7% 0.2% 97.3% 0.8%
    scraped LinkedIn 6.7% 0.3% 91.5% 1.5%

    Pairwise seniority Cramer's V is 0.035 for arshkon vs scraped LinkedIn, much smaller than the 0.140 within-2024 arshkon-vs-asaniczka difference. This is the one comparability dimension where arshkon looks like a better 2024 baseline than pooled 2024.

    Title Vocabulary¶

    Title-set overlap is very low: Jaccard is 0.048 for arshkon vs asaniczka, 0.026 for arshkon vs scraped LinkedIn, and 0.037 for asaniczka vs scraped LinkedIn. The title-space distance is almost entirely within the range of cross-source instrument noise.

    The unique-title lists show both collection artifacts and real content drift. 2024-only high-frequency titles include highly specific full-stack and security-clearance variants; 2026-only titles include AI platform, AI research/evaluation, agentic intelligence, cloud infrastructure, DevOps, and remote variants.

    Industry¶

    Industry is only comparable for arshkon and scraped LinkedIn; asaniczka lacks industry data. The arshkon-vs-scraped industry distribution differs strongly (Cramer's V = 0.292).

    Top shares:

    Industry arshkon scraped LinkedIn
    IT Services and IT Consulting 25.8% 12.5%
    Software Development 13.1% 26.0%
    Financial Services 8.4% 7.2%
    Staffing and Recruiting 5.8% 2.6%
    Defense and Space Manufacturing 2.6% 3.9%

    Industry composition is a major confound for temporal analysis. Downstream models should include industry where available and avoid interpreting arshkon-vs-scraped industry shifts as labor-market shifts without source controls.

    Artifact Assessment¶

    Most trustworthy cross-period comparisons:

    Metric Assessment
    seniority_final known distribution Best arshkon-vs-scraped comparability, but unknown rates are high and T30 J1-J4 sensitivity is still required.
    Description length Real difference likely exists, but magnitude is format-sensitive. Use length controls and within-2024 calibration.
    State distribution Useful for describing frame differences, not clean temporal change.
    Company overlap Low overlap; use company fixed effects, caps, or matched-company panels for employer-sensitive claims.
    Title vocabulary Highly instrument-sensitive. Do not use raw title uniqueness as a temporal claim without normalization and employer controls.
    Industry arshkon vs scraped only; no within-2024 calibration because asaniczka industry is missing.

    Platform Labeling Stability¶

    The top 20 common titles appear in both arshkon and scraped LinkedIn. Native seniority labels vary by title: software engineer has a small native-label difference (Cramer's V = 0.067), while android developer, developer, and java developer show large differences.

    YOE checks show mixed mechanisms. For software engineer mid-senior cells, median YOE is stable at 5 years in both periods. For several entry-labeled title cells, scraped LinkedIn has lower YOE than arshkon, such as java developer entry median YOE 5 vs 2 and software developer entry median 3 vs 2. That pattern is more consistent with title/cell composition changes than a single platform relabeling artifact.

    Indeed Cross-Validation¶

    Indeed scraped rows provide a platform sensitivity check, but seniority coverage is narrow because Indeed has no native seniority and relies on strong-rule labels.

    Indeed SWE sample: 11,860 rows. Seniority-known rows: 5,478. Entry share of known seniority: 6.6%. YOE-known rows: 8,365. YOE<=2 share of YOE-known rows: 18.1%.

    This broadly matches scraped LinkedIn's nonzero junior signal (seniority_final known-entry share 6.7%; T30 J3 scraped share-of-known 17.2%). The cross-platform check weakens a pure LinkedIn-labeling-artifact explanation, but it does not solve unknown-seniority or source-composition problems.

    Surprises & Unexpected Patterns¶

    • Arshkon and scraped LinkedIn are more similar on known-seniority distribution than arshkon and asaniczka are, despite being different periods.
    • Top-50 employer overlap is highest for arshkon vs scraped LinkedIn, not for the two 2024 Kaggle sources.
    • Title vocabulary is so sparse that raw title-set overlap is mostly an instrument diagnostic, not a market-change measure.
    • Indeed's aggregate entry signal is close to scraped LinkedIn despite Indeed's much weaker seniority instrumentation.

    Downstream Action Items¶

    • Use arshkon, not pooled 2024, as the main native-label sanity baseline for entry-level claims.
    • Report every junior/senior claim under the T30 panel; do not rely on one seniority definition.
    • Use within-2024 calibration whenever comparing 2024 vs 2026.
    • For text analyses, control for description length and use description_core_llm only when llm_extraction_coverage = 'labeled'.
    • Treat company, title, geography, and industry composition as first-order confounds, not background metadata.

    T18 Cross-Occupation Boundary Analysis¶

    Headline finding¶

    The occupation boundary shifts, but it does not collapse. SWE-adjacent vs SWE TF-IDF centroid similarity stays high at 0.80 in arshkon, 0.83 in asaniczka, 0.82 in scraped March, and 0.82 in scraped April. The stronger change is not wholesale convergence; it is that AI-tool language, scope language, and description length all rise across SWE, adjacent, and control groups, with SWE still carrying the densest bundle.

    What we learned¶

    The cleanest SWE-specific signal is AI-tool language. In the cleaned-text frame, AI-tool share rises from 1.98% to 20.38% for SWE, from 2.45% to 18.14% for adjacent roles, and only from 1.25% to 1.41% for controls. The broader template signal is real too. Raw median description length rises from 2,974 to 4,891 characters in SWE, from 3,599 to 5,136 in adjacent roles, and from 3,096 to 4,130 in controls. Raw scope share also rises in all three groups, including controls. SWE still has the densest requirements bundle. In the cleaned-text frame, SWE posts have higher tech density than adjacent or control roles in both periods, and the combined requirement-breadth signal is highest in SWE. The migration vocabulary is more about analytic and coordination language than a hard coding/noncoding split. Terms like scientist, data scientist, data science, sql, analytics, model, communicate, and stakeholders move closer to SWE-adjacent roles. Seniority remains a measurement issue, not a clean boundary result. Explicit entry is tiny under seniority_final, while the YOE proxy is materially broader in every occupation group.

    What surprised us¶

    The control group is not a zero line. It also gets longer and somewhat more AI- and scope-heavy in the raw-text sensitivity tables. The centroid similarity is high rather than low. That means adjacent and SWE already share a substantial common vocabulary surface; the change is in density and emphasis, not a sharp semantic wall.

    Evidence assessment¶

    The AI-tool result is strong. It survives the cleaned-text frame, the raw-text sensitivity table, aggregator exclusion, and title_lookup_llm exclusion with only small movement. The scope and length results are moderate but real. They are visible in both cleaned and raw text, but the raw-text view makes the field-wide template shift clearer than the cleaned-text view. The similarity analysis is moderate. The similarities are stable and high, so the result is not a collapse story; it is a porosity story. The sample is company-capped at 25 postings per company per source-period for the boundary text work, which reduces domination by a few prolific employers.

    Narrative evaluation¶

    RQ1 weakens if it is framed as a pure SWE-only restructuring story. The data support a stronger alternative: SWE is being densified inside a broader technical-posting template shift. RQ2 strengthens, but the best framing is not “requirements moved once.” It is that AI/workflow language, scope language, and stacked requirements diffuse across adjacent technical roles while remaining densest in SWE. RQ3 is untouched by this wave.

    Emerging narrative¶

    The market is not converging into one occupation. It is converging on a more standardized way of describing technical work, and SWE sits at the dense end of that standardized surface. The paper should not claim collapse. It should claim differential densification: SWE changes most, but the surrounding technical labor market changes in the same direction.

    Research question evolution¶

    RQ1 should become: how much of the 2024-to-2026 change is SWE-specific densification versus field-wide posting-template evolution? RQ2 should ask which requirement bundles migrate across occupation boundaries, especially AI-tool language, scope language, and tech-stack adjacency. The old “junior collapse” reading should remain a secondary possibility, not the lead framing.

    Gaps and weaknesses¶

    The biggest gap is that cleaned-text coverage is thinner in scraped 2026, especially outside SWE. The similarity analysis is a snapshot comparison, not a continuous trajectory. It tells us about the shape of the language surface, not about the underlying work itself.

    Direction for next wave¶

    The next wave should connect this boundary recomposition story to mechanism checks. The highest-value follow-ups are ghost-forensics, employer-requirement divergence, and within-firm versus between-firm decomposition.

    Current paper positioning¶

    The paper is strongest as an empirical restructuring study with a posting-template caveat. If we stopped here, the lead result would be: SWE postings are not collapsing into adjacent roles, but the technical posting surface is standardizing around AI, scope, and denser requirements, with SWE still the densest bundle.

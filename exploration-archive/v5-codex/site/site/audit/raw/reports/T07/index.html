    T07 External Benchmarks & Power Analysis¶

    Headline finding¶

    Most of the planned cross-period analyses are statistically feasible, but not all are equally strong. The clear binding constraint is the entry-level comparison: 175 arshkon entry postings versus 1,042 scraped entry postings gives a standardized binary MDE of 0.229, which I would treat as marginal. By contrast, mid-senior, senior, all-SWE, and pooled-2024 comparisons are well-powered by the same 80% / 0.05 standard. The geographic benchmark is also reassuring: our state-level SWE distribution is very close to BLS software-developer employment geography, with Pearson r ≈ 0.97 in every slice I could benchmark locally.

    What we learned¶

    • The current default-filter LinkedIn corpus contains 1,186,281 rows and 59,972 SWE rows. The SWE sample is split into 4,691 arshkon, 18,129 asaniczka, and 37,152 scraped SWE postings.
    • Entry-level work is the tightest sample. 175 arshkon entry postings versus 1,042 scraped entry postings yields MDE_binary = 0.229 and MDE_continuous = 0.229, so entry-share claims are feasible but not comfortable.
    • Everything above entry-level is fine for first-pass work. Mid-senior, senior, all SWE, and pooled 2024 all fall in the well-powered bucket, with standardized MDEs between 0.066 and 0.024.
    • Metro analysis is feasible, but strict metro panels are narrow. After excluding multi-location postings, the arshkon 2024-04 slice has 18 metros with at least 50 SWE postings and 8 with at least 100. The combined scraped 2026-03+04 slice has 18 metros that clear 50 in both periods and only 8 that clear 100 in both.
    • The overlap panel for company fixed-effects work is usable. There are 237 companies with at least 3 SWE postings in both arshkon and scraped, and 122 companies that clear 5 in both. Using pooled 2024 instead of arshkon alone expands that to 572 companies at >=3 and 323 at >=5.

    What surprised us¶

    • The geographic fit is stronger than I expected. Even with incomplete state coverage in our own postings frame, the BLS comparison still lands around r = 0.97 for arshkon 2024-04, scraped 2026-03, and scraped 2026-04.
    • The sample-size bottleneck is narrow and specific. The rest of the planned comparison families are not merely “possible”; they are comfortably powered under standard assumptions.
    • The company-overlap panel is smaller than the raw source totals might suggest, but still large enough to support fixed-effects or within-company decomposition work. The >=5 threshold is the real pressure point, not >=3.

    Evidence assessment¶

    Analysis n_group1 n_group2 MDE_binary MDE_continuous Verdict
    Entry arshkon vs scraped 175 1,042 0.229 0.229 marginal
    Mid-senior arshkon vs scraped 2,064 15,534 0.066 0.066 well-powered
    Senior arshkon vs scraped 2,077 15,799 0.065 0.065 well-powered
    All SWE arshkon vs scraped 4,691 37,152 0.043 0.043 well-powered
    Pooled 2024 vs scraped 22,820 37,152 0.024 0.024 well-powered

    I am treating the MDEs as standardized effect sizes (Cohen's h for binary and Cohen's d for continuous). That avoids arbitrary baseline assumptions and is the right level of abstraction for a feasibility screen.

    External benchmark context¶

    The state-level BLS comparison is strong enough to use as context, not as causal validation. The current 2024 OEWS state table I could scrape cleanly surfaces 15-1252 Software Developers directly; the combined 15-1256 naming in BLS guidance is a nomenclature caveat rather than a separate current state row in the table I finished. On the 51-state BLS benchmark, the overlap with our postings frame is high enough to be useful, and the Pearson correlations are consistently around 0.97:

    Period States matched Pearson r
    arshkon 2024-04 47 0.9718
    scraped 2026-03 31 0.9747
    scraped 2026-04 29 0.9729

    That tells me the sample is not geographically bizarre. It does not validate any substantive trend, but it does make state-level comparisons less fragile than I expected.

    The industry labels in our own sample are also unsurprising in broad outline. The largest categories are IT Services and IT Consulting (1,202), Software Development (611), Financial Services (391), Staffing and Recruiting (271), and Information Services (150). That is directionally consistent with the BLS story that software-developer employment is concentrated in software-heavy and systems-integration-heavy industries, but the company-industry field is too noisy and uneven to treat as a formal benchmark.

    Benchmark caveats¶

    • I stopped the FRED/JOLTS download before completion, so there is no hiring-cycle figure in this run. That is an external-benchmark gap, not a data problem.
    • Metro counts exclude multi-location postings, as required, and the excluded SWE count in the current default-filter frame is 3,901.
    • The BLS state benchmark is complete enough for context, but it should not be over-read as a test of causality or representativeness beyond geography.

    Emerging narrative¶

    The data can support a real restructuring story, but only if the analysis is disciplined about sample size. The broad market-level comparisons are safe; the entry-level story is the one that needs the most care. The geography is reassuringly aligned with the BLS benchmark, which helps the paper’s external validity, but the strong result here is still the feasibility map: most planned analyses are usable, and the entry-level slice is the only one that should be treated as fragile.

    Direction for next wave¶

    • Treat entry-level claims as provisional and always pair them with the label-independent YOE proxy.
    • Proceed with seniority, AI-language, and company-overlap analyses; those are well-powered.
    • Use metro work with a >=50 exploratory threshold and reserve >=100 for the strictest cross-period comparisons.
    • Use >=3 company overlap as the primary panel definition and >=5 as the robustness cut.

    Current paper positioning¶

    If the paper stops looking at the feasibility layer and moves into substantive findings, it should not lead with geography or benchmark validation. The best role for T07 is as a guardrail: it says which later analyses are credible and which ones are thin. The analysis phase should lead with well-powered cross-period and within-company restructuring results, and keep entry-level claims carefully qualified.

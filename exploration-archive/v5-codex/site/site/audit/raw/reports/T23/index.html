    T23 Employer-Requirement / Worker-Usage Divergence¶

    Headline finding¶

    This is not a clean “employers outrun workers” result. Posting-side AI language is clearly elevated, but the sign of the divergence depends on the benchmark and the text specification. On the primary section-filtered LLM core frame, AI-tool mentions are 30.3% and any-AI mentions are 42.6%; on the raw full-text sensitivity those rise to 40.7% and 58.4%. Against Stack Overflow’s 2024 “AI-assisted tech at work” benchmark (32.4%), the primary AI-tool rate is slightly lower while the raw-text sensitivity is higher. Against 2025 usage benchmarks (51% daily, 84% use-or-plan), postings are below. The safest conclusion is benchmark sensitivity, not robust divergence.

    Methodology¶

    Primary analysis uses the same section-filtered LLM core frame as T22. I separate:

    • ai_tool: Copilot, Cursor, LLMs, LangChain/LangGraph, prompt engineering, fine-tuning, agents, and related tools
    • ai_domain: ML, DL, NLP, computer vision, generative AI, and related model-building language
    • ai_general: generic AI / machine-learning language

    I report rates by source, period, seniority, and aggregator status. For the benchmark comparison I use public worker-usage sources:

    • Stack Overflow 2024 Professional Developers
    • Stack Overflow 2025 survey
    • GitHub’s 2024 U.S. developer survey

    Primary sources:

    • Stack Overflow 2024 Professional Developers
    • Stack Overflow 2025 survey
    • GitHub 2024 U.S. developer survey PDF

    The Anthropic Economic Index is useful directional context, but it reports occupational usage composition more than a directly comparable developer adoption rate, so I treat it as background rather than a main benchmark line.

    What we learned¶

    • AI requirement language is real and large in the 2026 scrape. On the section-filtered LLM core frame, the weighted source-period AI-tool rates are about 12.3% in arshkon, 18.9% in asaniczka, 32.9% in scraped 2026-03, and 31.9% in scraped 2026-04.
    • Any-AI rates are higher: 15.1% in arshkon, 28.1% in asaniczka, and roughly 45% in both 2026 months.
    • The junior-vs-nonjunior split is weak. Under the YOE proxy, 2026 AI-tool rates are nearly flat across junior-like and non-junior postings (30.0% vs 32.1% in 2026-04; 34.6% vs 32.7% in 2026-03). That weakens a seniority-specific divergence story.
    • Aggregators are not the main source of AI inflation. Direct employers are slightly higher on all AI metrics than aggregators in the primary core frame.

    What surprised us¶

    • The raw full-text sensitivity pushes AI rates up materially. That means the question is not whether AI is present, but how much of the observed rate is coming from boilerplate and document structure versus core requirements.
    • The 2024 baseline is already heterogeneous. Arshkon is much lower than asaniczka on AI language, so pooled 2024 comparisons are not enough by themselves.
    • The benchmark choice matters more than the posting-side measurement error. Depending on whether you compare to 32.4%, 51%, 84%, or 99%, the divergence story changes from “slightly below” to “far below.”

    Evidence assessment¶

    • Posting-side AI growth: strong. The 2026 increase is large and survives the text-source sensitivity.
    • Outpacing worker usage: weak / not established. The sign is benchmark-dependent.
    • Seniority-specific divergence: weak. Junior-like and non-junior-like rows look broadly similar on AI rates in the YOE proxy.
    • Aggregator effects: moderate. They matter, but they do not drive the main pattern.

    Sensitivity checks¶

    • Aggregator exclusion: direct employers remain slightly higher than aggregators, but the gap is not large.
    • Text source: the raw full-text sensitivity raises both ai_tool and any_ai substantially. That is exactly why the divergence claim is not robust.
    • Seniority operationalization: the YOE proxy shows little seniority gradient, so a seniority-based divergence narrative is not well supported.
    • Benchmark assumptions: the 50/65/75/85 sensitivity band is useful, but the qualitative answer is already clear: the benchmark choice can flip the sign.

    Narrative evaluation¶

    RQ3 should not be written as “requirements outpace usage” unless we are willing to qualify it heavily. The data support a more cautious statement: employers are clearly signaling AI, but whether that exceeds worker usage depends on how you measure both sides. Under the strict core-text measure, the gap is not robust.

    Emerging narrative¶

    The market signal is better described as elevated AI signaling under measurement uncertainty, not a clear divergence from worker usage. The posting-side signal is high enough to matter, but not high enough to support an unqualified anticipatory-restructuring claim on its own.

    Research question evolution¶

    Revise RQ3 to:

    RQ3: How large is the employer-side AI requirement signal relative to plausible worker-usage benchmarks, and how sensitive is that comparison to text source and benchmark choice?

    That question is answerable here. The stronger “outpaces usage” claim is not.

    Gaps and weaknesses¶

    • The benchmark objects are not directly interchangeable with job-posting language.
    • The text-source choice matters more than it should.
    • We still do not know how much of the AI signal is screening requirement versus aspirational signaling.

    Direction for next wave¶

    Use T22’s ghost/aspirational results as the interpretive guardrail for T23. The next synthesis should say that AI language is visible and rising, but its labor-market meaning is benchmark-sensitive and partly templated.

    Current paper positioning¶

    T23 is now a validity and calibration section, not a headline divergence result. The strongest paper claim is not “employers are ahead of workers.” It is that AI hiring language is substantial, but the relationship to worker usage is fragile enough that the paper should not overstate it.

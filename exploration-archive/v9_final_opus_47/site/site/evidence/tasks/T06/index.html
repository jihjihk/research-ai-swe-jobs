    T06. Company concentration deep investigation¶

    Agent C, Wave 1. Generated 2026-04-20.

    Question. Does a handful of companies drive the aggregate patterns in each dataset? Which downstream findings are likely concentration-driven, and what mitigation does each need?

    TL;DR. - Per-source concentration is moderate. Non-aggregator HHI ranges 14 (arshkon) to 23 (scraped) on a 0–10,000 scale; Gini 0.40–0.68. Scraped is more skewed than arshkon despite having 3.5× more companies. - The top 5 non-aggregator employers explain 4.4% (arshkon), 5.8% (asaniczka), 7.1% (scraped) of SWE postings. No single source is dominated by any one employer: max company share is 1.2%–2.2%. - Entry-level posting is highly concentrated at the company level. Among companies with ≥5 SWE postings, 58% (arshkon), 62% (asaniczka), 63% (scraped) have ZERO entry-labeled rows under J3 (yoe_llm ≤ 2). Under J1 (seniority_final='entry'), 77–87% have zero. Entry-share is a minority-employer activity, not a market-wide one. Any 2024→2026 "entry-share decline" is about a specific sub-population of employers. - Within-vs-between decomposition (arshkon∩scraped panel, 125 companies with ≥5 postings each): - Under J3 (yoe_llm ≤ 2): total change +5.0pp is almost entirely BETWEEN-company (+5.3pp); within-company is zero (−0.03pp). The entry-share rise 2024→2026 is compositional — different companies are in the 2026 sample, not the same companies posting more entry roles. - Under J4 (yoe_llm ≤ 3): total change +7.5pp — again almost entirely between (+7.9pp); within is +0.2pp. - Under J1 (seniority_final='entry'): total change +0.06pp — within component −0.48pp, between +0.34pp. Direction flips between J3 and J1 but both are small magnitudes. - AI mention share: total change +39pp, within +31pp, between +14pp. Mostly within — same companies adding AI mentions. - Description length: total change +1752 chars, within +970, between +777. Mostly within-company growth. - Tech-token count: total change +0.99 tokens/post, within +1.69, between −0.29. Within dominates; between is slightly negative. - 206 companies flagged as "entry specialists" (any J1/J2/J3/J4 variant > 60%) across all sources. 148 of these are flagged exclusively by J4 (yoe_llm ≤ 3) — this confirms J4 is too loose as an entry threshold. 53 are exclusively J3-flagged; 3 exclusively J1-flagged. Tech giants (Amazon, AWS, Microsoft, Google) flag under J4 because median YOE for big-tech entry tiers is 3 years (quantized at 2, 5, 8 in the YOE distribution). Use J3 as the primary entry-specialist flag. - Duplicate-template audit essentially clean. Stage-4 multi-location dedup is working: max-dup-ratio ≥ 2 with n≥5 exists for only 2 companies in arshkon, 1 in asaniczka, 6 in scraped. The residual 6 in scraped are worth checking but do not change the corpus. - Aggregator profile. 9.1%–16.2% of filtered SWE postings per source are from aggregators. Aggregators have lower description length in arshkon/scraped (2780 vs 3359 chars; 3899 vs 5097 chars) but higher description length in asaniczka (4276 vs 3805 chars). Aggregator entry shares are lower than non-aggregator in all sources except arshkon J1. Aggregator share differs across sources (arshkon 9.1% → asaniczka 16.2% → scraped 17.0%), so aggregator-stratified analyses are warranted for any cross-source comparison. - Returning-companies cohort (arshkon∪asaniczka ∩ scraped): 2,109 companies. Accounts for 55% of scraped SWE postings (24,927/45,317) while representing 25% of 2026 unique companies. The other 6,368 "new entrant" companies in 2026 account for 45% of postings. Returning companies have slightly higher J3 entry share in scraped (15.3% vs 12.8% for new entrants), slightly longer descriptions (5121 vs 4615 chars).

    Read this carefully. Among companies with ≥5 SWE postings that have ≥1 LLM-labeled row, 58–63% have ZERO entry-labeled postings under J3 (yoe_llm ≤ 2). Under J1 (seniority_final='entry'), 77–87% have zero.

    Entry posting is a specialty activity. The story of "does entry share rise or fall" is a story about a minority subset of employers that do entry hiring. This has several implications downstream:

    1. Aggregate entry-share changes are driven by changes in that minority's behavior, not by a market-wide redistribution of hiring activity to more junior levels.
    2. Entry-share changes driven by composition of the subset (different firms in the flagged set) may look very different from changes driven by the same firms changing behavior. See the decomposition results in Step 5.
    3. A 1pp shift in aggregate entry share can represent a substantial change in entry-posting density among the minority of firms that post entry roles.

    Surprises & unexpected patterns¶

    1. Entry-level posting is a minority employer activity, not a market-wide one. 58–63% of ≥5-post companies have ZERO entry-labeled postings under J3. This reframes every aggregate "entry share X" claim as really a claim about a specific minority subset. The "entry share" metric in aggregate is a weighted average where most firms contribute 0 — it has high leverage from a small number of employers.

    2. Within-company entry-share change was roughly zero (2024→2026) in the overlap panel under J3. The +5pp aggregate rise is almost entirely between-company. This is genuinely important for Wave ⅔ — "the entry-share gap" is compositional, not behavioral for the same firms.

    3. J4 (yoe_llm ≤ 3) flags Amazon, AWS, Google, Microsoft, PNC, Discover Financial as "entry specialists" with 60–80% shares. The reason is that big-tech uses quantized YOE bands — Google scraped has 187 roles at yoe_llm=2, 139 at 5, 111 at 8 (bimodal); Microsoft scraped has 83 at yoe=2, 83 at yoe=4. A "3-year" cutoff lands right in the middle of the lower band. Do not use J4 as a primary entry criterion in downstream work. The task spec was wise to designate J3 as primary.

    4. AI mention rate 2024→2026 is mostly within-company (+31pp out of +39pp total). This is one of the cleanest "same employer, new behavior" patterns in the panel. If the AI-hiring-wave claim is going to hold up, this is the clean version.

    5. Duplicate-template audit is essentially empty after Stage-4 multi-location dedup. Only 6 companies in scraped have any residual dup-ratio ≥ 2 with n ≥ 5. This is the strongest evidence that the multi-location collapse in Stage 4 is working as intended. Multi-location flag carries 7.4% of scraped SWE rows and concentrates in Meta, Deloitte, CoreWeave, Turing — all plausible cases of legitimate cross-metro postings.

    6. Asaniczka HHI triples when aggregators are included (19 non-agg → 54 all). Jobs-for-Humanity→Capital One aggregator resolution adds 571 more postings to an already-large Capital One cohort. Asaniczka is more aggregator-dependent than the other sources.

    7. The returning-companies cohort is a tighter analytical frame. 2,109 companies account for 55% of scraped SWE postings. Restricting cross-period comparisons to this cohort mechanically controls for the "new-entrant dilution" and "departed-company selection" effects. This is exactly why T37 exists, and this artifact is ready to consume.

    8. "Jobs via Dice" alone (1,934 postings, 4.3% of scraped SWE) is the single biggest concentration lever in any corpus-level scraped analysis. Excluding aggregators removes it; the is_aggregator flag does this correctly in the unified artifact.

    9. New entrants in 2026 (6,368 companies) have broader company representation but shorter postings (4,615 vs 5,121 chars) and lower J3 share (12.8% vs 15.3%) than returning companies. A "new entrants are drifting toward non-entry roles" pattern is visible but weak.

    10. Salesforce (not listed in top-20) and other SaaS vendors are under-represented relative to expectation. Observed in the top-20 scan but not in the top-20 itself for any source. Worth noting for Wave 2 tech-ecosystem analyses — if Salesforce ecosystem titles matter, they will come from the long tail.

    Recommended default for Wave ⅔ aggregates¶

    For any corpus-wide aggregate claim 2024→2026, the default should be to present at least two of: - Raw aggregate (what you naively get). - Per-company weight-capped (n_per_co ≤ 50) or weighted by 1/sqrt(n_per_co). - Restricted to the returning-company cohort (n=2,109 companies). - With and without is_aggregator exclusion. - Under both J1 and J3 for any entry-related metric.

    If two variants agree, the finding is robust. If they disagree, report both and explain the driver (composition vs within-firm).

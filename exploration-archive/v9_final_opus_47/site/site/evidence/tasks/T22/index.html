    T22. Ghost & aspirational requirements forensics¶

    Agent: M (Wave 3) Date: 2026-04-20 Cohort: SWE LinkedIn English date_flag='ok', is_swe=true, from data/unified_core.parquet. n=48,634 postings (arshkon 4,687 + asaniczka 18,125 + scraped 2026-03+04 25,822). Defaults: J3 (yoe_min_years_llm <= 2) primary for junior; S4 (yoe_min_years_llm >= 5) primary for senior. Pooled-2024 baseline with arshkon-only co-primary for senior cells. T30 panel not reproduced in full (not a seniority-trend task); all seniority-stratified metrics report J3 / S4 / entry_label side-by-side instead.

    Headline findings¶

    1. Kitchen-sink score (tech_count × scope_kitchen_count) rose 2.6× at aggregate — 2024 pooled mean 6.72 → 2026 scraped 17.36. The median jumped from 0 to 8 (every posting now carries at least some scope × tech breadth). The rise is NOT a length artifact: normalized per 1,000 chars it is 5.93 → 13.32, because text length grew only +16% (1,020 → 1,183 chars) while tech_count grew +37% and scope_kitchen_count doubled. Senior postings carry the largest breadth (S4 2026 mean 21.6, median 10); junior postings increased from 6.2 → 11.5.

    2. YOE-scope mismatch rose 2.6× for the J3 cell (yoe ≤ 2 asking for ≥3 senior-scope terms): 2.9% (2024) → 7.5% (2026). Under the rule-ablation (yoe_extracted ≤ 2): 2.2% → 6.0%. The strictest flag (≥5 senior-scope terms) is rare (0.4% even in 2026) but didn't exist in 2024.

    3. Aspirational language rose NEAR AI tokens specifically. Within AI-mentioning postings in 2026, the aspiration share (hedging / (hedging + firm)) is 50.3% near-AI windows vs 28.2% far-from-AI text — a 22 pp gap. 2024 pooled had a similar pattern but with very thin AI content (n=214 AI postings; 100% near-AI hedging vs 51% far). Interpretation: AI requirements are being written more as "nice-to-have" / "preferred" / "bonus" than as firm requirements — they pattern like wish-list items, not hard qualifications.

    4. Aggregators are LESS ghost-like on ghost_llm_inflated (2024: 5.4% vs 6.3% direct; 2026: 7.1% vs 4.9% direct — aggregators now slightly MORE inflated in 2026), but have LOWER kitchen-sink scores (2026: 13.7 vs 18.1 direct), LOWER aspiration share (2026: 19.6% vs 25.3% direct), and LOWER yoe-scope-mismatch rates. Aggregators post shorter, more templated JDs with higher tech_count mean (7.6 vs 7.3). The aggregator-vs-direct axis is NOT the dominant driver of ghost-like patterns — ghost-likeness is a DIRECT-employer phenomenon in 2026, if anything.

    5. LLM ghost-assessment (ghost_assessment_llm) is REMARKABLY STABLE cross-period: 6.1% inflated (2024 pooled) → 5.3% (2026 scraped). Ghost-likely rate 0.003% unchanged. Entry-label postings have the highest inflated rate (13.3% in 2024, 5.5% in 2026) — the ghost-LLM is calibrated across periods but it has NOT risen. If anything, scraped 2026 postings are slightly LESS LLM-rated-inflated than 2024.

    6. Template saturation is RARE. Of 400 company-period cells with ≥10 SWE postings, only 2 flag at mean pairwise cosine > 0.8: Syms Strategic Group LLC (SSG) at 0.89 in 2024 (n=117) and 0.89 in 2026 (n=20) — a single consistent offender. The next-highest 2024 cell is KPMG US at 0.78 (n=61). Most companies do NOT copy-paste templates across SWE postings. The "everyone is posting the same ghost JD" narrative is not supported.

    7. Credential impossibility is NEAR-ZERO in cleaned text but slightly higher when using raw description for YOE. is_entry_label × yoe_rule_max ≥ 7 (from raw): 0.1% pooled-2024, 0.3% scraped-2026. Bound by a tiny denominator (entry-label SWE postings are rare: 177 arshkon + 238 asaniczka + 1,203 scraped). has_no_degree × has_MS co-occurrence is vanishingly rare (0.02% scraped, 0% 2024) — contradictory-credential ghost-ness is not a widespread pattern in this corpus.

    8. Pattern validation pipeline delivers 6 patterns at ≥0.92 precision and 1 at 1.00. All V1 rebuilt patterns pass 0.80 semantic threshold. The firm-requirement pattern was the only initial rebuild that failed (0.54) and was re-engineered to 1.00 precision (drop plain "requirements" document-noun, keep only unambiguous firm-qualification tokens).

    20 most ghost-like entry-level postings¶

    Full listing at tables/T22/top20_ghost_entry.csv. Pattern highlights:

    • 3 top-20 slots are PNNL / DataVisor / PayPal senior-backend AI roles mislabeled (by seniority_final) as entry due to sparse YOE signal in the LLM output. These are REAL senior scopes (architect / kubernetes / distributed systems) but pass the entry filter because yoe_min_years_llm ≤ 2 coincides with "experience not explicitly required" phrasings.
    • 4 identical Visa "Software Engineer, New College Grad — 2026" postings across 4 cities, asking for LLM fine-tuning / GPT integration / MLops familiarity. These are labeled seniority_final='entry' by seniority_rule (title-keyword "new college grad"). They carry 21 tech_count × 6 scope_kitchen_count = 126 kitchen-sink score. These are real roles. They are also genuinely ghost-like for a new-grad rung: the posting asks new grads for LLM fine-tuning, GPT integration, and MLOps exposure.
    • 2 identical Citian "Data Engineer" postings — same JD posted twice (both Software Development and Technology/Information/Internet industries). Architect pipelines, distributed systems, real-time dashboards required of an unlabeled/unknown-seniority candidate.
    • Nue.io "Full-Stack Software Engineer (New Grad)" — asks for AI-forward workflow, system design in distributed systems, billing integration expertise. New-grad labelled, senior-scope language.

    Interpretation: the "ghost entry" phenomenon is real but takes a specific form: new-grad / entry-labeled roles at late-stage tech companies asking for LLM / AI specialist skills that would typically require 3+ years of specialization. This is NOT mass-produced copy-paste ghost recruiting (template saturation disproves that) — it is a visible content-shift where AI skills are creeping into entry-level JDs as opportunistic wish-list items.

    Reading: - Ghost-LLM inflation did not rise on aggregator postings (5.4% → 7.1% — slight rise but within noise); direct-employer postings saw the ghost-LLM rate FALL (6.3% → 4.9%). - Aggregators post LOWER kitchen-sink / LOWER aspiration share — they are MORE BOILERPLATE, NOT MORE GHOST. Their templates are simpler, not padded with scope inflation. - Aggregators have LOWER yoe-scope-mismatch — this is important: the "entry posting asking for architect" phenomenon is a DIRECT-employer behavior, not a staffing-agency artifact. - Tech count is slightly higher at aggregators (7.6 vs 7.3 direct 2026), but scope language is much lower (1.56 vs 2.18). Aggregators list tech stacks flatly; they don't pad with "cross-functional stakeholder end-to-end ownership" scope language.

    Implication for Wave 3.5 and the paper: the "aggregator exclusion as sensitivity" default can be REVERSED here — aggregators are a CLEANER control than direct employers for ghost-like indicators. If the ghost-inflation concern is specifically about scope padding, aggregator-only subsetting would SHRINK the effect size rather than confound it. The dispatch preamble's note that aggregator-vs-direct is the PRIMARY axis is supported: it is the primary axis but the direction is that ghost-likeness is CONCENTRATED AT DIRECT EMPLOYERS, not at aggregators.

    Firm-pattern rebuild¶

    The T22 firm-pattern was iteratively rebuilt from an initial \b(must have|required|requirement(?:s)?|minimum|mandatory|...)\b (precision 0.54 — "requirements" as document-noun was 60%+ of matches) to a tightened version requiring unambiguous firm-qualification tokens (must have, must possess, must hold, required qualifications, required skills, required experience, minimum qualifications, minimum requirements, mandatory, at least N, etc.). Precision 1.00 on 50 samples. This is the primary firm-requirement pattern for Wave 3.5 T33 aspiration-ratio testing.

    Recommended (per dispatch): - None additional — aggregator-vs-direct is the only essential.

    Other sensitivities not covered: - Entry-specialist exclusion (Gate 1 pre-commit 5) — not re-run for this descriptive task; T22's indicators are not seniority-share claims. Flagged for Wave 3.5 T33 consumers. - ML-Engineer source stratification — not applicable (T22 does not make ML/AI prevalence claims; it measures aspiration/scope rather than AI-binary).

    Surprises and unexpected patterns¶

    1. Ghost-LLM inflated RATE DID NOT RISE cross-period. We expected scope inflation and kitchen-sink growth to push the LLM toward more "inflated" judgments. Instead the LLM-judged inflated rate FELL slightly (6.1% → 5.3%). Either: (a) the LLM classifier's concept of "inflated" is conditioned on 2024-style baselines it's been trained against (so 2026's now-norm of kitchen-sink breadth is not flagged as excess); (b) the LLM is correctly noting that 2026 JDs, while broader, pair that breadth with matching senior YOE — the broader JDs are for senior roles, and they're genuinely asking for senior breadth; © or the measurable ghost indicators we compute (kitchen-sink, YOE-scope mismatch) are orthogonal to what the LLM flags.

    2. Aggregators are the cleaner cohort. Our prior was that aggregators would be worse for ghost forensics. Reverse is true: aggregators post shorter, more templated, less scope-padded, less aspiration-heavy JDs. The ghost-like pattern is concentrated at direct employers and specifically at their senior / AI / architecture-heavy postings.

    3. AI tokens sit NEXT TO hedging language. The 50.3% vs 28.2% near-AI vs far-from-AI aspiration split in 2026 is a strong signal that AI requirements are being written as wish-list items, not firm must-haves. This is a novel operationalization that didn't exist in prior ghost-job literature and directly supports the RQ3 divergence narrative (employers are wishing for AI talent rather than demanding it).

    4. Template saturation is RARE in our corpus. Syms Strategic Group at 0.89 mean cosine is the only clear copy-paste offender. Major employers (Google 0.32, Infosys 0.29, Wells Fargo 0.24, Palo Alto Networks 0.24) all post highly distinct JDs per posting. This contradicts the "everyone is reusing templates" priors and it means scope-inflation findings cannot be dismissed as templating artifact.

    5. J3 yoe-scope-mismatch went from 2.88% → 7.45% (LLM primary). This is a 2.6× rise, equal in magnitude to the kitchen-sink mean rise. But the absolute rate is still small (< 10% of J3 postings). The headline "entry roles ask for senior scope" story is real but applies to ~7% of entry postings, not to the majority.

    6. "Senior-scope in entry" pattern is visibly AI-driven. Of the top-20 ghost-entry postings, the majority request LLM / GPT / RAG / MLOps knowledge of entry or new-grad candidates. This is a specific, documentable behavior — not generic scope creep, but a specific creep of AI specialization into entry JDs. It matches the within-company AI rewriting finding from T06 / T08: same firms, same level, much more AI.

    7. Credential-impossibility rates are genuinely low. Pre-T22 priors expected more visible contradiction (e.g., "10+ years for entry"). The rule-based YOE extraction from raw text shows it is rare (<0.5% even in 2026). The "10+ years for entry" ghost-job trope may be salient in media reporting but is NOT pervasive in LinkedIn SWE data. The headline stat needs different framing: it's AI scope creep, not impossible-YOE asks.

    8. Firm-pattern rebuild was the single-largest methodological issue. The initial pattern (capturing the word "requirements") had 0.54 precision because "requirements" is overwhelmingly used as a document-noun ("requirements gathering", "customer requirements") not as a firm-qualification marker. This is a concrete lesson for downstream tasks: cross-period text measures MUST validate that their regex captures the intended semantic. T22's pattern-validation pipeline catches this kind of failure systematically.

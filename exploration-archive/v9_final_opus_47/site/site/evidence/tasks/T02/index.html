    T02. Asaniczka associate as a junior proxy¶

    Agent: A, Wave 1 Date: 2026-04-20 Input: data/unified.parquet; subset is_swe AND source_platform='linkedin' AND is_english AND date_flag='ok' Output tables/figures: exploration/tables/T02/*, exploration/figures/T02/* Script: exploration/scripts/T02_asaniczka_associate_proxy.py

    Narrow question¶

    Asaniczka has zero native entry-level labels in seniority_native (only mid-senior and associate). Can its 101,093 LinkedIn associate-labeled rows (1,820 SWE LinkedIn default) be used as a junior proxy in analyses that need a 2024 baseline and cannot pool with arshkon?

    Verdict: NOT USABLE as a pooled entry proxy. Asaniczka associate fails the "at least 3 of 4 signals" decision rule — it matches arshkon entry on only 2 of 4 signals (top-title Jaccard and YOE distribution). It fails on explicit title cues (senior-cue rate too high) and on LLM re-read of descriptions (35% become mid-senior, far above arshkon-entry's 9%). The label has a broad seniority span that leans toward mid-senior.

    Downstream: do not pool asaniczka associate into a 2024 entry bucket. Use T30's J2 (combined entry+associate under seniority_final) or one of the YOE variants (J3: yoe_min_years_llm ≤ 2; J4: yoe_min_years_llm ≤ 3) instead.

    • (3) YOE distribution: like entry ✅ (mean, frac ≤2, frac ≤3 all closer to arshkon-entry than arshkon-mid-senior)
    • (4) LLM re-read: not like entry — asaniczka-associate descriptions get mid-senior 35% of the time under LLM re-read; arshkon-entry gets mid-senior 9% ❌

    2 of 4 signals match. Decision rule (≥3 of 4) fails. Verdict: not usable as a pooled junior proxy.

    Interpretation¶

    Asaniczka's associate label is a broad LinkedIn platform label that includes meaningful mid-level representation, not a "junior only" bucket. The YOE distribution (mean 4.44, median 4) and the LLM re-read (35% mid-senior) both point to a label universe that spans roughly 2–7 years YOE — somewhere between arshkon-entry (mean 4.21) and arshkon-mid-senior (mean 5.92), but leaning senior.

    Importantly, asaniczka associate is also not equivalent to arshkon associate — the arshkon associate label has a lower mean YOE (3.90 vs 4.44) and a lower senior-tail share (≥5 = 34.3% vs 49.5%). So even restricting to "label-matched associate" does not give a consistent 2024 2024 operationalization across the two Kaggle snapshots.

    Under the seniority_final schema, asaniczka still has 277 entry + 39 associate SWE rows in the LLM-labeled default frame. That's an n for entry-level of 277 in asaniczka and 177 + 15 = 192 in arshkon and 1,650 + 190 = 1,840 in scraped. The LLM does the entry-labeling work on asaniczka; the native label is just too coarse to substitute.

    Observations:

    1. Every 2024 entry-level row comes from the LLM. There are essentially zero title_keyword-sourced entry labels in Kaggle SWE — this is because Kaggle titles rarely carry the word "junior"/"entry"; the LLM is doing 100% of the entry identification. Scraped is different: 447 of 1,650 scraped entry rows (27%) come from title_keyword, reflecting more explicit junior titling in 2026 postings.

    2. Associate counts are tiny. arshkon associate n=15, asaniczka associate n=39. Neither is usable as a standalone cohort; combining entry+associate (T30 J2) is essential.

    3. J2 (entry+associate) effective sample sizes: arshkon 192, asaniczka 277, scraped 2026-03 = 874 (847+27; from T01 data), scraped 2026-04 = 872 (803+69). J3/J4 (YOE-based) will typically have larger N because they don't require the LLM to find an explicit label.

    4. Rule-based title_keyword entry-only effective sample size (scraped-only): 447 — the T30 J5 ablation "rule-only entry" is feasible for 2026 cross-checks but not for 2024 (both Kaggle sources have essentially zero title_keyword entry).

    Why the title cue signals are weak on Kaggle¶

    The extreme sparsity of junior/senior title cues in Kaggle-source data (0.22% junior in arshkon-mid-senior, 0.95% in arshkon-entry) was itself a surprise. Looking at the top titles:

    • arshkon seniority_native='entry' top 15: "software engineer" (52), "data engineer" (24), "developer" (23), "systems engineer" (19), "java developer" (18). Almost no explicit "junior" or "entry-level" in title.
    • arshkon seniority_native='mid-senior' top 15: "software engineer" (244), "data engineer" (82), "devops engineer" (53). Also no explicit "senior" in many titles.

    Kaggle snapshots use LinkedIn's platform-level seniority label (applied by employer selection in the posting flow), but titles are generic. This is different from explicit-title conventions in tech industry (Google, Amazon, etc.) and may reflect either smaller employers or different employer norms in January 2024. In scraped 2026 data, explicit-title share is much higher (e.g., seniority_native='entry' SWE in scraped has 226 junior-cue hits out of 5,247 = 4.3%, vs 0.95% in arshkon 2024).

    Implication for Wave 2+: title-based seniority heuristics are much more useful in 2026 scraped data than in 2024 Kaggle data, which could confound period-comparison findings. Be skeptical of any "explicit junior title % rose between 2024 and 2026" claim — it may be a source-selection artifact rather than a real market signal.

    Surprises & unexpected patterns¶

    1. Asaniczka associate is MORE senior-skewed than arshkon associate on YOE (≥5 share 49.5% vs 34.3%). This was unexpected — the expectation was that the two sources' associate labels would be comparable. They are not. Asaniczka's associate is a broader bucket spanning mid-level.

    2. 35% of asaniczka associate SWE descriptions are labeled mid-senior by the LLM, vs 21% of arshkon associate. Either asaniczka's platform labels are noisier in the junior→mid boundary or asaniczka's descriptions use more senior-signaling language in practice.

    3. Kaggle SWE titles are surprisingly generic. Only 0.8% of arshkon seniority_native='mid-senior' SWE titles contain senior / lead / etc. This is much lower than expected and much lower than 2026 scraped data. Either the Kaggle snapshots are drawing from a different employer universe (smaller companies?) or employers in 2024 labeled "mid-senior" without using senior words in the title. A direct check of same-title × same-source across 2024 → 2026 in Wave 3 would help disambiguate.

    4. Top-title Jaccard is low across the board. Even within-source same-seniority groups have Jaccard ~0.4–0.5; cross-source or cross-seniority drops to 0.28–0.43. The SWE title universe is fragmented — there is no dominant "top 50" that both sources agree on. This has implications for same-title cohort designs (T31): truly overlapping same-title cohorts may be small.

    5. Arshkon associate → seniority_final='entry' at 8%, which is higher than arshkon entry → seniority_final='entry' at 7%. In other words, the LLM re-reads arshkon descriptions and produces similar entry rates regardless of whether the native label was entry or associate. The native entry label is thus only weakly informative relative to what the description says — another argument against over-weighting seniority_native in cross-period comparisons.

    6. Asaniczka associate senior-cue rate (1.65%) is above arshkon-associate (1.14%) and about half of arshkon mid-senior (2.91%). This is consistent with asaniczka-associate being a broader bucket than arshkon-associate.

    Data quality caveats¶

    • All LLM-based signals are restricted to llm_classification_coverage='labeled' — deferred / not_selected rows are out-of-frame. For arshkon and asaniczka this is 99%+ of SWE, so the bias is minimal, but it does exclude a tiny fraction.
    • Top-title Jaccard uses top-50 by count. Different top-k choices would shift the values slightly but should not change the rank-order conclusion.
    • "Entry"-rate fractions should be interpreted as rates of LLM matching an explicit description signal, not actual entry-level prevalence. These are label-independent to the extent that they use raw text input to the LLM.

    Action items for downstream tasks¶

    1. T30 (Agent B) should carry a footnote that asaniczka associate is not a junior proxy, and compute every entry-level claim via the 4-row panel (J1/J2/J3/J4), treating J3 (yoe_min_years_llm ≤ 2 within the LLM frame) as the primary because it avoids the seniority_native asymmetry altogether.

    2. T19 / any cross-period junior trend (Wave 3) should not frame "asaniczka associate" as a proxy for 2024 junior. Either:

    3. Use arshkon-only 2024 baseline (sacrificing n), or
    4. Pool 2024 via yoe_min_years_llm ≤ 2 on all LLM-labeled 2024 rows (robust; recommended), or

    5. Use seniority_final='entry' pooled across arshkon+asaniczka (n=415, reasonable).

    6. T31 (Agent Q) (same-company × same-title drift) should be aware the SWE title distribution has low within-source Jaccard (~0.4–0.5 at top-50), so same-title cohorts will be small.

    7. All entry-level findings in Wave 2+ must report the T30 panel. Because 2 of 4 asaniczka-proxy signals agree and 2 disagree, any single-operationalization claim is fragile and could flip. The panel protects against that.

    8. Wave 3 should investigate why Kaggle SWE titles are generic (0.22% senior-cue in mid-senior) vs scraped 2026 (13% senior-cue in mid-senior, per cross-check of earlier data). This is a potential confound for "scope-inflation" claims that depend on title-level seniority reading.

    Essential sensitivities already in the tables¶

    • Rule-based YOE distribution (yoe_rule_distribution.csv) mirrors the LLM YOE distribution in shape, confirming the LLM isn't distorting the finding. Both extractors put asaniczka-associate (mean 4.44 / 4.51) between arshkon-entry (mean 4.21 / 4.18) and arshkon-mid-senior (mean 5.92 / 5.94). The LLM is systematically slightly higher on YOE mean for all cells but the relative ordering is identical.
    • Scraped-source comparisons are out of scope for this narrow T02 test but included in T01's summary; the same question for scraped seniority_native='associate' is best answered by T30's panel rather than a standalone proxy test.

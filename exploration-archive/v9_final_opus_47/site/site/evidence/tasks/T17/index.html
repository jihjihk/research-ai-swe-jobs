    T17 — Geographic Market Structure¶

    Agent J · Wave 3 · 2026-04-20

    Scope: SWE, LinkedIn-only, default filter. 68,137 SWE LinkedIn rows. Multi-location collapse (n=4,354) and null-metro-non-multi (n=14,184) excluded from metro rollups as specified. Total null-metro rows: 18,538 (27% of SWE frame).

    AI-strict = V1-validated. Scope = V1-validated. J3 = llm_classification_coverage='labeled' AND yoe_min_years_llm ≤ 2.

    TL;DR¶

    1. 26 metros have ≥50 SWE in both eras. Tech-hub vs non-tech-hub (5 vs 21) shows modest hub premium on J3 rise (+6.6 pp vs +4.0 pp) and AI-strict rise (+9.9 pp vs +8.6 pp) — both directions hub > non-hub, but gaps <2 pp.
    2. AI-strict metro deltas are highly above-noise (median SNR ≈ 10, max >250 for Chicago). Cross-period AI rise is not a tech-hub phenomenon — it's universal and uniform across metros (SD across metros = 3.1 pp; range 4.7–14.5 pp).
    3. J3 metro deltas are near-noise for ~half the panel. Within-2024 arshkon-vs-asaniczka median metro J3 abs-diff = 4.8 pp. Cross-period J3 rises above this bar: SF (+4.9), Seattle (+13.6), LA (+10.9), Portland (+11.6), Philadelphia (+11.5), San Diego (+14.9), Phoenix (+7.9), Nashville (+8.6). Below-noise or flat: Austin (+0.7), Atlanta (+0.4), Chicago (+1.3), Dallas (−0.3), Houston (−1.4), Charlotte (−2.7), Detroit (−5.1).
    4. Metro-level correlation AI Δ × J3 Δ is mildly NEGATIVE (Pearson −0.22, Spearman −0.15, n=26). Metros with bigger AI surges do NOT have bigger junior-share surges. The two phenomena are largely independent geographically — they don't co-concentrate.
    5. Metro-level correlation AI Δ × scope Δ is mildly positive (Pearson +0.29, Spearman +0.27). Weak coupling — AI rise and scope broadening partially co-move at metro level, but not tightly.
    6. Remote is a 2026 category, not a metro feature. 19.9% of 2026 scraped postings are is_remote=true with NULL metro_area (3,983 2026-03 + 5,024 2026-04). 2024 remote is ~0% (Gate 1 data artifact). Remote postings are AI-richer (11.1% vs 10.5% metro) and slightly less junior (13.1% vs 14.2% metro J3).
    7. Multi-location (n=2,373 2026) has the HIGHEST AI-strict (13.7%), HIGHEST J3 share (17.7%), HIGHEST scope terms (1.51) — multi-location postings are the "aggregator of aggregators" with distinct content profile. Since they're excluded from metro rollups by design, this is a side-channel finding.
    8. ML/LLM archetype geographic concentration rose in tech hubs (+8.8 pp Seattle, +4.4 pp SF — though SF already had 1.8% baseline) but also in unexpected places: Minneapolis +13.0 pp, Tampa Bay +11.4 pp, Portland +9.1 pp, Raleigh-Durham +8.6 pp. ML/LLM is spreading, not concentrating in hubs.
    9. Archetype HHI rose in most metros (archetype mix concentrated). Top HHI risers: Philadelphia (+0.164), Chicago (+0.153), Austin (+0.084), Minneapolis (+0.080), LA (+0.072). Suggests metro-level technology specialization. Seattle/SF HHI roughly flat.
    10. Aggregator-exclusion sensitivity: metro-level AI and J3 deltas are robust (<2 pp median shift). Company-capping at 20 postings per metro × era also robust. Findings are not driven by single large employers.

    2. Rankings¶

    2.1 J3 entry-share Δ (top and bottom 5)¶

    • Top: San Diego (+14.9 pp), Seattle (+13.6), Portland (+11.6), Philadelphia (+11.5), Los Angeles (+10.9)
    • Bottom: Detroit (−5.1 pp), Charlotte (−2.7), Houston (−1.4), Dallas-Fort Worth (−0.3), Atlanta (+0.4)

    Pattern: west-coast + northeast tech/pharma clusters gain, sun-belt/midwest manufacturing centers lose or flat. Detroit is the clearest decliner — 2024 was 25.9% J3 (auto-industry intern cycles); dropped to 10.2% in 2026.

    2.2 AI-strict Δ (top and bottom 5)¶

    • Top: Tampa Bay (+14.5 pp), Atlanta (+14.1), Miami (+12.9), Salt Lake City (+12.4), New York City (+11.6)
    • Bottom: Pittsburgh (+4.7), Detroit (+5.3), Columbus (+5.4), Denver (+5.7), San Diego (+6.0)

    Surprise: the AI rise leaders are Florida + UT + NYC, not SF/Seattle/Boston. SF (+11.2) and NYC (+11.6) are in the upper tier but not the leaders. Atlanta's +14.1 pp is a standout — from 1.0% to 15.1% AI mention in two years.

    2.3 Scope-term count Δ (top and bottom 5)¶

    • Top: NYC (+1.00 terms/posting), Chicago (+0.82), Charlotte (+0.74), Houston (+0.73), Salt Lake City (+0.72)
    • Bottom: Minneapolis (+0.36), Washington DC (+0.41), Dallas (+0.43), Philadelphia (+0.45), Denver (+0.47)

    NYC leads decisively on scope-language growth (V1 scope terms: ownership, end-to-end, cross-functional, initiative, stakeholder). Washington DC is at the low end — consistent with T09's observation that DC has the largest DoD-clearance cluster, which uses distinctive non-scope language.

    2.4 Description length median Δ¶

    • Top: Phoenix (+1,478), Seattle (+1,472), Philadelphia (+1,422), San Diego (+1,403), Detroit (+1,326)
    • Bottom: Salt Lake City (−100), Raleigh-Durham (+140), Miami (+313), Pittsburgh (+377), Denver (+440)

    Salt Lake City's slight median decline is notable — consistent with a shift to tighter-written, AI-focused JDs there.

    Artifact: exploration/tables/T17/metro_rankings.csv.

    4. Metro-level correlations¶

    X (Δ) Y (Δ) Pearson r Spearman ρ n
    AI-strict J3 share −0.22 −0.15 26
    AI-strict Scope terms +0.29 +0.27 26
    AI-strict Desc length (median) −0.37 −0.40 26
    AI-strict Tech count (median) +0.07 +0.05 26
    J3 share Scope terms −0.26 −0.38 26

    Key readings.

    • AI surge and J3 surge are NOT metro-coupled (r=−0.22). Phoenix, Atlanta, Tampa, Salt Lake have big AI surges with moderate J3 moves; Seattle/Philadelphia/San Diego have the biggest J3 surges with moderate AI moves.
    • AI surge and scope-term growth are weakly coupled (r=+0.29). NYC leads on both; Chicago and Salt Lake City also pair up.
    • AI surge negatively correlates with median description length growth (r=−0.37). Metros with the biggest median length growth (Phoenix, Seattle, Philadelphia) are middle-of-pack on AI. Possibly: description length growth is driven by boilerplate expansion and responsibilities expansion (per T13), which is not tightly coupled to AI adoption at the metro level.
    • J3 surge and scope-term growth negatively correlate (r=−0.26, ρ=−0.38). Metros with bigger J3 shares have LESS scope-term inflation. Consistent with T11/T16 finding that scope inflation is senior-led: metros with larger junior pools see less aggregate scope growth.

    Artifact: exploration/tables/T17/metro_correlations.csv.

    10. Surprises & unexpected patterns¶

    1. AI rise leaders are not SF/Seattle — they're Atlanta, Tampa Bay, Miami, Salt Lake City. SF is in the upper tier (+11.2 pp) but not the leader. This is consistent with T11's finding that financial-services and sun-belt enterprise firms dominate the 2026 AI-saturated outlier set. The tech-hub premium for AI mention is small (+1.3 pp mean vs non-hub); the absolute adoption is spreading fast into non-hub metros.
    2. J3 rise is concentrated geographically, AI rise is universal. J3 deltas range from −5.1 pp (Detroit) to +14.9 pp (San Diego) — 20 pp spread. AI deltas range from +4.7 pp (Pittsburgh) to +14.5 pp (Tampa Bay) — 10 pp spread, all positive. The two phenomena respect very different geographic patterns. Researcher intuition (and Gate 1 prior) that AI and junior-rise co-locate is falsified at metro level (r=−0.22).
    3. Detroit J3 DROPPED −5.1 pp. 2024 arshkon/asaniczka Detroit was 25.9%/12.8% J3 (auto-industry new-grad cycles); 2026 is 10.2%. This is the clearest "junior hiring in traditional-industry metros actually fell" finding in the panel. Detroit also has rising archetype concentration (HHI +0.029) and the largest tech-count median rise (+4 techs) — Detroit's 2026 postings are thicker (more techs per posting) but lower-junior.
    4. ML/LLM archetype concentration is regional-uniform, not hub-led. Minneapolis, Tampa Bay, Portland, Raleigh-Durham all gained more ML/LLM share than Seattle. Seattle's +8.8 pp is leading among major tech hubs but not the overall top. SF gained +3.8 pp — middle of the pack. The ML/LLM growth is geographically widespread, not a tech-hub concentration story.
    5. Archetype HHI rose in every metro measured — average +0.061 increase in concentration. No metro became more diversified across archetypes 2024→2026. Geographic complement to T09's domain-axis dominance: not only are archetypes the dominant structural axis, but individual metros increasingly specialize into their dominant archetypes.
    6. NYC was essentially absent from arshkon 2024 (n=67) but huge in 2026 (n=2,220). Interpreting NYC's +1.00 scope-term delta or +7.5 pp J3 delta depends on whether we weight by sample or by era-specific arshkon baseline. Asaniczka 2024 NYC n=2,123 — the pooled 2024 is comparable to 2026. Paper should cite NYC using pooled 2024 baseline.
    7. Washington DC has the lowest scope-term delta (+0.41) — DC is the DoD/clearance cluster center (per T09), which uses distinct "systems/clearance/requirements" language rather than scope-broadening V1 terms. Geographic coherence between archetype archetype and linguistic profile.
    8. Multi-location postings (n=2,373) are content-richest — highest AI, highest J3, highest scope. These are likely roles posted in multiple cities from aggregators/enterprise HR systems. Excluding them from metro rollups is methodologically correct but hides that a nontrivial chunk of the 2026 labor market is operating trans-geographically. Wave 3.5 should consider whether multi-location deserves its own analytical lane.
    9. Remote posting AI prevalence (11.1%) is NOT dramatically higher than metro AI (10.5%). Pre-Wave-3 prior was that remote postings would be AI-heavy (AI-native roles often remote). In practice it's 0.6 pp — not materially different. Remote is not a proxy for AI-forward.
    10. Chicago has off-the-chart AI SNR (255×) because its within-2024 arshkon-vs-asaniczka AI diff was 0.04 pp — both sources agree on Chicago's 2024 baseline AI mention within noise. Combined with a +9.3 pp cross-period rise, Chicago is the most calibrated-clean metro-level AI finding in the panel.

    11. Handoff notes¶

    To Gate 3 synthesis¶

    • AI rise is geographically uniform and above-noise everywhere. A paper that leads with AI doesn't need geographic stratification — the signal is robust at every metro. Highlight the uniformity as a contribution (vs hub-centric tech coverage narrative).
    • J3 rise is geographically heterogeneous and below-noise on ~half the panel. Claims about junior-share should be aggregate or cite the 5 above-SNR metros (SF, LA, Philadelphia, Denver, Seattle borderline). Detroit decline is a real finding.
    • Metro-level AI × J3 decoupling (r=−0.22) reframes RQ1/RQ3: AI-forward hiring and junior-heavy hiring are not the same geographic phenomenon. This supports the Gate 2 "scope inflation universal, senior > junior" framing — AI is an industry-wide content rewrite, junior hiring is concentrated in specific growth metros.
    • ML/LLM archetype geographic diffusion parallels T09's archetype-dominance finding: domain structure is nationally organized, not hub-concentrated. The tech-hub narrative for ML hiring is weaker than casual reading would suggest.

    To Wave 3.5¶

    • T31 (same-co × same-title drift): geographic heterogeneity in J3 suggests doing pair-level drift stratified by growth-metro vs decline-metro. Detroit same-co same-title drift is likely different in shape from Seattle's.
    • T37 (sampling-frame sensitivity): the NYC arshkon→scraped volume gap (67 → 2,220) is a sampling-frame issue with metro granularity. T37 should include a metro-level check.
    • T38 (hiring-selectivity × scope): scope_terms_delta per metro is available — can be correlated with metro-level 2026/2024 volume ratio as a hiring-pace proxy.

    To future work¶

    Multi-location (n=2,373 2026, 5.2% of SWE scraped) deserves its own cut. They're not metro, not remote, and they have the most distinctive content profile in the panel.

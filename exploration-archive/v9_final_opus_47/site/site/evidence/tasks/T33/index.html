    T33 — Requirements-section change as hiring-bar signal (H_B)¶

    Wave 3.5, Agent R. Run: 2026-04-20. Inputs: data/unified.parquet (SWE LinkedIn default filter, 68,137 rows), exploration/artifacts/shared/T13_readability_metrics.parquet, exploration/artifacts/shared/T11_posting_features.parquet, exploration/artifacts/shared/swe_archetype_labels.parquet, exploration/tables/T16/ panels. Scripts: exploration/scripts/T33_run.py, exploration/scripts/T33_simple_regex_classifier.py.

    Orientation: DISCOVERY.

    Headline verdict — REJECTED at classifier precision; primary finding is classifier sensitivity¶

    H_B (hiring bar lowering → requirements contraction correlates with lower YOE / credential / tech / education asks) is NOT SUPPORTED by either the T13 classifier or the simple-regex alternative. The two classifiers disagree on the SIGN of the period effect (T13: −1.8 pp; simple-regex: +3.1 pp, both with n=67,962 and HC3-robust p<1e−13). The hiring-bar proxy correlations are uniformly weak (|ρ|≤0.20) and DIFFER IN SIGN across classifiers — under T13 ρ(req_share, YOE)≈+0.05, under simple-regex ρ(req_share, YOE)≈−0.08. Within-company Δ(req)×Δ(J3) is essentially zero under both classifiers (r=−0.064 T13, +0.008 simple). The narrative 50-sample check found 0 postings with substantive requirement loosening language; 56% of the largest-contraction postings contained extensive technical-requirement language distributed into responsibilities sections. Under the T13 classifier the within-2024 arshkon/asaniczka share gap (0.026) is as large as the cross-period gap (0.025) — SNR 0.97 — so even the headline direction is at calibration-noise.

    What survives cleanly: the hypothesis that requirements-section size is a hidden-hiring-bar thermometer is rejected. Requirements shrinkage in T13 is NOT correlated with lower explicit YOE/credential/tech/education asks in 2026. The "shrinkage" is more credibly narrative reallocation — with heavy section reclassification noise — than a substantive hiring-bar shift.

    1. Two-classifier comparison — period means + within-2024 calibration¶

    File: tables/T33/section_classifier_comparison.csv, tables/T33/within_2024_calibration.csv.

    Classifier 2024 mean req_share 2026 mean req_share Δ share 2024 mean req_chars 2026 mean req_chars Δ chars
    T13 0.2380 0.2126 −0.0254 887 984 +98
    simple_regex 0.2244 0.2773 +0.0529 926 1,430 +503

    Direction FLIPS between classifiers on both share and chars. The simple-regex classifier attributes more of the raw text to "requirements" in 2026 because scraped postings preserve markdown bold headers that the simple classifier picks up cleanly, while it misses many of the compound / un-headered 2024 sections that T13's richer phrase catalog catches.

    Within-2024 calibration (SNR on share)¶

    Classifier within-2024 share gap (arsh vs asan) cross-period share gap SNR
    T13 0.0261 0.0254 0.97
    simple_regex 0.0502 0.0529 1.05

    Both classifiers are at/below the within-2024 calibration floor. The cross-period share shift is not distinguishable from instrument noise under either operationalization. This is a stronger form of V1's "T13 J3 requirements shrank flips under alt classifier" finding: the aggregate shift is also classifier-flipping AND at-noise under each classifier.

    2. Period-effect regression¶

    File: tables/T33/period_regression.csv. Model: req_section_share ~ period_2026 + C(seniority_final) + C(archetype) + is_aggregator_int + log_desc_len + period_2026×seniority_final + period_2026×archetype. HC3 robust SEs; baseline seniority = mid-senior.

    Classifier Subset n period_2026 coef 95% CI p
    T13 full corpus 67,962 −0.0193 [−0.0241, −0.0145] <1e−13
    simple_regex full corpus 67,962 +0.0296 [+0.0235, +0.0357] <1e−13
    T13 LLM frame only 48,277 −0.0219 [−0.0270, −0.0168] <1e−16
    simple_regex LLM frame only 48,277 +0.0301 [+0.0235, +0.0367] <1e−18

    Interpretation: - Both classifiers produce tight, significant, but OPPOSITELY-SIGNED coefficients. - Adjusting for seniority, archetype, aggregator status, and description length does not close the gap. - Restricting to the LLM frame (as a text-source sensitivity) does not change signs — so the flip is not an llm_cleaned artifact. - Raw pooled 2024→scraped T13 Δ of −2.5 pp attenuates to −1.9 pp after covariate adjustment; a 25% reduction — some of the raw decline is composition-driven (aggregator mix, seniority mix) but most survives after covariate adjustment. Same thing holds for simple-regex at +5.3 pp raw → +3.0 pp adjusted.

    3. Hiring-bar proxy correlations within 2026 scraped¶

    File: tables/T33/hiring_bar_correlations.csv. Spearman and Pearson correlations of req_section_share with YOE (LLM), credential_stack_depth (T11), tech_count (T11), education_level (T11), within the scraped 2026 labeled-only subset.

    Subset Classifier ρ(req, YOE) ρ(req, stack) ρ(req, tech) ρ(req, edu)
    all 2026 scraped T13 +0.050 +0.074 +0.079 +0.028
    all 2026 scraped simple_regex −0.077 +0.134 −0.011 +0.194
    J3 (YOE≤2) T13 −0.088 +0.007 +0.034 −0.082
    J3 (YOE≤2) simple_regex +0.049 +0.122 −0.074 +0.277
    S4 (YOE≥5) T13 +0.042 +0.039 +0.066 +0.019
    S4 (YOE≥5) simple_regex −0.023 +0.123 −0.031 +0.171

    Read: all correlations are small (|ρ|≤0.28). Several correlations flip sign across classifiers. Under the hiring-bar-lowering hypothesis we would expect a UNIFORM set of positive correlations (more requirements section → higher bar); instead we see a mixed pattern that depends on what the classifier counts as requirements.

    The single correlation that is directionally consistent and moderately strong across classifiers is ρ(req_share, credential_stack_depth), at +0.07 (T13) / +0.13 (simple-regex). Postings with more requirement section text do tend to have slightly more credential categories listed. But the magnitude is too small to support hiring-bar interpretation at meaningful scale.

    Figure: figures/T33/hiring_bar_correlation_heatmap.png.

    4. Within-company cross-metric test (arshkon∩scraped overlap)¶

    File: tables/T33/within_company_scatter.csv.

    Companies with ≥5 SWE postings in BOTH periods: n=356. Per-company Δ:

    Classifier corr(Δ req_share, Δ J3 share) n
    T13 −0.064 356
    simple_regex +0.008 356

    Neither correlation is meaningfully different from zero. Firms that contracted their requirements sections are NOT the same firms that increased their J3 (YOE≤2) share. Hiring-bar-lowering at the within-firm level is not supported.

    This is consistent with the Gate 2 finding (V1-corrected T16) that within-firm J3 rise is subsample-specific and small; and with T28's finding that J3 entry-share rise is within-domain rather than within-firm. The mechanism that moves J3 share within firms (if it exists) is not the same mechanism that shapes requirements-section prose.

    Figure: figures/T33/within_company_scatter.png.

    5. Alternative-explanation check: Δ(req_share) vs Δ(desc_length)¶

    File: tables/T33/alt_explanation_deltalen.csv.

    Classifier corr(Δ req_share, Δ desc_length) corr(Δ req_chars, Δ desc_length) n companies
    T13 −0.113 +0.322 356
    simple_regex +0.016 +0.379 356

    Read. Under both classifiers, Δ(req_CHARS) — absolute requirements length — is positively correlated with Δ(description length) at r≈+0.35. Companies whose descriptions grew also grew their requirements. The SHARE declined because OTHER sections grew faster (responsibilities, benefits, about, legal — per T13 absolute terms). This supports the "narrative expansion dominating" framing over the "hiring bar lowering" framing: requirements are not shrinking in absolute terms; they are getting smaller as a share of a longer posting.

    Under the simple-regex classifier, Δ(req_share) is barely correlated with Δ(desc_len) (+0.02). Under T13 it is weakly negative (−0.11) — so T13 partially sees share shrinkage as disproportional. But the correlation is weak in both cases, and both confirm that any "shrinkage" is RELATIVE, not absolute.

    6. Narrative 50-sample classification¶

    File: tables/T33/narrative_sample_50.csv. Sample: 50 2026 scraped postings with largest requirement-share contraction relative to the same (company, title) 2024 mean (fallback to company mean if exact title pair missing). Filter: raw length ≥ 1000 chars. Classification is heuristic (regex-based keyword-count decision tree) — not an LLM adjudication — so precision is rough but directionally informative.

    Class n share Decision rule
    a) Tech-req migration to responsibilities (≥5 tech keywords) 28 56% Technical-requirement language present but distributed in responsibilities/summary
    b) Culture/benefits expansion (≥4 benefit keywords, ≤3 tech) 15 30% Heavy perk/benefits language; thin technical content
    c) Substantive requirement loosening 0 0% No matches for "no degree required", "self-taught OK", "entry-level welcome", "0+ years", etc.
    d) Other 7 14% None of the above patterns

    Read. Zero of the 50 largest-contraction postings contain explicit requirement-loosening language. 56% contain extensive technical language that the T13 classifier attributed to non-requirements sections (responsibilities, summary). 30% are dominated by benefits / perks / culture expansion. This is a directional signal that T13 requirement shrinkage is narrative-distribution, not hiring-bar-lowering — and the simple-regex's directional opposite view is not artifactual either; it likely picks up the tech content that T13 re-attributed.

    Inspection of exemplars: - Many Capgemini / Actalent / aggregator postings have large benefits / disclaimers blocks that dilute the requirements section's share to ~0. - The "requirements=0 in 2026" tail is dominated by aggregator-style postings; aggregator-exclusion in section-classifier comparisons (not reported as a separate run here due to dim-a coverage in T13's parent run) would likely shrink the magnitude of the flip in both directions.

    7. Essential sensitivities (dimensions per spec)¶

    • (a) Aggregator: not re-fit as a standalone comparison row here (T13 already reports aggregator sensitivity: excluding aggregators moves req share by <0.3 pp). The narrative-sample result showed aggregator-style culture-heavy postings are the largest contributors to the extreme-contraction tail.
    • © T30 panel: hiring-bar correlations reported separately for J3, S4, J2, S1 in tables/T33/hiring_bar_correlations.csv. The weakness of the correlations is uniform across cells.
    • (d) Text source: regression re-fit with llm_extraction_coverage='labeled' subset only (n=48,277); direction and magnitude for both classifiers match the full-corpus run (T13 −0.022 vs full −0.019; simple +0.030 vs full +0.030). Flipping is NOT a text-source artifact.
    • (f) Within-2024 calibration: done in §1. T13 share SNR 0.97, simple-regex share SNR 1.05 — both at noise. Point estimates should not be cited as "real" — only direction, and direction disagrees.

    8. Surprises and unexpected patterns¶

    1. The T13 vs simple-regex direction flip for aggregate req_share is even larger than V1's J3-only flip. V1 showed the direction flips for J3 chars. T33 shows it flips for aggregate share across the full corpus, across all seniorities, and survives covariate adjustment. Classifier-sensitivity is not a J3-specific issue — it is a property of how requirement-section boundaries are drawn in longitudinal posting studies.

    2. Within-2024 SNR of req_share under T13 is 0.97 — essentially at calibration-noise for the aggregate shift. This was not obvious from T13's report, which characterized the shift as "below noise" only at the chars level (SNR 0.45); at the share level the cross-period signal is only marginally larger than within-2024 noise. Under-reported in T13 and flagged here.

    3. Regression diagnostics have very low R² (0.008 T13, 0.034 simple-regex). Requirement-share is extraordinarily noisy at posting level — almost none of the variation is explained by period, seniority, archetype, aggregator status, and length combined. This is a posting-level property, not a research-methods weakness.

    4. ZERO of the 50 largest-contraction postings contain explicit requirement-loosening language. The hiring-bar-lowering hypothesis predicted some non-zero fraction of postings with "no degree required", "0+ years", "self-taught OK", etc. We found none in a 50-row random-tail sample. H_B via literal textual loosening is firmly rejected.

    5. Δ(req_chars) positively correlated with Δ(desc_length) at r≈+0.35 across classifiers. Absolute requirements content GREW when overall posting length grew. The "shrinkage" is relative only. This is the single most interpretable result from T33 and it weakens the case for hiring-bar-lowering to "narrative expansion dominating." If anything, employers ask for more stuff in 2026 — they just wrap it in more prose.

    6. J3 subset hiring-bar correlations under T13 are all small-negative or small-positive. If H_B were supported at the junior tier, we would expect ρ(req_share, YOE) > 0 (more requirements → higher YOE asks, within J3 still) and ρ(req_share, credential) > 0 strongly. We see ρ(req_share, YOE) = −0.09 T13 / +0.05 simple, and ρ(req_share, credential) = +0.01 T13 / +0.12 simple. Modest positive under simple-regex; near-zero under T13. The story for juniors specifically is weak.

    7. Under simple-regex, education_level has the strongest correlation of any proxy (ρ=+0.19 all, +0.28 J3). This is interesting in isolation — postings with bigger requirement sections do mention more education credential language — but the magnitude is small and the pattern doesn't appear under T13.

    8. Director-level (S2) and associate-level analyses were not reported because the cells are too thin for proxy correlations with meaningful power (n~300 for directors post-labeled-filter). S1 (mid-senior + director) was run and looks similar to S4.

    9. Verdict on H_B¶

    Hiring-bar-lowering via requirements-section contraction: REJECTED at the level of rigor T33 was dispatched to test.

    Why rejected (not "partial"): - Period-effect direction flips between classifiers. - Within-2024 noise is as large as cross-period signal under both classifiers. - Hiring-bar proxies correlate at |ρ|≤0.28, with some sign flips between classifiers. - Within-company Δreq × ΔJ3 ≈ 0 under both classifiers. - Narrative 50-sample check found 0% explicit loosening language. - The direction that survives most cleanly is that absolute requirements chars track description-length growth (r≈+0.35), suggesting narrative reallocation rather than substantive content contraction.

    What T33 DOES contribute: - The classifier-sensitivity footnote in Gate 2 becomes a first-order finding at the aggregate level, not just J3. Any longitudinal posting study that reports "requirements shrank" needs to report both a compound-phrase classifier (like T13) and a simple-header classifier, and adjudicate explicitly. - Within-2024 SNR for req_share is 0.97 under T13 — below noise. Previous T13 citation was chars SNR 0.45; share SNR is comparable. This strengthens the Gate 2 demotion. - Requirements-section size is not a hiring-bar thermometer. It is primarily a narrative-structure variable influenced by markdown preservation, aggregator prose conventions, and recruiter style.

    Paper implication: the "junior requirements shrank" line from T13 should be dropped from lead claims; the responsibilities +49% and benefits +89% findings stay (they are classifier-independent because they are dominated by sections whose within-2024 calibration is clean per T13 §5). Section-anatomy evidence remains useful as a description of posting FORMAT evolution, not hiring BAR evolution.

    Caveats¶

    1. The simple-regex classifier is designed to LOSE recall on compound / non-standard headers. It is not a better classifier than T13; it is a minimally-overlapping sensitivity probe. T13 is clearly the more faithful classifier on the underlying text. The useful fact is that two reasonable operationalizations disagree — not that one is correct and the other wrong.
    2. Narrative-50 classification is regex-heuristic, not LLM adjudication. The 0% figure for requirement-loosening is robust because the regex is narrow (explicit "no degree"/"self-taught"/"0+ years" phrasing). Postings with subtle loosening (e.g., "degree or equivalent experience") would slip through — but those are borderline cases, not Tier-1 loosening.
    3. Within-company corr ≈ 0 includes noise from the 28 companies with NaN J3 in company_change_vectors.csv. Using T13's raw metrics re-computed here gives a clean n=356. The near-zero correlation is robust to panel choice (arshkon-only min-5 subset also near-zero; not reported separately).
    4. Classifier precision was not independently validated. T13 has documented high-recall, mixed-precision character (per T13 §caveats). The simple-regex classifier has even lower recall by design. Adjudicating "which is more correct" requires human-adjudicated section labels on a sample, which is out of scope.

    Headline claims for SYNTHESIS¶

    1. Requirements-section contraction is NOT a hiring-bar-lowering signal. — evidence: T33 hiring-bar correlations |ρ|≤0.28 mixed-sign across 2 classifiers; narrative 50-sample 0% loosening language; Δreq_chars positively correlated with Δdesc_len at r≈+0.35. sensitivity verdict: robust across classifier choice and within-company / cross-section frames. This demotes any "junior hiring bar is falling" inference from T13's requirement-share decline.

    2. The aggregate requirements-share direction is CLASSIFIER-DEPENDENT: T13 −2.5 pp vs simple-regex +5.3 pp; adjusted period coefficient −0.019 vs +0.030 (both p<1e−13). — evidence: T33 period regression table. sensitivity verdict: flagged — both classifiers reasonable; neither is ground truth. Longitudinal requirements-share claims need dual-classifier reporting.

    3. Within-2024 calibration SNR for req_share under T13 is 0.97 — at calibration-noise floor for the aggregate shift (not just J3). — evidence: T33 within-2024 calibration table. sensitivity verdict: robust — corroborates Gate 2 demotion and extends it from J3-specific to aggregate.

    T05. Cross-dataset comparability¶

    Agent C, Wave 1. Generated 2026-04-20.

    Question. Are the three LinkedIn datasets (kaggle_arshkon, kaggle_asaniczka, scraped) measuring the same underlying labor market, or are their differences artifacts of data collection?

    TL;DR. - All three pairs differ significantly on every dimension, but the arshkon↔asaniczka gap (both 2024, same platform, different snapshots) is roughly the within-period noise floor. The 2024↔2026 (scraped) gaps consistently sit well above that floor on length and geography, and well below it on seniority. - Description length is the most differentiated signal. KS D = 0.32 for arshkon↔scraped (non-agg), vs 0.11 for arshkon↔asaniczka. Scraped postings are ~1750 chars longer on average than 2024 arshkon. - Seniority_final distributions differ mainly between 2024 sources and asaniczka. arshkon↔scraped Cramér's V = 0.022 (trivial); asaniczka↔scraped V = 0.117 (moderate). Asaniczka is the odd one out on seniority — 97% mid-senior — reflecting the known zero-native-entry gap, not a real labor market fact. - Geographic distributions differ substantially (V ≈ 0.27–0.34 between 2024 and scraped). California share jumps from 17% to 25% in scraped. Mostly an artifact of the scraper's metro-weighted search design, not a 2024→2026 geographic shift. - Company overlap is low everywhere. Pairwise Jaccard ranges 0.10–0.13. The "measured-market" defined by these three sources is mostly disjoint at the company level. Top-50 employers are more stable (Jaccard 0.10–0.28). - Title vocabulary overlap is even lower — raw Jaccard 0.03–0.05. Filtering to titles with ≥3 occurrences raises overlap to 0.10–0.25. - Platform labeling stability test (novel finding). Within the same 20 most-common SWE titles, the seniority_native mid-senior share rises systematically from 2024 arshkon to 2026 scraped — often from ~50% to ~75%. YOE means are roughly stable across the same cells. This is the platform's own labeling drifting toward mid-senior, not the market changing underneath. The entry-share drop in native labels 2024→2026 is partly a platform relabeling artifact. - Indeed cross-validation supports the platform-artifact reading. On the same 20 titles, scraped-Indeed entry indicators (rule-based yoe_extracted ≤ 2) track LinkedIn-scraped reasonably closely, suggesting the 2026 entry-share number is not a LinkedIn-only artifact. - Aggregator sensitivity is small. Excluding aggregators moves Cramér's V numbers by at most 0.03 and never flips a sign. Aggregator exclusion does not save any comparison. - Trustworthy comparisons (in order): YOE means per title > seniority_final distributions (mid/senior only) > description length (interpret carefully). Least trustworthy: geographic distributions (scraper design artifact), seniority_native-based trends (platform drift), raw title vocabulary (long-tail noise).

    Scripts: exploration/scripts/T05_cross_dataset_comparability.py, exploration/scripts/T05_figures.py. Tables in exploration/tables/T05/. Figures in exploration/figures/T05/.

    Data scope and filters¶

    SWE (is_swe = true), LinkedIn (source_platform = 'linkedin'), is_english = true, date_flag = 'ok'. For the top-20 title stability test (step 9), Indeed is included as a cross-platform sensitivity for scraped only.

    Filtered SWE counts on LinkedIn:

    source total LinkedIn LinkedIn SWE filtered non-agg agg multi-loc
    kaggle_arshkon 110,619 4,691 4,261 430 116
    kaggle_asaniczka 892,554 18,129 15,186 2,943 894
    scraped 281,008 45,317 37,609 7,708 3,344

    Indeed SWE filtered: 14,245 (scraped only).

    Summary effect-size table (non-aggregator)¶

    source_a source_b desc_len_ks_D company_jac top50_co_jac seniority_V state_V title_jac
    arshkon asaniczka 0.115 0.108 0.099 0.127 0.142 0.051
    arshkon scraped 0.321 0.103 0.220 0.022 0.269 0.026

    (Table: effect_size_summary.csv; aggregator sensitivity: agg_sensitivity_effect_sizes.csv.)

    Baseline noise floor (within-2024 calibration — arshkon vs asaniczka): these two are both 2024 LinkedIn snapshots 3 months apart from different scrapers. The values in that row set the expected "same market, different snapshot" magnitudes. Anything substantially above those numbers in 2024↔2026 comparisons is in principle candidate real change + artifact; anything similar or below is within the noise floor.

    Step-by-step findings¶

    Step 1. Description length¶

    KS test, all three pairwise, non-aggregator:

    pair n_a n_b mean_a mean_b D p
    arshkon vs asaniczka 4,261 15,186 3,359 3,805 0.115 ~0
    arshkon vs scraped 4,261 37,609 3,359 5,097 0.321 ~0
    asaniczka vs scraped 15,186 37,609 3,805 5,097 0.252 ~0

    Within-2024 mean gap ≈450 chars. 2024→2026 gap ≈1,300–1,750 chars. The 2024→2026 shift is large relative to the noise floor — it is not purely a scrape-design artifact, because arshkon and asaniczka also differ from each other substantially below the 2024↔2026 gap.

    Artifact diagnosis. Part of the scraped excess is likely LinkedIn's own boilerplate growth (disclaimers, EEO/DEI blocks, benefits boilerplate); the rest plausibly reflects job-content inflation. Downstream analyses must use description_core_llm (LLM-cleaned text) rather than raw description when measuring content — T05 shows the raw length signal is a mix of content and boilerplate.

    Figure: figures/T05/description_length_hist.png.

    Step 2. Company overlap (Jaccard)¶

    Pairwise Jaccard of company_name_canonical sets (non-aggregator):

    pair n_a n_b intersect union Jaccard top-50 Jaccard
    arshkon vs asaniczka 2,235 4,814 687 6,362 0.108 0.099
    arshkon vs scraped 2,235 7,740 932 9,043 0.103 0.220
    asaniczka vs scraped 4,814 7,740 1,461 11,093 0.132 0.163

    Key observation. The arshkon↔scraped top-50 Jaccard (0.22) is actually higher than arshkon↔asaniczka top-50 (0.10) — despite being 2 years apart. This is because the top-50 in each LinkedIn snapshot is dominated by persistent large employers (Amazon, Capital One, Microsoft, Google, Meta), and the arshkon top-50 is a reasonable snapshot of those. Asaniczka's top-50, however, is dominated by aggregator-like names (ClearanceJobs, Jobs for Humanity, ClickJobs.io) that are almost absent from scraped.

    Step 3. Geographic (state-level chi-squared)¶

    Non-aggregator, multi-location rows excluded:

    pair n_states total_a total_b chi2 V
    arshkon vs asaniczka 50 3,152 14,129 347 0.142
    arshkon vs scraped 43 3,142 32,166 2,559 0.269
    asaniczka vs scraped 50 14,129 32,166 5,403 0.342

    California share: arshkon 16.6%, asaniczka 16.8%, scraped 25.1%. The +8 percentage-point California jump is the single largest driver of the state-chi2. WA (+3pp), NY (+3pp), MA (+2pp) also move up; FL (-2.5pp), OH (-1.4pp), MI (-0.5pp) move down.

    Artifact diagnosis. The scraper's metro-targeted search design over-represents tech-heavy metros (per docs/data-sources-and-prompts.md). This is almost certainly a scrape design artifact, not a 2024→2026 geographic re-concentration of SWE hiring. Any geographic finding from the T05–T38 pipeline must not interpret raw scraped state shares as a national measure — either restrict to per-metro analyses (where the frame is stable) or use arshkon/asaniczka as the market-wide benchmark.

    See state_shares.csv; figure state_shares_top15.png.

    Step 4. Seniority (seniority_final)¶

    Non-aggregator, excluding unknown:

    source entry associate mid-senior director n_labeled
    kaggle_arshkon 8.1% 0.7% 90.4% 0.8% 2,051
    kaggle_asaniczka 2.2% 0.4% 96.8% 0.6% 9,608
    scraped 7.5% 0.6% 90.3% 1.6% 18,960

    Chi-squared (non-aggregator):

    pair chi2 dof p V
    arshkon vs asaniczka 187.5 3 ~0 0.127
    arshkon vs scraped 10.1 3 0.018 0.022
    asaniczka vs scraped 391.5 3 ~0 0.117

    Finding — arshkon and scraped are effectively interchangeable on seniority_final distribution. Cramér's V = 0.022 is trivial. Asaniczka is the outlier (entry share 2.2%, pulled down by zero native entry labels), consistent with its documented caveat.

    Bar chart: figures/T05/seniority_final_bar.png.

    Step 5. Title vocabulary Jaccard¶

    Raw Jaccard is driven by long-tail uniqueness:

    pair non-agg, min_count=1 min_count=3
    arshkon vs asaniczka 0.051 0.245
    arshkon vs scraped 0.026 0.102
    asaniczka vs scraped 0.037 0.185

    With the min_count ≥ 3 filter, arshkon↔asaniczka Jaccard (0.245) is ~2.4× the arshkon↔scraped Jaccard (0.102). This is larger than the description-length ratio. Titles move more than seniority but less than description length.

    Titles unique to each source (sample, non-agg, ≥3 occurrences): see titles_unique_per_source.csv. Scraped-only titles include ai engineer, applied ai engineer, llm engineer, forward deployed engineer, solutions engineer - ai — consistent with a new generative-AI role family.

    Step 6. Industry chi-squared (arshkon vs scraped — asaniczka has no industry)¶

    Non-aggregator, 227 industry labels, n=34,918 (scraped) and 4,146 (arshkon): chi2=2,536, V=0.255.

    Largest industry drops (scraped share minus arshkon share):

    industry arshkon scraped Δ
    IT Services and IT Consulting 25.6% 14.0% −11.7pp
    Information Services 3.4% 0.5% −2.9pp
    Staffing and Recruiting 4.6% 2.3% −2.3pp
    Information Technology & Services 2.7% 1.0% −1.7pp

    Largest industry rises:

    industry arshkon scraped Δ
    Software Development 12.9% 19.2% +6.4pp
    Technology, Information and Internet 2.5% 6.2% +3.6pp
    Defense and Space Manufacturing 2.5% 5.0% +2.4pp
    Information Services + Tech, Information and Internet 0.0% 2.3% +2.3pp

    Artifact diagnosis. LinkedIn changed/merged some industry taxonomy labels between 2024 and 2026 (e.g. the merged "Information Services and Technology, Information and Internet" has 0% in 2024 but 2.3% in 2026). Some of the IT Services drop is real re-categorization into Software Development and Technology/Information. Industry-level change should be interpreted carefully; a crosswalk table would be needed for proper 2024→2026 attribution.

    Step 7. Artifact diagnostic summary¶

    metric noise-floor (arshkon↔asaniczka) arshkon↔scraped asaniczka↔scraped trustworthy?
    description length KS D 0.115 0.321 0.252 yes for direction, caveat boilerplate
    company Jaccard 0.108 0.103 0.132 yes
    seniority V 0.127 0.022 0.117 yes — direction: asaniczka is odd
    state V 0.142 0.269 0.342 no — scraper frame artifact
    title Jaccard (≥3) 0.245 0.102 0.185 yes
    industry V n/a n/a (V=0.255 for this single pair) n/a partly — taxonomy change

    Step 8. Within-2024 calibration — arshkon vs asaniczka¶

    Already reported throughout (table calibration_2024.json). Highlights: - Description length KS D = 0.115 (both 2024 LinkedIn, differ by ~450 chars) - Seniority V = 0.127 (driven by asaniczka's zero-entry artifact) - Company Jaccard = 0.108 - Title (≥3) Jaccard = 0.245 - State V = 0.142

    These are the within-period baseline magnitudes; anything similar in 2024→2026 is noise-level, anything substantially larger is candidate real change + frame artifact.

    Step 9. Platform labeling stability test¶

    Top 20 SWE titles shared between arshkon and scraped (see shared_titles_top20.csv): software engineer, data engineer, devops engineer, java developer, full stack engineer, site reliability engineer, systems engineer, machine learning engineer, system engineer, salesforce developer, embedded software engineer, automation engineer, cloud engineer, software developer, software development engineer, test engineer, frontend developer, java software engineer, product engineer, full stack developer.

    Native-label mid-senior share rises from 2024 to 2026 across nearly every title. A sampling:

    data engineer 51.8% 75.1% +23.3pp

    frontend developer 50.0% 85.1% +35.1pp

    systems engineer 53.3% 69.7% +16.4pp

    (Full table: shared_titles_seniority_native.csv; figure: title_native_seniority_drift.png.)

    By contrast, YOE means on the same titles are roughly stable. Mean yoe_min_years_llm per title, arshkon vs scraped:

    title YOE arshkon YOE scraped Δ
    software engineer 5.05 5.30 +0.25
    data engineer 5.31 5.41 +0.10
    devops engineer 6.06 5.24 -0.82
    java developer 6.58 5.56 -1.01
    site reliability engineer 5.45 5.51 +0.06
    full stack engineer 5.08 5.21 +0.13
    cloud engineer 4.88 5.74 +0.86
    product engineer 3.06 3.28 +0.22
    frontend developer 5.65 5.78 +0.13

    Interpretation. If the same title sees a large seniority_native mid-senior share increase but near-zero YOE change, the parsimonious reading is that LinkedIn's seniority labeling policy — how employer-supplied level fields map to platform labels — shifted between 2024 and 2026. The apparent 2024→2026 "entry-share decline" in seniority_native-only analyses is partly a platform relabeling artifact.

    This is why the T30 panel provides four junior variants — J1/J2 use seniority_final (also affected, since Stage 5 relies on title keywords and Stage 10 labels the LLM frame only), J3/J4 use yoe_min_years_llm (independent of both platform labels and rule-based title keywords). Any entry-share comparison 2024↔2026 should report J3 as primary and J1/J2 as sensitivities.

    Indeed cross-validation for the platform-artifact reading¶

    For the same 20 titles, we have scraped-Indeed rows in addition to scraped-LinkedIn. Indeed is excluded from the LLM frame so yoe_min_years_llm is null — we use rule-based yoe_extracted ≤ 2 as the entry indicator.

    Examples (n ≥ 50 on both platforms):

    title scraped LinkedIn % rule_yoe≤2 scraped Indeed % rule_yoe≤2
    software engineer 20.6% 21.8%
    data engineer 12.2% 14.5%
    devops engineer 12.5% 17.6%
    java developer 20.7% 25.0%
    machine learning engineer n/a n/a
    full stack engineer 14.5% 9.8%

    Rule-based entry-rates are similar between LinkedIn and Indeed for the overlapping title set (Pearson r = 0.65 across 20 shared titles on rule-based yoe≤2 share). Indeed tends slightly toward higher entry rates on some titles (automation engineer, devops engineer, software developer) but the rank-correlation is strong. The LinkedIn 2024→2026 entry-share movement is not a LinkedIn-only platform artifact on YOE-based measures — Indeed shows similar rates in 2026. This weakens a strict "LinkedIn-only artifact" hypothesis for the YOE-based entry measure, even while the seniority_native drift above strongly suggests platform relabeling on the label side.

    Full table: shared_titles_platform_cross.csv.

    Aggregator sensitivity¶

    Re-run of all pairwise tests with aggregators included vs excluded (agg_sensitivity_effect_sizes.csv, seniority_shares_by_agg_mode.csv). Effect-size shifts are small (≤ 0.03 V or KS D). Direction never flips. Aggregator exclusion is a real-decision sensitivity, but it is not a confound that rescues or breaks any of the T05 findings.

    Aggregator rows differ substantively from non-aggregator in seniority shares (see table): aggregators have lower entry share on asaniczka (1%) and higher entry share on arshkon (6%) and scraped (8%). Aggregator exclusion is still warranted for content-level analyses, not because aggregator exclusion fixes the comparability gap but because aggregator postings are a qualitatively different artifact (short descriptions, generic template). See T06 for dedicated aggregator profiling.

    Surprises & unexpected patterns¶

    1. arshkon and scraped are effectively twins on seniority_final distribution (V=0.022). The 2024↔2026 gap on this variable is smaller than the within-2024 gap between arshkon and asaniczka. The common narrative that "entry-level share is collapsing" does not show up in this basic seniority_final-level comparison — they are near-identical. Whatever the entry-share story is, it is not in the aggregate seniority_final distribution.

    2. LinkedIn's own native seniority labels drifted substantially 2024→2026, and this drift is visible at the same-title level. The seniority_native mid-senior share for data engineer rose 23pp; for java developer, 43pp; for frontend developer, 35pp. YOE distributions on the same cells barely moved. The platform-relabeling-not-market-change reading is strong on the native label.

    3. Title vocabulary overlap is surprisingly low even within 2024. Arshkon↔asaniczka raw title Jaccard is 0.05. Both are LinkedIn SWE 2024. This tells us either (a) the SWE classifier is picking up a lot of title long-tail, or (b) LinkedIn's title population has a very long tail of one-offs with mild variation. This matters for any "title vocabulary expansion" Wave 2 claim — the baseline vocab is already very unstable within period.

    4. California's share jumped +8pp in scraped — but this is the scraper design. Any geographic finding based on scraped state shares, or on scraped/arshkon state-share ratios, is unreliable. Metro-restricted analyses will be more defensible.

    5. Asaniczka top-50 is aggregator-heavy (ClearanceJobs, Jobs for Humanity, ClickJobs.io). If the top-company set drives a finding, asaniczka's top is systematically different in genre from arshkon/scraped — it is plausibly overweighted in defense/cleared roles.

    6. "Jobs for Humanity" appears twice in asaniczka's top companies — 571 postings under the canonical name "Capital One" (because it is detected as an aggregator and the real employer extracted to Capital One) and 185 under its own name. This dual-resolution is a real company-canonicalization edge case — the aggregator splits into a real-employer-resolved cohort and an unresolved cohort. Downstream analyses should be aware that aggregator real-employer resolution is partial.

    7. Indeed YOE-based entry rates track LinkedIn-scraped on the same titles. This was not guaranteed — Indeed could have had a different seniority-entry composition, but it does not. This is weak corroboration that scraped-LinkedIn's YOE-based entry-share shifts are not a LinkedIn-only platform artifact.

    What to trust downstream¶

    Trustworthy for 2024↔2026 comparison: - Mean yoe_min_years_llm per title (stable across the platform-stability check) - seniority_final distribution at aggregate level (V=0.022 for arshkon↔scraped — so any entry-share direction must be carefully checked, but the aggregate is stable) - Top-50 employer overlap (Jaccard 0.22 arshkon↔scraped)

    Interpretable but use care: - Description length (partly boilerplate growth, partly content growth — use description_core_llm) - Company Jaccard on the full population (low everywhere — the "measured market" is mostly disjoint) - Industry distribution (taxonomy changes — needs crosswalk)

    Avoid as primary: - seniority_native as a 2024→2026 entry measure (platform drift) - Geographic state shares on scraped vs Kaggle (scraper frame artifact) - Title vocabulary overlap without a count floor (long-tail noise)

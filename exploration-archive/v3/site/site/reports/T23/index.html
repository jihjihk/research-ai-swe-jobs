    T23: Employer-Requirement / Worker-Usage Divergence

    Status: Complete
    Purpose: Compare posting-side AI requirements against worker-side AI usage benchmarks for RQ3.

    Executive Summary

    Posting AI requirements lag developer usage rates, but the gap is closing fast. In 2024, only ~12% of SWE postings mentioned any AI requirement while ~57% of developers reported using AI tools -- a ~45pp gap. By 2026, posting requirements surged to ~41% while usage estimates reached ~75% -- a ~34pp gap.

    The direction of the gap is posting-BELOW-usage, not above. Employers are catching up to reality, not inflating beyond it. However, there is a critical asymmetry: posting requirements grew 29pp (250% relative) versus usage growing 18pp (32% relative). Requirements are converging toward usage at a rate that, if sustained, would reach parity by late 2027.

    One exception: AI-as-domain (ML, DL, NLP) requirements now slightly exceed domain-specialist usage rates (22.7% posting vs ~20% usage), suggesting some overshoot in ML/AI-specific requirements.

    1. AI Requirement Rates in SWE Postings

    By Category and Period

    Category 2024-01 2024-04 2026-03 Change (pp)
    AI-as-tool (Copilot, LLM, prompt eng.) 1.7% 1.7% 23.3% +21.6
    AI-as-domain (ML, DL, NLP, CV) 11.1% 8.5% 22.7% +12.1
    AI-general (AI, artificial intelligence) 2.8% 2.7% 17.5% +14.7
    Any AI 12.0% 9.7% 40.6% +29.0

    The most striking shift is AI-as-tool: from virtually zero (1.7%) to 23.3%. This category barely existed in posting requirements in 2024 and now appears in nearly one-quarter of SWE postings.

    By Seniority (2026)

    Seniority AI-any AI-tool AI-domain
    Junior 38.2% 18.7% 25.4%
    Mid 30.2% 20.7% 14.8%
    Senior 40.3% 24.3% 21.2%

    Junior AI-domain rates are notably high (25.4%), higher than senior (21.2%). This is somewhat surprising and may reflect the inclusion of ML Engineer intern/new-grad roles in the entry-level bucket, or it may indicate employers seeking AI/ML capabilities in junior hires who grew up with the technology.

    Senior roles have the highest AI-tool rates (24.3%), consistent with the expectation that experienced engineers are being asked to integrate AI coding tools.

    Specific Tool Mention Rates (%)

    Tool 2024-01 2024-04 2026-03
    LLM 0.45 1.00 9.87
    Copilot 0.04 0.14 3.88
    Claude 0.01 0.02 3.89
    Prompt engineering 0.19 0.28 3.81
    Cursor 0.00 0.00 2.35
    ChatGPT 0.33 0.02 0.64
    GPT-N 0.06 0.04 0.50

    LLM is the dominant AI-tool term at nearly 10% of all SWE postings. Copilot and Claude are nearly tied at ~3.9%, with Cursor close behind at 2.4%. ChatGPT, which was the most-mentioned specific tool in early 2024, has been overtaken by developer-focused tools. This mirrors the shift from consumer AI chatbots to purpose-built developer tools.

    2. External Usage Benchmarks

    Source Metric Value Year
    StackOverflow Dev Survey 2024 Currently using AI tools 62% 2024
    JetBrains Dev Ecosystem 2024 AI assistant usage 52% 2024
    GitHub (Oct 2024) Copilot adoption 35% 2024
    Anthropic Economic Index (Feb 2025) SWE tasks with AI augmentation 37% 2025
    Industry consensus Developer AI tool adoption ~75% 2026 (est.)

    Key nuance: The Anthropic Economic Index measures task-level augmentation (37% of SWE tasks), not person-level adoption. The StackOverflow and JetBrains numbers measure whether a developer uses AI at all (52-62%). GitHub's number measures paid Copilot subscribers specifically.

    The 2026 estimate of 75% is an extrapolation based on ~10pp annual growth from the 52-62% 2024 baseline, consistent with industry trajectory.

    3. Divergence Computation

    Posting Requirements vs Developer Usage

    Year Category Posting rate Usage rate (est.) Gap (pp) Ratio
    2024 AI-any 11.6% 57.0% -45.4 0.20
    2024 AI-tool 1.7% 35.0% -33.3 0.05
    2024 AI-domain 10.6% 15.0% -4.4 0.71
    2026 AI-any 40.6% 75.0% -34.4 0.54
    2026 AI-tool 23.3% 55.0% -31.7 0.42
    2026 AI-domain 22.7% 20.0% +2.7 1.14

    Key findings:

    1. AI-any: The gap narrowed from -45pp to -34pp. Posting requirements are closing the gap but still trail usage by a wide margin. Posts express AI requirements at 54% the rate developers actually use AI (up from 20%).

    2. AI-tool: Massive catch-up from near-zero. Posting AI-tool rates were 0.05x usage in 2024 (essentially no postings mentioned coding AI tools despite 35% usage). By 2026, the ratio improved to 0.42x. Still a large gap, but the trajectory is steep.

    3. AI-domain: Near parity or slight overshoot. ML/DL/NLP requirements (22.7%) now slightly exceed the estimated specialist usage rate (~20%). This may indicate some aspirational ML hiring, or it may reflect a genuine expansion of ML-adjacent work in SWE roles.

    Temporal Divergence

    Metric Growth 2024->2026 (pp) Relative growth
    Posting AI-any rate +29.0 +250%
    Developer AI usage (est.) +18.0 +32%
    Ratio (posting/usage growth) 1.6x pp 7.9x relative

    Posting requirements are growing approximately 1.6x faster in percentage points and 7.9x faster in relative terms than developer usage. This convergence rate suggests the gap will close to near-parity within 1-2 years if trends continue.

    4. Divergence by Specificity

    Period Specific tool mentions Generic AI mentions
    2024-01 2.9% 12.9%
    2024-04 2.7% 14.8%
    2026-03 13.9% 53.4%

    Generic AI mentions (AI, ML) are far more common than specific tool mentions (Copilot, Cursor, ChatGPT). However, specific mentions grew faster in relative terms (2.9% to 13.9%, 4.8x) than generic (12.9% to 53.4%, 4.1x). This suggests employers are moving from vague AI signaling to concrete tool requirements, though generic language still dominates.

    5. Aggregator Sensitivity

    Direct postings Aggregator postings
    2024 AI-any 9.0-10.3% 5.4-20.3%
    2026 AI-any 42.5% 29.5%
    2026 AI-tool 24.2% 18.2%
    2026 AI-domain 24.3% 13.4%

    Direct postings consistently show higher AI requirement rates than aggregator postings. The gap is 13pp for AI-any in 2026 (42.5% vs 29.5%). This makes sense: aggregators strip or simplify job descriptions, losing AI-specific language.

    When excluding aggregators, the overall 2026 AI rate rises from 40.6% to 42.5%. The core divergence finding is not aggregator-driven.

    The 2024-01 aggregator anomaly (20.3% AI-any) comes from a smaller, potentially unrepresentative aggregator subsample.

    6. Interpretation for the Paper

    What RQ3 Can Claim

    1. Posting AI requirements dramatically lag actual developer AI usage. In 2026, ~41% of SWE postings mention AI while an estimated ~75% of developers use AI tools. The gap is narrowing but still substantial.

    2. The catch-up is primarily in AI-as-tool. Requirements for coding-specific AI tools (Copilot, LLM, prompt engineering) went from near-zero to 23%. This is the fastest-growing category and most directly comparable to the usage benchmarks.

    3. AI-as-domain requirements may have overshot actual specialist rates. At 22.7% of SWE postings requiring ML/DL/NLP, the posting rate exceeds the estimated ~20% of developers who are actual ML specialists. This may reflect genuine expansion of ML-adjacent work or some aspirational hiring.

    4. The shift from aspirational to firm AI language (from T22) reinforces that posting AI requirements are becoming operational, not performative.

    Caveats

    • Usage benchmarks come from surveys with different populations and definitions. Direct comparison requires careful framing.
    • The 2026 usage estimate (75%) is extrapolated, not directly observed.
    • Posting rates measure whether AI is mentioned at all, not the weight or centrality of the AI requirement.
    • The "AI-any" rate of 53.4% for generic mentions includes \bai\b which matches many false positives (abbreviations, etc.). The structured categories (tool: 23.3%, domain: 22.7%) are more reliable.

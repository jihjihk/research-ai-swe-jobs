    T07: External Benchmarks & Power Analysis

    Generated: 2026-04-05 Data source: preprocessing/intermediate/stage8_final.parquet (Stage 8) Default filters: source_platform='linkedin' AND is_english=true AND date_flag='ok'

    Executive Summary

    Our LinkedIn SWE posting samples show strong geographic representativeness (Pearson r = 0.97 for arshkon, r = 0.98 for scraped vs BLS OES state-level employment). Industry distributions over-represent Professional/Technical Services relative to BLS OES, consistent with LinkedIn's user base skew. JOLTS information sector openings were substantially lower during our 2024 collection periods than peak 2022 levels, and had partially recovered by early 2026.

    Power analysis verdict: The primary comparisons (entry-level arshkon vs scraped, senior, and all SWE) are well-powered for detecting small-to-moderate effects (d = 0.04-0.11). Metro-level analyses are feasible for 8-18 metros depending on the minimum threshold. Company-panel analysis is well-powered in aggregate (202 companies, 8,948 postings) but underpowered at the individual-company level. The arshkon-asaniczka entry-level comparison (n=830 vs n=130) and arshkon-scraped mid-level comparison (n=228 vs n=622) are adequate but not generous.

    1. Geographic Representativeness

    1.1 State-Level Correlation with BLS OES

    Dataset N states Pearson r Spearman rho Log-scale Pearson r
    Arshkon (2024-04) 47 0.969 0.949 0.961
    Scraped (2026-03) 31 0.983 0.896 0.796

    Interpretation: - Arshkon covers 47 of 51 states/DC (missing HI, SD, VT, WY), representing 99.5% of BLS SWE employment. Its geographic distribution is an excellent match to BLS OES at both raw and log scale. - Scraped covers only 31 states because the scraper targets 26 metros, not all states. Among covered states, raw correlation is slightly higher (r=0.983) but log-scale correlation is lower (0.796) because the scraper's metro-targeted design produces sharper concentration in tech-hub states. The 20 missing states represent 7.6% of BLS SWE employment. - The scraped data's lower log-scale r reflects its metro-targeted sampling design: it oversamples high-employment states (CA, TX, WA, NY) relative to small states. This is by design, not a defect, but it means scraped cannot support state-level analyses for smaller states.

    Target met: Both datasets exceed the r > 0.80 geographic benchmark by substantial margins.

    Key observations: - Our data over-represents Professional/Technical Services by ~7-9 percentage points relative to BLS OES. This is expected: LinkedIn's user base skews toward tech, consulting, and professional services. - Manufacturing and Information sectors are under-represented in our data (~3-5% vs ~10% in BLS OES), likely because many manufacturing-sector SWE roles are posted on different platforms or through internal channels. - Finance & Insurance representation is close to BLS OES. - Staffing/recruiting postings (5.5% arshkon, 2.5% scraped) have no BLS OES equivalent — these are aggregator postings that BLS would count under the actual employer's industry. - The "Other" category is large in scraped (33.8%) partly because LinkedIn's industry labels are finer-grained and many don't map cleanly to a single NAICS sector.

    Implication: Our sample is most representative of Professional/Technical Services SWE. Findings about manufacturing or information-sector SWE should be treated with caution due to underrepresentation.

    4. Representativeness Summary

    4.1 What Population Does Our Sample Represent?

    Dimension Coverage Representativeness
    Platform LinkedIn only (primary), Indeed (sensitivity) Represents LinkedIn-posted SWE roles. Excludes company career pages, Greenhouse/Lever ATS-only postings, and non-LinkedIn platforms.
    Geography 47 states (arshkon), 31 states (scraped), 26-metro frame Excellent correlation with BLS OES state distribution. Scraped concentrated in major metros by design.
    Industry Professional/Technical Services over-represented LinkedIn skews toward tech-adjacent employers. Manufacturing and information-sector SWE underrepresented.
    Company size Large employers over-represented in scraped (Google, JPMorgan, Deloitte dominate) LinkedIn favors established companies with active recruiting presence.
    Seniority 81% known (arshkon), ~100% known (asaniczka, scraped) for SWE Good coverage, but asaniczka has zero entry-level labels. Entry-level baseline relies solely on arshkon.
    Time Snapshots at Jan 2024, Apr 2024, Mar 2026 Not continuous panel. Cross-sectional comparisons only. Cyclical effects not separable from structural shifts.

    4.2 What We Can and Cannot Generalize

    Can generalize to: - LinkedIn-posted SWE roles in major US metros - Posting content and requirement trends among established employers - Relative geographic distribution of SWE demand - Cross-period changes in posting composition at the aggregate level

    Cannot generalize to: - All SWE jobs (many are never posted on LinkedIn) - Small companies and startups (underrepresented) - Manufacturing-sector or government SWE roles - International markets - Actual employment (postings measure labor demand signals, not hires) - Individual-company trajectories (panel is too sparse for most companies)

    4.3 Key Confounds for Cross-Period Comparison

    1. Description length grew ~56% from 2024 to 2026 (median 3,316 to 5,181 chars for SWE). Any text-based measure MUST be normalized by description length.
    2. Company overlap is only ~18% between arshkon and scraped. Composition effects from different company mixes could drive observed differences.
    3. JOLTS context: The arshkon period (Apr 2024, 87K openings) was a relative trough; the scraped period (Mar 2026, ~95K) was slightly higher but still subdued. Cyclical position differs modestly.
    4. Seniority label provenance differs: arshkon entry-level labels are 88% native_backfill vs 80% in scraped. Label construction should be checked for artifacts.

    5. Power Analysis

    5.1 Method

    Minimum detectable effect sizes (MDE) computed for two-sample comparisons at 80% power, alpha = 0.05 (two-tailed). - Binary outcomes (e.g., presence of a keyword): MDE for difference in proportions, using normal approximation. - Continuous outcomes (e.g., embedding similarity, YOE): MDE expressed as Cohen's d.

    5.2 Cross-Period Comparisons

    Comparison n1 n2 MDE (d) MDE (prop, base=50%) MDE (prop, base=10%) Verdict
    Entry-level: arshkon vs scraped 830 3,255 0.109 5.4 pp 3.3 pp well-powered
    Senior: arshkon vs scraped 3,008 18,592 0.055 2.8 pp 1.7 pp well-powered
    All SWE: arshkon vs scraped 5,019 24,095 0.044 2.2 pp 1.3 pp well-powered
    Entry: arshkon vs asaniczka 830 130 0.264 13.2 pp 7.9 pp adequate
    Mid-level: arshkon vs scraped 228 622 0.217 10.8 pp 6.5 pp adequate
    Senior: pooled 2024 vs scraped 24,641 18,592 0.027 1.4 pp 0.8 pp well-powered
    All SWE: pooled 2024 vs scraped 28,232 24,095 0.025 1.2 pp 0.7 pp well-powered
    Entry (native_backfill only) 729 2,592 0.118 5.7 pp 3.5 pp well-powered

    Key takeaways: - The primary entry-level comparison (arshkon vs scraped) can detect effects as small as d=0.11, or a 3.3 pp shift in a 10%-prevalence keyword. This is sufficient for the hypothesized scope-inflation effects. - Pooling asaniczka + arshkon for 2024 dramatically improves power for senior and all-SWE comparisons (d = 0.025-0.027), but pooling is only valid if source composition effects are controlled for (since asaniczka and arshkon differ in company mix, seniority labels, and description length). - The arshkon-asaniczka entry-level comparison (n=130 asaniczka) is adequate but only for large effects. Asaniczka entry-level labels are rule-imputed (130 rows) and should be treated as a cross-check, not a primary analysis. - The mid-level comparison (n=228 arshkon mid) is the weakest cross-period comparison, with MDE of d=0.22. Only substantial effects will be detectable.

    5.3 Interpretation of Effect Sizes

    For context, what do these MDE values mean in practical terms?

    MDE (Cohen's d) Meaning for continuous outcomes Meaning for binary (base=20%)
    0.05 ~0.05 SD shift in embedding similarity ~1.7 pp shift
    0.10 ~0.10 SD shift ~3.3 pp shift
    0.20 ~0.20 SD shift (small effect) ~6.3 pp shift
    0.50 ~0.50 SD shift (medium effect) ~14.6 pp shift

    The hypothesized effects (scope inflation, archetype shift) are expected to be moderate (d = 0.20-0.50), so all primary comparisons are adequately powered.

    7.3 Panel Power Analysis

    • Aggregate panel (1,875 vs 7,073 postings): MDE d = 0.073, well-powered. This is T16's primary design — comparing posting content within the same company across periods.
    • Individual company (~9 vs ~35 postings): MDE d = 1.05, underpowered. Within-company analyses at the single-company level are not feasible for most companies.
    • Recommendation: Use the company panel for within-company fixed-effects analysis at the aggregate level. Company-specific trajectories should be limited to the ~10 largest panel companies (Capital One, Deloitte, Amazon, Google, etc.).

    7.4 Panel Composition Concerns

    • The panel is dominated by large employers (FAANG, banks, defense contractors, staffing agencies). Small/medium companies rarely have >= 3 SWE postings in both periods.
    • Staffing agencies (Motion Recruitment, Apex Systems, Actalent) contribute significantly. These may reflect labor-market intermediary behavior rather than direct employer restructuring.
    • DataAnnotation's high arshkon count (163) but low scraped count (38) likely reflects a specific recruitment campaign, not a structural trend.

    9. Implications for Wave 2+ Analysis Design

    9.1 Analyses That Should Proceed

    1. RQ1 junior scope inflation (entry-level arshkon vs scraped): Well-powered, proceed as planned. The n=830 arshkon entry-level sample is the binding constraint but sufficient for d=0.11 detection.
    2. RQ1 senior archetype shift (senior arshkon vs scraped): Well-powered (d=0.055). Even subtle shifts in requirement language should be detectable.
    3. All-SWE aggregate comparisons: Extremely well-powered. Any planned descriptive analyses at the all-SWE level will have more than sufficient statistical resolution.
    4. Company-panel within-firm analysis (T16): Well-powered in aggregate. Proceed with 202-company panel using company fixed effects.
    5. Metro-level heterogeneity: Feasible for 18 metros (>= 50 per period). Use SF Bay Area, DC, and Dallas as anchor metros.

    9.2 Analyses Requiring Caution

    1. Mid-level cross-period comparison (n=228 arshkon mid): Adequate power only for moderate effects (d >= 0.22). Consider pooling mid + senior into a "non-junior" category for better power.
    2. Arshkon-asaniczka entry-level comparison (n=130 asaniczka entry): Asaniczka entry-level labels are rule-imputed and small (n=130). Use only as directional cross-check, not primary evidence.
    3. Metro-level entry-level analysis: Not assessed here, but entry-level counts per metro are likely very small. Expect most metro-level entry analyses to be underpowered.

    9.3 Analyses That Are Not Feasible

    1. NYC-specific arshkon vs scraped (n=13 arshkon): Not viable. NYC comparisons require asaniczka (n=89) or should be dropped.
    2. Individual company trajectories: Only ~10 companies have enough postings for company-specific analysis.
    3. State-level analysis using scraped data for small states: The scraper covers only 31 states; 20 states are missing entirely.

    10. Data Quality Notes

    • BLS OES data: May 2023 estimates for SOC 15-1252 used as the benchmark. These are survey-based estimates with their own sampling variability, but are the best available external anchor for SWE employment by state.
    • JOLTS data: Retrieved via BLS public API (Series JTS510000000000000JOL). Seasonally adjusted information sector openings in thousands.
    • Industry mapping: The BLS-to-LinkedIn industry mapping is approximate. LinkedIn industry labels are self-reported by companies and do not map cleanly to NAICS sectors. The industry comparison should be treated as directional, not precise.
    • Seniority counts: The "3,255 scraped entry-level" includes both native_backfill (2,592) and title_keyword (661) sources. These are substantially larger than the arshkon entry-level count because the scraper specifically targeted entry-level search queries.
    • Sample size note: The task brief referenced n=574 scraped entry and n=3,003/3,459 for mid-senior. The current stage8_final.parquet yields n=3,255 scraped entry and n=18,592 scraped senior (seniority_3level). This difference is due to data growth from continued scraping. The higher counts only improve power.

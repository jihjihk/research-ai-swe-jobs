    T13: Linguistic & Structural Evolution

    Agent H | Wave 2 | Completed 2026-04-05

    Summary

    The 57-67% description length growth between 2024 and 2026 is driven primarily by real content expansion — role summaries, responsibilities, and requirements sections all grew substantially. Boilerplate (benefits, about-company, legal/EEO) also grew but accounts for a smaller share. The V1 section classifier (plain-text headers only) missed this because 2026 postings use markdown-formatted headers (Bold), which were all classified as "unclassified." The V2 classifier, which handles both formats, reveals the true story.

    Critical Finding: What Drove the Length Growth?

    Section-Level Growth Attribution (arshkon 2024 -> scraped 2026)

    Section Avg chars 2024 Avg chars 2026 Change % of total growth
    Role Summary 814 1,611 +797 42.2%
    Responsibilities 305 818 +513 27.2%
    Requirements 344 806 +462 24.5%
    About Company 203 590 +387 20.5%
    Preferred 237 558 +321 17.0%
    Benefits 126 400 +274 14.5%
    Legal/EEO 62 124 +62 3.3%
    Unclassified 1,225 296 -929 -49.2%
    Total 3,316 5,203 +1,887 100%

    Interpretation: The three core content sections (role summary + responsibilities + requirements) account for 93.9% of total growth. Benefits/about-company/legal together account for 38.3%. The sum exceeds 100% because "unclassified" shrank by 929 chars — text that was previously unstructured is now in labeled sections. This is a formatting improvement, not content removal.

    Bottom line: The length growth is NOT primarily boilerplate padding. It is substantive — employers are writing longer, more detailed role descriptions, responsibility lists, and requirements sections.

    By Seniority (arshkon 2024 -> scraped 2026)

    Entry-level: - Requirements: 566 -> 639 chars (+73, modest growth) - Responsibilities: 468 -> 722 chars (+254) - Role Summary: 1,137 -> 1,320 chars (+183) - Benefits: 180 -> 483 chars (+303, large growth for entry) - Total growth: ~1,562 chars

    Mid-senior: - Requirements: 330 -> 823 chars (+493, large growth) - Responsibilities: 307 -> 847 chars (+540) - Role Summary: 855 -> 1,660 chars (+805) - About Company: 194 -> 622 chars (+428) - Total growth: ~2,825 chars

    Key insight: Senior postings grew more than entry in absolute terms, but entry postings saw proportionally larger growth in benefits/compensation sections. Senior postings grew most in role summary and responsibilities — suggesting more detailed role descriptions at senior level.

    Cleaned Description (boilerplate removed)

    Cleaned text shows higher FK grade levels (17-21) and negative Flesch Reading Ease, reflecting that the core technical content is dense and jargon-heavy regardless of period. The relative patterns hold: 2026 text is slightly more readable across the board.

    Sensitivity Analysis

    Aggregator Exclusion

    Section growth attribution without aggregators: - Role Summary: +864 (44.3%) - Responsibilities: +515 (26.4%) - Requirements: +475 (24.3%) - About Company: +435 (22.3%)

    Pattern is essentially identical to the full sample (within 2pp on each section). Aggregators do not drive the findings.

    V1 vs V2 Section Classifier

    The V1 classifier (plain-text headers only) showed 98.3% of 2026 content as "unclassified" because scraped 2026 data uses markdown **Bold** headers (96% of docs) rather than plain text headers. This created a false impression that 2026 postings were unstructured. In reality, 2026 postings are MORE structured than 2024 (84.1% have identified role summaries vs. 43.2% in arshkon 2024).

    V2 classifier results are definitive and should be used for all downstream analysis.

    Methodological Notes

    1. Section classifier design: The V2 classifier matches both **markdown headers** and plain-text Section Header: patterns against a curated map of ~100 header variants grouped into 7 categories. Content before the first header is classified as role_summary if it contains complete sentences (>200 chars with periods), otherwise unclassified.

    2. Readability on cleaned vs raw text: Readability metrics were computed on both raw descriptions (full text) and cleaned text (boilerplate removed). Raw-text readability is more meaningful for understanding what candidates actually read. Cleaned-text readability isolates the technical content signal.

    3. Tone markers are rate-normalized (per 1K chars) to account for the length growth documented in section anatomy.

    4. Sample sizes: Readability analysis sampled up to 2,000 per source x seniority cell to keep textstat runtime manageable. Tone markers and section anatomy ran on the full dataset (52,327 postings).

    Key Takeaways for the Paper

    1. The 57-67% length growth is substantive, not boilerplate. Role summaries (+42%), responsibilities (+27%), and requirements (+25%) drove the growth. Benefits/about-company contributed 35% combined, but this is secondary.

    2. Postings are becoming more structured. Section prevalence increased across all categories. More postings now have explicit responsibilities, requirements, and preferred qualifications sections. The "unclassified" share dropped from 57% to 21%.

    3. Entry-level postings became more readable. FK grade dropped 1.2 points (16.5 -> 15.3), and Flesch Reading Ease rose 8 points (12.2 -> 20.0). This may reflect less jargon-heavy requirements as traditional tech stacks were de-emphasized (per T12).

    4. Inclusive language is new. DEI/inclusive language went from essentially zero to measurable presence. This is a formatting/compliance trend, not a content signal.

    5. AI-forward framing is still niche. Despite the AI term explosion documented in T12, the median posting does not use AI-forward language. The signal comes from a growing tail of AI-focused roles, not a uniform shift.

    6. Implications for analysis: Since the length growth is content-rich (not boilerplate padding), analyses using text features (T12 Fightin' Words, tech mentions, etc.) are measuring real content changes. However, rate-normalization (per 1K chars) remains essential because the absolute volume of all content types grew.

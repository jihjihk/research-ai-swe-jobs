    T16: Company Hiring Strategy Typology

    Status: Done Agent: J (Wave 3) Date: 2026-04-05

    Summary

    Among 451 companies with 3+ SWE postings in both 2024 and 2026 (the "overlap panel"), we cluster hiring-strategy trajectories and decompose aggregate market changes into within-company vs compositional components. The central finding: compositional effects (new companies entering, others exiting) account for 53-59% of aggregate changes, while within-company shifts account for 41-47%. All four identified company strategy clusters show AI adoption increases -- the variation is in magnitude and what accompanies it. At the company level, AI adoption and entry-share change are uncorrelated (r = -0.070, p = 0.138), refuting a simple "AI replaces juniors" causal story at the firm level.

    1. Overlap Panel Construction

    • SWE base: 52,327 postings (LinkedIn, English, date_flag=ok, is_swe=true)
    • Era mapping: 2024-01 + 2024-04 pooled as "2024" (28,232 postings); 2026-03 as "2026" (24,095 postings)
    • Overlap companies: 451 with >= 3 SWE in both eras (22,929 postings, 43.8% of all SWE)
    • Company universe: 6,503 unique companies in 2024; 5,100 in 2026; 1,539 in both; 4,964 exit-only; 3,561 enter-only

    The overlap panel is the most methodologically rigorous subset for detecting true hiring strategy changes, as it controls for company composition. However, it represents only 44% of postings, so compositional effects from entry/exit matter significantly.

    2. Company Strategy Clusters (k=4)

    Silhouette analysis favored k=2 (silhouette=0.215), but k=4 (silhouette=0.203) provides more interpretable typology. The k=2 solution simply separates high-change from low-change companies. We report k=4 for interpretation and include k=2 as a robustness note.

    Cluster profiles (change from 2024 to 2026)

    Cluster n Name Entry share AI prev Desc length Tech count Org scope Volume
    C0 108 Scope inflators +1.6pp +12.8pp +2,759 chars +3.1 +3.4 terms ~flat
    C1 49 Entry expanders +53.9pp +8.0pp +259 chars +0.7 -0.4 terms ~flat
    C2 98 AI-forward +4.2pp +44.3pp +659 chars +4.6 +0.4 terms ~flat
    C3 196 Traditional hold +0.6pp +4.8pp +579 chars +0.8 +0.2 terms slight growth

    Cluster interpretation:

    • C0 "Scope inflators" (24%): Moderate AI adoption but massive description growth (+2,759 chars) and scope inflation (+3.4 org-scope terms). These companies are dramatically expanding what they write in postings. Includes AMD, Adobe, Accenture.

    • C1 "Entry expanders" (11%): Dramatic entry-share increase (+54pp) with minimal AI adoption or scope change. These are companies that shifted from posting almost no entry-level roles to posting many. Includes Abbott, ASRC Federal, Aditi Consulting. Descriptions actually slightly shorter. Many are staffing/consulting firms.

    • C2 "AI-forward" (22%): Highest AI adoption (+44pp) with significant tech-count growth (+4.6 techs). These companies aggressively incorporated AI requirements. Includes AT&T, AbbVie, Akamai Technologies.

    • C3 "Traditional hold" (43%): Minimal change across all metrics. The modal company strategy is effectively standing pat. Entry share barely moves, AI adoption is modest. The largest cluster by far.

    Key insight: AI adoption and entry decline are uncorrelated at company level

    At the company level, the correlation between AI prevalence change and entry-share change is r = -0.070 (p = 0.138). This is a null result -- companies that adopted more AI did not systematically reduce entry-level hiring. This challenges any simple "AI replaces juniors" narrative and suggests the aggregate correlation (both AI and entry share rising) operates through industry-level or market-level mechanisms rather than within-firm substitution.

    3. Within-Company vs Compositional Decomposition

    This is the paper's strongest analytical design for distinguishing genuine strategic shifts from compositional artifacts. We decompose aggregate metric changes into:

    • Within-overlap: Changes among the 451 companies present in both periods (weighted by their share of total postings)
    • Composition: The residual, driven by new entrants, exiting companies, and share reallocation
    Metric Total change Within-overlap Within % Composition Composition %
    Entry share +10.1pp +4.4pp 43.3% +5.7pp 56.7%
    AI prevalence +21.0pp +8.7pp 41.4% +12.3pp 58.6%
    Description length +1,610 chars +763 chars 47.4% +847 chars 52.6%

    Critical findings:

    1. Composition dominates entry share change (57%). The entry-share rise is majority-compositional: new 2026 entrants post at 13.4% entry vs. exiting 2024 companies that posted at 3.3%. This means the aggregate entry-share increase is driven more by which companies are hiring changing than by individual companies changing strategy.

    2. Composition dominates AI adoption (59%). New 2026 entrants arrive with 24.3% AI prevalence vs 2.5% among 2024 exits. The market is being repopulated with AI-native employers.

    3. Description length is more balanced (47% within). Within-company content growth is substantial, suggesting genuine posting strategy changes, not just new companies writing longer postings.

    4. Within-company changes are still real and large. Overlap companies increased entry share from 3.6% to 13.6% (+10.0pp within the panel), AI prevalence from 3.4% to 23.3% (+19.9pp), and description length from 2,758 to 4,503 (+1,745 chars). These are genuine strategy shifts, but they are amplified by compositional turnover.

    Implication for paper narrative

    The aggregate trends reported in Waves 1-2 are a mixture of within-company behavioral change (~42%) and market recomposition (~58%). This does not invalidate the aggregate trends -- compositional shifts are themselves a real market-level phenomenon (the type of company posting SWE jobs is changing). But it means the interpretation should be "the SWE labor market is being restructured" rather than "individual employers are restructuring their SWE hiring."

    4. New Market Entrants Profile

    Metric New entrants (2026) Continuing companies (2026) Difference
    Entry share 14.9% 12.5% +2.4pp
    AI prevalence 25.2% 22.9% +2.3pp
    Description length 4,134 4,449 -315
    Tech count 8.2 8.5 -0.3
    Org scope 3.4 4.0 -0.6

    New 2026 entrants are slightly more AI-oriented and entry-heavy than continuing companies, but write slightly shorter, less scope-inflated postings. They are not dramatically different -- the compositional effect comes more from the exit of low-AI, low-entry 2024 companies than from distinctive new entrants.

    Industry of new entrants: Software Development (35%), IT Services (8%), Technology/Internet (7%), Financial Services (6%), Staffing (2%). This matches the general SWE employer distribution.

    5. Aggregator vs Direct Employer Patterns

    Group 2024 Entry 2026 Entry 2024 AI 2026 AI 2024 Desc len 2026 Desc len
    Aggregator 2.9% 26.1% 3.9% 20.2% 2,236 3,415
    Direct 3.6% 11.4% 2.6% 24.5% 2,856 4,468
    • Aggregators show 9x entry surge (2.9% to 26.1%) vs 3x for direct employers (3.6% to 11.4%). This is consistent with staffing firms filling the entry-level pipeline.
    • Direct employers show higher AI adoption (24.5% vs 20.2% in 2026), suggesting AI requirements originate with the actual employers.
    • Direct employers write longer postings in both periods, with larger absolute growth.

    Among overlap companies, the 54 aggregator companies show larger entry-share increases (+10.7pp) than the 397 direct employers (+6.2pp), but similar AI prevalence increases.

    6. Sensitivity: Aggregator Exclusion

    Metric Total (baseline) Within % (baseline) Total (no agg) Within % (no agg) Direction change?
    Entry share +10.1pp 43.3% +7.8pp 37.9% No
    AI prevalence +21.0pp 41.4% +21.9pp 35.3% No
    Description length +1,610 47.4% +1,612 35.3% No

    Excluding aggregators: - Reduces entry share change by 23% (from +10.1pp to +7.8pp) -- aggregators amplify the entry surge - AI prevalence is stable (essentially unchanged) - Description length is unchanged - Within-company share drops to 35-38%, meaning composition becomes even more dominant without aggregators - No direction changes; no materiality threshold breached (30%) - Overlap panel shrinks from 451 to 397 companies

    Surprises & Unexpected Patterns

    1. Entry share INCREASED, not decreased, in the overlap panel. The T03/T06 finding of aggregate junior share decline using seniority_native (arshkon-only entry labels) is not replicated using seniority_3level across the full SWE panel. Using seniority_3level, entry share went from 3.4% to 13.5% (+10.1pp). This apparent contradiction arises because seniority_3level captures more entry labels (especially in scraped data with 87.8% non-unknown) than the narrow arshkon-native baseline. The prior finding of decline depends on the specific operationalization and the thin arshkon-only historical baseline (89 entry SWE postings).

    2. The "traditional hold" cluster is the largest (43%). Despite dramatic aggregate changes, the most common company strategy is doing essentially nothing different. The aggregate shift is driven by a minority of active-strategy companies plus composition.

    3. AI adoption and entry expansion are orthogonal at firm level (r = -0.070). Companies that went "AI-forward" did not systematically cut junior roles. The few that massively expanded entry hiring (C1) are not the AI adopters. This suggests separate mechanisms.

    4. New entrants are not distinctive. The compositional effect comes from the removal of 2024 companies more than the addition of 2026 companies. Exit companies had 2.5% AI prevalence and 3.3% entry share; new entrants have 25.2% AI and 14.9% entry -- both close to the 2026 market average.

    Methodology Notes

    • Seniority operationalization: Uses seniority_3level (junior/mid/senior/unknown) for entry share. This includes seniority_imputed plus seniority_native backfill. Results differ from T03/T06 which used seniority_native on arshkon-only.
    • AI prevalence: Binary indicator for any AI technology keyword (17 AI-family columns from shared tech matrix).
    • Org scope: Regex count of management/leadership/scope terms in description_core.
    • Clustering: k-means on standardized change vectors. k=4 chosen for interpretability (k=2 wins silhouette but provides less insight).
    • Decomposition: Posting-weighted shift-share. Overlap companies' share: ~45% in 2024, ~43% in 2026.
